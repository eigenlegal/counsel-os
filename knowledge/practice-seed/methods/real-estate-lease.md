## Real Estate Lease

# Real Estate Lease Checklist

## Premises and Use

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- Premises clearly described with suite number, floor, and square footage — ambiguous premises descriptions cause disputes about what space is included
- Measurement standard specified (BOMA, usable vs. rentable) — rentable square footage includes common area load factor; usable is actual space
- Permitted use clause broad enough for current and anticipated business operations — overly narrow use clauses prevent business pivot without landlord consent
- Exclusive use provision protects against competing tenants (if applicable) — in retail, a competitor in the same building can destroy your business
- Building systems and utilities serving the premises identified — HVAC, electrical, plumbing capacity must support your operations

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- Right to verify square footage measurement
- Parking allocation and cost specified
- Signage rights (building directory, suite door, exterior)
- Access hours (24/7 access vs. building hours)
- Storage space allocation (if applicable)

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- Rooftop access for telecommunications equipment
- Loading dock and freight elevator access
- Common area amenities and shared spaces

## Rent and Escalations

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- Base rent amount and payment schedule clearly stated — the fundamental economic obligation of the lease
- Rent escalation methodology specified (fixed percentage, CPI, market reset) — uncapped CPI escalation creates budget unpredictability
- Operating expense pass-through structure defined (NNN, modified gross, full service) — determines which costs beyond base rent the tenant pays
- Base year or expense stop established for operating expenses — without a base year, tenant pays 100% of operating expenses from day one
- CAM (Common Area Maintenance) charges capped or controllable expenses excluded — management fees, capital expenses, and landlord overhead should be excluded or capped

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- Gross-up provision for partially occupied building
- Right to audit landlord's operating expense calculations
- Capital expenditure exclusions or amortization requirements
- Real estate tax protest rights
- Utility metering (direct vs. sub-metered vs. included in rent)

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- Holdover rent rate specified
- Late payment grace period and interest rate
- Percentage rent provisions (retail leases)

## Build-Out and Improvements

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- Tenant improvement allowance (TIA) amount and disbursement process — TIA is a key economic concession; disbursement timing affects cash flow
- Landlord's work vs. tenant's work clearly delineated — ambiguity about who builds what causes construction disputes
- Construction schedule and delivery date with remedy for delay — delayed delivery without remedy means paying rent elsewhere while waiting
- Right to make alterations during the term (with/without landlord consent thresholds) — inability to modify space limits operational flexibility
- Restoration obligations at lease end clearly defined — overbroad restoration requirements can cost hundreds of thousands

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- TIA excess carried over as rent credit (if construction under budget)
- Construction management fee cap (typically 3-5%)
- Architect and contractor approval process
- Insurance and indemnification during construction
- Punch list and acceptance process

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- Landlord's standard building specifications and finishes
- ADA compliance responsibility for tenant improvements
- Sustainability and green building requirements (LEED, ENERGY STAR)

## Term and Renewal

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- Lease commencement and expiration dates clearly stated — term certainty is essential for financial planning
- Renewal option terms specified (rent, duration, notice period) — renewal options provide long-term flexibility but must be clearly defined
- Renewal rent methodology fair (FMV with floor and ceiling, or fixed increase) — uncapped FMV resets can result in dramatic rent increases
- Early termination right with clearly defined conditions and costs — provides exit flexibility if business needs change
- Delivery condition and commencement date adjustments for construction delays — protects against paying rent before the space is usable

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- Expansion options and right of first offer/refusal on adjacent space
- Contraction rights (ability to give back space)
- Renewal notice period adequate (typically 9-12 months before expiration)
- Holdover provisions and month-to-month terms

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- Right to extend renewal option if exercised in good faith but late
- Arbitration for fair market value disputes
- Right of first offer on other floors or buildings in the complex

## Default and Remedies

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- Default defined with specificity (monetary and non-monetary) — vague default provisions give landlord excessive discretion
- Cure period adequate (5-10 days for monetary, 30 days for non-monetary) — short cure periods increase risk of default from administrative oversight
- Landlord's remedies proportionate (no acceleration of all future rent without offset) — rent acceleration without mitigation obligation is punitive
- Tenant's remedies for landlord default specified (self-help, rent abatement, termination) — leases often grant landlord remedies but omit tenant remedies
- Waiver of right to jury trial (verify enforceability in jurisdiction) — commercial lease disputes are typically better resolved by a judge

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- Landlord's mitigation obligation upon tenant default
- Late payment grace period before default is triggered
- Cross-default provisions limited (not tied to other locations)
- Guarantor obligations and release triggers
- Security deposit terms and return conditions

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- Landlord's right to cure tenant's default and charge back
- Receivership and bankruptcy provisions
- Prevailing party attorney's fee provision

## Assignment and Subletting

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- Assignment and subletting permitted with landlord consent (not to be unreasonably withheld) — prohibition without consent right creates lock-in
- Landlord's criteria for consent clearly defined — subjective criteria like "satisfactory to landlord" create consent uncertainty
- Profit-sharing on sublease or assignment reasonable (50/50 after costs is market) — 100% recapture to landlord eliminates incentive to sublease
- Recapture right (landlord's right to terminate instead of consenting) is waivable or limited — unlimited recapture right means you lose the space when you need flexibility most

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- Affiliate transfers and corporate restructurings excluded from consent requirement
- Landlord response timeline for consent requests
- Sublease profits calculation methodology
- Original tenant release upon qualifying assignment

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- Desk-sharing and co-working arrangements addressed
- License arrangements distinguished from subleases
- Name change and entity conversion provisions

## Insurance and Liability

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- Tenant's insurance requirements specified and reasonable — excessive requirements increase occupancy costs
- Mutual waiver of subrogation included — prevents landlord's insurer from suing tenant (and vice versa) after an insured loss
- Indemnification obligations mutual and proportionate — one-sided indemnification shifting all risk to tenant is unfair
- Landlord's liability for building systems failures and common area conditions — landlord should be responsible for building infrastructure

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- Property insurance responsibility clarified
- Business interruption coverage addressed
- Environmental liability allocation
- Certificate of insurance exchange process

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- Terrorism insurance provisions
- Umbrella/excess coverage requirements
- Workers' compensation verification

## Miscellaneous Provisions

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- Casualty and condemnation provisions allow tenant termination for material damage — without termination right, tenant remains obligated to pay rent after a fire or taking
- Subordination and non-disturbance agreement (SNDA) from landlord's lender — SNDA protects tenant's lease if landlord's lender forecloses

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- Estoppel certificate obligations and timeline
- Quiet enjoyment covenant from landlord
- Hazardous materials provisions and environmental compliance
- ADA compliance responsibility allocation
- Telecommunications and technology infrastructure

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- Building rules and regulations attached and reasonable
- Landlord's right to relocate tenant limited
- Sustainability and energy efficiency provisions
