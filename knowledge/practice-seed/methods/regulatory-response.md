# Regulatory Response

Reference guide for responding to regulatory investigations, subpoenas, civil investigative demands, enforcement actions, consent orders, or any formal communication from a government regulatory body. Covers inquiries from SEC, FTC, DOJ, state attorneys general, data protection authorities, OSHA, EPA, and sector-specific regulators.

---

## Classification Framework

| Classification | Examples | Urgency |
|---|---|---|
| **Formal Investigation / Enforcement Action** | Subpoena, CID, formal order of investigation, Wells notice, complaint | Engage outside counsel immediately. Board notification within 48 hours. Litigation hold within 24 hours. |
| **Informal Inquiry** | Voluntary information request, regulatory audit, routine examination | Assess scope within 48 hours. Outside counsel recommended but not mandatory for routine matters. |
| **Consent / Settlement** | Proposed consent order, assurance of voluntary compliance, settlement offer | Engage outside counsel. Strategic assessment within 1 week. |

---

## Deadline Reference Ranges

| Instrument | Typical Deadline |
|---|---|
| Federal grand jury subpoena | 14-21 days |
| Civil investigative demand (CID) | 30-60 days |
| Wells notice response | 30 days |
| SEC comment letter response | 10 business days |
| Regulatory subpoenas | Varies by agency |

Extensions are generally available and customary — most regulators will grant one extension for good cause.

---

## Parallel Proceedings Decision Framework

If there is any indication of a parallel criminal investigation (DOJ involvement, grand jury subpoena, FBI contact):
- Immediately engage white-collar defense counsel
- Do not respond to any inquiry (civil or criminal) without coordinated counsel advice
- Fifth Amendment and joint defense considerations become critical
- Assess whether civil regulatory cooperation could create criminal exposure

### Scope Assessment Questions
- What time period does the inquiry cover?
- What subject matter or conduct is at issue?
- Which business units, products, or individuals are implicated?
- Is this a company-specific inquiry or an industry-wide sweep?
- Are there parallel proceedings?

## Preservation Scope Decision

| Inquiry Scope | Preservation Approach |
|---|---|
| Narrowly scoped (specific product, specific time period) | Targeted hold to custodians directly involved |
| Broad or unclear | Company-wide hold to all employees who may have relevant information, plus IT systems administrators |

When in doubt, preserve broadly. The cost of over-preservation is far less than sanctions for spoliation.

### Preservation Categories
Email, Slack/Teams, documents, databases, system logs, backup tapes, mobile devices. For regulated industries: regulatory filings, compliance records, audit trail data.

---

## Individual vs. Company Counsel Decision Criteria

| Situation | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Individual employee may be personally at risk (named in inquiry, potential witness, potential target) | Advise of right to personal counsel at company expense (per D&O policy and indemnification obligations). Do not assume company's counsel represents individuals. |
| Potential conflicts between company's interests and individual officers/directors | Separate counsel is mandatory |
| No individual exposure identified | Company counsel represents the entity |

## Cooperation Strategy Decision Framework

| Strategy | When Appropriate | Approach |
|---|---|---|
| **Full Cooperation** | (1) No or minimal company culpability, (2) conduct was by rogue individuals, (3) company self-reported, (4) cooperation credit is significant (DOJ/SEC guidelines) | Proactive disclosure, full document production, employee interviews. Benefits: reduced penalties, DPA/NPA eligibility, favorable settlement. |
| **Selective Cooperation** | (1) Some limited culpability exists, (2) cooperation on some topics but not others serves the company, (3) preserving privilege over internal investigation findings | Comply fully with document requests; cooperate on facts while reserving legal arguments. |
| **Adversarial Defense** | (1) Regulatory theory is legally wrong, (2) facts strongly favor the company, (3) settlement penalties are disproportionate, (4) precedent-setting concerns outweigh cost of defense | Comply with compulsory process; challenge overreach; assert privileges vigorously. |

### Key Factors for Strategy Selection
- Severity of potential penalties (monetary, injunctive, license revocation, criminal referral)
- Strength of the company's factual and legal position
- Regulatory enforcement history and negotiation tendencies of the specific agency
- Reputational impact
- Impact on ongoing business operations and relationships
- Insurance coverage for fines, penalties, and defense costs

## Response Type Guidance

### Document Subpoenas / CIDs
- Parse document requests item by item: scope, time period, relevant custodians
- Raise specific objections where appropriate (overbreadth, undue burden, privilege, work product, relevance) — avoid boilerplate
- Negotiate scope before producing (most regulators expect a meet-and-confer)
- Prepare a privilege log for withheld documents (date, author, recipient, subject, privilege basis)
- Produce in specified format (or negotiate: native, PDF, TIFF, load file)

### Interrogatories / Written Questions
- Draft responses under oath, reviewed by counsel
- Use precise language — every word in a sworn response can be used later
- Distinguish between facts the company knows and facts it has investigated

### Testimony / Depositions
- Prepare witnesses thoroughly with counsel (review documents, mock examination)
- Instruct witnesses to answer only the question asked, truthfully and concisely
- Assert privilege where appropriate during testimony

### Settlement / Consent Negotiations
- Assess the regulatory "ask" against the company's risk assessment
- Evaluate monetary penalties, injunctive relief, compliance commitments, and monitoring requirements
- Draft counterproposals with specific, measurable compliance commitments the company can actually fulfill
- Avoid admissions of liability where possible (neither admit nor deny)
