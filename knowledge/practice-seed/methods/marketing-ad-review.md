# Marketing & Ad Review

Reference guide for reviewing marketing copy, ad creative, and campaigns before launch — web pages, paid ads, social posts, email/SMS sends, influencer programs, and promotions. The organizing rule: every objective claim needs substantiation in hand before publication, and every disclosure must be as conspicuous as the claim it qualifies. General framework in `law/advertising-media/advertising-standards.md` and `law/consumer-protection/ftc-udap.md`.

## Intake

Collect before reviewing — reviewing copy in isolation misses most of the risk:

- Final creative in context (the actual ad as it will appear, with placement, not a text draft)
- A claims inventory: every objective statement, express or implied, with the support file for each
- Channels and formats (paid/organic, platform, email/SMS), territory, and flight dates
- Audience and targeting — child/teen targeting, vulnerable populations, or regulated-product audiences change the rules
- Talent and asset list: music, images, footage, fonts, people, marks, UGC — with the license or release for each
- Whether any competitor is named or identifiable

## Risk Tiers

| Tier | Indicators | Review depth |
|---|---|---|
| **Fast track** | Organic social, no objective claims, no third-party people/marks/music, no promotion mechanics | Copy scan for implied claims and disclosure placement. Minutes. |
| **Standard** | Objective product claims with a support file, licensed assets, established channels | Claims-to-substantiation match, disclosure check, clearance verification. |
| **Full review** | Anything in Escalation Triggers below | Clause-level review, substantiation file scrutiny, outside counsel where flagged. |

## Claim Substantiation

- A reasonable basis must exist **before** the claim runs — post-hoc substantiation is itself a violation. Ask marketing for the support file at intake; "we'll get the data" means the claim doesn't run.
- Match proof to claim type: health, safety, and performance claims need competent and reliable evidence (often testing); "studies show" requires studies that actually show it, on the product as sold.
- Review implied claims, not just literal text — visuals, demos, and before/after imagery make claims too. The test is net impression on a reasonable consumer.
- Puffery ("the best coffee in town") is non-actionable only while unmeasurable; "50% faster" and "#1 rated" are objective claims needing proof. When in doubt, treat it as objective.
- Specific numbers, survey results, and awards: verify currency and scope. A 2019 award cited in 2026 copy, or a survey of 40 users billed as "users agree," are deception findings waiting to happen.

## Disclosure Mechanics

Disclosures qualify claims only if consumers actually see and understand them. The clear-and-conspicuous standard, applied per format:

- Proximity: the disclosure travels with the claim — same screen, adjacent placement. A hyperlink does not cure a misleading claim; links are acceptable only for non-essential detail.
- Video: on-screen long enough to read, and in audio if the claim is in audio. Stories and short-form video need the disclosure superimposed on the content itself.
- Space-constrained ads: required disclosures must fit, or the claim must come out — platform constraints are not a defense.
- Native advertising: paid content must be identifiable as advertising before consumers engage with it ("Sponsored," "Paid Partnership") — formatting that mimics editorial content without a label is deceptive regardless of the content's accuracy.

## Endorsements, Testimonials, and Influencers

FTC Endorsement Guides apply to anyone with a material connection to the company — paid influencers, employees, free-product recipients, affiliates. Details in `law/consumer-protection/endorsements-testimonials.md` and `law/advertising-media/influencer-disclosures.md`.

- Material connections must be disclosed clearly and conspicuously within the endorsement itself — "#ad" buried in 20 hashtags, disclosure only in a bio, or below-the-fold placement all fail.
- Endorsements must reflect honest opinions of actual users, and the advertiser is liable for what influencers say — substantiation requirements flow through to influencer claims about the product.
- Atypical results need clear context: "results not typical" disclaimers no longer cure a misleading typicality impression; the depicted result should be what consumers can generally expect.
- Employee posts about the company's products need an employment disclosure — cover this in the social media policy, not post-by-post review.
- Contracts with influencers/agencies should require compliant disclosure, prohibit unsubstantiated claims, grant takedown rights, and include monitoring — the FTC expects an actual monitoring program, not just contract language.

## Comparative Advertising and Lanham Act Risk

- Naming a competitor is legal and effective — and converts the audience from regulators to a motivated adversary with Lanham Act false-advertising standing. The substantiation bar is practically higher: expect the comparison to be litigated.
- Comparative claims must be accurate, current, and apples-to-apples (same product tiers, same test conditions, dated benchmarks refreshed). Superiority claims ("faster than X") need head-to-head support.
- Using a competitor's logo or mark is permissible for truthful comparison but review for implied endorsement or dilution (`law/ip-and-technology/trademarks.md`). Disparagement that crosses from product comparison to false statements of fact about the company raises defamation exposure (`law/advertising-media/defamation.md`).

## Pricing and "Free" Claims

- "Free" means free — conditions must be disclosed at the first mention, and the non-free component cannot be marked up to recover the cost. "Free trial" converting to paid triggers the negative-option regime (`law/consumer-protection/auto-renewal.md`).
- Strikethrough/was-now pricing requires that the reference price was actually charged for a meaningful period — fictitious former prices are a leading state-enforcement and class-action target.
- Drip pricing (fees revealed late in checkout) is treated as a dark pattern and, in several states, a statutory violation — advertised price should be the all-in price absent genuinely optional add-ons (`law/consumer-protection/dark-patterns.md`).
- "Up to X" claims: consumers read these as typical performance; the appreciable-number-of-consumers standard means outlier maxima need reframing.

## Sweepstakes, Contests, and Promotions

- The lottery test: prize + chance + consideration = illegal lottery. Remove one element — the standard fix is a free alternative method of entry (AMOE) that is genuinely equal in odds and prominence to the paid path.
- Skill contests escape chance but trigger their own state rules (judging criteria disclosure; some states regulate entry-fee skill contests).
- Official rules required before launch: eligibility, dates, odds, prize value/ARV, winner selection, publicity rights. Registration and bonding in some states above prize thresholds (notably NY and FL for sweepstakes over $5,000) — check `law/consumer-protection/state-consumer-laws.md` for current state specifics.
- Social-platform promotions also need platform-rule compliance and a release disclaiming platform sponsorship; "tag a friend to enter" mechanics raise consent issues for the tagged party.

## Email and SMS Campaigns

- **Email:** CAN-SPAM requires accurate headers and subject lines, a physical postal address, a functioning opt-out honored within 10 business days, and identification of the message as an ad. Sender liability extends to messages sent on the company's behalf. Details in `law/consumer-protection/can-spam.md`.
- **SMS/calls:** TCPA is strict-liability with statutory damages per text — the class-action math is unforgiving. Prior express written consent for marketing texts, honored opt-outs (including nonstandard replies), quiet hours, and reassigned-number checks. Treat any new texting program as high-risk and check `law/consumer-protection/tcpa.md` for the current consent and revocation rules before launch.
- Lead-generation and purchased lists: consent must be to receive messages from *us*, not just from the lead generator — review the consent flow and records, not the vendor's assurances.

## Green and ESG Claims

- Environmental claims need the same substantiation as performance claims, plus FTC Green Guides specificity: unqualified "eco-friendly," "sustainable," or "carbon neutral" claims are presumptively overbroad. Qualify with the specific attribute and basis ("packaging made from 80% recycled material").
- "Recyclable" and "compostable" turn on what facilities are actually available to a substantial majority of consumers, not technical possibility.
- Carbon-neutrality and offset-based claims face active greenwashing scrutiny in the US, EU, and UK — treat as escalation-level claims. See `law/environmental-esg/sustainability-representations.md`.

## Reviews and Review Gating

- Review gating — soliciting reviews only from customers known to be satisfied, or routing dissatisfied customers away from public review platforms — is prohibited; displayed reviews must represent the solicited population.
- Fake reviews, AI-generated reviews, paid reviews without disclosure, and insider reviews are direct FTC rule violations with civil penalties. Buying or suppressing reviews through third-party vendors does not launder liability.
- The Consumer Review Fairness Act voids contract terms penalizing customers for honest negative reviews — check form contracts and settlement templates for gag clauses. See `law/consumer-protection/endorsements-testimonials.md`.

## IP and Clearance

- **Music:** sync licenses required for any music in video/social content — a platform's library license covers organic posts, usually not paid ads. "Royalty-free" still has license terms; read them.
- **Images and footage:** verify license scope covers the use (commercial, paid media, modification, territory, term) and that model/property releases exist for recognizable people and locations. Stock licenses frequently exclude sensitive-context uses.
- **People:** using a person's name, image, or voice for commercial advantage requires consent — right of publicity survives in many states even after death, and AI-generated likenesses/voices are squarely covered. See `law/advertising-media/right-of-publicity.md`.
- **Marks and content:** clear third-party trademarks in copy and creative (`law/ip-and-technology/trademarks.md`); confirm rights in licensed or user-generated content extend to advertising use (`law/advertising-media/content-licensing.md`, `law/ip-and-technology/copyrights.md`). UGC reuse needs the poster's permission — platform terms do not grant the brand a license.
- AI-generated creative: confirm the generator's terms permit commercial use, and clear outputs that resemble identifiable people, marks, or existing works.

## Escalation Triggers

- Health, safety, financial-performance, or earnings claims in any channel
- Comparative campaign naming a competitor
- New SMS/texting program or purchased lead lists
- Sweepstakes or contest with paid entry, high prize value, or multi-state scope
- Carbon-neutral, net-zero, or offset-based environmental claims
- Influencer campaign aimed at children or teens (overlaps `law/data-privacy/coppa.md`)
- Regulator inquiry, NAD challenge, or competitor demand letter about a claim
- Claims about regulated products or services (supplements, financial products, alcohol, healthcare)
