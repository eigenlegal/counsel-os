## Automatic Stay

# Automatic Stay

## Applicability

Applies immediately upon the filing of a bankruptcy petition under any chapter (7, 11, 12, or 13). The automatic stay under Section 362 of the Bankruptcy Code is one of the most fundamental protections for debtors, halting virtually all collection and enforcement actions against the debtor and property of the estate.

## Key Requirements

### Scope of the Stay (Section 362(a))

- **Actions Stayed** / The stay prohibits: (1) commencement or continuation of lawsuits against the debtor, (2) enforcement of judgments, (3) acts to obtain possession of estate property, (4) acts to create, perfect, or enforce liens, (5) setoff of pre-petition debts. / **Consequence**: Actions taken in violation of the stay are void (majority view) or voidable (minority view, including the Second Circuit); the distinction matters for whether the violating party can cure by returning to the status quo.
- **Property of the Estate** / The stay protects all property of the estate (Section 541), which includes all legal and equitable interests of the debtor as of the petition date, wherever located. / **Consequence**: Property of the estate is broadly defined — includes causes of action, contract rights, intellectual property, and contingent interests. The stay extends to property even if held by third parties.
- **Co-Debtor Stay** / In Chapter 12 and 13 cases only, the stay extends to co-debtors (guarantors, co-signers) on consumer debts. / **Consequence**: In Chapter 7 and 11, there is NO co-debtor stay; creditors may pursue guarantors and co-obligors notwithstanding the debtor's bankruptcy filing.

### Exceptions to the Stay (Section 362(b))

- **Police and Regulatory Power** / Government exercise of police or regulatory power is not stayed, including criminal proceedings, tax audits, and environmental enforcement. / **Consequence**: EPA can pursue environmental cleanup orders against a debtor notwithstanding the stay (but money judgments for pre-petition contamination ARE stayed). The distinction between regulatory action and money collection is heavily litigated.
- **Family Law** / Domestic support obligations (child support, alimony), paternity proceedings, and domestic violence proceedings are not stayed. / **Consequence**: Family courts retain jurisdiction over these matters; income withholding orders for domestic support continue in effect.
- **Perfection of Security Interests** / Creditors may perfect a security interest within the applicable post-petition grace period (typically **30 days** after the petition) under Section 362(b)(3). / **Consequence**: A creditor who files a UCC continuation statement or mortgage within the grace period preserves its priority; missing the window may result in the lien being avoided as unperfected.
- **Setoff in Financial Contracts** / Safe-harbored financial contracts (swaps, repos, securities contracts) are exempt from the automatic stay under Sections 555-561. / **Consequence**: Counterparties to financial contracts may exercise contractual rights of setoff, netting, and liquidation without violating the stay; this exception is a critical protection for financial markets.

### Relief from the Automatic Stay (Section 362(d))

- **For Cause (Section 362(d)(1))** / Court may grant relief from stay "for cause, including the lack of adequate protection." / **Consequence**: "Cause" is not defined in the Code and is determined on a case-by-case basis. Lack of adequate protection (declining collateral value without protection payments) is the most common ground. Relief may be granted as to specific property or specific creditors.
- **Lack of Equity + Not Necessary for Reorganization (Section 362(d)(2))** / Relief granted if the debtor has no equity in the property AND the property is not necessary for an effective reorganization. / **Consequence**: Both elements must be satisfied; if the debtor has even minimal equity, this ground fails. If the property is necessary for reorganization (e.g., the debtor's primary operating facility), relief under (d)(2) is unavailable.
- **Single Asset Real Estate (Section 362(d)(3))** / In single asset real estate cases, the debtor must file a plan with a reasonable possibility of confirmation or commence monthly payments equal to the non-default contract rate of interest within **90 days** of the petition date (or 30 days after the court determines the case is a SARE case). / **Consequence**: Failure to meet the 90-day deadline results in automatic relief from stay for the secured creditor; this provision prevents debtor abuse of Chapter 11 to delay foreclosure on passive real estate investments.
- **Adequate Protection** / Forms include: periodic cash payments, additional or replacement liens, and "indubitable equivalent" of the creditor's interest. / **Consequence**: If adequate protection proves insufficient (collateral declines in value despite protection payments), the creditor has a superpriority administrative claim under Section 507(b); this claim has priority over all other administrative expenses.

### Willful Violation of the Stay

- **Standard** / A violation is "willful" under Section 362(k) if the creditor knew of the stay and intentionally took the action — knowledge that the action violated the stay is NOT required. / **Consequence**: Individuals injured by willful violations may recover actual damages, costs, attorney's fees, and in appropriate cases, **punitive damages**. Corporate debtors may also recover damages in some circuits.
- **Damages** / Actual damages include: loss of property, emotional distress (individuals), lost business opportunity, and attorney's fees incurred to enforce the stay. / **Consequence**: Punitive damages for egregious violations have ranged from **$25,000 to $1 million+** in reported cases; repeated violations or violations in bad faith increase the likelihood of punitive awards.
- **Contempt** / Courts may hold stay violators in civil contempt under Section 105(a). / **Consequence**: Contempt sanctions include per-day fines (commonly **$1,000-$10,000/day**) until the violation is cured, attorney's fees, and in extreme cases, imprisonment for individuals.

### Duration and Termination

- **Chapter 7** / Stay terminates as to property of the estate when the property is no longer property of the estate (abandonment or closing of the case); stay as to the debtor terminates at discharge or closing. / **Consequence**: Property abandoned by the trustee is no longer protected by the stay; creditors may pursue foreclosure or collection against abandoned property.
- **Chapter 11** / Stay continues until the case is closed, dismissed, or discharge is granted; plan confirmation may modify the stay as to specific property. / **Consequence**: The plan of reorganization may provide for the stay to continue as to certain property or may lift the stay to allow creditors to pursue rights against specific collateral.
- **Serial Filers** / If a debtor files a second case within **1 year** of dismissal, the stay terminates after **30 days** unless extended for cause. Third filing within 1 year: **no stay at all** unless the debtor demonstrates the filing is in good faith. / **Consequence**: Serial filing provisions (Section 362(c)(3) and (4)) prevent debtor abuse; creditors should monitor for serial filings and promptly move for stay termination if applicable.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Environmental & ESG** — Government environmental enforcement actions are exempt from the stay under the police power exception, but money judgments for pre-petition environmental liability are stayed
- **Employment** — Employee wage claims may be partially exempt from the stay; WARN Act violations may be litigated
- **Real Estate** — Relief from stay is the primary mechanism for lenders to resume foreclosure in bankruptcy
- **Financial Services** — Financial contract safe harbors provide complete exemption from the automatic stay for qualifying contracts

## Creditor Rights

# Creditor Rights

## Applicability

Applies when a creditor seeks to protect or enforce its claims in a bankruptcy proceeding, including filing proofs of claim, asserting secured status, seeking adequate protection, participating in DIP financing, or contesting plan confirmation. Covers the full spectrum of creditor participation from case commencement through plan confirmation and distribution.

## Key Requirements

### Proof of Claim (Section 501)

- **Filing Requirement** / Creditors must file a proof of claim to participate in distributions. The bar date (deadline for filing) is set by the court — typically **70 days** after the petition date in Chapter 7 and **90 days** after the first meeting of creditors in Chapter 11. / **Consequence**: Failure to file by the bar date permanently bars the claim from receiving any distribution (with narrow exceptions for lack of notice). Government units have **180 days** from the petition date to file.
- **Claim Presumption** / A filed proof of claim is deemed allowed unless a party in interest objects (Section 502(a)). / **Consequence**: The burden shifts to the objecting party to demonstrate the claim is invalid, duplicative, or improperly classified. Claims supported by documentation (invoices, contracts) are more difficult to challenge.
- **Disputed, Contingent, and Unliquidated Claims** / Claims may be filed even if disputed, contingent, or unliquidated. / **Consequence**: The bankruptcy court may estimate disputed or contingent claims under Section 502(c) for purposes of allowance; estimation can result in significant reduction of claimed amounts.

### Priority of Claims (Section 507)

- **Administrative Expenses (Section 507(a)(2))** / Post-petition claims essential to preserving the estate: professional fees, operating expenses, goods received within **20 days** before petition (Section 503(b)(9)). / **Consequence**: Administrative claims are paid in full before any distribution to pre-petition unsecured creditors. The 20-day goods claim is a critical protection for trade creditors — goods delivered within 20 days of the petition are treated as administrative priority.
- **Wage Priority (Section 507(a)(4))** / Employee wages, salaries, and commissions earned within **180 days** before the petition date, up to **$15,150** per employee. / **Consequence**: The cap is adjusted every 3 years (most recent adjustment effective April 2022); amounts above the cap are general unsecured claims. Includes vacation pay, severance, and sick leave.
- **Employee Benefit Plan Contributions (Section 507(a)(5))** / Contributions to employee benefit plans arising from services rendered within **180 days** before the petition, up to **$15,150** per employee (reduced by amounts paid under wage priority). / **Consequence**: Covers health insurance, retirement plan contributions, and other benefit obligations; calculation is complex when combined with the wage priority cap.
- **Customer Deposits (Section 507(a)(7))** / Deposits for undelivered goods or services by individual consumers, up to **$3,350** per individual. / **Consequence**: Protects individual (not business) customers who prepaid for goods or services the debtor never delivered; amounts above the cap are general unsecured claims.
- **Tax Claims (Section 507(a)(8))** / Income taxes for taxable years ending within **3 years** before the petition; employment taxes; sales/use taxes; and certain property taxes. / **Consequence**: Priority tax claims are nondischargeable and must be paid in full under a Chapter 11 plan (may be paid in installments over **5 years** with interest). Failure to pay priority taxes prevents plan confirmation.

### Secured vs. Unsecured Claims

- **Secured Claim (Section 506(a))** / A claim is secured to the extent of the value of the creditor's interest in the estate's property; the remainder is an unsecured claim ("bifurcation"). / **Consequence**: Undersecured creditors (debt exceeds collateral value) have a secured claim up to collateral value and an unsecured deficiency claim. The valuation date and methodology are critical — courts use replacement value for Chapter 11 (Associates Commercial Corp. v. Rash, 1997).
- **Oversecured Creditors** / Creditors whose collateral value exceeds the claim amount are entitled to post-petition interest, fees, and costs under Section 506(b). / **Consequence**: Only oversecured creditors earn post-petition interest; undersecured creditors are frozen at the petition-date amount. The cushion of equity determines entitlement.
- **Lien Avoidance** / The trustee may avoid unperfected liens (Section 544(a), strong-arm power), preferential liens (Section 547), and fraudulent transfers (Section 548). / **Consequence**: A creditor whose lien is avoided becomes unsecured; perfection of security interests before bankruptcy is essential to preserving secured status.

### Adequate Protection (Section 361)

- **Purpose** / Adequate protection compensates a secured creditor for the decline in value of its collateral during the bankruptcy case while the automatic stay prevents foreclosure. / **Consequence**: If adequate protection is inadequate and the collateral declines in value, the creditor has a superpriority administrative claim under Section 507(b) — senior to all other administrative claims, including DIP financing.
- **Forms** / Cash payments, additional or replacement liens, and other relief that provides the "indubitable equivalent" of the creditor's interest. / **Consequence**: Courts determine adequate protection on a case-by-case basis; the debtor bears the burden of proposing and demonstrating adequate protection. Failure to provide adequate protection is grounds for relief from the automatic stay.

### DIP Financing (Section 364)

- **Unsecured Credit in the Ordinary Course (Section 364(a))** / Post-petition unsecured credit in the ordinary course of business automatically has administrative expense priority. / **Consequence**: Trade creditors extending credit to a debtor-in-possession receive administrative priority for post-petition deliveries — a significant improvement over pre-petition unsecured status.
- **Secured DIP Financing (Section 364(d))** / The court may authorize the debtor to obtain post-petition credit secured by a senior or equal lien on estate property ("priming lien"), if existing lienholders are adequately protected. / **Consequence**: DIP lenders demand and receive superpriority claims, priming liens, and roll-up provisions (converting pre-petition debt to post-petition status). Existing secured creditors must receive adequate protection — often monthly interest payments and replacement liens.
- **DIP Financing Terms** / Market terms include: commitment fees (**1-3%**), interest rate at SOFR + **300-600 bps**, unused line fees (**0.5-1%**), mandatory prepayment events, and milestones (plan filing within **90-120 days**, confirmation within **180-240 days**). / **Consequence**: DIP financing covenants effectively set the timetable for the bankruptcy case; failure to meet milestones triggers events of default and may force liquidation or sale.

### Cramdown (Section 1129(b))

- **Requirements** / If a class of claims rejects the plan, the court may confirm over the objection ("cramdown") if the plan: (a) does not discriminate unfairly, and (b) is "fair and equitable" as to the dissenting class. / **Consequence**: Cramdown is the debtor's most powerful tool against holdout creditors; it forces acceptance of plan terms that meet the statutory requirements.
- **Fair and Equitable — Secured Claims** / The plan must provide the secured creditor with: (a) retention of lien and deferred cash payments equal to the present value of the allowed secured claim, OR (b) sale of the property with lien attaching to proceeds, OR (c) the "indubitable equivalent" of the secured claim. / **Consequence**: "Indubitable equivalent" is the highest standard — it must eliminate any reasonable doubt about the creditor receiving the value of its claim. Cash payment of the allowed secured claim satisfies this standard.
- **Fair and Equitable — Unsecured Claims (Absolute Priority Rule)** / No junior class may receive or retain any property under the plan unless the senior dissenting class is paid in full. / **Consequence**: In practice, the absolute priority rule means equity holders receive nothing unless all unsecured creditors are paid in full. Exceptions: individual debtors in Chapter 11 (BAPCPA carve-out) and negotiated "gifting" by consenting senior classes.
- **No Unfair Discrimination** / Dissenting class must be treated comparably to other classes of the same priority level. / **Consequence**: The debtor cannot favor one class of unsecured creditors over another without justification; different treatment requires legitimate business reasons (e.g., trade creditor payments necessary for operations).

## Executory Contracts

# Executory Contracts

## Applicability

Applies when a bankruptcy case involves contracts or unexpired leases where material performance remains due on both sides. Section 365 of the Bankruptcy Code governs the debtor's power to assume, reject, or assign executory contracts and unexpired leases, which is one of the most powerful tools in bankruptcy reorganization.

## Key Requirements

### Definition and Identification

- **Countryman Test** / A contract is executory if the obligations of both parties are so far unperformed that failure of either to complete performance would constitute a material breach. / **Consequence**: The test is applied at the time of the bankruptcy filing. Contracts where only one party has material obligations remaining (e.g., a fully performed sale with only a payment obligation) are generally not executory and cannot be rejected under Section 365.
- **Common Executory Contracts** / Equipment leases, real property leases, supply agreements, license agreements, franchise agreements, employment contracts, service agreements. / **Consequence**: Each category has specific treatment rules; real property leases and IP licenses have special provisions (Sections 365(d) and 365(n)) that override the general rules.

### Assumption (Section 365(b))

- **Cure of Defaults** / To assume a contract, the debtor must cure all existing defaults (or provide adequate assurance of prompt cure). / **Consequence**: Cure amounts include monetary defaults (unpaid rent, missed payments) and non-monetary defaults that are curable. Some non-monetary defaults (e.g., failure to operate continuously in a retail lease) may be incurable, preventing assumption.
- **Compensation for Pecuniary Loss** / The debtor must compensate the non-debtor party for actual pecuniary losses resulting from the default. / **Consequence**: Compensation covers out-of-pocket losses caused by the default — not lost future profits or consequential damages. The amount is determined by the court if disputed.
- **Adequate Assurance of Future Performance** / The debtor must demonstrate ability to perform going forward. / **Consequence**: Adequate assurance is fact-specific — courts consider the debtor's financial condition, operational capacity, and reorganization plan. For shopping center leases, Section 365(b)(3) imposes enhanced requirements (see below).
- **Ipso Facto Clauses** / Contract provisions that terminate or modify the contract upon bankruptcy filing are unenforceable under Section 365(e). / **Consequence**: Anti-assignment clauses triggered solely by bankruptcy or insolvency are overridden; however, clauses triggered by non-bankruptcy events (e.g., failure to maintain financial ratios) that happen to coincide with bankruptcy are more complex.

### Rejection (Section 365(a))

- **Business Judgment Standard** / The court approves rejection if the debtor's decision is a reasonable exercise of business judgment — not the best possible decision, just a reasonable one. / **Consequence**: Courts rarely second-guess rejection decisions; the threshold is low. The non-debtor party's remedy is a pre-petition unsecured claim for rejection damages.
- **Rejection Damages** / Rejection constitutes a breach of the contract as of immediately before the petition date. Damages are treated as a pre-petition general unsecured claim. / **Consequence**: Rejection damage claims are subject to the same distribution as other general unsecured claims — often receiving **cents on the dollar**. For real property leases, Section 502(b)(6) caps the landlord's rejection damage claim.
- **Landlord Damage Cap (Section 502(b)(6))** / Landlord's rejection damage claim is capped at the greater of: (a) **1 year** of rent, or (b) **15%** of the remaining lease term (not to exceed **3 years**) of rent, plus any unpaid rent due under the lease as of the earlier of the petition date or the date the landlord repossessed. / **Consequence**: For long-term leases, the cap significantly reduces the landlord's recovery. "Rent" is broadly interpreted to include base rent, CAM, taxes, and other charges under the lease.

### Assignment (Section 365(f))

- **Anti-Assignment Override** / Section 365(f) allows the debtor to assign executory contracts notwithstanding anti-assignment provisions in the contract or applicable law. / **Consequence**: This is one of the most commercially significant provisions in bankruptcy — the debtor can sell valuable contracts (favorable leases, below-market supply agreements) to third parties over the non-debtor party's objection.
- **Requirements for Assignment** / The contract must first be assumed (all defaults cured), and the assignee must provide adequate assurance of future performance. / **Consequence**: Adequate assurance for the assignee is an independent requirement — the assignee must demonstrate financial capacity and operational ability to perform. The non-debtor party can object if adequate assurance is insufficient.
- **Exceptions to Assignment** / Contracts for personal services, contracts dependent on the debtor's unique skills, and certain financial accommodations (loans) cannot be assigned over objection. / **Consequence**: The personal services exception is narrowly construed; routine service contracts (janitorial, IT support) are generally assignable. Government contracts have additional restrictions.

### Timing Deadlines

- **Chapter 7 — 60-Day Deadline** / The trustee must assume or reject executory contracts within **60 days** of the order for relief, or the contract is deemed rejected. / **Consequence**: The 60-day deadline can be extended for cause by court order, but automatic deemed rejection occurs if no action is taken and no extension is granted.
- **Chapter 11 — Plan Confirmation** / The debtor may assume or reject executory contracts any time before plan confirmation. / **Consequence**: Contracts remain in limbo during the Chapter 11 case, which can last months or years; the non-debtor party may file a motion under Section 365(d)(2) to compel the debtor to decide within a court-specified period.
- **Nonresidential Real Property Leases (Section 365(d)(4))** / The debtor must assume or reject nonresidential real property leases within **120 days** of the petition date, with one **90-day extension** available (total maximum: **210 days** without landlord consent). / **Consequence**: After 210 days, the lease is deemed rejected unless the landlord consents to further extensions. This is a hard deadline — courts cannot extend beyond 210 days without landlord agreement.

### Shopping Center Special Rules (Section 365(b)(3))

- **Adequate Assurance Requirements** / For shopping center leases, adequate assurance requires: (a) percentage rent will not decline substantially, (b) assumption is subject to all lease terms (including radius, use, and exclusivity), (c) no disruption to tenant mix, (d) the assignee is financially capable. / **Consequence**: Shopping center provisions give landlords significantly more leverage to oppose assumption and assignment; tenant mix and use clause compliance are independently enforceable.

### Intellectual Property Licenses

- **Section 365(n) Election** / When a licensor rejects an IP license, the licensee may elect to retain its rights under the license (including exclusivity) for the remaining term, provided it continues to make royalty payments. / **Consequence**: The licensee's election under 365(n) preserves the right to use the IP but does not preserve affirmative obligations of the licensor (e.g., maintenance, updates, support). See `ip-licenses-in-bankruptcy.md` for detailed treatment.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Real Estate** — Nonresidential real property lease treatment, landlord damage caps, shopping center rules

- **Employment** — Collective bargaining agreements (Section 1113), retiree benefits (Section 1114)
- **Financial Services** — Financial contracts may be exempt from Section 365 under safe harbor provisions

## Ip Licenses In Bankruptcy

# IP Licenses in Bankruptcy

## Applicability

Applies when a licensor of intellectual property files for bankruptcy, when a licensee's rights are at risk due to a licensor's financial distress, or when a debtor seeks to assume, reject, or assign IP license agreements. Section 365(n) of the Bankruptcy Code provides critical protections for licensees, but its scope is limited and subject to significant interpretive disputes.

## Key Requirements

### Section 365(n) — Licensee Election

- **Rejection by Licensor** / When a debtor-licensor rejects an IP license under Section 365(a), the licensee has a binary election: (a) treat the rejection as a breach and assert a pre-petition damages claim, OR (b) retain its rights under the license for the duration of the license term (including renewals enforceable under applicable non-bankruptcy law). / **Consequence**: The election must be made in writing; failure to timely elect may default to option (a), leaving the licensee with only an unsecured damages claim — typically worth cents on the dollar.
- **Retained Rights Under Election** / If the licensee elects to retain rights, it retains: (a) the right to use the IP as provided in the license, (b) exclusivity provisions, and (c) any right to enforce a claim of exclusivity. / **Consequence**: Retained rights are limited to rights under the license agreement — the licensee cannot expand the scope of the license or claim rights not granted in the original agreement.
- **Royalty Obligations** / A licensee electing to retain rights must continue making all royalty payments due under the license for the duration of the retained term. / **Consequence**: Royalty payments continue to the debtor's estate or the licensor's successor; failure to make timely payments may forfeit the licensee's retained rights.
- **Waiver of Setoff** / A licensee retaining rights waives any right of setoff with respect to the license. / **Consequence**: The licensee cannot offset rejection damages against royalty payments; it must continue full royalty payments and pursue its damages claim separately through the bankruptcy claims process.

### Definition of "Intellectual Property" (Section 101(35A))

- **Included** / Trade secrets, U.S. patents and patent applications, plant variety protections, copyrights (subject to Section 365(n) protections). / **Consequence**: Licensees of patents, copyrights, and trade secrets have clear Section 365(n) protections; the statutory definition provides a safe harbor for these IP types.
- **Excluded — Trademarks** / Trademarks are NOT included in the Section 101(35A) definition of "intellectual property." / **Consequence**: This is the most significant gap in Section 365(n). The legislative history indicates Congress intended to revisit trademark treatment but never did. The exclusion creates substantial risk for trademark licensees (franchisees, brand licensees, co-branding partners).
- **Lubrizol Legacy** / Lubrizol Enterprises v. Richmond Metal Finishers (4th Cir. 1985) held that rejection of an IP license terminated the licensee's right to use the IP — Section 365(n) was enacted in 1988 to overrule Lubrizol for patents, copyrights, and trade secrets but NOT trademarks. / **Consequence**: For trademark licenses, Lubrizol's holding may still apply — rejection terminates the licensee's right to use the mark. However, several courts have declined to follow Lubrizol for trademarks (see Sunbeam, Tempnology below).

### Trademark Licenses — Evolving Law

- **Sunbeam Products (7th Cir. 2012)** / Judge Easterbrook held that rejection of a trademark license does not terminate the licensee's rights — rejection is a breach, and the non-breaching party retains its contractual rights. / **Consequence**: Under Sunbeam (controlling in the 7th Circuit), trademark licensees retain the right to use the mark after rejection, subject to quality control provisions. This directly contradicts Lubrizol.
- **Mission Product Holdings v. Tempnology (S. Ct. 2019)** / The Supreme Court held that rejection under Section 365 is a breach, not a rescission or termination — but explicitly declined to resolve the trademark question, stating the issue was not before the court. / **Consequence**: Tempnology supports the Sunbeam position in principle (rejection = breach, not termination) but did not establish a definitive rule for trademark licenses. Circuit split persists.
- **Practical Risk** / Trademark licensees in circuits that have not adopted Sunbeam face significant risk that rejection terminates their trademark rights. / **Consequence**: Licensees should consider protective measures: (a) include IP types covered by Section 101(35A) in the same license, (b) negotiate express survival provisions, (c) obtain a separate covenant not to sue, (d) seek assignment of the marks to a bankruptcy-remote entity.

### Licensor Rejection — Practical Effects

- **Loss of Affirmative Obligations** / Even when a licensee retains rights under 365(n), the debtor-licensor's affirmative obligations (maintenance, updates, technical support, quality assurance, prosecution of the IP) are terminated. / **Consequence**: For software licenses, loss of updates and maintenance can render the retained license worthless over time. Licensees should ensure they have source code escrow or self-help provisions independent of 365(n).
- **Source Code Escrow** / Pre-petition escrow arrangements for software source code may survive the bankruptcy if properly structured. / **Consequence**: The escrow agreement must be with a third-party escrow agent (not the licensor), and the release trigger conditions must be clearly defined. Escrow agreements that are merely executory contracts of the debtor are themselves subject to rejection.

### Licensee Bankruptcy

- **Assumption of IP Licenses** / A debtor-licensee may assume an IP license under Section 365, subject to cure of defaults and adequate assurance of future performance. / **Consequence**: The licensor can object to assumption if the debtor cannot demonstrate ability to maintain quality control and perform license obligations.
- **Assignment of IP Licenses** / Section 365(f) overrides anti-assignment provisions, but Section 365(c)(1) creates an exception: a contract that is not assignable under applicable non-bankruptcy law may not be assumed or assigned if the non-debtor party does not consent. / **Consequence**: Patent licenses are generally non-assignable without consent under federal patent law; this creates a "hypothetical test" vs. "actual test" circuit split on whether the debtor can even ASSUME (not just assign) the license. The First, Fourth, Ninth, and Eleventh Circuits follow the "hypothetical test" (no assumption without consent); the Third and Seventh Circuits follow the "actual test" (assumption permitted if no actual assignment).

### Protective Measures for Licensees

- **Structural Protections** / License the IP from a bankruptcy-remote SPE; ensure the SPE is not consolidated with the licensor's estate. / **Consequence**: If properly structured, the SPE does not file for bankruptcy with the licensor, and the license is not subject to Section 365. Requires true legal separation (separate governance, assets, and capitalization).
- **Contractual Protections** / Step-in rights, source code escrow, express survival-of-rejection clauses, covenant not to sue. / **Consequence**: Contractual protections are subject to bankruptcy court review and may be overridden by the Code, but they provide additional layers of defense and bargaining leverage.
- **Insurance** / IP insurance and supply chain disruption insurance may cover losses from licensor bankruptcy. / **Consequence**: Coverage must be specifically negotiated for bankruptcy scenarios; standard IP insurance policies do not typically cover losses from licensor rejection of the license.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **IP and Technology** — License structuring, source code escrow, patent assignment vs. license distinction
- **Executory Contracts** — Section 365 general framework applies; 365(n) is a specific carve-out for IP
- **Corporate** — SPE structuring for bankruptcy remoteness, true sale/true license analysis
- **Real Estate** — Trademark and trade dress in franchise and retail lease contexts

## Preference Actions

# Preference Actions

## Applicability

Applies when a trustee or debtor-in-possession seeks to avoid pre-petition transfers as preferential under Section 547 of the Bankruptcy Code, or when a creditor has received payments or transfers in the period before a bankruptcy filing and needs to assess avoidance risk. Preference actions are among the most common types of bankruptcy litigation.

## Key Requirements

### Elements of a Preference (Section 547(b))

- **Transfer of Property** / Any transfer of an interest of the debtor in property, including payments, security interest grants, and setoffs. / **Consequence**: "Transfer" is defined broadly under Section 101(54) — includes every mode of disposing of property, whether direct or indirect, voluntary or involuntary. Even a transfer by operation of law (e.g., judgment lien) qualifies.
- **To or for the Benefit of a Creditor** / The transfer must benefit a creditor — payments to a creditor, guarantor payments that benefit the guaranteed creditor, or liens securing a creditor's claim. / **Consequence**: Indirect benefits count; a payment to a creditor's agent or a transfer to a third party that satisfies a debt to the creditor can be avoided.
- **On Account of Antecedent Debt** / The transfer must be on account of a debt that existed before the transfer was made. / **Consequence**: Transfers made simultaneously with the incurrence of new debt (contemporaneous exchanges) are not on account of antecedent debt and are not preferential — but see the contemporaneous exchange defense below for the formal requirements.
- **Made While Insolvent** / The debtor must be insolvent at the time of the transfer. Section 547(f) creates a **rebuttable presumption** of insolvency during the **90 days** before the petition date. / **Consequence**: The presumption shifts the burden to the creditor to prove the debtor was solvent at the time of the transfer; outside the 90-day window, the trustee must affirmatively prove insolvency.
- **Within the Preference Period** / The transfer must have been made within **90 days** before the petition date for non-insiders, or within **1 year** before the petition date for insiders. / **Consequence**: "Insider" includes directors, officers, persons in control, and their relatives (Section 101(31)). Insider preference actions are powerful weapons against management self-dealing.
- **More Than Creditor Would Receive** / The transfer must enable the creditor to receive more than it would in a Chapter 7 liquidation. / **Consequence**: If unsecured creditors would receive 100% in a hypothetical liquidation (solvent debtor filing), the preference fails. In practice, most cases involve insolvent debtors where unsecured creditors receive far less than 100%.

### Defenses to Preference Actions

- **Ordinary Course of Business (Section 547(c)(2))** / A transfer is not avoidable if it was: (a) in payment of a debt incurred in the ordinary course of business of both the debtor and the creditor, AND (b) made in the ordinary course of business or financial affairs of the debtor and the creditor, OR made according to ordinary business terms. / **Consequence**: Post-2005 BAPCPA amendments require only ONE of the two prongs (subjective OR objective), making the defense easier to establish. Subjective prong: consistent with the parties' own historical payment practices. Objective prong: consistent with industry norms.
- **Payment Timing Analysis** / Courts examine the historical payment pattern between the parties — typically over the **2-year period** before the preference period. / **Consequence**: If the debtor historically paid invoices at **30-45 days** and the preferential payments were at **35 days**, the ordinary course defense is strong. If the debtor historically paid at **30 days** and the preferential payments were at **90 days** after dunning, the defense fails.
- **Contemporaneous Exchange (Section 547(c)(1))** / A transfer intended by both parties to be a contemporaneous exchange for new value, and that was in fact substantially contemporaneous. / **Consequence**: Protects COD transactions where goods and payment are exchanged at approximately the same time. A delay of more than **10-15 days** between delivery and payment may defeat the defense, though courts vary.
- **Subsequent New Value (Section 547(c)(4))** / A transfer is not avoidable to the extent the creditor provided new value to the debtor AFTER the preferential transfer, which new value was not itself secured by an otherwise unavoidable security interest. / **Consequence**: The new value "nets down" the preference — if the creditor received a $100,000 preferential payment and subsequently shipped $60,000 in new goods, only $40,000 is avoidable. New value must come AFTER the preferential transfer.
- **Purchase Money Security Interest (Section 547(c)(3))** / A security interest in property acquired by the debtor is not avoidable if: (a) the creditor gave new value enabling the debtor to acquire the property, (b) the debtor acquired the property, and (c) the creditor perfected within **30 days** of the debtor's receipt of possession. / **Consequence**: Protects equipment financing and similar purchase-money transactions; the 30-day perfection window is strictly enforced.
- **Small Preference Threshold** / Preference actions may not be commenced if the aggregate value of all transfers to the creditor is less than **$7,575** (consumer debtor) or **$7,575** (non-consumer — updated periodically). / **Consequence**: The de minimis threshold prevents small-dollar preference litigation; amounts are adjusted every 3 years under Section 104. Check current thresholds.

### Procedural Considerations

- **Statute of Limitations** / Preference actions must be commenced within **2 years** of the order for relief (petition date in voluntary cases) or within **1 year** after the trustee is appointed, whichever is later. / **Consequence**: Trustees and DIPs often file preference actions in bulk near the deadline; creditors may receive demand letters well before the statute runs. Responding promptly with defense evidence is critical.
- **Venue** / After the 2005 amendments, preference actions involving claims of less than **$25,000** against non-insiders must be filed in the district where the defendant resides. / **Consequence**: This provision prevents debtors from dragging small creditors to distant bankruptcy courts; venue objections should be raised promptly.
- **Burden of Proof** / The trustee bears the burden of proving all elements of the preference (Section 547(g)); the creditor bears the burden of proving affirmative defenses. / **Consequence**: Creditors should maintain detailed records of payment history, shipping records, and business terms to support ordinary course and new value defenses.

### Strategic Considerations

- **Demand Letter Phase** / Most preference actions begin with a demand letter from the trustee or estate professional; settlement rates are high (**60-70%** of claims settle before litigation). / **Consequence**: Early engagement with detailed defense analysis can significantly reduce settlement amounts; creditors who ignore demand letters may face more aggressive litigation.
- **Preference Exposure Analysis** / Creditors should analyze exposure upon learning of a customer's financial distress — map all payments received in the 90 days before the expected filing date. / **Consequence**: Proactive preference exposure analysis allows creditors to adjust trade terms, demand COD or prepayment, and build ordinary course defense evidence before the bankruptcy filing.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Financial Services** — Financial contract safe harbors (Sections 546(e), 555-561) protect certain transfers from preference avoidance
- **Corporate** — Insider preference actions against directors and officers (1-year lookback period)
- **Real Estate** — Mortgage payments and real property transfers within the preference period
- **Employment** — Wage payments within the preference period (typically defended as ordinary course)

## Safe Harbors

# Safe Harbors for Financial Contracts

## Applicability

Applies when bankruptcy intersects with financial contracts — securities contracts, commodities contracts, forward contracts, repurchase agreements (repos), swap agreements, and master netting agreements. Sections 546(e) and 555-561 of the Bankruptcy Code provide broad protections for counterparties to these contracts, exempting them from the automatic stay, preference avoidance, and fraudulent transfer recovery.

## Key Requirements

### Protected Financial Contracts

- **Securities Contracts (Section 741(7))** / Contracts for the purchase, sale, or loan of securities; margin loans; security agreements related to securities; and any agreement or transaction similar to the foregoing. / **Consequence**: Defined extremely broadly — includes stock purchase agreements, merger agreements with securities components, and even certain employment agreements with equity compensation elements. Courts have expanded the definition over time.
- **Commodities Contracts (Section 761(4))** / Contracts for the purchase or sale of a commodity for future delivery on or subject to the rules of a commodities exchange. / **Consequence**: Covers futures contracts, options on futures, and related agreements traded on regulated exchanges.
- **Forward Contracts (Section 101(25))** / Contracts (other than a commodity contract) for the purchase, sale, or transfer of a commodity or any similar good, article, service, right, or interest, with a maturity date more than **2 days** after the trade date. / **Consequence**: "Forward contract" has been broadly interpreted to include physical commodity forwards, energy supply agreements, and even some M&A agreements structured as forward purchases of equity.
- **Repurchase Agreements (Section 101(47))** / Agreements to sell and repurchase securities, mortgage-related securities, mortgage loans, or interests therein, against the transfer of funds with a simultaneous agreement to repurchase. / **Consequence**: Repo agreements are critical to short-term funding markets; safe harbor protection ensures repos can be unwound rapidly without bankruptcy interference.
- **Swap Agreements (Section 101(53B))** / Interest rate swaps, currency swaps, equity swaps, credit default swaps, total return swaps, commodity swaps, and any similar agreements. / **Consequence**: The definition was significantly expanded by the 2005 BAPCPA amendments; includes virtually all derivative transactions, whether cleared or bilateral.
- **Master Netting Agreements (Section 101(38A))** / Agreements providing for the netting of payment obligations under two or more qualified financial contracts between the same parties. / **Consequence**: ISDA Master Agreements are the most common form; the safe harbor protects the exercise of close-out netting across multiple transactions.

### Section 546(e) — Settlement Payment Defense

- **Scope** / The trustee may NOT avoid a transfer that is a margin payment or settlement payment made by or to (or for the benefit of) a financial institution, financial participant, stockbroker, securities clearing agency, commodity broker, forward contract merchant, or repo/swap participant. / **Consequence**: Protects payments made through the financial system — including payments in leveraged buyouts, merger consideration, and redemptions — if they flow through a qualifying financial intermediary.
- **Merit Management (2018)** / The Supreme Court in Merit Management Group v. FTI Consulting held that Section 546(e) does not protect transfers where the financial institution is a mere conduit — the overarching transfer must be "made by or to" a financial institution in its capacity as a beneficiary. / **Consequence**: Significantly narrowed the settlement payment defense for LBO and M&A transactions; transfers to shareholders through a financial institution acting merely as an intermediary are no longer automatically protected. However, the financial institution itself remains protected.
- **Continued Breadth** / Despite Merit Management, Section 546(e) remains a powerful defense when the financial institution is a party to the transaction (not just a conduit), or when the transfer qualifies as a "securities contract" settlement. / **Consequence**: Structuring transactions to ensure financial institution involvement as a principal (not mere agent) can preserve the safe harbor. Legal analysis of the transaction structure is essential.

### Exemption from Automatic Stay (Sections 555-561)

- **Close-Out Rights** / Counterparties to protected financial contracts may exercise contractual rights of liquidation, termination, and acceleration notwithstanding the automatic stay. / **Consequence**: Unlike other creditors who must seek relief from stay, financial contract counterparties can immediately close out positions, calculate damages, and net obligations. This exemption prevents systemic risk from the inability to manage financial exposure.
- **Setoff and Netting** / Counterparties may exercise setoff rights under master netting agreements without violating the automatic stay. / **Consequence**: Close-out netting under ISDA Master Agreements is fully protected; the non-debtor counterparty can net all transactions under the master agreement to a single payment obligation.
- **Ipso Facto Protection** / Unlike other contracts where ipso facto clauses are unenforceable (Section 365(e)), financial contract counterparties MAY enforce termination provisions triggered by bankruptcy filing. / **Consequence**: The ipso facto override in Section 365(e) does not apply to financial contracts; bankruptcy itself is a valid termination trigger for swaps, repos, and securities contracts.

### Protections from Avoidance Actions

- **Preference Protection** / Margin payments and settlement payments under financial contracts are protected from preference avoidance under Section 546(e). / **Consequence**: Routine margin calls, variation margin payments, and settlement payments made within the 90-day preference period are not avoidable even if they meet all elements of a preference under Section 547.
- **Fraudulent Transfer Protection** / Transfers under financial contracts are protected from constructive fraudulent transfer avoidance (Section 548(a)(1)(B)) under Sections 546(e) and 548(d)(2). / **Consequence**: Settlement payments and margin payments cannot be avoided as constructive fraudulent transfers. However, transfers made with actual intent to defraud (Section 548(a)(1)(A)) are NOT protected by the safe harbor.
- **Actual Fraud Carve-Out** / Safe harbors do NOT protect transfers made with actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud creditors. / **Consequence**: If a financial transaction is used as a vehicle for actual fraud (e.g., sham transactions designed to strip assets), the safe harbor will not apply. The trustee bears the burden of proving actual fraudulent intent.

### Qualifying Counterparties

- **Financial Institution** / Includes banks, trust companies, savings institutions, credit unions, and entities the majority of whose business is lending or financial activities (Section 101(22)). / **Consequence**: A broadly defined category; even non-traditional financial entities that primarily engage in financial activities may qualify. The entity's role in the specific transaction matters post-Merit Management.
- **Financial Participant** / An entity that, on the date of filing, has gross mark-to-market positions of at least **$1 billion** in outstanding financial contracts with the debtor or its affiliates, or has gross mark-to-market positions of at least **$100 million** in outstanding financial contracts. / **Consequence**: Provides safe harbor protection to large counterparties that may not otherwise qualify as financial institutions; designed to protect systemically important market participants.
- **Stockbroker / Securities Clearing Agency / Commodity Broker** / Broker-dealers registered under the Securities Exchange Act, clearing agencies registered under the Exchange Act, and futures commission merchants registered under the Commodity Exchange Act. / **Consequence**: These regulated entities automatically qualify for safe harbor protection for transactions within their regulated activities.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Financial Services** — Safe harbors are designed to protect the financial system; understanding these provisions is essential for structuring financial transactions with potential bankruptcy exposure
- **Preference Actions** — Safe harbor provides a complete defense to preference claims for qualifying transfers
- **Corporate** — LBO and M&A transaction structuring to preserve (or challenge) Section 546(e) protection post-Merit Management
- **Automatic Stay** — Financial contract safe harbors are the primary exception to the otherwise comprehensive automatic stay
