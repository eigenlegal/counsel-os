## Executory Contracts

# Executory Contracts

## Applicability

Applies when a bankruptcy case involves contracts or unexpired leases where material performance remains due on both sides. Section 365 of the Bankruptcy Code governs the debtor's power to assume, reject, or assign executory contracts and unexpired leases, which is one of the most powerful tools in bankruptcy reorganization.

## Key Requirements

### Definition and Identification

- **Countryman Test** / A contract is executory if the obligations of both parties are so far unperformed that failure of either to complete performance would constitute a material breach. / **Consequence**: The test is applied at the time of the bankruptcy filing. Contracts where only one party has material obligations remaining (e.g., a fully performed sale with only a payment obligation) are generally not executory and cannot be rejected under Section 365.
- **Common Executory Contracts** / Equipment leases, real property leases, supply agreements, license agreements, franchise agreements, employment contracts, service agreements. / **Consequence**: Each category has specific treatment rules; real property leases and IP licenses have special provisions (Sections 365(d) and 365(n)) that override the general rules.

### Assumption (Section 365(b))

- **Cure of Defaults** / To assume a contract, the debtor must cure all existing defaults (or provide adequate assurance of prompt cure). / **Consequence**: Cure amounts include monetary defaults (unpaid rent, missed payments) and non-monetary defaults that are curable. Some non-monetary defaults (e.g., failure to operate continuously in a retail lease) may be incurable, preventing assumption.
- **Compensation for Pecuniary Loss** / The debtor must compensate the non-debtor party for actual pecuniary losses resulting from the default. / **Consequence**: Compensation covers out-of-pocket losses caused by the default — not lost future profits or consequential damages. The amount is determined by the court if disputed.
- **Adequate Assurance of Future Performance** / The debtor must demonstrate ability to perform going forward. / **Consequence**: Adequate assurance is fact-specific — courts consider the debtor's financial condition, operational capacity, and reorganization plan. For shopping center leases, Section 365(b)(3) imposes enhanced requirements (see below).
- **Ipso Facto Clauses** / Contract provisions that terminate or modify the contract upon bankruptcy filing are unenforceable under Section 365(e). / **Consequence**: Anti-assignment clauses triggered solely by bankruptcy or insolvency are overridden; however, clauses triggered by non-bankruptcy events (e.g., failure to maintain financial ratios) that happen to coincide with bankruptcy are more complex.

### Rejection (Section 365(a))

- **Business Judgment Standard** / The court approves rejection if the debtor's decision is a reasonable exercise of business judgment — not the best possible decision, just a reasonable one. / **Consequence**: Courts rarely second-guess rejection decisions; the threshold is low. The non-debtor party's remedy is a pre-petition unsecured claim for rejection damages.
- **Rejection Damages** / Rejection constitutes a breach of the contract as of immediately before the petition date. Damages are treated as a pre-petition general unsecured claim. / **Consequence**: Rejection damage claims are subject to the same distribution as other general unsecured claims — often receiving **cents on the dollar**. For real property leases, Section 502(b)(6) caps the landlord's rejection damage claim.
- **Landlord Damage Cap (Section 502(b)(6))** / Landlord's rejection damage claim is capped at the greater of: (a) **1 year** of rent, or (b) **15%** of the remaining lease term (not to exceed **3 years**) of rent, plus any unpaid rent due under the lease as of the earlier of the petition date or the date the landlord repossessed. / **Consequence**: For long-term leases, the cap significantly reduces the landlord's recovery. "Rent" is broadly interpreted to include base rent, CAM, taxes, and other charges under the lease.

### Assignment (Section 365(f))

- **Anti-Assignment Override** / Section 365(f) allows the debtor to assign executory contracts notwithstanding anti-assignment provisions in the contract or applicable law. / **Consequence**: This is one of the most commercially significant provisions in bankruptcy — the debtor can sell valuable contracts (favorable leases, below-market supply agreements) to third parties over the non-debtor party's objection.
- **Requirements for Assignment** / The contract must first be assumed (all defaults cured), and the assignee must provide adequate assurance of future performance. / **Consequence**: Adequate assurance for the assignee is an independent requirement — the assignee must demonstrate financial capacity and operational ability to perform. The non-debtor party can object if adequate assurance is insufficient.
- **Exceptions to Assignment** / Contracts for personal services, contracts dependent on the debtor's unique skills, and certain financial accommodations (loans) cannot be assigned over objection. / **Consequence**: The personal services exception is narrowly construed; routine service contracts (janitorial, IT support) are generally assignable. Government contracts have additional restrictions.

### Timing Deadlines

- **Chapter 7 — 60-Day Deadline** / The trustee must assume or reject executory contracts within **60 days** of the order for relief, or the contract is deemed rejected. / **Consequence**: The 60-day deadline can be extended for cause by court order, but automatic deemed rejection occurs if no action is taken and no extension is granted.
- **Chapter 11 — Plan Confirmation** / The debtor may assume or reject executory contracts any time before plan confirmation. / **Consequence**: Contracts remain in limbo during the Chapter 11 case, which can last months or years; the non-debtor party may file a motion under Section 365(d)(2) to compel the debtor to decide within a court-specified period.
- **Nonresidential Real Property Leases (Section 365(d)(4))** / The debtor must assume or reject nonresidential real property leases within **120 days** of the petition date, with one **90-day extension** available (total maximum: **210 days** without landlord consent). / **Consequence**: After 210 days, the lease is deemed rejected unless the landlord consents to further extensions. This is a hard deadline — courts cannot extend beyond 210 days without landlord agreement.

### Shopping Center Special Rules (Section 365(b)(3))

- **Adequate Assurance Requirements** / For shopping center leases, adequate assurance requires: (a) percentage rent will not decline substantially, (b) assumption is subject to all lease terms (including radius, use, and exclusivity), (c) no disruption to tenant mix, (d) the assignee is financially capable. / **Consequence**: Shopping center provisions give landlords significantly more leverage to oppose assumption and assignment; tenant mix and use clause compliance are independently enforceable.

### Intellectual Property Licenses

- **Section 365(n) Election** / When a licensor rejects an IP license, the licensee may elect to retain its rights under the license (including exclusivity) for the remaining term, provided it continues to make royalty payments. / **Consequence**: The licensee's election under 365(n) preserves the right to use the IP but does not preserve affirmative obligations of the licensor (e.g., maintenance, updates, support). See `ip-licenses-in-bankruptcy.md` for detailed treatment.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Real Estate** — Nonresidential real property lease treatment, landlord damage caps, shopping center rules
- **IP and Technology** — Section 365(n) protections for IP licensees, trademark exclusion debate
- **Employment** — Collective bargaining agreements (Section 1113), retiree benefits (Section 1114)
- **Financial Services** — Financial contracts may be exempt from Section 365 under safe harbor provisions
