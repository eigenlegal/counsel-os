## Creditor Rights

# Creditor Rights

## Applicability

Applies when a creditor seeks to protect or enforce its claims in a bankruptcy proceeding, including filing proofs of claim, asserting secured status, seeking adequate protection, participating in DIP financing, or contesting plan confirmation. Covers the full spectrum of creditor participation from case commencement through plan confirmation and distribution.

## Key Requirements

### Proof of Claim (Section 501)

- **Filing Requirement** / Creditors must file a proof of claim to participate in distributions. The bar date (deadline for filing) is set by the court — typically **70 days** after the petition date in Chapter 7 and **90 days** after the first meeting of creditors in Chapter 11. / **Consequence**: Failure to file by the bar date permanently bars the claim from receiving any distribution (with narrow exceptions for lack of notice). Government units have **180 days** from the petition date to file.
- **Claim Presumption** / A filed proof of claim is deemed allowed unless a party in interest objects (Section 502(a)). / **Consequence**: The burden shifts to the objecting party to demonstrate the claim is invalid, duplicative, or improperly classified. Claims supported by documentation (invoices, contracts) are more difficult to challenge.
- **Disputed, Contingent, and Unliquidated Claims** / Claims may be filed even if disputed, contingent, or unliquidated. / **Consequence**: The bankruptcy court may estimate disputed or contingent claims under Section 502(c) for purposes of allowance; estimation can result in significant reduction of claimed amounts.

### Priority of Claims (Section 507)

- **Administrative Expenses (Section 507(a)(2))** / Post-petition claims essential to preserving the estate: professional fees, operating expenses, goods received within **20 days** before petition (Section 503(b)(9)). / **Consequence**: Administrative claims are paid in full before any distribution to pre-petition unsecured creditors. The 20-day goods claim is a critical protection for trade creditors — goods delivered within 20 days of the petition are treated as administrative priority.
- **Wage Priority (Section 507(a)(4))** / Employee wages, salaries, and commissions earned within **180 days** before the petition date, up to **$15,150** per employee. / **Consequence**: The cap is adjusted every 3 years (most recent adjustment effective April 2022); amounts above the cap are general unsecured claims. Includes vacation pay, severance, and sick leave.
- **Employee Benefit Plan Contributions (Section 507(a)(5))** / Contributions to employee benefit plans arising from services rendered within **180 days** before the petition, up to **$15,150** per employee (reduced by amounts paid under wage priority). / **Consequence**: Covers health insurance, retirement plan contributions, and other benefit obligations; calculation is complex when combined with the wage priority cap.
- **Customer Deposits (Section 507(a)(7))** / Deposits for undelivered goods or services by individual consumers, up to **$3,350** per individual. / **Consequence**: Protects individual (not business) customers who prepaid for goods or services the debtor never delivered; amounts above the cap are general unsecured claims.
- **Tax Claims (Section 507(a)(8))** / Income taxes for taxable years ending within **3 years** before the petition; employment taxes; sales/use taxes; and certain property taxes. / **Consequence**: Priority tax claims are nondischargeable and must be paid in full under a Chapter 11 plan (may be paid in installments over **5 years** with interest). Failure to pay priority taxes prevents plan confirmation.

### Secured vs. Unsecured Claims

- **Secured Claim (Section 506(a))** / A claim is secured to the extent of the value of the creditor's interest in the estate's property; the remainder is an unsecured claim ("bifurcation"). / **Consequence**: Undersecured creditors (debt exceeds collateral value) have a secured claim up to collateral value and an unsecured deficiency claim. The valuation date and methodology are critical — courts use replacement value for Chapter 11 (Associates Commercial Corp. v. Rash, 1997).
- **Oversecured Creditors** / Creditors whose collateral value exceeds the claim amount are entitled to post-petition interest, fees, and costs under Section 506(b). / **Consequence**: Only oversecured creditors earn post-petition interest; undersecured creditors are frozen at the petition-date amount. The cushion of equity determines entitlement.
- **Lien Avoidance** / The trustee may avoid unperfected liens (Section 544(a), strong-arm power), preferential liens (Section 547), and fraudulent transfers (Section 548). / **Consequence**: A creditor whose lien is avoided becomes unsecured; perfection of security interests before bankruptcy is essential to preserving secured status.

### Adequate Protection (Section 361)

- **Purpose** / Adequate protection compensates a secured creditor for the decline in value of its collateral during the bankruptcy case while the automatic stay prevents foreclosure. / **Consequence**: If adequate protection is inadequate and the collateral declines in value, the creditor has a superpriority administrative claim under Section 507(b) — senior to all other administrative claims, including DIP financing.
- **Forms** / Cash payments, additional or replacement liens, and other relief that provides the "indubitable equivalent" of the creditor's interest. / **Consequence**: Courts determine adequate protection on a case-by-case basis; the debtor bears the burden of proposing and demonstrating adequate protection. Failure to provide adequate protection is grounds for relief from the automatic stay.

### DIP Financing (Section 364)

- **Unsecured Credit in the Ordinary Course (Section 364(a))** / Post-petition unsecured credit in the ordinary course of business automatically has administrative expense priority. / **Consequence**: Trade creditors extending credit to a debtor-in-possession receive administrative priority for post-petition deliveries — a significant improvement over pre-petition unsecured status.
- **Secured DIP Financing (Section 364(d))** / The court may authorize the debtor to obtain post-petition credit secured by a senior or equal lien on estate property ("priming lien"), if existing lienholders are adequately protected. / **Consequence**: DIP lenders demand and receive superpriority claims, priming liens, and roll-up provisions (converting pre-petition debt to post-petition status). Existing secured creditors must receive adequate protection — often monthly interest payments and replacement liens.
- **DIP Financing Terms** / Market terms include: commitment fees (**1-3%**), interest rate at SOFR + **300-600 bps**, unused line fees (**0.5-1%**), mandatory prepayment events, and milestones (plan filing within **90-120 days**, confirmation within **180-240 days**). / **Consequence**: DIP financing covenants effectively set the timetable for the bankruptcy case; failure to meet milestones triggers events of default and may force liquidation or sale.

### Cramdown (Section 1129(b))

- **Requirements** / If a class of claims rejects the plan, the court may confirm over the objection ("cramdown") if the plan: (a) does not discriminate unfairly, and (b) is "fair and equitable" as to the dissenting class. / **Consequence**: Cramdown is the debtor's most powerful tool against holdout creditors; it forces acceptance of plan terms that meet the statutory requirements.
- **Fair and Equitable — Secured Claims** / The plan must provide the secured creditor with: (a) retention of lien and deferred cash payments equal to the present value of the allowed secured claim, OR (b) sale of the property with lien attaching to proceeds, OR (c) the "indubitable equivalent" of the secured claim. / **Consequence**: "Indubitable equivalent" is the highest standard — it must eliminate any reasonable doubt about the creditor receiving the value of its claim. Cash payment of the allowed secured claim satisfies this standard.
- **Fair and Equitable — Unsecured Claims (Absolute Priority Rule)** / No junior class may receive or retain any property under the plan unless the senior dissenting class is paid in full. / **Consequence**: In practice, the absolute priority rule means equity holders receive nothing unless all unsecured creditors are paid in full. Exceptions: individual debtors in Chapter 11 (BAPCPA carve-out) and negotiated "gifting" by consenting senior classes.
- **No Unfair Discrimination** / Dissenting class must be treated comparably to other classes of the same priority level. / **Consequence**: The debtor cannot favor one class of unsecured creditors over another without justification; different treatment requires legitimate business reasons (e.g., trade creditor payments necessary for operations).

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment** — Employee wage and benefit priority claims; WARN Act claims; retiree benefit obligations under Section 1114
- **Environmental & ESG** — Environmental cleanup costs may be treated as administrative priority claims; government environmental enforcement is excepted from the stay
- **Real Estate** — Secured real estate lenders and adequate protection; Section 363 sales of real property
- **Financial Services** — DIP financing terms and conditions; financial contract safe harbors
- **Corporate** — Fiduciary duties of directors in the zone of insolvency; equity committee formation
