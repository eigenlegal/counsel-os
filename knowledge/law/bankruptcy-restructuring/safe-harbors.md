## Safe Harbors

# Safe Harbors for Financial Contracts

## Applicability

Applies when bankruptcy intersects with financial contracts — securities contracts, commodities contracts, forward contracts, repurchase agreements (repos), swap agreements, and master netting agreements. Sections 546(e) and 555-561 of the Bankruptcy Code provide broad protections for counterparties to these contracts, exempting them from the automatic stay, preference avoidance, and fraudulent transfer recovery.

## Key Requirements

### Protected Financial Contracts

- **Securities Contracts (Section 741(7))** / Contracts for the purchase, sale, or loan of securities; margin loans; security agreements related to securities; and any agreement or transaction similar to the foregoing. / **Consequence**: Defined extremely broadly — includes stock purchase agreements, merger agreements with securities components, and even certain employment agreements with equity compensation elements. Courts have expanded the definition over time.
- **Commodities Contracts (Section 761(4))** / Contracts for the purchase or sale of a commodity for future delivery on or subject to the rules of a commodities exchange. / **Consequence**: Covers futures contracts, options on futures, and related agreements traded on regulated exchanges.
- **Forward Contracts (Section 101(25))** / Contracts (other than a commodity contract) for the purchase, sale, or transfer of a commodity or any similar good, article, service, right, or interest, with a maturity date more than **2 days** after the trade date. / **Consequence**: "Forward contract" has been broadly interpreted to include physical commodity forwards, energy supply agreements, and even some M&A agreements structured as forward purchases of equity.
- **Repurchase Agreements (Section 101(47))** / Agreements to sell and repurchase securities, mortgage-related securities, mortgage loans, or interests therein, against the transfer of funds with a simultaneous agreement to repurchase. / **Consequence**: Repo agreements are critical to short-term funding markets; safe harbor protection ensures repos can be unwound rapidly without bankruptcy interference.
- **Swap Agreements (Section 101(53B))** / Interest rate swaps, currency swaps, equity swaps, credit default swaps, total return swaps, commodity swaps, and any similar agreements. / **Consequence**: The definition was significantly expanded by the 2005 BAPCPA amendments; includes virtually all derivative transactions, whether cleared or bilateral.
- **Master Netting Agreements (Section 101(38A))** / Agreements providing for the netting of payment obligations under two or more qualified financial contracts between the same parties. / **Consequence**: ISDA Master Agreements are the most common form; the safe harbor protects the exercise of close-out netting across multiple transactions.

### Section 546(e) — Settlement Payment Defense

- **Scope** / The trustee may NOT avoid a transfer that is a margin payment or settlement payment made by or to (or for the benefit of) a financial institution, financial participant, stockbroker, securities clearing agency, commodity broker, forward contract merchant, or repo/swap participant. / **Consequence**: Protects payments made through the financial system — including payments in leveraged buyouts, merger consideration, and redemptions — if they flow through a qualifying financial intermediary.
- **Merit Management (2018)** / The Supreme Court in Merit Management Group v. FTI Consulting held that Section 546(e) does not protect transfers where the financial institution is a mere conduit — the overarching transfer must be "made by or to" a financial institution in its capacity as a beneficiary. / **Consequence**: Significantly narrowed the settlement payment defense for LBO and M&A transactions; transfers to shareholders through a financial institution acting merely as an intermediary are no longer automatically protected. However, the financial institution itself remains protected.
- **Continued Breadth** / Despite Merit Management, Section 546(e) remains a powerful defense when the financial institution is a party to the transaction (not just a conduit), or when the transfer qualifies as a "securities contract" settlement. / **Consequence**: Structuring transactions to ensure financial institution involvement as a principal (not mere agent) can preserve the safe harbor. Legal analysis of the transaction structure is essential.

### Exemption from Automatic Stay (Sections 555-561)

- **Close-Out Rights** / Counterparties to protected financial contracts may exercise contractual rights of liquidation, termination, and acceleration notwithstanding the automatic stay. / **Consequence**: Unlike other creditors who must seek relief from stay, financial contract counterparties can immediately close out positions, calculate damages, and net obligations. This exemption prevents systemic risk from the inability to manage financial exposure.
- **Setoff and Netting** / Counterparties may exercise setoff rights under master netting agreements without violating the automatic stay. / **Consequence**: Close-out netting under ISDA Master Agreements is fully protected; the non-debtor counterparty can net all transactions under the master agreement to a single payment obligation.
- **Ipso Facto Protection** / Unlike other contracts where ipso facto clauses are unenforceable (Section 365(e)), financial contract counterparties MAY enforce termination provisions triggered by bankruptcy filing. / **Consequence**: The ipso facto override in Section 365(e) does not apply to financial contracts; bankruptcy itself is a valid termination trigger for swaps, repos, and securities contracts.

### Protections from Avoidance Actions

- **Preference Protection** / Margin payments and settlement payments under financial contracts are protected from preference avoidance under Section 546(e). / **Consequence**: Routine margin calls, variation margin payments, and settlement payments made within the 90-day preference period are not avoidable even if they meet all elements of a preference under Section 547.
- **Fraudulent Transfer Protection** / Transfers under financial contracts are protected from constructive fraudulent transfer avoidance (Section 548(a)(1)(B)) under Sections 546(e) and 548(d)(2). / **Consequence**: Settlement payments and margin payments cannot be avoided as constructive fraudulent transfers. However, transfers made with actual intent to defraud (Section 548(a)(1)(A)) are NOT protected by the safe harbor.
- **Actual Fraud Carve-Out** / Safe harbors do NOT protect transfers made with actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud creditors. / **Consequence**: If a financial transaction is used as a vehicle for actual fraud (e.g., sham transactions designed to strip assets), the safe harbor will not apply. The trustee bears the burden of proving actual fraudulent intent.

### Qualifying Counterparties

- **Financial Institution** / Includes banks, trust companies, savings institutions, credit unions, and entities the majority of whose business is lending or financial activities (Section 101(22)). / **Consequence**: A broadly defined category; even non-traditional financial entities that primarily engage in financial activities may qualify. The entity's role in the specific transaction matters post-Merit Management.
- **Financial Participant** / An entity that, on the date of filing, has gross mark-to-market positions of at least **$1 billion** in outstanding financial contracts with the debtor or its affiliates, or has gross mark-to-market positions of at least **$100 million** in outstanding financial contracts. / **Consequence**: Provides safe harbor protection to large counterparties that may not otherwise qualify as financial institutions; designed to protect systemically important market participants.
- **Stockbroker / Securities Clearing Agency / Commodity Broker** / Broker-dealers registered under the Securities Exchange Act, clearing agencies registered under the Exchange Act, and futures commission merchants registered under the Commodity Exchange Act. / **Consequence**: These regulated entities automatically qualify for safe harbor protection for transactions within their regulated activities.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Financial Services** — Safe harbors are designed to protect the financial system; understanding these provisions is essential for structuring financial transactions with potential bankruptcy exposure
- **Preference Actions** — Safe harbor provides a complete defense to preference claims for qualifying transfers
- **Corporate** — LBO and M&A transaction structuring to preserve (or challenge) Section 546(e) protection post-Merit Management
- **Automatic Stay** — Financial contract safe harbors are the primary exception to the otherwise comprehensive automatic stay
