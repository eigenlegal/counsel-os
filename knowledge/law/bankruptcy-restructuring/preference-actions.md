## Preference Actions

# Preference Actions

## Applicability

Applies when a trustee or debtor-in-possession seeks to avoid pre-petition transfers as preferential under Section 547 of the Bankruptcy Code, or when a creditor has received payments or transfers in the period before a bankruptcy filing and needs to assess avoidance risk. Preference actions are among the most common types of bankruptcy litigation.

## Key Requirements

### Elements of a Preference (Section 547(b))

- **Transfer of Property** / Any transfer of an interest of the debtor in property, including payments, security interest grants, and setoffs. / **Consequence**: "Transfer" is defined broadly under Section 101(54) — includes every mode of disposing of property, whether direct or indirect, voluntary or involuntary. Even a transfer by operation of law (e.g., judgment lien) qualifies.
- **To or for the Benefit of a Creditor** / The transfer must benefit a creditor — payments to a creditor, guarantor payments that benefit the guaranteed creditor, or liens securing a creditor's claim. / **Consequence**: Indirect benefits count; a payment to a creditor's agent or a transfer to a third party that satisfies a debt to the creditor can be avoided.
- **On Account of Antecedent Debt** / The transfer must be on account of a debt that existed before the transfer was made. / **Consequence**: Transfers made simultaneously with the incurrence of new debt (contemporaneous exchanges) are not on account of antecedent debt and are not preferential — but see the contemporaneous exchange defense below for the formal requirements.
- **Made While Insolvent** / The debtor must be insolvent at the time of the transfer. Section 547(f) creates a **rebuttable presumption** of insolvency during the **90 days** before the petition date. / **Consequence**: The presumption shifts the burden to the creditor to prove the debtor was solvent at the time of the transfer; outside the 90-day window, the trustee must affirmatively prove insolvency.
- **Within the Preference Period** / The transfer must have been made within **90 days** before the petition date for non-insiders, or within **1 year** before the petition date for insiders. / **Consequence**: "Insider" includes directors, officers, persons in control, and their relatives (Section 101(31)). Insider preference actions are powerful weapons against management self-dealing.
- **More Than Creditor Would Receive** / The transfer must enable the creditor to receive more than it would in a Chapter 7 liquidation. / **Consequence**: If unsecured creditors would receive 100% in a hypothetical liquidation (solvent debtor filing), the preference fails. In practice, most cases involve insolvent debtors where unsecured creditors receive far less than 100%.

### Defenses to Preference Actions

- **Ordinary Course of Business (Section 547(c)(2))** / A transfer is not avoidable if it was: (a) in payment of a debt incurred in the ordinary course of business of both the debtor and the creditor, AND (b) made in the ordinary course of business or financial affairs of the debtor and the creditor, OR made according to ordinary business terms. / **Consequence**: Post-2005 BAPCPA amendments require only ONE of the two prongs (subjective OR objective), making the defense easier to establish. Subjective prong: consistent with the parties' own historical payment practices. Objective prong: consistent with industry norms.
- **Payment Timing Analysis** / Courts examine the historical payment pattern between the parties — typically over the **2-year period** before the preference period. / **Consequence**: If the debtor historically paid invoices at **30-45 days** and the preferential payments were at **35 days**, the ordinary course defense is strong. If the debtor historically paid at **30 days** and the preferential payments were at **90 days** after dunning, the defense fails.
- **Contemporaneous Exchange (Section 547(c)(1))** / A transfer intended by both parties to be a contemporaneous exchange for new value, and that was in fact substantially contemporaneous. / **Consequence**: Protects COD transactions where goods and payment are exchanged at approximately the same time. A delay of more than **10-15 days** between delivery and payment may defeat the defense, though courts vary.
- **Subsequent New Value (Section 547(c)(4))** / A transfer is not avoidable to the extent the creditor provided new value to the debtor AFTER the preferential transfer, which new value was not itself secured by an otherwise unavoidable security interest. / **Consequence**: The new value "nets down" the preference — if the creditor received a $100,000 preferential payment and subsequently shipped $60,000 in new goods, only $40,000 is avoidable. New value must come AFTER the preferential transfer.
- **Purchase Money Security Interest (Section 547(c)(3))** / A security interest in property acquired by the debtor is not avoidable if: (a) the creditor gave new value enabling the debtor to acquire the property, (b) the debtor acquired the property, and (c) the creditor perfected within **30 days** of the debtor's receipt of possession. / **Consequence**: Protects equipment financing and similar purchase-money transactions; the 30-day perfection window is strictly enforced.
- **Small Preference Threshold** / Preference actions may not be commenced if the aggregate value of all transfers to the creditor is less than **$7,575** (consumer debtor) or **$7,575** (non-consumer — updated periodically). / **Consequence**: The de minimis threshold prevents small-dollar preference litigation; amounts are adjusted every 3 years under Section 104. Check current thresholds.

### Procedural Considerations

- **Statute of Limitations** / Preference actions must be commenced within **2 years** of the order for relief (petition date in voluntary cases) or within **1 year** after the trustee is appointed, whichever is later. / **Consequence**: Trustees and DIPs often file preference actions in bulk near the deadline; creditors may receive demand letters well before the statute runs. Responding promptly with defense evidence is critical.
- **Venue** / After the 2005 amendments, preference actions involving claims of less than **$25,000** against non-insiders must be filed in the district where the defendant resides. / **Consequence**: This provision prevents debtors from dragging small creditors to distant bankruptcy courts; venue objections should be raised promptly.
- **Burden of Proof** / The trustee bears the burden of proving all elements of the preference (Section 547(g)); the creditor bears the burden of proving affirmative defenses. / **Consequence**: Creditors should maintain detailed records of payment history, shipping records, and business terms to support ordinary course and new value defenses.

### Strategic Considerations

- **Demand Letter Phase** / Most preference actions begin with a demand letter from the trustee or estate professional; settlement rates are high (**60-70%** of claims settle before litigation). / **Consequence**: Early engagement with detailed defense analysis can significantly reduce settlement amounts; creditors who ignore demand letters may face more aggressive litigation.
- **Preference Exposure Analysis** / Creditors should analyze exposure upon learning of a customer's financial distress — map all payments received in the 90 days before the expected filing date. / **Consequence**: Proactive preference exposure analysis allows creditors to adjust trade terms, demand COD or prepayment, and build ordinary course defense evidence before the bankruptcy filing.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Financial Services** — Financial contract safe harbors (Sections 546(e), 555-561) protect certain transfers from preference avoidance
- **Corporate** — Insider preference actions against directors and officers (1-year lookback period)
- **Real Estate** — Mortgage payments and real property transfers within the preference period
- **Employment** — Wage payments within the preference period (typically defended as ordinary course)
