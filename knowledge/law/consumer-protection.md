## Auto Renewal

# Automatic Renewal and Subscription Billing

## Applicability

This sub-file applies whenever a matter involves subscription services, recurring billing, free trial offers, auto-renewal terms, cancellation procedures, negative option offers, continuity plans, or click-to-cancel compliance. Also applies when reviewing SaaS agreements, mobile app subscriptions, membership agreements, or any contract with automatic payment continuation features — including B2B contracts where the counterparty may qualify as a "consumer" under applicable state law.

## Key Requirements

### ROSCA — Restore Online Shoppers' Confidence Act (15 U.S.C. §§ 8401-8405)

- **What**: Prohibits charging consumers for goods or services sold in transactions effected on the Internet through a negative option feature unless three conditions are met: (1) the seller clearly and conspicuously discloses all material terms of the transaction before obtaining billing information, (2) the seller obtains the consumer's informed affirmative consent to the agreement, and (3) the seller provides simple mechanisms for stopping recurring charges and canceling the purchase.
- **Threshold**: "Clearly and conspicuously" means the disclosure must be difficult to miss, easily understandable, and placed where consumers will look before making a purchase decision. "Material terms" include the cost, frequency of charges, and how to cancel. "Simple mechanism" requires the cancellation method be easy to find and use.
- **Consequence**: Violations are treated as unfair or deceptive acts under FTC Act Section 5. Enforcement by the FTC and state AGs. Penalties can include injunctions, consumer refunds, and civil penalties exceeding $50,120 per violation for rule violations.

### FTC Negative Option Rule (16 C.F.R. Part 425)

- **What**: Governs pre-notification negative option plans (e.g., book-of-the-month, record clubs). Requires sellers to clearly and conspicuously disclose the material terms of the plan, including the obligation to notify the seller to avoid receiving and being charged for merchandise.
- **Threshold**: Sellers must provide a form for rejecting merchandise, allow at least 10 days to respond after receipt of the announcement, and promptly credit returns of unwanted merchandise. If the seller ships merchandise after receiving a rejection or after the member cancels, the member may treat it as unordered merchandise.
- **Consequence**: Violations are unfair trade practices under FTC Act Section 5. The FTC can seek injunctions, civil penalties, and consumer redress.

### FTC Click-to-Cancel Rule (Finalized October 2024)

- **What**: Comprehensive update to the FTC's negative option framework requiring that cancellation be at least as easy as sign-up. Applies to all negative option programs regardless of the medium (online, phone, in-person).
- **Threshold**: (1) Sellers must not misrepresent any material fact made to induce a person to pay for goods or services through a negative option feature. (2) Sellers must provide clear and conspicuous disclosures of all material terms before obtaining billing information. (3) Sellers must obtain the consumer's unambiguously affirmative consent to the negative option feature, separate from any other portion of the transaction. (4) Sellers must provide a simple mechanism for cancellation — if sign-up was online, cancellation must be available online. Sellers cannot require consumers to speak with a live agent if sign-up did not require one. (5) Sellers must send annual reminders before each renewal for plans that auto-renew.
- **Consequence**: Violations subject to FTC enforcement, including civil penalties up to $50,120+ per violation. The rule creates clear compliance benchmarks that, if violated, support enforcement actions without needing to prove the full deception or unfairness test.

### California Automatic Renewal Law (ARL, Bus. & Prof. Code §§ 17600-17606)

- **What**: Imposes specific disclosure, consent, and cancellation requirements on businesses making automatic renewal or continuous service offers to California consumers.
- **Threshold**: (1) Clear and conspicuous disclosure of the auto-renewal offer terms, including the recurring charge amount and frequency, before the consumer completes the purchase. (2) The consumer must provide affirmative consent to the auto-renewal terms, which must be documented or recorded. (3) An acknowledgment/confirmation must be provided after sign-up that includes the auto-renewal terms, cancellation policy, and contact information for cancellation. (4) For offers that include a free trial or promotional rate, the disclosure must state the price that will be charged after the trial/promotion, the length of the trial/promotion, and the deadline and method for cancellation. (5) If the consumer signs up online, the business must provide an online cancellation mechanism — a "click to cancel" capability (effective July 1, 2022 amendments). (6) Businesses must provide a toll-free number, email address, or other mechanism for cancellation in the acknowledgment.
- **Consequence**: Non-compliant auto-renewal provisions are void and unenforceable, meaning the business cannot enforce the renewal or collect payment. Goods or services provided under a void provision are deemed an unconditional gift to the consumer. The California AG can enforce with civil penalties. Private right of action under the UCL and CLRA. This is the strictest consequence in any U.S. jurisdiction — rendering the entire auto-renewal provision void.

### New York General Business Law § 527

- **What**: Requires clear disclosure and easy cancellation for automatic renewal and continuous service offers to New York consumers.
- **Threshold**: Disclosure of auto-renewal terms must be "clear and conspicuous." The consumer must affirmatively consent. Acknowledgment must be provided. Cancellation must be available through at least the same method used for enrollment.
- **Consequence**: AG enforcement. Violations may also support claims under GBL § 349 (deceptive practices), providing private rights of action with attorney's fees and treble damages.

### Illinois Automatic Contract Renewal Act (815 ILCS 601)

- **What**: Requires written disclosure of automatic renewal terms in consumer and some commercial contracts, and requires consumer acknowledgment.
- **Threshold**: Written disclosure of the automatic renewal provision must be presented "clearly and conspicuously" in the contract or contract offer. The disclosure must include the cancellation procedure. Notice of renewal must be provided 30-60 days before the renewal date for contracts with terms of 12 months or more.
- **Consequence**: If the seller fails to comply, the automatic renewal provision is not enforceable against the consumer. AG enforcement authority.

### Oregon Automatic Renewal Law (ORS 646A.295)

- **What**: Requires clear and conspicuous disclosures, affirmative consent, and easy cancellation for automatic renewal offers.
- **Threshold**: Disclosure must be presented in a manner that is "clear, conspicuous, and in visual proximity" to the request for consent. Must provide a toll-free number, email, or other easy mechanism for cancellation.
- **Consequence**: Non-compliant auto-renewal provisions are unenforceable. The consumer is not obligated to pay for goods or services under a non-compliant renewal. AG enforcement.

### Virginia Consumer Protection Act — Auto-Renewal (Va. Code § 59.1-207.45 et seq.)

- **What**: Requires disclosure, affirmative consent, and easy cancellation for automatic renewal offers to Virginia consumers.
- **Threshold**: Clear and conspicuous disclosure of automatic renewal terms before the consumer's initial order. Affirmative consent documented. Free trial offers must clearly state post-trial charges and cancellation methods.
- **Consequence**: AG enforcement. Violations may also trigger Virginia Consumer Protection Act remedies (actual damages, attorney's fees, treble damages for willful violations).

### Common Requirements Across All Jurisdictions

### Free Trial Conversion Rules

- **What**: Free trials that convert to paid subscriptions are subject to heightened disclosure requirements under ROSCA, the FTC Click-to-Cancel Rule, and most state auto-renewal laws.
- **Threshold**: The trial-to-paid conversion must be disclosed before the consumer provides billing information. The disclosure must include: the post-trial price, the date or event that triggers the first charge, and clear instructions for canceling before conversion. California ARL requires that free trial disclosures appear in visual proximity to the sign-up mechanism.
- **Consequence**: Failure to properly disclose free trial conversion terms may result in the auto-renewal being void (California), FTC enforcement, or state UDAP liability. Charges collected without proper disclosure may need to be refunded.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection — FTC**: ROSCA is enforced by the FTC under its Section 5 authority. FTC enforcement actions frequently target auto-renewal practices as deceptive or unfair. The FTC Click-to-Cancel Rule provides the most comprehensive federal framework.
- **Consumer Protection — State Laws**: State auto-renewal laws supplement general state UDAP statutes. Violations may trigger both auto-renewal-specific consequences (voiding the provision) and general consumer protection remedies (treble damages, attorney's fees).
- **Consumer Protection — Dark Patterns**: Dark pattern prohibitions in cancellation flows directly intersect with auto-renewal compliance. See `dark-patterns.md` for restrictions on obstruction, nagging, and forced action in cancellation user flows.
- **Data Privacy**: Auto-renewal disclosures and cancellation mechanisms must be accessible and functional, which may interact with cookie consent, account deletion rights (e.g., CCPA's right to delete), and data retention obligations.
- **IP and Technology**: SaaS and digital subscription agreements must comply with auto-renewal laws even in B2B contexts when the customer may qualify as a consumer. Subscription billing logic must be designed to facilitate compliance with disclosure and cancellation requirements.
- **Litigation**: Auto-renewal violations are a frequent basis for consumer class actions, particularly in California under the ARL's void-and-unenforceable remedy. The "unconditional gift" consequence makes these cases attractive for plaintiff's counsel.

## Can Spam

# CAN-SPAM Act

## Applicability

This sub-file applies whenever a matter involves commercial email marketing, transactional or relationship email communications, email service provider agreements, bulk email compliance, opt-out/unsubscribe mechanisms, email header and routing information accuracy, or any agreement that allocates responsibility for email marketing compliance between parties (e.g., affiliate marketing agreements, co-registration arrangements, email list rental/purchase agreements).

## Key Requirements

### Statutory Framework (15 U.S.C. §§ 7701-7713, 16 C.F.R. Part 316)

- **What**: The CAN-SPAM Act (Controlling the Assault of Non-Solicited Pornography and Marketing Act of 2003) establishes requirements for commercial electronic mail messages, gives recipients the right to stop receiving emails, and imposes penalties for violations. It applies to any electronic mail message the primary purpose of which is the commercial advertisement or promotion of a commercial product or service.
- **Threshold**: A single commercial email is sufficient to trigger CAN-SPAM obligations. The Act applies to all commercial email sent to or from the United States, regardless of whether the recipient is a customer. No prior relationship is required for CAN-SPAM to apply — it is not an anti-spam "opt-in" law but rather a "right to opt-out" framework.
- **Consequence**: Civil penalties up to $50,120 per violation (per non-compliant email, as adjusted for inflation). Criminal penalties — including imprisonment up to 5 years — for aggravated violations involving fraud, harvesting, or dictionary attacks. Enforcement by the FTC, state AGs, and ISPs. No private right of action for individual consumers.

### Prohibition on False or Misleading Header Information

- **What**: It is unlawful to initiate transmission of a commercial email with header information (including the "From," "To," "Reply-To," and routing information) that is materially false or materially misleading.
- **Threshold**: The originating email address, domain name, and IP address must accurately identify the person who initiated the message. Header information is "materially misleading" if it would likely mislead a reasonable recipient about the origin of the message.
- **Consequence**: Violation is both a civil and criminal offense. Criminal penalties apply when false header information is used in connection with other fraudulent conduct.

### Prohibition on Deceptive Subject Lines

- **What**: It is unlawful to use a subject line that would be likely to mislead a recipient, acting reasonably under the circumstances, about a material fact regarding the contents or subject matter of the message.
- **Threshold**: The subject line must accurately reflect the content of the message. "Act now!" or "Urgent" subject lines are not per se violations, but subject lines that affirmatively misrepresent the message content (e.g., "Re: Your Order" when there is no order) violate the Act.
- **Consequence**: Civil penalties up to $50,120 per violation.

### Identification as Advertisement

- **What**: Commercial email must include a "clear and conspicuous identification that the message is an advertisement or solicitation."
- **Threshold**: The disclosure must be clear and conspicuous but the Act does not mandate specific wording. The FTC has stated that the identification can appear anywhere in the message. If the recipient has given affirmative consent to receive the message, the identification requirement does not apply.
- **Consequence**: Civil penalties up to $50,120 per violation.

### Opt-Out Mechanism Requirements

- **What**: Every commercial email must include a clear and conspicuous mechanism for the recipient to opt out of future commercial email from the sender. The opt-out must be functional for at least 30 days after the message is sent.
- **Threshold**: The opt-out mechanism must be available via return email or another internet-based mechanism that is clearly and conspicuously displayed. The sender must process opt-out requests within 10 business days. After opt-out, the sender may not send or cause to be sent any commercial email to that address. The sender may not require the recipient to pay a fee, provide information beyond an email address, or take any steps other than replying to the email or visiting a single internet page to opt out. Opt-out requests cannot be conditioned on the recipient visiting more than a single page (no multi-page opt-out flows).
- **Consequence**: Failure to provide a functioning opt-out mechanism or failure to honor opt-out requests within 10 business days subjects the sender to civil penalties up to $50,120 per subsequent non-compliant email.

### Physical Postal Address

- **What**: Every commercial email must include a valid physical postal address of the sender.
- **Threshold**: The address must be a valid current street address, a PO Box registered with the United States Postal Service, or a private mailbox registered with a commercial mail receiving agency under USPS regulations. The address must be the sender's actual address or one where the sender receives mail.
- **Consequence**: Civil penalties up to $50,120 per violation.

### Transactional or Relationship Message Exemption

- **What**: Messages with a "transactional or relationship" primary purpose are exempt from most CAN-SPAM requirements (identification as ad, opt-out mechanism, physical address) but must still comply with the prohibition on false/misleading headers.
- **Threshold**: Transactional messages include: (1) facilitating or confirming a transaction the recipient agreed to, (2) providing warranty, recall, safety, or security information, (3) subscription or membership notifications (not marketing), (4) account balance or other account status information, and (5) delivery of goods or services under a transaction, including product updates. The "primary purpose" test determines classification — if the primary purpose is commercial promotion, it is a commercial message regardless of whether it also contains transactional content.
- **Consequence**: Misclassifying a commercial email as transactional to avoid CAN-SPAM requirements is itself a violation.

### Aggravated Violations — Criminal Penalties

- **What**: The CAN-SPAM Act imposes criminal penalties for specific aggravated conduct in connection with commercial email.
- **Threshold**: Criminal penalties apply for: (1) accessing a computer without authorization to send commercial email (up to 5 years imprisonment), (2) using a computer for open relay or open proxy without authorization (up to 3 years), (3) falsifying header information in multiple messages (up to 3 years), (4) registering for multiple email accounts or domain names using false information to send commercial email (up to 5 years), and (5) falsely representing yourself as the registrant of 5 or more IP addresses to send commercial email (up to 5 years).
- **Consequence**: Imprisonment of up to 5 years and/or fines for the above offenses. These criminal provisions are enforced by the DOJ.

### Sender Liability and "Procure" Standard

- **What**: Both the sender and the person who "initiates" the message are liable for CAN-SPAM violations. A person "initiates" a message if they procure the origination of the message or cause the origination of the message. The "procure" standard means that a company can be liable for commercial emails sent by affiliates, contractors, or marketing partners on its behalf.
- **Threshold**: If a company hires an email marketing vendor or has affiliates send emails promoting its products, the company may be jointly liable as the "sender" if the recipients reasonably conclude the email is from the company. Both the "sender" (the entity whose product is promoted) and the "initiator" (the entity that technically sent the message) are responsible for compliance.
- **Consequence**: Companies cannot avoid CAN-SPAM liability by outsourcing email campaigns to third parties. Vendor agreements must include compliance requirements and indemnification provisions.

### Preemption of State Laws

- **What**: CAN-SPAM expressly preempts state laws that regulate the use of electronic mail for commercial purposes, except to the extent that such state laws prohibit falsity or deception in commercial email.
- **Threshold**: State laws that impose opt-in requirements for commercial email or impose requirements beyond CAN-SPAM on commercial email content are preempted. However, state fraud-based claims (e.g., state UDAP claims based on deceptive email content) survive preemption. State laws regulating non-email electronic communications (e.g., text messages) are not preempted.
- **Consequence**: Companies do not need to comply with state-specific commercial email requirements that go beyond CAN-SPAM, but must still comply with state fraud and deception laws applicable to email content.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection — TCPA**: Text messages (SMS/MMS) are regulated by the TCPA, not CAN-SPAM. However, commercial messages sent to an email address that routes to a wireless device may trigger both CAN-SPAM and TCPA obligations under FCC wireless domain rules.
- **Consumer Protection — FTC/UDAP**: The FTC enforces CAN-SPAM and may also pursue deceptive email practices under Section 5 of the FTC Act. State UDAP claims based on deceptive email content survive CAN-SPAM preemption.
- **Data Privacy**: Email addresses are personal data under CCPA, GDPR, and other privacy frameworks. CAN-SPAM's opt-out requirements do not satisfy GDPR consent requirements — GDPR requires opt-in consent for marketing emails in most EU jurisdictions. The two frameworks must be harmonized when emailing internationally.
- **Consumer Protection — Dark Patterns**: Opt-out mechanisms that use dark patterns (multi-step unsubscribe flows, guilt-tripping language, confusing UI) may violate CAN-SPAM's requirement for a simple opt-out mechanism.

## Dark Patterns

# Dark Patterns

## Applicability

This sub-file applies whenever a matter involves user interface design requirements for consumer-facing digital products, cancellation flow design, consent mechanisms (especially for data collection, subscriptions, or recurring charges), allegations of deceptive design or manipulative interfaces, checkout flow compliance, cookie consent and privacy choice architecture, or regulatory compliance reviews for digital products, mobile apps, or e-commerce platforms. Also applies when evaluating design and product requirements in technology vendor agreements.

## Key Requirements

### FTC Enforcement Against Dark Patterns

- **What**: The FTC has declared dark patterns to be unfair or deceptive acts or practices under FTC Act Section 5. The FTC defines dark patterns as design practices that "trick or manipulate consumers into making choices they would not otherwise have made and that may cause harm." The FTC issued a staff report, "Bringing Dark Patterns to Light" (September 2022), outlining its enforcement framework.
- **Threshold**: The FTC evaluates dark patterns under both the deception standard (is the design likely to mislead a reasonable consumer?) and the unfairness standard (does the design cause substantial injury not reasonably avoidable by consumers?). The FTC has identified specific categories of concern: (1) misleading consumers into purchasing products or services, (2) tricking consumers into disclosing personal information, (3) misleading consumers into agreeing to charges or terms, and (4) making it difficult to cancel subscriptions or memberships.
- **Consequence**: FTC enforcement actions have resulted in consent orders, civil penalties (up to $50,120+ per violation), consumer refunds, and injunctive relief requiring redesign of interfaces. Notable enforcement actions include Fortnite/Epic Games ($520 million total settlement in 2022, partly for dark pattern charges targeting children), Age of Empires/ABCmouse ($10 million for deceptive cancellation practices), and Credit Karma ($3 million for misleading "pre-approved" credit claims).

### Dark Pattern Categories (FTC Framework)

#### Nagging

- **What**: Repeated prompts, interruptions, or requests that pressure users into taking an action (e.g., repeated pop-ups asking users to enable notifications or upgrade to a paid plan).
- **Threshold**: Nagging becomes a legal issue when it interferes with the consumer's ability to use the product as intended, when it obscures or delays the consumer's desired action, or when the frequency makes the product effectively unusable without capitulating.
- **Consequence**: May constitute unfairness (substantial injury through interference) or deception (if nagging obscures material information or choices).

#### Obstruction

- **What**: Making a desired action (typically cancellation, opt-out, or account deletion) significantly more difficult than the corresponding sign-up action. Includes requiring phone calls to cancel when sign-up was online, multi-step cancellation flows, "save" offers that obscure the cancellation option, and timed delays.
- **Threshold**: The FTC Click-to-Cancel Rule (2024) establishes a bright-line test: cancellation must be at least as easy as sign-up. If sign-up required no phone call, cancellation cannot require a phone call. If sign-up was completed in 2 clicks, cancellation should not require 10 steps. Mandatory "retention" or "save" offers during cancellation are restricted — the consumer must be able to bypass them easily.
- **Consequence**: Violations of the Click-to-Cancel Rule subject sellers to civil penalties. Obstruction in cancellation flows is one of the most actively enforced dark pattern categories.

#### Sneaking

- **What**: Adding items to a consumer's cart without consent, hiding charges until checkout, presenting pre-selected add-ons, or using "drip pricing" to reveal the full cost only at the final stage of a transaction.
- **Threshold**: The FTC and state laws require that material costs be disclosed before the consumer provides payment information. Drip pricing — where mandatory fees are revealed incrementally — is the subject of an FTC proposed rule (2023) that would prohibit hiding mandatory fees from advertised prices. Pre-checked add-ons that increase the total cost are considered deceptive if not clearly and conspicuously disclosed.
- **Consequence**: FTC enforcement for deceptive pricing. State AG enforcement under UDAP statutes. Private class actions for deceptive pricing practices.

#### Forced Action

- **What**: Requiring consumers to take an unrelated action to access desired functionality. Includes requiring account creation to make a one-time purchase, requiring consent to marketing as a condition of service, or requiring personal data disclosure beyond what is necessary for the transaction.
- **Threshold**: Forced action becomes legally problematic when the required action involves: (1) consent that cannot lawfully be a condition of the transaction (e.g., TCPA prohibits requiring consent-to-call as a condition of purchase), (2) data collection that violates privacy law minimization requirements, or (3) terms that are substantively unconscionable under state contract law.
- **Consequence**: May violate the TCPA (consent bundling), GDPR (freely given consent requirement), state privacy laws (data minimization), or state consumer protection statutes (unconscionability).

#### Social Proof Manipulation and Confirmshaming

- **What**: Using fake or misleading social proof (fabricated reviews, fake urgency timers, false scarcity indicators) or "confirmshaming" language (e.g., "No, I don't want to save money") to manipulate consumer choices.
- **Threshold**: Fabricated reviews and fake social proof are deceptive practices under FTC Act Section 5. The FTC's proposed rule on fake reviews (2023) would specifically prohibit the creation, purchase, or dissemination of fake or misleading reviews. False urgency (countdown timers that reset) and false scarcity ("only 2 left!" when inventory is abundant) are deceptive if they do not reflect reality. Confirmshaming is not per se illegal but contributes to overall deceptive context.
- **Consequence**: FTC enforcement for fake reviews and false urgency/scarcity. State UDAP claims for deceptive practices. Platform terms of service may also restrict these practices.

### EU Digital Services Act (DSA) — Dark Pattern Provisions

- **What**: The EU DSA (Regulation 2022/2065), effective February 2024, explicitly prohibits online platforms from using dark patterns in their interfaces. Article 25 states that providers of online platforms "shall not design, organise or operate their online interfaces in a way that deceives or manipulates the recipients of their service or in a way that otherwise materially distorts or impairs the ability of the recipients of their service to make free and informed decisions."
- **Threshold**: The DSA prohibition applies to all "online platforms" (as defined under the DSA) serving EU users. It covers interface design that: (1) gives undue prominence to certain choices, (2) repeatedly requests users to make a choice where they have already made one, (3) makes the procedure for cancellation more difficult than sign-up, or (4) makes certain choices more difficult or time-consuming than others through deceptive design. Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs — those with 45M+ monthly EU active users) face additional transparency and compliance obligations.
- **Consequence**: Fines of up to 6% of global annual turnover for DSA violations. National Digital Services Coordinators enforce the DSA in each member state. The European Commission has direct enforcement authority over VLOPs.

### California Dark Pattern Provisions (CCPA/CPRA)

- **What**: California regulations under the CPRA define "dark patterns" and provide that consent obtained through dark patterns does not constitute valid consent. Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.140(l): "Agreement obtained through use of dark patterns does not constitute consent."
- **Threshold**: The CPRA regulations define a "dark pattern" as "a user interface designed or manipulated with the substantial effect of subverting or impairing user autonomy, decision-making, or choice." If a company uses dark patterns to obtain consumer "consent" for data practices, that consent is not legally valid — the data practices are treated as non-consensual.
- **Consequence**: Data practices based on consent obtained through dark patterns are treated as violations of the CCPA/CPRA, subject to enforcement by the California AG and California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA). Penalties of $2,500 per violation or $7,500 per intentional violation. Private right of action for data breach claims.

### Colorado Privacy Act — Dark Pattern Definition

- **What**: The Colorado Privacy Act (CPA) similarly defines dark patterns and provides that consent obtained through dark patterns is not valid consent.
- **Threshold**: The CPA defines "dark pattern" as "a user interface designed or manipulated with the substantial effect of subverting or impairing user autonomy, decisionmaking, or choice." Consent for data processing obtained through dark patterns is not valid under the CPA.
- **Consequence**: AG enforcement with civil penalties up to $20,000 per violation.

### Enforcement Trends

- **What**: Dark pattern enforcement is intensifying across all levels — federal (FTC), state (AGs and privacy regulators), and international (EU DSA). Enforcement actions are increasingly targeting specific design choices rather than just overall deceptive marketing.
- **Threshold**: Recent enforcement trends focus on: (1) cancellation flow obstruction (most frequently targeted), (2) consent mechanism design (pre-checked boxes, confusing toggle defaults), (3) pricing transparency (drip pricing, hidden fees), (4) children's interfaces (heightened scrutiny under COPPA), and (5) privacy choice architecture (making "accept all cookies" prominent while burying "reject all").
- **Consequence**: Regulators are increasingly requiring prospective design compliance — not just monetary penalties but court-ordered redesign of interfaces and pre-approval of UI changes.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection — Auto-Renewal**: Dark pattern prohibitions in cancellation flows directly implement ROSCA and the FTC Click-to-Cancel Rule. Obstruction patterns in cancellation are the most actively enforced category. See `auto-renewal.md`.
- **Consumer Protection — FTC/UDAP**: Dark patterns are enforced under the existing FTC Act Section 5 deception and unfairness framework. No new statute is required — the FTC treats dark patterns as applications of existing law. See `ftc-udap.md`.
- **Consumer Protection — State Laws**: State UDAP statutes provide private rights of action for dark pattern practices that the FTC Act does not. California, Massachusetts, and other states with broad unfairness coverage are particularly active. See `state-consumer-laws.md`.
- **Data Privacy**: Dark pattern definitions in CCPA/CPRA and Colorado Privacy Act specifically invalidate consent obtained through manipulative design. Cookie consent interfaces are a primary enforcement focus. Dark pattern restrictions intersect with privacy-by-design requirements under GDPR.
- **Consumer Protection — CAN-SPAM**: Opt-out mechanisms that use dark patterns (multi-step unsubscribe, guilt-tripping language) may violate CAN-SPAM's simple opt-out requirement.

## Endorsements Testimonials

# Endorsements and Testimonials

## Applicability

This sub-file applies whenever a matter involves influencer marketing agreements, affiliate marketing programs, sponsored content, product reviews (paid or incentivized), testimonials in advertising, celebrity endorsements, employee advocacy or ambassador programs, material connection disclosures, user-generated content used in marketing, review generation campaigns, or any agreement that governs how third parties represent a company's products or services to consumers.

## Key Requirements

### FTC Endorsement Guides (16 C.F.R. Part 255)

- **What**: The FTC Endorsement Guides provide guidance on the application of FTC Act Section 5 to endorsements and testimonials in advertising. The Guides were significantly updated in June 2023 to address modern marketing practices including social media, influencer marketing, and online reviews. Endorsements that are deceptive — because they misrepresent the endorser's experience, fail to disclose material connections, or make unsubstantiated claims — violate Section 5.
- **Threshold**: An "endorsement" is any advertising message that consumers are likely to believe reflects the opinions, beliefs, findings, or experiences of a party other than the sponsoring advertiser. This includes social media posts, online reviews, blog posts, podcast mentions, video content, and any other communication where a third party appears to be expressing their own views about a product or service.
- **Consequence**: Violations are enforced under FTC Act Section 5 as deceptive practices. Civil penalties can reach $50,120+ per violation. Both the endorser and the advertiser may be liable. The 2023 update makes clear that individual social media influencers can face direct FTC enforcement.

### Material Connection Disclosure

- **What**: If there is a "material connection" between an endorser and the advertiser — any connection that might affect the weight or credibility a consumer gives to the endorsement — that connection must be "clearly and conspicuously" disclosed.
- **Threshold**: Material connections include: (1) monetary payment (including commissions, affiliate fees, revenue sharing), (2) free or discounted products, (3) employment relationships, (4) family or personal relationships, (5) business ownership or financial interests, (6) contest or sweepstakes entries, (7) any other benefit the endorser receives. Disclosure must be: (a) in clear and unambiguous language (e.g., "#ad," "Paid partnership," "Company X gave me this product"), (b) placed where consumers will notice it before engaging with the endorsement (not buried at the end of a post or in hashtag strings), (c) in the same medium as the endorsement (visual disclosure for video, audio disclosure for podcasts), and (d) unavoidable — not requiring the consumer to click "more" or scroll.
- **Consequence**: Failure to disclose material connections is a deceptive practice. The FTC has brought enforcement actions against both companies and individual influencers for inadequate disclosures.

### Honest Opinions and Genuine Experience

- **What**: Endorsements must reflect the honest opinions, findings, beliefs, or experiences of the endorser. An endorser may not make claims about a product that the endorser does not actually believe or has not actually experienced.
- **Threshold**: The endorser must have actually used the product or service in the manner described. The endorser's statements must be truthful and not misleading. If the endorser's experience is atypical, the advertisement must clearly disclose what results consumers can generally expect, or the endorser's experience must be accompanied by a disclaimer that results may vary — and that disclaimer must be clear and conspicuous, not fine print.
- **Consequence**: Deceptive endorsements are FTC Act violations. The advertiser is liable for deceptive endorsements if the advertiser knew or should have known the endorsement was deceptive — creating a duty to monitor endorser claims.

### Typical Results and "Results Not Typical" Disclaimers

- **What**: The 2023 Endorsement Guides update eliminated the prior safe harbor for "results not typical" disclaimers. Previously, an advertiser could use an atypical consumer testimonial with a "results not typical" disclosure. Now, if an endorsement conveys a performance claim, the advertiser must have substantiation for the claim as interpreted by the audience.
- **Threshold**: If a testimonial communicates a specific result (e.g., "I lost 30 pounds"), the advertiser must either (1) have substantiation that the results shown are typical, or (2) clearly and conspicuously disclose what results consumers can generally expect. A vague "results may vary" disclaimer is no longer sufficient. The advertiser must provide specific information about typical outcomes.
- **Consequence**: Failure to substantiate the results communicated through testimonials or failure to adequately disclose typical outcomes is a deceptive practice.

### Expert Endorsements

- **What**: When an advertisement represents that an endorser has expertise in a relevant area, the endorsement must be supported by an actual exercise of that expertise. The expert must have actually evaluated the product using the expert's professional judgment.
- **Threshold**: The expert must have qualifications sufficient to support the implied level of expertise. The expert must have performed an evaluation that an expert in the field would conduct. The advertiser must have a reasonable basis for claims the expert makes. An expert endorsement of a product that the expert has not actually tested or evaluated is deceptive.
- **Consequence**: Deceptive expert endorsements subject both the advertiser and the expert to FTC enforcement.

### Consumer Endorsements and Testimonials

- **What**: Consumer endorsements (testimonials from real consumers about their experience with a product) must be truthful and reflect genuine experiences. Fabricated or incentivized reviews that do not disclose the incentive are deceptive.
- **Threshold**: If a consumer was incentivized (free product, payment, discount, contest entry) to provide a review or testimonial, that incentive must be disclosed. Advertisements may not use fake consumer reviews or testimonials. The FTC's 2023 proposed rule on fake reviews would specifically prohibit: (1) buying, selling, or procuring fake reviews, (2) suppressing negative reviews, (3) using insider reviews without disclosure, and (4) creating or controlling review websites that appear independent.
- **Consequence**: The FTC has imposed penalties exceeding $4 million for fake review practices (Fashion Nova, $4.2 million in 2022 for suppressing negative reviews). Companies face both FTC enforcement and state AG actions.

### Influencer-Specific Disclosure Requirements

- **What**: Social media influencers who have material connections with brands must disclose those connections in each post or piece of content that mentions, promotes, or features the brand's products or services.
- **Threshold**: (1) The disclosure must use unambiguous language — "#ad" or "Paid partnership with [Brand]" are acceptable; "#sp," "#ambassador," or "#collab" alone are not sufficient. (2) The disclosure must appear at the beginning of the post, not after a "more" cut or buried in a long list of hashtags. (3) For video content (YouTube, TikTok, Instagram Reels), the disclosure must be both visual and verbal and must appear at the beginning of the video, not just in the description. (4) For stories or ephemeral content, the disclosure must appear in each individual story segment. (5) Platform-specific disclosure tools (Instagram "Paid partnership" tag, YouTube "Includes paid promotion") may supplement but should not replace text disclosures if the platform tool is not sufficiently conspicuous.
- **Consequence**: Both the influencer and the brand can face FTC enforcement. The brand has a duty to provide clear disclosure guidance to influencers and to monitor compliance. Failure to train and monitor influencers can result in brand liability even if the brand did not direct the specific non-compliant post.

### Brand Responsibility and Monitoring Obligations

- **What**: Advertisers are responsible for ensuring that endorsers comply with FTC requirements. This includes providing clear guidance, monitoring compliance, and taking corrective action when violations are identified.
- **Threshold**: Advertisers must: (1) clearly communicate disclosure requirements to endorsers in writing, (2) provide examples of compliant and non-compliant disclosures, (3) periodically monitor endorser posts and content for compliance, (4) take action when non-compliant content is identified (requesting correction or removal), and (5) terminate relationships with endorsers who repeatedly fail to comply. A written influencer/endorser agreement that includes disclosure requirements is necessary but not sufficient — the advertiser must also monitor compliance.
- **Consequence**: Advertisers who fail to adequately train and monitor endorsers may be held liable for the endorser's non-compliance. The FTC has brought enforcement actions against brands for failing to monitor influencer disclosures (Lord & Taylor, 2016; Sunday Riley, 2019).

### Reviews and Ratings

- **What**: The FTC proposed a rule in 2023 specifically addressing fake reviews and testimonials, which would complement the existing Endorsement Guides by creating specific prohibitions with civil penalty authority.
- **Threshold**: Under the proposed rule (and current enforcement practice), businesses may not: (1) create or procure fake consumer reviews (positive or negative), (2) pay for reviews without requiring disclosure of the payment, (3) suppress or hide negative reviews to manipulate overall ratings, (4) use company insiders (employees, officers, agents) to write reviews without disclosing the relationship, (5) create or control fake review websites, or (6) misrepresent that reviews represent independent consumer opinion when they are curated or influenced by the company.
- **Consequence**: The proposed rule would authorize civil penalties of $50,120+ per violation. Under current law, these practices are enforceable as deceptive acts under Section 5.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection — FTC/UDAP**: Endorsement and testimonial requirements are enforced under FTC Act Section 5. The Endorsement Guides are interpretive guidelines, not rules — but the FTC enforces them through Section 5 enforcement actions. See `ftc-udap.md`.
- **Consumer Protection — State Laws**: State UDAP statutes provide private rights of action for deceptive endorsement practices. Consumers deceived by undisclosed paid endorsements may bring state consumer protection claims with treble damages. See `state-consumer-laws.md`.
- **Consumer Protection — Dark Patterns**: Social proof manipulation (fake reviews, fabricated testimonials, deceptive ratings) is a category of dark pattern. See `dark-patterns.md`.
- **IP and Technology**: Influencer agreements must address intellectual property rights (content ownership, license grants, usage rights). Platform terms of service may impose additional disclosure requirements. App store review policies supplement FTC requirements.
- **Data Privacy**: Influencer and endorser agreements may involve personal data sharing, requiring privacy compliance. Review platforms collecting consumer data must comply with applicable privacy laws.

## Sources

- [16 C.F.R. Part 255 — FTC Endorsement Guides (2023 Update)](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-16/chapter-I/subchapter-B/part-255) — FTC Guides concerning use of endorsements and testimonials in advertising.
- [FTC Endorsement Guides: What People Are Asking](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/ftc-endorsement-guides-what-people-are-asking) — FTC FAQ and practical guidance on endorsement compliance.
- [FTC Staff Report: Bringing Dark Patterns to Light (2022)](https://www.ftc.gov/reports/bringing-dark-patterns-light) — Includes discussion of social proof manipulation and fake reviews.
- [FTC Proposed Rule on Fake Reviews and Testimonials (2023)](https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/federal-register-notices/trade-regulation-rule-use-consumer-reviews-testimonials) — Proposed rule creating specific prohibitions on fake and deceptive reviews.

## Ftc Udap

# FTC Act Section 5 and UDAP

## Applicability

This sub-file applies whenever a matter involves advertising claims, marketing practices, consumer-facing product or service representations, endorsements, testimonials, FTC enforcement risk, or any allegation of unfair or deceptive commercial conduct. Also applies to privacy and data security representations made to consumers, as the FTC treats misleading privacy policies as deceptive acts.

## Key Requirements

### Deception Standard (FTC Policy Statement on Deception, 1983)

- **What**: A representation, omission, or practice is deceptive if (1) there is a representation, omission, or practice likely to mislead the consumer, (2) the consumer's interpretation is reasonable under the circumstances, and (3) the misleading representation, omission, or practice is material.
- **Threshold**: Materiality is presumed for express claims, claims involving health and safety, and claims where the seller knew or should have known the representation would be important to consumers. Implied claims must be evaluated by the overall net impression of the advertisement.
- **Consequence**: FTC may issue cease-and-desist orders, seek consumer redress, require corrective advertising, or impose civil penalties for violations of consent orders.

### Unfairness Standard (15 U.S.C. § 45(n))

- **What**: An act or practice is unfair if (1) it causes or is likely to cause substantial injury to consumers, (2) the injury is not reasonably avoidable by consumers themselves, and (3) the injury is not outweighed by countervailing benefits to consumers or to competition.
- **Threshold**: Substantial injury typically means monetary harm. Emotional harm alone is generally insufficient unless accompanied by other tangible injury. Trivial or speculative harm does not qualify.
- **Consequence**: Same enforcement powers as deception — administrative complaints, consent orders, injunctions, and monetary remedies.

### Advertising Substantiation Doctrine

- **What**: Advertisers must possess a reasonable basis for all objective advertising claims before disseminating them. The level of substantiation required depends on the type of claim.
- **Threshold**: Health and safety claims require "competent and reliable scientific evidence," which the FTC defines as tests, analyses, research, or studies conducted and evaluated by qualified persons using generally accepted procedures. General product performance claims require a reasonable basis, which may include consumer surveys, expert testing, or other reliable evidence appropriate to the claim.
- **Consequence**: Failure to have adequate substantiation at the time a claim is made constitutes a deceptive act. The FTC can require substantiation be produced and can challenge claims where substantiation is inadequate.

### FTC Enforcement Powers

- **What**: The FTC enforces Section 5 through administrative proceedings, federal court actions, and rulemaking.
- **Threshold**: Civil penalties for violations of FTC rules or consent orders are adjusted annually for inflation — currently exceeding $50,120 per violation (as adjusted post-2021 under the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act). Each day of a continuing violation or each affected consumer may constitute a separate violation.
- **Consequence**: Remedies include cease-and-desist orders, civil penalties, consumer redress (refunds), disgorgement of ill-gotten gains, corrective advertising, and compliance monitoring. After the Supreme Court's decision in AMG Capital Management v. FTC (2021), the FTC's ability to seek monetary relief under Section 13(b) is limited; monetary remedies now primarily come through Section 19 (following administrative proceedings) or through Penalty Offense Authority under Section 5(m)(1)(B).

### Holder Rule (16 C.F.R. Part 433)

- **What**: The FTC Holder Rule preserves consumers' claims and defenses when consumer credit contracts are assigned to third-party holders. Sellers must include a specific notice in consumer credit contracts.
- **Threshold**: Applies to all purchase money loans and credit sales involving consumer goods or services. The required notice must appear in 10-point bold type in the consumer credit contract.
- **Consequence**: Failure to include the Holder Rule notice is an unfair act or practice. The notice preserves all of the consumer's claims and defenses against subsequent holders of the credit contract, up to the amount paid under the contract.

### Health Claims Substantiation

- **What**: Health-related advertising claims — including claims about disease treatment, prevention, cure, or mitigation — require a higher level of substantiation than general product claims.
- **Threshold**: The FTC generally requires at least two adequate and well-controlled human clinical studies for disease-related claims. Structure/function claims (e.g., "supports immune health") require competent and reliable scientific evidence, though typically less rigorous than disease claims. Claims must not be misleading even if technically truthful.
- **Consequence**: The FTC has brought numerous enforcement actions against health claims, particularly for dietary supplements, homeopathic products, and health devices. Penalties can include full refunds to consumers and bans on future health claims without substantiation.

### Made in USA Claims (16 C.F.R. Part 323)

- **What**: Products advertised as "Made in USA" must be "all or virtually all" made domestically — meaning all significant parts, processing, and labor are of U.S. origin. The FTC Made in USA Rule codifies this standard.
- **Threshold**: "All or virtually all" means that all or virtually all significant inputs are domestic and the product was last substantially transformed in the U.S. Qualified claims (e.g., "Assembled in USA from imported parts") must be truthful and non-deceptive. The FTC Rule applies to product labels and implies penalties for violations.
- **Consequence**: Civil penalties up to $50,120 per violation for violations of the Made in USA Rule. The FTC can also pursue enforcement under Section 5 for misleading origin claims not covered by the specific Rule.

### Native Advertising and Sponsored Content

- **What**: Advertising that mimics editorial content must be clearly identifiable as advertising to avoid being deceptive. The FTC's Enforcement Policy Statement on Deceptively Formatted Advertising (2015) and the Endorsement Guides govern these practices.
- **Threshold**: Disclosure must be clear and conspicuous, using language the consumer will understand, placed where the consumer will see it before engaging with the content. "Sponsored" or "ad" labels must be prominent and unambiguous.
- **Consequence**: The FTC has brought enforcement actions against both advertisers and publishers for deceptive native advertising. Failure to disclose the commercial nature of content is treated as deceptive.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Data Privacy**: The FTC actively enforces data privacy and security practices under Section 5 unfairness and deception authority. Misleading privacy policies are deceptive acts. Inadequate data security can be an unfair practice. This overlaps with CCPA, state privacy laws, and sector-specific federal privacy laws.
- **Consumer Protection — State**: State UDAP statutes often provide broader protections, private rights of action, and treble damages not available under the FTC Act (which has no private right of action). Federal FTC enforcement and state AG enforcement are complementary, not exclusive.
- **Consumer Protection — Auto-Renewal**: ROSCA and FTC negative option rules directly govern subscription and auto-renewal practices under FTC Section 5 authority.
- **Consumer Protection — Endorsements/Testimonials**: The FTC Endorsement Guides (16 C.F.R. Part 255) are enforced through Section 5 deception authority. See `endorsements-testimonials.md` for detailed guidance.
- **Consumer Protection — Dark Patterns**: The FTC increasingly targets manipulative user interface design as unfair or deceptive practices. See `dark-patterns.md`.
- **IP and Technology**: FTC Endorsement Guides apply to technology reviews, app store descriptions, SaaS marketing, and software product claims.
- **Securities**: Advertising and marketing claims about investment products or financial performance may trigger both FTC and SEC scrutiny.
- **Financial Services**: The FTC enforces consumer financial protection through the Telemarketing Sales Rule, Holder Rule, and other trade regulation rules.

## Magnuson Moss

# Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act

## Applicability

This sub-file applies whenever a matter involves consumer product warranties (written or implied), warranty disclaimers in consumer sales, extended warranty or service contract agreements, product return and refund policies for warranted goods, consumer product labeling, or any transaction where a written warranty is offered on a consumer product costing more than a de minimis amount. Also applies when evaluating warranty-related litigation risk, including class actions under the Act's private enforcement provisions.

## Key Requirements

### Statutory Framework (15 U.S.C. §§ 2301-2312)

- **What**: The Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act is the federal law governing consumer product warranties. It does not require any person to provide a written warranty, but if one is given, it must comply with the Act's disclosure, designation, and substantive requirements. The Act also restricts the ability to disclaim implied warranties when a written warranty is provided.
- **Threshold**: Applies to "consumer products" — tangible personal property normally used for personal, family, or household purposes. Applies to written warranties on products costing more than $15 (for disclosure requirements) and more than $5 (for designation as "full" or "limited"). The Act does not apply to services alone, real property, or commercial/industrial products not used for personal purposes.
- **Consequence**: The FTC enforces the Act's requirements. Consumers have a private right of action in state or federal court. Attorney's fees are available to prevailing consumers. Class actions are authorized with specific requirements.

### Written Warranty Disclosure Requirements (16 C.F.R. Part 701)

- **What**: Any written warranty on a consumer product costing more than $15 must clearly and conspicuously disclose specific information in a single document, in simple and readily understood language.
- **Threshold**: Required disclosures include: (1) the identity of the warrantor(s), (2) the identity of the party to whom the warranty is extended (e.g., original purchaser only or subsequent owners), (3) the products or parts covered, (4) what the warrantor will do in the event of a defect, malfunction, or failure to conform, including items or services the warrantor will pay for or provide, and the duration of such coverage, (5) the date the warranty term begins if different from the purchase date, (6) step-by-step procedures for obtaining warranty performance, (7) information about any informal dispute resolution mechanism, (8) any limitations on the duration of implied warranties (which may not be less than the duration of the written warranty), and (9) any exclusions or limitations on relief such as incidental or consequential damages, with a statement about whether such exclusions may be prohibited in the consumer's state.
- **Consequence**: Failure to make required disclosures is a violation enforceable by the FTC. Non-compliant warranty documents may also support consumer claims for breach of warranty under state law.

### Full vs. Limited Warranty Designation (15 U.S.C. § 2303)

- **What**: Written warranties on consumer products costing more than $5 must be designated as either "Full" or "Limited."
- **Threshold**: A "Full Warranty" must meet the Federal Minimum Standards for Warranty (15 U.S.C. § 2304): (1) the warrantor must remedy defects within a reasonable time and without charge, (2) no unreasonable duty may be imposed on the consumer as a precondition for receiving warranty service (e.g., requiring return of a heavy appliance), (3) the warranty may not be limited to the original purchaser, (4) if the warrantor cannot repair the product after a reasonable number of attempts, the consumer may elect either a replacement or a full refund ("lemon" provision), and (5) the warrantor may not impose any limitation on the duration of implied warranties. Any warranty that does not meet all of these standards must be designated "Limited."
- **Consequence**: Misdesignating a warranty as "Full" when it does not meet the Federal Minimum Standards is a violation of the Act. The designation affects the consumer's remedies and the warrantor's obligations.

### Implied Warranty Protections (15 U.S.C. § 2308)

- **What**: The Act prohibits the disclaimer of implied warranties (including the implied warranty of merchantability and the implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose) whenever a written warranty is provided or a service contract is sold.
- **Threshold**: If a supplier provides ANY written warranty on a consumer product, implied warranties on that product cannot be disclaimed. Implied warranties may be limited in duration to the duration of the written warranty, but only if the limitation is conscionable, set forth in clear and unmistakable language, and prominently displayed on the face of the warranty. The minimum implied warranty duration that can be set is the duration of the written warranty.
- **Consequence**: Any attempt to disclaim implied warranties when a written warranty is given is void and unenforceable. This is a critical constraint: offering even a minimal written warranty eliminates the ability to disclaim implied warranties, which could significantly expand the warrantor's liability exposure under the UCC.

### Anti-Tying Provision (15 U.S.C. § 2302(c))

- **What**: A warrantor may not condition a written or implied warranty on the consumer's use of any article or service identified by brand, trade, or corporate name, unless the article or service is provided free of charge or the FTC has specifically granted a waiver.
- **Threshold**: The warrantor cannot require consumers to use only branded replacement parts, authorized service providers, or specific maintenance products as a condition of warranty coverage — unless those items are provided at no cost. The warrantor may recommend branded products but cannot require them.
- **Consequence**: Violation renders the tying requirement unenforceable. The FTC can pursue enforcement. This provision is particularly relevant for technology products, automobiles, and equipment where manufacturers may try to restrict aftermarket parts or independent repair.

### Service Contracts (15 U.S.C. § 2306)

- **What**: Service contracts (extended warranties) are subject to specific Magnuson-Moss requirements. A service contract is defined as a contract in writing to perform services relating to the maintenance or repair of a consumer product over a fixed period of time.
- **Threshold**: Service contracts must be clearly identified as such and must state conspicuously that the service contract is not a warranty. If a supplier sells a service contract, the supplier may not disclaim implied warranties on the covered product. Service contracts are not subject to the Full/Limited designation requirement.
- **Consequence**: Selling a service contract triggers the same implied warranty protection as providing a written warranty — implied warranties cannot be disclaimed.

### Private Right of Action and Class Actions (15 U.S.C. § 2310)

- **What**: Consumers may bring individual or class actions in state or federal court for breach of written warranty, breach of implied warranty, or violation of the Act's obligations.
- **Threshold**: Federal court jurisdiction requires: (1) the amount in controversy of each individual claim must exceed $25 (effectively any warranty claim), (2) for class actions, there must be at least 100 named plaintiffs, the amount in controversy must exceed $50,000 (exclusive of interest and costs), and the matter must meet standard class action requirements. State court actions have no minimum thresholds under the Act.
- **Consequence**: Courts may award damages, equitable relief, and attorney's fees based on actual time expended. Attorney's fee shifting is a significant incentive for consumer warranty litigation. Informal dispute resolution may be required before filing suit if the warrantor has established an FTC-complying mechanism.

### Informal Dispute Settlement Mechanisms (16 C.F.R. Part 703)

- **What**: Warrantors may establish informal dispute settlement mechanisms (arbitration, mediation) and require consumers to use them before filing suit, but only if the mechanisms comply with FTC minimum requirements.
- **Threshold**: The mechanism must be adequately funded and staffed, must not charge the consumer, must render decisions within 40 days of receipt of the dispute, must be audited annually, and must comply with procedural requirements including the right to present evidence and receive a written decision with reasons. The mechanism's decision is not binding on the consumer — the consumer retains the right to file suit if unsatisfied.
- **Consequence**: If the warrantor requires use of an informal dispute settlement mechanism, the consumer must use it before filing suit. But if the mechanism does not comply with FTC Part 703 requirements, the warrantor cannot enforce the exhaustion requirement.

### Interaction with UCC Warranty Provisions

- **What**: The Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act supplements but does not replace state UCC warranty law. UCC Article 2 governs warranty creation, scope, and remedies in sales of goods. Magnuson-Moss adds federal requirements on top of UCC rules.
- **Threshold**: Under UCC § 2-316, implied warranties may generally be disclaimed with conspicuous language (e.g., "as is" or "with all faults"). However, Magnuson-Moss overrides UCC § 2-316 whenever a written warranty is provided — making implied warranty disclaimers void. This creates a critical interaction: a seller must decide whether to offer any written warranty at all, knowing that doing so eliminates the ability to disclaim implied warranties.
- **Consequence**: The strategic decision of whether to offer a written warranty must account for the Magnuson-Moss restriction on implied warranty disclaimers. Some sellers deliberately avoid written warranties to preserve the ability to disclaim implied warranties under the UCC.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection — FTC/UDAP**: The FTC enforces Magnuson-Moss and may also challenge deceptive warranty practices under Section 5. Warranty representations in advertising that exceed actual warranty coverage are deceptive acts.
- **Consumer Protection — State Laws**: State consumer protection statutes (CLRA, GBL § 349, 93A, DTPA) frequently provide additional remedies for warranty breaches, including treble damages and fee-shifting not available under Magnuson-Moss alone.
- **IP and Technology**: The anti-tying provision intersects with right-to-repair issues and aftermarket parts disputes, particularly for technology products, IoT devices, and connected products.
- **Litigation**: Magnuson-Moss class actions have specific numerosity and amount-in-controversy requirements that differ from Rule 23. The 100-named-plaintiff requirement for federal court class actions is a significant procedural hurdle.

## State Consumer Laws

# State Consumer Protection Laws

## Applicability

This sub-file applies whenever a consumer-facing business operates in specific states, state AG enforcement is a concern, state-specific consumer rights are at issue, treble damages or fee-shifting exposure exists, multi-state consumer class action risk is present, or choice-of-law and forum selection clauses in consumer contracts need evaluation. Also load when assessing whether a contract provision may be unenforceable under state unconscionability doctrines.

## Key Requirements

### California — Unfair Competition Law (UCL, Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200)

- **What**: Prohibits any "unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent" business act or practice and any "unfair, deceptive, untrue, or misleading" advertising. The UCL has an extremely broad "unlawful" prong — any violation of any law can serve as the predicate for a UCL claim.
- **Threshold**: Standing requires "lost money or property" as a result of the unfair competition. No intent to deceive is required. The "unfair" prong under Cel-Tech (when competitors sue) requires the practice threaten an incipient antitrust violation or violate the spirit of an antitrust law; for consumers, courts apply a balancing test.
- **Consequence**: Remedies include restitution and injunctive relief, but no damages and no attorney's fees. A 4-year statute of limitations applies. Representative actions are available. Disgorgement of profits is not available after Kwikset v. Superior Court clarified standing requirements.

### California — Consumer Legal Remedies Act (CLRA, Civ. Code § 1750)

- **What**: Prohibits 27 specific deceptive practices in consumer transactions for goods or services, including misrepresenting the source, sponsorship, approval, characteristics, uses, benefits, or quantities of goods or services.
- **Threshold**: Applies to transactions intended to result primarily in the sale or lease of goods or services to a consumer. Requires a 30-day demand letter before filing suit for damages (Civ. Code § 1782). Injunctive relief can be sought without the demand letter.
- **Consequence**: Actual damages, punitive damages (for intentional violations), restitution, injunctive relief, and attorney's fees. Statutory minimum damages are not provided, but attorney's fee shifting is a powerful incentive for plaintiff's attorneys. Any waiver of CLRA rights is void and unenforceable.

### California — False Advertising Law (FAL, Bus. & Prof. Code § 17500)

- **What**: Prohibits untrue or misleading advertising, including any advertising that is "untrue or misleading, and which is known, or which by the exercise of reasonable care should be known, to be untrue or misleading."
- **Threshold**: Applies to any form of advertising, including online, print, broadcast, and social media. The "reasonable care" standard means negligence is sufficient — no intent required.
- **Consequence**: Injunctive relief and restitution. Civil penalties up to $2,500 per violation when brought by a prosecutor. No private right to damages, but available in representative actions.

### New York — General Business Law §§ 349 and 350

- **What**: GBL § 349 prohibits deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any business, trade, or commerce in New York. GBL § 350 specifically addresses false advertising. Both provide private rights of action.
- **Threshold**: § 349 requires (1) consumer-oriented conduct, (2) that is materially misleading, and (3) injury. Reliance is not required — the plaintiff need only show the act was "likely to mislead a reasonable consumer." § 350 requires actual reliance on the false advertising.
- **Consequence**: Actual damages or $50 statutory damages (whichever is greater) under § 349. Treble damages up to $1,000 for willful or knowing violations. Attorney's fees available. 3-year statute of limitations.

### Massachusetts — Chapter 93A

- **What**: Prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices" in trade or commerce. Uniquely applies to both consumer and business-to-business transactions (§ 11 covers B2B claims).
- **Threshold**: A 30-day demand letter is required before filing suit. The demand letter creates an opportunity for the defendant to make a reasonable settlement offer, which can limit subsequent liability. No reliance requirement — the standard is whether the practice is within "at least the penumbra of some common-law, statutory, or other established concept of unfairness."
- **Consequence**: Treble damages are mandatory for willful or knowing violations. Attorney's fees and costs awarded to prevailing plaintiffs. Up to 5x damages in egregious cases per some court interpretations. 4-year statute of limitations.

### Illinois — Consumer Fraud and Deceptive Business Practices Act (ICFA, 815 ILCS 505)

- **What**: Prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices, including the use of any deception, fraud, false pretense, false promise, or misrepresentation in the conduct of trade or commerce.
- **Threshold**: Private plaintiffs must show (1) a deceptive or unfair act or practice, (2) intent that the plaintiff rely on the deception, (3) the deception occurred in the course of trade or commerce, and (4) actual damages. The AG has broader standing and does not need to prove individual reliance.
- **Consequence**: Actual damages, punitive damages, attorney's fees and costs. Civil penalties up to $50,000 per violation when brought by the AG ($50,000 for acts targeting persons 65+ or disabled). Injunctive relief. 3-year statute of limitations.

### Texas — Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA, Bus. & Com. Code Ch. 17)

- **What**: Prohibits false, misleading, or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of trade or commerce. Enumerates a "laundry list" of 27+ specific prohibited practices. Also provides a cause of action for breach of warranty and unconscionable actions.
- **Threshold**: "Consumer" is defined as anyone who seeks or acquires goods or services by purchase or lease. Business entities can be consumers. A 60-day pre-suit notice is required. Damages must exceed $100 (the former $500 threshold was removed for certain claims).
- **Consequence**: Economic damages, plus treble damages for knowing violations. Attorney's fees to prevailing consumers. Mental anguish damages available for knowing violations. 2-year statute of limitations.

### Florida — Deceptive and Unfair Trade Practices Act (FDUTPA, Ch. 501)

- **What**: Prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices in trade or commerce. The statute is construed liberally in favor of consumers.
- **Threshold**: No individual reliance required. The standard is whether the practice was likely to deceive a consumer acting reasonably under the circumstances. No demand letter required before filing suit.
- **Consequence**: Actual damages, injunctive relief, and attorney's fees for prevailing party. No treble or punitive damages under FDUTPA itself (but other Florida statutes may provide them). Civil penalties up to $10,000 per violation in AG actions. 4-year statute of limitations.

### Preemption Considerations

- **What**: Certain federal statutes expressly preempt state consumer protection claims in specific areas. FCRA preempts state claims related to credit reporting furnisher conduct. TILA preempts some state disclosure requirements. CAN-SPAM preempts state email regulation (except fraud-based claims).
- **Threshold**: Preemption analysis is statute-specific. Express preemption provisions must be read narrowly. State claims based on general fraud or deception theories may survive preemption even when the underlying conduct touches a federally regulated area.
- **Consequence**: Improperly asserting preempted state claims wastes litigation resources. Failing to assert non-preempted state claims forfeits potentially significant remedies (treble damages, attorney's fees).

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection — FTC**: State UDAP statutes provide private enforcement and enhanced remedies that supplement federal FTC Act protections (which have no private right of action). Federal and state enforcement are complementary.
- **Consumer Protection — Auto-Renewal**: Many states have enacted specific auto-renewal laws separate from general UDAP statutes. Violations may trigger both auto-renewal-specific penalties and general UDAP remedies.
- **Consumer Protection — TCPA**: State mini-TCPA laws provide additional private remedies for telemarketing violations. Florida and Washington have aggressive state telephone solicitation statutes.
- **Data Privacy**: State privacy laws (CCPA, CPRA, state comprehensive privacy acts) and state UDAP statutes may both apply to the same data practices, with different enforcement mechanisms, remedies, and standing requirements.
- **Corporate**: State consumer protection compliance must be factored into corporate structuring, multi-state registration, and entity selection.
- **Litigation**: State UDAP claims are common in consumer class actions and often accompany breach of contract, tort, and warranty claims. Fee-shifting provisions make these claims attractive to plaintiff's counsel.

## Tcpa

# Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA)

## Applicability

This sub-file applies whenever a matter involves telemarketing calls, marketing text messages (SMS/MMS), automated dialing systems (ATDS), prerecorded or artificial voice messages, robocalls, do-not-call registry compliance, consent-to-contact clauses in contracts, lead generation that involves phone or text outreach, or any agreement allocating responsibility for TCPA compliance between parties (e.g., vendor agreements, lead buyer/seller contracts).

## Key Requirements

### Statutory Framework (47 U.S.C. § 227)

- **What**: The TCPA restricts the use of automated telephone equipment to make calls or send text messages. It prohibits (1) calls using an automatic telephone dialing system (ATDS) or prerecorded/artificial voice to wireless numbers without prior express consent, (2) prerecorded/artificial voice calls to residential landlines without prior express consent, and (3) unsolicited fax advertisements.
- **Threshold**: The statute applies to any call or text message. A single non-compliant call or text is actionable. The TCPA does not require a pattern of conduct or minimum number of contacts.
- **Consequence**: $500 per violation (per call or text). Trebled to $1,500 per violation for willful or knowing violations. Private right of action. No statutory cap on aggregate damages. Class actions are common and can result in exposure of hundreds of millions of dollars.

### ATDS Definition (Post-Facebook v. Duguid, 2021)

- **What**: The Supreme Court in Facebook, Inc. v. Duguid, 593 U.S. 395 (2021), narrowed the definition of an ATDS. An ATDS must have the capacity to (1) store or produce telephone numbers using a random or sequential number generator and (2) dial such numbers. Equipment that merely stores and dials telephone numbers — such as a typical smartphone or CRM with a click-to-dial feature — is not an ATDS.
- **Threshold**: The key question is whether the device uses a random or sequential number generator to either store or produce the numbers it calls. Systems that dial from pre-existing contact lists without random/sequential generation do not qualify as an ATDS under the narrowed federal definition.
- **Consequence**: The narrowed ATDS definition reduces federal TCPA liability for calls/texts made from curated contact lists, but does NOT eliminate liability for prerecorded voice messages or for violations of the do-not-call provisions, which do not require ATDS use.

### Prior Express Written Consent (Marketing Calls/Texts)

- **What**: Telemarketing calls or texts using an ATDS or prerecorded voice to wireless numbers require "prior express written consent" under FCC regulations (47 C.F.R. § 64.1200(a)(2)).
- **Threshold**: Prior express written consent must be (1) in writing (including electronic signatures under E-SIGN), (2) signed by the consumer, (3) include the telephone number to which calls may be placed, (4) include a clear disclosure that the consumer is authorizing telemarketing calls/texts using an ATDS or prerecorded voice, and (5) state that consent is not required as a condition of purchase. Consent obtained as a condition of sale is not valid consent. Consent is revocable at any time through any reasonable means.
- **Consequence**: Calls or texts without valid prior express written consent are actionable at $500-$1,500 per call/text. The burden of proving consent rests on the caller.

### Prior Express Consent (Informational Calls/Texts)

- **What**: Non-marketing calls or texts using an ATDS to wireless numbers (e.g., appointment reminders, account notifications, delivery alerts) require "prior express consent" — a lower standard than prior express written consent.
- **Threshold**: Prior express consent may be implied from the consumer providing their phone number in the context of the transaction. For example, providing a phone number when scheduling an appointment implies consent to receive appointment-related calls/texts. Consent must be specific to the type of communication — giving a number for shipping updates does not imply consent for marketing messages.
- **Consequence**: Calls or texts exceeding the scope of consent (e.g., marketing messages sent under informational consent) are actionable at $500-$1,500 per violation.

### Do-Not-Call Requirements

- **What**: The TCPA established the National Do-Not-Call Registry (administered by the FTC) and requires telemarketers to maintain internal do-not-call lists. Telemarketers may not call numbers on the National DNC Registry unless they have prior express written consent or an established business relationship.
- **Threshold**: An established business relationship (EBR) exemption lasts 18 months from the last transaction or 3 months from the last inquiry. Companies must honor internal do-not-call requests within 30 days. The National DNC Registry must be checked at least every 31 days.
- **Consequence**: Violations are actionable at $500-$1,500 per call. The FTC can impose civil penalties up to $50,120 per violation under the Telemarketing Sales Rule.

### Prerecorded Voice Messages (Robocalls)

- **What**: Prerecorded or artificial voice calls to residential landlines require prior express consent (with limited exemptions). Prerecorded or artificial voice calls to wireless numbers for marketing require prior express written consent.
- **Threshold**: Exemptions exist for calls made for emergency purposes, calls made with prior express consent of the called party, calls made by or on behalf of tax-exempt nonprofit organizations, calls delivering health care messages (subject to HIPAA compliance), and calls from package delivery services with relevant information.
- **Consequence**: $500-$1,500 per call. FCC enforcement actions can impose forfeiture penalties of up to $23,727 per call, with a cap of $118,632 for a single continuing violation.

### State Mini-TCPA Laws

#### Florida Telephone Solicitation Act (Fla. Stat. § 501.059)

- **What**: Florida enacted a significantly stricter telephone solicitation law (effective July 2021) that goes beyond the federal TCPA. It defines ATDS more broadly than the federal definition post-Duguid, restricts calls and texts to Florida numbers, and requires prior express written consent for all telephonic sales calls.
- **Threshold**: Written consent must include a "clear and conspicuous" disclosure, be signed and dated, and specifically authorize the caller. The law restricts calls to the hours of 8:00 a.m. to 8:00 p.m. local time. No more than 3 calls in a 24-hour period per person. Consent expires 18 months after given. "Telephonic sales call" is broadly defined.
- **Consequence**: $500 per violation, trebled to $1,500 for willful violations. Private right of action with attorney's fees.

#### Oklahoma Telephone Solicitation Act (24 Okla. Stat. § 1101 et seq.)

- **What**: Requires prior express written consent for automated calls and texts to Oklahoma numbers. Broadly defines automated systems.
- **Threshold**: Written consent must specifically authorize automated calls or texts and be obtained in compliance with state requirements.
- **Consequence**: $500-$1,500 per violation with private right of action.

#### Washington Automatic Dialing and Announcing Device Statute (RCW 80.36.400)

- **What**: Restricts use of automatic dialing and announcing devices. Requires the called party to consent before the message is delivered.
- **Threshold**: Live operator must introduce the call and obtain consent before playing a prerecorded message. Calls restricted to 8:00 a.m. to 9:00 p.m.
- **Consequence**: Knowing violations are a misdemeanor. Civil remedies available under Washington Consumer Protection Act.

### Consent Revocation

- **What**: The FCC has confirmed that consumers may revoke consent through any reasonable means, including orally, in writing, or by any standard method within a reasonable time. Callers cannot restrict revocation to a single method (e.g., "text STOP" only).
- **Threshold**: Any clearly expressed desire to stop receiving calls or texts constitutes revocation. The caller must honor revocation within a reasonable time, generally interpreted as within a few days. The caller cannot impose unreasonable barriers to revocation.
- **Consequence**: Calls or texts made after valid revocation are actionable at $500-$1,500 per violation.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection — State Laws**: State mini-TCPA laws (Florida, Oklahoma, Washington, and others) may impose stricter requirements than the federal TCPA, including broader ATDS definitions, shorter consent expiration periods, and additional restrictions. Always apply the strictest applicable standard.
- **Consumer Protection — CAN-SPAM**: Text messages that constitute commercial electronic messages may also trigger CAN-SPAM requirements, though the FCC has primary jurisdiction over wireless marketing messages under the TCPA.
- **Data Privacy**: Phone numbers are personal data under CCPA, GDPR, and other privacy frameworks. TCPA consent management intersects with privacy consent management and data minimization obligations. Consent records must be retained and producible.
- **Consumer Protection — Dark Patterns**: Consent obtained through dark patterns (pre-checked boxes, misleading disclosures) may not constitute valid prior express written consent under the TCPA.
- **Litigation**: TCPA class actions are among the most common consumer class actions filed in federal court. Exposure is driven by per-violation statutory damages multiplied across the class. Cases frequently settle for tens of millions of dollars.
