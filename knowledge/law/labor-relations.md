## Collective Bargaining

# Collective Bargaining

## Applicability

Applies to the process of negotiating collective bargaining agreements (CBAs) between employers and unions representing employee bargaining units. Covers mandatory and permissive subjects of bargaining, the duty to bargain in good faith, impasse procedures, successor employer obligations, and contract administration.

## Key Requirements

### Mandatory Subjects of Bargaining

- **Scope** / Employer and union must bargain in good faith over "wages, hours, and other terms and conditions of employment" (Section 8(d)) / **Consequence**: Refusal to bargain over mandatory subjects = ULP under Section 8(a)(5) (employer) or 8(b)(3) (union); unilateral change to mandatory subject without bargaining = ULP
- **Wages and Compensation** / Base pay, overtime, bonuses, incentive pay, shift differentials, severance pay, merit increases, cost-of-living adjustments / **Consequence**: Any change to compensation structure requires bargaining to agreement or good-faith impasse
- **Hours of Work** / Scheduling, shift assignments, overtime requirements, break periods, workweek structure / **Consequence**: Unilateral schedule changes = ULP; even operational changes that affect hours require bargaining
- **Benefits** / Health insurance, retirement/pension, paid leave (vacation, sick, personal), life insurance, disability / **Consequence**: Mid-term benefit changes without bargaining = ULP; open enrollment modifications may require bargaining
- **Working Conditions** / Safety rules, work rules, discipline procedures, seniority, job classifications, layoff and recall procedures, subcontracting (if it affects unit work), plant closings / **Consequence**: Broad category; virtually anything affecting the employment relationship may be mandatory
- **Grievance Procedures** / Process for resolving disputes over CBA interpretation and application / **Consequence**: Most CBAs include binding arbitration as final step; grievance/arbitration is mandatory subject

### Permissive Subjects of Bargaining

- **Definition** / Subjects that are lawful but not required to be bargained; either party may raise but cannot insist to impasse / **Consequence**: Insisting on permissive subject to point of impasse = ULP; bargaining to impasse is only permitted on mandatory subjects
- **Examples** / Internal union affairs (dues structure, internal governance), composition of the bargaining team, interest arbitration clauses, management decisions concerning the scope and direction of the business (closing, relocating, or restructuring where not primarily labor-cost motivated), retiree benefits (not current employees) / **Consequence**: Must correctly classify subjects; misclassification can result in ULP charges

### Duty to Bargain in Good Faith

- **Standard** / Mutual obligation to meet at reasonable times and confer in good faith; no obligation to agree or make concessions / **Consequence**: Good faith is assessed by totality of conduct; single indicator not dispositive
- **Surface Bargaining Prohibition** / Going through motions of bargaining without genuine intent to reach agreement / **Threshold**: Indicators: unreasonable proposals, take-it-or-leave-it positions without explanation, excessive delays, refusing to provide information, refusing to make counterproposals / **Consequence**: Surface bargaining = 8(a)(5) or 8(b)(3) ULP; may result in bargaining order or extension of status quo obligation
- **Information Requests** / Union may request information relevant and necessary to bargaining or contract administration; employer must provide within reasonable time / **Threshold**: Presumptively relevant: wage data, benefit information for bargaining unit; non-presumptive information: employer must show relevance / **Consequence**: Failure to provide or unreasonable delay = ULP; but employer may assert legitimate confidentiality concerns and propose accommodations

### Impasse Procedures

- **Impasse** / Occurs when parties have exhausted possibilities of reaching agreement; genuine deadlock on mandatory subjects / **Threshold**: Must have bargained in good faith; premature declaration of impasse = ULP / **Consequence**: After genuine impasse, employer may implement last best offer on mandatory subjects; union may strike
- **Mediation** / Voluntary use of neutral mediator to facilitate agreement; Federal Mediation and Conciliation Service (FMCS) provides free mediation services / **Threshold**: FMCS notification required 30 days before contract expiration for initial contracts; 60 days for existing contracts / **Consequence**: Mediation does not break impasse for legal purposes; parties still in negotiations
- **Factfinding** / Neutral third party investigates and issues non-binding recommendations / **Consequence**: Used primarily in public sector; private sector use is voluntary
- **Economic Weapons After Impasse** / Employer: lockout, implement last best offer; Union: strike / **Consequence**: See `strikes-lockouts.md` for detailed analysis of economic weapons

### Successor Employer Obligations

#### Burns International Security (1972)

- **Recognition Obligation** / Successor employer (acquiring employer that continues substantially the same business with substantially the same workforce) must recognize and bargain with the union / **Threshold**: Majority of successor's workforce in the unit comes from predecessor's bargaining unit employees / **Consequence**: Must bargain with incumbent union; but not bound by predecessor's CBA (may set initial terms unilaterally, except recognition and bargaining obligation)
- **Hiring Discrimination** / Successor cannot discriminate against predecessor's employees based on their union membership or activity / **Consequence**: Refusal to hire predecessor's union employees to avoid bargaining obligation = ULP

#### Fall River Dyeing (1987)

- **Substantial Continuity Test** / Successor obligations apply when there is "substantial continuity" between enterprises: same business, same employees, same location, same customers, same supervisors / **Threshold**: No single factor dispositive; totality of circumstances / **Consequence**: Continuity test determines when successor obligations attach; may be assessed at "substantial and representative complement" of workforce

#### Contract Bar Doctrine

- **Rule** / Valid CBA bars election petition during its term, up to 3 years maximum / **Threshold**: 3-year maximum bar; petition may be filed during 30-day window (between 90 and 60 days before contract expiration) / **Consequence**: Contract bar provides stability; but does not prevent voluntary recognition of different union or decertification during window period

### Zipper Clauses

- **Definition** / Contract provision stating that the CBA represents the complete agreement and waives the right to bargain over any subject not covered during the term / **Consequence**: Effective zipper clause may relieve employer of obligation to bargain over mid-term changes not covered by CBA; but clear and unmistakable waiver required; broadly worded clauses may not waive specific rights

### Management Rights Clauses

- **Scope** / Provision reserving to employer the right to manage the business, direct the workforce, and make operational decisions / **Consequence**: Broad management rights clause preserved employer discretion on non-mandatory subjects; but does not eliminate duty to bargain over mandatory subjects or effects of management decisions on mandatory subjects
- **Residual Rights Theory** / Employer retains all management rights not specifically limited by the CBA / **Consequence**: Extent of retained rights depends on CBA language, bargaining history, and past practice

### Contract Administration

- **Grievance-Arbitration** / Most CBAs provide multi-step grievance procedure culminating in binding arbitration / **Consequence**: Arbitrator's interpretation of CBA is final and binding; court review limited to whether arbitrator's award "draws its essence" from the CBA (Steelworkers Trilogy)
- **Past Practice** / Established practices may become binding even if not in CBA; requires: (1) unequivocal practice, (2) acted upon over reasonable period, (3) accepted by both parties / **Consequence**: Past practice can create implied contract terms; employer cannot unilaterally discontinue binding past practice without bargaining
- **No-Strike Clauses** / Most CBAs include no-strike clause during contract term / **Consequence**: Strike during CBA term in violation of no-strike clause is unprotected; employer may discipline; union may be liable for damages (Section 301)

## Nlra

# National Labor Relations Act (NLRA)

## Applicability

Applies to most private-sector employers and employees in the United States. The National Labor Relations Act (29 U.S.C. 151-169) establishes the right of employees to organize, bargain collectively, and engage in concerted activity, while prohibiting unfair labor practices by employers and unions. Administered by the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB).

## Key Requirements

### Section 7 — Employee Rights

- **Right to Organize** / Employees have the right to self-organization, to form, join, or assist labor organizations / **Consequence**: Employer interference with organizational rights = unfair labor practice (ULP)
- **Right to Bargain Collectively** / Employees have the right to bargain collectively through representatives of their own choosing / **Consequence**: Employer refusal to bargain with certified representative = ULP under Section 8(a)(5)
- **Right to Engage in Concerted Activity** / Employees have the right to engage in other concerted activities for the purpose of collective bargaining or other mutual aid or protection / **Consequence**: Protects group complaints, walkouts, social media discussions about workplace conditions, and other collective action even without a union; discipline for protected concerted activity = ULP
- **Right to Refrain** / Employees also have the right to refrain from any or all of the above activities / **Consequence**: Union cannot coerce employees into joining or supporting; applies equally in union and non-union workplaces

### Section 8(a) — Employer Unfair Labor Practices (5 Types)

- **8(a)(1) — Interference, Restraint, Coercion** / Employer may not interfere with, restrain, or coerce employees in the exercise of Section 7 rights / **Threshold**: Broad catch-all; any employer conduct that tends to discourage Section 7 activity may violate / **Consequence**: Remedies include cease-and-desist order, notice posting, reinstatement, back pay; every other employer ULP also violates 8(a)(1)
- **8(a)(2) — Domination of Labor Organization** / Employer may not dominate or interfere with the formation or administration of any labor organization, or contribute financial or other support to it / **Consequence**: Employer-formed "employee involvement committees" that deal with terms and conditions of employment may be unlawful company unions; disestablishment ordered
- **8(a)(3) — Discrimination** / Employer may not discriminate in hiring, tenure, or any term of employment to encourage or discourage membership in a labor organization / **Consequence**: Discriminatory discharge for union activity = reinstatement with back pay; damages may be substantial for long delays
- **8(a)(4) — Retaliation for NLRB Activity** / Employer may not discharge or discriminate against an employee for filing charges or giving testimony under the NLRA / **Consequence**: Same remedies as 8(a)(3); retaliatory discharge is per se violation
- **8(a)(5) — Refusal to Bargain** / Employer may not refuse to bargain collectively with the representative designated by a majority of employees in an appropriate bargaining unit / **Consequence**: Includes failure to bargain in good faith, unilateral changes to mandatory subjects without bargaining, surface bargaining, failure to provide requested information; see `collective-bargaining.md`

### Section 8(b) — Union Unfair Labor Practices

- **8(b)(1) — Coercion of Employees** / Union may not restrain or coerce employees in the exercise of Section 7 rights (including right to refrain) / **Consequence**: Physical intimidation, threats of loss of employment, mass picketing blocking ingress/egress
- **8(b)(2) — Causing Employer Discrimination** / Union may not cause or attempt to cause an employer to discriminate against an employee in violation of 8(a)(3) / **Consequence**: Union demand to fire employee for non-membership reasons (beyond dues obligation) = ULP
- **8(b)(3) — Refusal to Bargain** / Union may not refuse to bargain collectively with the employer / **Consequence**: Same obligations as employer; must bargain in good faith
- **8(b)(4) — Secondary Boycotts and Strikes** / Union may not engage in strikes, picketing, or boycotts aimed at neutral (secondary) employers to pressure the primary employer / **Consequence**: Injunctive relief available (NLRB can seek 10(l) injunction); damages under Section 303; see `strikes-lockouts.md`
- **8(b)(7) — Recognitional Picketing** / Union may not picket for recognition for more than 30 days without filing an election petition / **Consequence**: NLRB may seek injunction; expedited election may be ordered

### NLRB Jurisdiction

- **Jurisdictional Standards (Annual Revenue)** / NLRB asserts jurisdiction based on annual business volume:
  - Non-retail: $50,000 outflow or inflow of goods across state lines
  - Retail: $500,000 gross annual volume
  - Office buildings: $100,000 gross annual revenue
  - Health care institutions: $250,000 gross annual revenue
  - Private universities: $1,000,000 gross annual revenue
  - US Postal Service: jurisdiction regardless of revenue
  / **Consequence**: Employers below thresholds = no NLRB jurisdiction; state labor boards may apply
- **Interstate Commerce** / NLRA applies to employers "affecting commerce"; essentially all private employers meeting jurisdictional thresholds / **Consequence**: Very broad reach; even indirect effect on interstate commerce sufficient

### Exclusions from Coverage

- **Supervisors** / Individuals with authority to hire, fire, discipline, assign, reward, or responsibly direct employees using independent judgment / **Consequence**: Supervisors have no Section 7 rights under NLRA; but employer may not discipline supervisors for refusing to commit ULPs
- **Managers** / Individuals who formulate and effectuate management policies by expressing and making operative the decisions of their employer / **Consequence**: Managers are excluded from bargaining units and Section 7 protections
- **Agricultural Laborers** / Excluded from NLRA coverage / **Consequence**: Some states have separate agricultural labor relations statutes (e.g., California Agricultural Labor Relations Act)
- **Independent Contractors** / Common law agency test determines employee vs. independent contractor status / **Consequence**: Misclassification = failure to recognize Section 7 rights; NLRB has recently adopted broader employee definitions
- **Confidential Employees** / Employees who assist and act in a confidential capacity to persons who formulate, determine, and effectuate management labor relations policies / **Consequence**: Excluded from bargaining units; narrow definition — must relate to labor relations, not just access to confidential business information
- **Federal, State, and Local Government Employees** / Excluded from NLRA; covered by separate statutes (Federal Service Labor-Management Relations Statute; state public employee labor laws) / **Consequence**: Different frameworks apply; some states have no public sector bargaining rights
- **Railway and Airline Employees** / Covered by Railway Labor Act, not NLRA / **Consequence**: Different election, bargaining, and strike rules apply

### NLRB Procedures

- **ULP Charge Filing** / Filed with regional NLRB office within 6 months of alleged violation / **Timeline**: 6-month statute of limitations; strict deadline / **Consequence**: Late filing = charge dismissed; no tolling for continuing violations in most circumstances
- **Investigation and Complaint** / Regional Director investigates; if meritorious, issues complaint; settlement encouraged; if not settled, hearing before Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) / **Timeline**: Investigation typically 7-14 weeks; complaint to hearing 6-12 months / **Consequence**: ALJ decision appealable to full NLRB Board; Board decisions appealable to federal circuit court
- **Remedies** / Cease and desist; notice posting (physical and electronic); reinstatement with back pay (less interim earnings); make-whole relief; bargaining order in egregious cases / **Consequence**: No punitive damages under NLRA; but back pay liability accumulates during proceedings; NLRB has expanded make-whole remedies to include consequential damages (Thryv, 2022)

## Strikes Lockouts

# Strikes and Lockouts

## Applicability

Applies to work stoppages, economic actions, and related activities by unions and employers. Covers the legal framework for economic strikes, unfair labor practice strikes, lockouts, picketing, replacement workers, secondary activity, and related doctrines. Critical intersection of labor law, constitutional law (First Amendment), and tort law.

## Key Requirements

### Economic Strikes

- **Definition** / Strike called to pressure employer on mandatory bargaining subjects (wages, hours, conditions) after impasse or to obtain a first contract / **Consequence**: Economic strikers are "employees" under NLRA; retain certain rights but may be permanently replaced
- **Permanent Replacement — Mackay Radio Doctrine** / Employer may hire permanent replacements for economic strikers (NLRB v. Mackay Radio, 1938) / **Consequence**: Permanently replaced economic strikers retain reinstatement rights only to substantially equivalent positions as they become available; must make unconditional offer to return; placed on preferential recall list (Laidlaw, 1968)
- **Reinstatement Rights** / Permanently replaced economic strikers who make unconditional offer to return must be placed on preferential recall list; recalled as positions become available, in order of seniority (unless business justification for different order) / **Timeline**: Recall rights continue for reasonable period (12 months typically; NLRB has extended in some cases) / **Consequence**: Failure to recall when position available = ULP; employer cannot hire new employees while replaced strikers are on recall list
- **Temporary Replacement** / Employer may also hire temporary replacements; economic strikers whose replacements are temporary have right to reinstatement when strike ends / **Consequence**: Distinction between permanent and temporary replacement is crucial; employer must clearly communicate status of replacement at time of hire

### Unfair Labor Practice (ULP) Strikes

- **Definition** / Strike caused in whole or in part by employer's unfair labor practices / **Consequence**: ULP strikers have stronger protections than economic strikers
- **No Permanent Replacement** / Employer may not permanently replace ULP strikers; may only use temporary replacements / **Consequence**: Upon unconditional offer to return, ULP strikers are entitled to immediate reinstatement, even if temporary replacements must be discharged
- **Reinstatement Right** / ULP strikers entitled to immediate reinstatement upon unconditional offer to return to work / **Timeline**: Employer must reinstate within reasonable time (typically 5 business days) after unconditional offer / **Consequence**: Failure to reinstate = additional ULP (8(a)(3)); back pay accrues from date of offer to return
- **Conversion** / An initially economic strike may convert to a ULP strike if employer commits ULPs during the strike that prolong or aggravate it / **Consequence**: Conversion retroactively changes replacement workers' status from permanent to temporary; significantly increases employer exposure

### Sympathy Strikes

- **Definition** / Refusal by employees to cross a picket line established by another union or at another employer's facility / **Consequence**: Generally protected activity under Section 7 unless the employee's CBA contains a no-strike clause that encompasses sympathy strikes
- **No-Strike Clause Interpretation** / Broad no-strike clauses ("employees will not engage in any strike") may waive sympathy strike rights; narrow clauses may not / **Threshold**: Clear and unmistakable waiver standard; ambiguous no-strike clause does not waive sympathy strike right / **Consequence**: Arbitrability of sympathy strike issue may be contested; discipline of sympathy strikers requires clear contractual waiver

### Partial and Intermittent Strikes

- **Partial Strike** / Strike by some but not all bargaining unit employees; or slowdowns, work-to-rule actions / **Consequence**: Partial strikes and slowdowns are generally unprotected activity; employer may discipline participants without committing ULP
- **Intermittent Strike** / Repeated short-duration work stoppages designed to disrupt operations without full strike / **Consequence**: Intermittent strikes are unprotected; employees engaging in intermittent strikes may be discharged; distinguishable from single protected strike followed by return to work

### Lockouts

- **Offensive/Economic Lockout** / Employer locks out employees to apply economic pressure during bargaining / **Threshold**: Permitted after genuine impasse in bargaining (American Ship Building v. NLRB, 1965); may also be permitted pre-impasse in multi-employer bargaining (NLRB v. Brown, 1965) / **Consequence**: Employer may use temporary replacements during lockout; lockout must be for legitimate business purpose, not to destroy union
- **Defensive Lockout** / Employer locks out to prevent harm from threatened or anticipated strike (e.g., orderly shutdown to protect perishable inventory, coordinate with business operations) / **Consequence**: Defensive lockout permitted when employer has reasonable basis to believe strike is imminent; may act preemptively
- **Permanent Replacements During Lockout** / Employer generally cannot hire permanent replacements during lockout (unlike economic strike) / **Consequence**: Temporary replacements during lockout are permitted; but permanent replacements may convert lawful lockout into ULP
- **Lockout Duration** / No time limit on lawful lockout; continues until parties reach agreement or employer terminates lockout / **Consequence**: Extended lockout may create financial pressure on both parties; locked-out employees may seek other employment

### Picketing

#### Primary Picketing

- **Protected Activity** / Picketing at the struck employer's premises to publicize the dispute and discourage employees and public from entering / **Consequence**: Protected by First Amendment and NLRA; employer cannot enjoin peaceful primary picketing (Norris-LaGuardia Act limits federal injunctions)
- **Restrictions** / Must be peaceful; no violence, threats, mass picketing blocking ingress/egress, property damage / **Consequence**: Violence or blocking = unprotected; state courts may enjoin; criminal prosecution possible

#### Secondary Picketing (Section 8(b)(4))

- **Prohibition** / Union may not picket, strike, or boycott a neutral (secondary) employer to pressure the primary employer / **Consequence**: Secondary activity = ULP; NLRB may seek expedited injunction (Section 10(l)); union liable for damages under Section 303
- **Publicity Proviso** / Permitted to distribute handbills (but not picket) at secondary employer if: (1) truthfully advises public; (2) does not induce work stoppage by secondary employer's employees / **Consequence**: Handbilling is protected; picketing at secondary site is generally prohibited; distinction between handbilling and picketing is important

#### Common Situs Picketing

- **Multi-Employer Worksites** / When primary and neutral employers share a common worksite (e.g., construction site), picketing may inadvertently affect neutral employers / **Threshold**: Moore Dry Dock standards: picket only when primary employer is present; picket as close to primary employer's operations as practical; clearly identify dispute is with primary employer / **Consequence**: Picketing that meets Moore Dry Dock criteria is lawful primary picketing; failure to meet standards = unlawful secondary picketing

#### Reserved Gate Doctrine (General Electric)

- **Separate Gates** / When an employer establishes separate gates for its employees and for neutral contractor employees, union must limit picketing to the gate used by the struck employer's employees / **Consequence**: Picketing at gate reserved for neutral contractors = unlawful secondary activity; reserved gate must be clearly marked and consistently used
- **Exception** / If neutral contractor's work is "related to" the struck employer's normal operations, picketing at all gates is permissible / **Consequence**: Distinction between related and unrelated contractor work is frequently litigated

### Ally Doctrine

- **Definition** / Neutral employer that performs struck work (work normally done by striking employees) loses its neutral status and becomes an "ally" subject to primary picketing and boycott / **Consequence**: Struck employer's subcontracting of work to third party during strike makes third party an ally; union may picket ally
- **Single Employer / Alter Ego** / Entities that are commonly owned and controlled and share interrelated operations may be treated as a single employer for labor law purposes / **Consequence**: Picketing at alter ego entity is primary, not secondary activity

### Notice Requirements

- **FMCS Notice** / 60 days notice before contract expiration for existing CBA; 30 days for initial contract; must notify FMCS and state mediation agencies / **Consequence**: Strike or lockout during 60-day notice period = unprotected; employees lose Section 7 protections
- **Healthcare Institutions** / 10-day advance notice of strike required at healthcare institutions (Section 8(g)) / **Consequence**: Strike without 10-day notice = unprotected; notice must specify date and time

### No-Strike Obligations

- **CBA No-Strike Clause** / Most CBAs include no-strike clause prohibiting work stoppages during contract term / **Consequence**: Strike in violation of no-strike clause = unprotected; employer may discipline; union may be liable for damages under Section 301 (Boys Markets injunction available)
- **Implied No-Strike Obligation** / Arbitration clause may imply no-strike obligation even without express no-strike clause (Gateway Coal v. UMWA) / **Consequence**: Broad arbitration clause creates implied obligation not to strike over arbitrable grievances

## Union Organizing

# Union Organizing

## Applicability

Applies to employers facing or anticipating union organizing campaigns, including election petitions, voluntary recognition, card-check campaigns, and neutrality agreements. Covers NLRB election procedures, employer campaign rules, the Cemex doctrine, and Gissel bargaining orders.

## Key Requirements

### Election Petitions

- **RC Petition (Certification of Representative)** / Filed by union or employees seeking to certify a union as bargaining representative / **Threshold**: Must demonstrate 30% showing of interest (authorization cards or petition signatures) among employees in proposed bargaining unit / **Consequence**: NLRB conducts investigation; if appropriate unit exists and showing of interest is valid, election ordered
- **RD Petition (Decertification)** / Filed by employees seeking to remove an existing union as bargaining representative / **Threshold**: 30% showing of interest among bargaining unit employees; cannot be filed during contract bar period (except during 30-day window) / **Consequence**: Decertification election conducted; if majority votes against representation, union decertified
- **RM Petition (Employer-Filed)** / Filed by employer when presented with a claim of representation from a union / **Threshold**: Employer must have objective reasonable doubt about union's majority status; or union demands recognition / **Consequence**: Election ordered if valid question concerning representation exists

### Election Timeline and Process

- **NLRB Quickie Election Rule (2014/2023)** / Streamlined election procedures reducing time from petition to election / **Timeline**: Pre-election hearing within 8 days of petition filing; election typically 20-25 days after direction of election (can be as short as 11 days after hearing) / **Consequence**: Compressed timeline reduces employer campaign period; employer must be prepared for rapid election
- **Voter Eligibility** / Employees in the bargaining unit employed during the eligibility period (payroll period immediately preceding direction of election) / **Consequence**: Unit determination (who is included/excluded) is critical; contested unit issues resolved at pre-election hearing or post-election
- **Excelsior List** / Employer must provide union with list of eligible voters (names, home addresses, phone numbers, email addresses, work locations, shifts, job classifications) within 2 business days of direction of election / **Consequence**: Failure to provide timely and accurate list = grounds for setting aside election; expanded from names/addresses to include electronic contact information (2014 rule)
- **Election Conduct** / Secret ballot election at worksite (or by mail ballot in certain circumstances); NLRB agent supervises; observers from both sides permitted / **Consequence**: Election results certified if no objections; challenged ballots resolved if determinative

### Employer Campaign Rules — TIPS

- **No Threats** / Employer may not threaten employees with adverse consequences for supporting a union (e.g., plant closure, job loss, benefit reduction, more onerous conditions) / **Consequence**: Threats = 8(a)(1) ULP; may result in election being set aside; in egregious cases, Gissel bargaining order
- **No Interrogation** / Employer may not systematically interrogate employees about their union sympathies, activities, or the sympathies of coworkers / **Consequence**: Coercive interrogation = 8(a)(1) ULP; even casual questioning may be unlawful depending on context (Rossmore House factors: timing, relationship, circumstances)
- **No Promises** / Employer may not promise benefits to employees to discourage union support (e.g., wage increase, promotion, improved conditions if employees vote against union) / **Consequence**: Promise of benefit = 8(a)(1) ULP; timing of benefit changes during campaign period is heavily scrutinized
- **No Surveillance** / Employer may not surveil or create impression of surveillance of employee union activities (monitoring union meetings, photographing picketers, tracking who attends organizing events) / **Consequence**: Surveillance or impression thereof = 8(a)(1) ULP; social media monitoring during campaigns raises similar concerns

### Employer Permitted Activity — FORE

- **Facts** / Employer may communicate factual information about unionization, costs of dues, union history, contract negotiation process / **Consequence**: Factual communications are protected employer speech under Section 8(c)
- **Opinions** / Employer may express opinions about unionization as long as they do not contain threats or promises / **Consequence**: Fine line between opinion ("I don't think a union would be good for us") and threat ("If the union comes in, we'll have to consider closing")
- **Rules** / Employer may enforce existing workplace rules consistently (no-solicitation during working time, no-distribution in work areas) / **Consequence**: Rules must be facially neutral and consistently enforced; rules adopted in response to organizing may be unlawful
- **Examples** / Employer may share examples of negative union experiences, unfulfilled union promises at other companies, strike history / **Consequence**: Must be accurate; misleading examples may be viewed as threats

### Gissel Bargaining Orders

- **Standard** / NLRB may order employer to bargain with a union without an election when employer ULPs are so egregious that a fair election is unlikely / **Threshold**: Union must demonstrate majority support (authorization cards); employer misconduct must be "outrageous and pervasive" (Category I) or make fair election "unlikely" (Category II) / **Consequence**: Employer loses right to election; must bargain immediately with union; remedy for severe employer misconduct

### Voluntary Recognition and Card Check

- **Authorization Cards** / Union collects signed cards from majority of employees authorizing the union as bargaining representative / **Threshold**: Majority (50% + 1) of bargaining unit employees must sign cards / **Consequence**: Cards alone do not compel recognition (employer may insist on election under Linden Lumber); but see Cemex doctrine below
- **Voluntary Recognition** / Employer may voluntarily recognize union based on demonstrated majority support (card check) without election / **Consequence**: Once voluntarily recognized, employer must bargain; recognition bar doctrine provides reasonable period of bargaining without challenge to representation (typically 6-12 months)
- **Neutrality Agreements** / Employer agrees not to campaign against union; may include card-check recognition provision / **Consequence**: Enforceable as private contract; increasingly common in organized sectors; may include access agreements and expedited procedures

### NLRB 2023 Cemex Doctrine

- **Rule** / When a union requests recognition based on majority support (authorization cards) and the employer commits ULPs that would require setting aside an election, the NLRB will issue a bargaining order rather than re-running the election / **Threshold**: (1) Union demonstrates majority in appropriate unit; (2) union requests recognition; (3) employer refuses and commits ULPs that would prevent a fair election; OR (4) employer fails to petition for election within 2 weeks of demand / **Consequence**: Fundamentally changes the organizing landscape; employer that commits ULPs during an election campaign risks automatic bargaining order; employer that receives card-check demand should promptly petition for election if it does not wish to voluntarily recognize
- **Impact on Employer Strategy** / Employer that commits even moderate ULPs during election campaign may face bargaining order instead of re-run election / **Consequence**: Dramatically increases stakes of employer misconduct; training of supervisors and managers is critical before any campaign
- **Election Petition Obligation** / Employer that receives recognition demand based on majority cards must either recognize the union or promptly file RM petition (within 2 weeks) / **Consequence**: Failure to file petition = may be ordered to bargain; eliminates historical employer strategy of simply refusing to recognize

### Bargaining Unit Determination

- **Community of Interest** / NLRB determines appropriate unit based on: similarity of skills, duties, wages, benefits, working conditions; common supervision; degree of interchange; geographic proximity; desires of employees; history of bargaining / **Consequence**: Unit determination affects election outcome; broader units may dilute union support; narrower units may increase it
- **Micro-Units** / NLRB has permitted smaller, specialized units within larger workforces (Specialty Healthcare, 2011) / **Consequence**: Employer opposing micro-unit bears burden of showing overwhelming community of interest with excluded employees
- **Accretion** / Addition of new employees or positions to existing bargaining unit without election / **Threshold**: New positions share sufficient community of interest with existing unit; no separate identity / **Consequence**: Employer must apply CBA to accreted employees; avoid inadvertent accretion by maintaining separate supervision and conditions
