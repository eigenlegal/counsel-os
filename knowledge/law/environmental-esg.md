## Climate Disclosure

# Climate Disclosure

## Applicability

Applies when a company is subject to or may become subject to mandatory climate-related disclosure requirements. Relevant to public companies, large private companies, and any entity in the value chain of a reporting company. Triggered by SEC filings, EU operations, California revenue thresholds, or voluntary climate commitments that may become binding representations.

## Key Requirements

### SEC Climate Disclosure Rules

- **Scope 1 and Scope 2 Emissions** / Large accelerated filers must disclose Scope 1 (direct) and Scope 2 (purchased energy) greenhouse gas emissions, subject to materiality. / **Consequence**: Emissions data must be audited by an independent attestation provider; phased timeline begins with limited assurance, moving to reasonable assurance. Material misstatement triggers securities fraud liability under Section 10(b) and Rule 10b-5.
- **Scope 3 Emissions** / Disclosure of Scope 3 (value chain) emissions if material or if the company has set a Scope 3 target. Safe harbor for Scope 3 data applies. / **Consequence**: Scope 3 disclosure is the most challenging — requires supply chain data that may be unreliable; safe harbor protects against private litigation but not SEC enforcement for knowingly false statements.
- **Material Climate Risks** / Disclosure of material climate-related risks (physical and transition), their actual and potential impacts on strategy, business model, and financial statements. / **Consequence**: Must quantify financial impact where reasonably estimable; qualitative disclosure required at minimum. Failure to disclose material climate risks may constitute omission under Rule 10b-5.
- **Governance and Risk Management** / Board oversight of climate-related risks, management's role, and integration into enterprise risk management. / **Consequence**: Creates accountability trail; directors may face derivative claims if governance disclosures do not match actual oversight practices.

### EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)

- **Applicability Thresholds** / Large EU companies and listed SMEs. Non-EU companies with **EUR 150 million** net turnover in the EU and at least one EU subsidiary or branch. / **Consequence**: First reporting cycle for large companies: fiscal years starting **January 1, 2024**; non-EU companies: fiscal years starting **January 1, 2028**. Non-compliance penalties set by member states, potentially up to **5% of annual turnover**.
- **Double Materiality** / Must report on both financial materiality (outside-in: how sustainability affects the company) and impact materiality (inside-out: how the company affects environment and society). / **Consequence**: Broader than SEC's financial materiality standard; requires assessment of the company's impact on climate regardless of financial relevance to the company.
- **European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS)** / Mandatory detailed standards covering climate change (ESRS E1), pollution, water, biodiversity, and circular economy. / **Consequence**: ESRS E1 requires transition plan disclosure, Scope 1/2/3 emissions, and Paris-alignment analysis; far more prescriptive than SEC requirements.

### California Climate Disclosure Laws

- **SB 253 (Climate Corporate Data Accountability Act)** / Companies doing business in California with total annual revenue exceeding **$1 billion** must report Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions annually. / **Consequence**: Scope 1 and 2 reporting begins **2026** (for 2025 data); Scope 3 begins **2027**. Third-party assurance required: limited assurance by **2026**, reasonable assurance by **2030**. Penalties up to **$500,000** per reporting year for non-filing.
- **SB 261 (Climate-Related Financial Risk Act)** / Companies doing business in California with total annual revenue exceeding **$500 million** must prepare and publish biennial climate-related financial risk reports in accordance with TCFD framework. / **Consequence**: First report due **January 1, 2026**. Penalties up to **$50,000** per reporting year for non-compliance. Reports must be publicly available.
- **"Doing Business in California"** / Threshold broadly interpreted — includes any company with California revenue, employees, or property. / **Consequence**: Captures many out-of-state and international companies; the revenue threshold is based on worldwide revenue, not California-specific revenue.

### ISSB Standards (International Sustainability Standards Board)

- **IFRS S1 (General Sustainability Disclosures)** / Requires disclosure of material sustainability-related financial information, including governance, strategy, risk management, and metrics/targets. / **Consequence**: Effective for annual reporting periods beginning **January 1, 2024** (adoption varies by jurisdiction). Jurisdictions adopting ISSB include the UK, Canada, Australia, Japan, Singapore, and others.
- **IFRS S2 (Climate-Related Disclosures)** / Climate-specific standard requiring Scope 1/2/3 emissions, transition plans, climate-related targets, and use of carbon credits. / **Consequence**: Scope 3 relief in first year of application; must disclose the approach used for measuring emissions (GHG Protocol or equivalent). Builds on and supersedes TCFD recommendations.

### Penalties and Enforcement

- **SEC Enforcement** / Securities fraud claims for material misstatements or omissions in climate disclosures. / **Consequence**: Civil penalties up to **$2.1 million** per violation for entities; officer/director liability for certifications; private class action exposure.
- **State AG Enforcement** / State attorneys general (particularly New York, California) have pursued greenwashing and climate disclosure cases under state consumer protection and securities laws. / **Consequence**: Penalties vary by state; New York Martin Act does not require scienter, lowering the enforcement threshold.
- **EU Enforcement** / CSRD non-compliance enforced by member state competent authorities. / **Consequence**: Sanctions include financial penalties, public statements, and potential delisting; auditor liability for assurance opinions on sustainability reports.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Securities** — Climate disclosures are part of SEC filings; materiality analysis under securities law applies
- **Corporate** — Board governance and oversight requirements; fiduciary duty to manage climate risk
- **Consumer Protection** — Climate claims in marketing must be consistent with formal disclosures
- **Data Privacy** — Supply chain data collection for Scope 3 may implicate data sharing agreements
- **Environmental Liability** — Physical climate risks overlap with environmental compliance obligations

## Environmental Due Diligence

# Environmental Due Diligence

## Applicability

Applies when a transaction, financing, or business combination requires assessment of environmental liabilities, contamination risk, regulatory compliance, or environmental insurance needs. Critical for real estate acquisitions, M&A involving industrial operations, and lending secured by real property or operating businesses.

## Key Requirements

### Phase I Environmental Site Assessment

- **ASTM E1527-21** / Current standard for Phase I ESAs; superseded E1527-13 effective **February 13, 2024**. Phase I is a records-review and site-reconnaissance investigation (no sampling). / **Consequence**: A Phase I conducted under the superseded standard after the effective date does not satisfy All Appropriate Inquiry (AAI) requirements; the innocent purchaser, contiguous property owner, and bona fide prospective purchaser defenses under CERCLA are unavailable.
- **All Appropriate Inquiry (AAI)** / Federal standard (40 CFR Part 312) defining the due diligence required to claim CERCLA liability defenses. Phase I ESA under ASTM E1527-21 satisfies AAI. / **Consequence**: AAI must be conducted BEFORE acquisition; post-closing Phase I does not qualify for CERCLA defenses. The inquiry must be conducted by an "environmental professional" as defined in the rule.
- **Shelf Life** / Phase I ESA is valid for **180 days** from the date of the site visit. If the report is more than **90 days** old at closing, certain components must be updated. / **Consequence**: Government records review, interviews, and site reconnaissance must be updated after **180 days**; title search and regulatory database reviews must be updated after **90 days**. Using an expired Phase I eliminates CERCLA defenses.
- **Recognized Environmental Conditions (RECs)** / Phase I identifies RECs (conditions indicating existing, past, or material threat of release), Controlled RECs (CRECs — remediated with institutional controls in place), and Historical RECs (HRECs — past releases with no remaining impact). / **Consequence**: RECs trigger recommendation for Phase II investigation; proceeding to close without investigating RECs undermines the innocent purchaser defense and demonstrates knowledge of potential contamination.
- **Data Gaps** / Phase I environmental professional must identify data gaps (information that could not be obtained) and assess their significance. / **Consequence**: Significant data gaps that cannot be resolved may prevent the environmental professional from rendering an opinion; the Phase I report may be incomplete for CERCLA defense purposes.

### Phase II Environmental Site Assessment

- **Sampling Scope** / Phase II involves intrusive investigation — soil borings, groundwater monitoring wells, soil vapor sampling, building material testing — to evaluate RECs identified in Phase I. / **Consequence**: Phase II scope should be tailored to the RECs identified; costs range from **$10,000 for a limited investigation** to **$100,000+ for comprehensive multi-media sampling** depending on property size and complexity.
- **Regulatory Screening Levels** / Sample results compared against applicable federal (EPA Regional Screening Levels) and state cleanup standards. / **Consequence**: Exceedance of screening levels triggers reporting obligations in most states — typically within **24 hours to 30 days** of confirmed results. Failure to report known contamination is independently actionable and may constitute criminal conduct.
- **Vapor Intrusion** / Subsurface contamination (especially VOCs like TCE, PCE) can migrate as vapors into overlying buildings, creating indoor air quality risks. / **Consequence**: EPA's **2015 OSWER Technical Guide** for vapor intrusion assessment requires investigation when volatile contaminants are present; mitigation systems cost **$50,000-$500,000** per building.
- **Background Conditions** / Naturally occurring or area-wide contamination that is not attributable to site activities. / **Consequence**: Background concentrations that exceed screening levels may not require remediation, but the burden is on the property owner to demonstrate background conditions.

### Remediation Cost Estimates

- **Range of Estimates** / Remediation costs for contaminated properties range from **$50,000** (minor soil removal) to **$50 million+** (groundwater treatment systems). / **Consequence**: Cost estimates must be range-based (low/mid/high) to account for uncertainty; estimates inform purchase price adjustments, indemnity caps, and insurance coverage.
- **Remediation Timelines** / Soil remediation: **6-18 months**. Groundwater remediation: **5-30 years** depending on contaminant type and hydrogeology. / **Consequence**: Long-duration groundwater remediation creates ongoing management obligations, monitoring costs, and institutional controls that affect property use and value.
- **Cost Recovery** / CERCLA Section 107 allows parties who incur cleanup costs to recover from other PRPs. / **Consequence**: Recovery is limited to costs "consistent with the National Contingency Plan"; voluntary cleanups should align with NCP standards to preserve cost recovery rights.

### Environmental Insurance

- **Pollution Legal Liability (PLL)** / Covers third-party bodily injury and property damage claims, cleanup costs, and defense costs arising from pollution conditions. / **Consequence**: PLL policies typically have terms of **3-10 years** with premiums of **3-7%** of the policy limit; policies fill the gap where contractual indemnities may be insufficient (seller insolvency, expired survival periods).
- **Cleanup Cost Cap (Stop-Loss)** / Covers cost overruns above a self-insured retention (SIR) when remediation costs exceed estimates. / **Consequence**: SIR is typically set at **100-125%** of the estimated remediation cost; cap coverage applies above the SIR. Essential for projects with uncertain remediation scope.
- **Representations and Warranties Insurance (Environmental Endorsement)** / R&W insurance policies can include environmental endorsements to cover breaches of environmental representations. / **Consequence**: Underwriters typically exclude known contamination from R&W environmental endorsements; pre-existing conditions require separate PLL coverage.
- **Premium Factors** / Environmental insurance premiums depend on: contamination history, Phase I/II results, property use (industrial vs. commercial), remediation status, and policy terms. / **Consequence**: Phase I/II results directly affect insurability and premium — a clean Phase I significantly reduces premiums; known contamination may result in exclusions or dramatically higher costs.

### Compliance History Review

- **EPA ECHO Database** / EPA's Enforcement and Compliance History Online database provides inspection, violation, and enforcement data for regulated facilities. / **Consequence**: Compliance history affects transaction risk assessment; a facility with multiple violations is more likely to face future enforcement and may have undisclosed contamination.
- **State Regulatory Records** / State environmental agencies maintain records of permits, violations, enforcement orders, spill reports, and cleanup programs. / **Consequence**: State records may reveal environmental conditions not captured in federal databases; records requests should be filed **4-8 weeks** before due diligence deadlines to allow for response time.
- **FOIA/Records Requests** / Freedom of Information Act requests to EPA and state agencies for facility-specific enforcement files, inspection reports, and correspondence. / **Consequence**: FOIA responses may take **20-60 business days** (federal) or longer; expedited processing available in limited circumstances. Plan requests early in the due diligence timeline.

### Regulatory Database Searches

- **Standard Database Searches** / Phase I includes searches of federal and state databases: NPL, CERCLIS, RCRA CORRACTS/TSD, state equivalent lists, leaking UST registries, within specified search distances (**0.25 to 1.0 mile** depending on database). / **Consequence**: Database hits within search distances create "adjoining property" RECs that may indicate off-site contamination migrating onto the subject property; these RECs require further evaluation.
- **Historical Use Records** / Sanborn fire insurance maps, aerial photographs, city directories, building permits, and historical topographic maps. / **Consequence**: Historical records revealing former gas stations, dry cleaners, auto repair shops, or industrial operations are strong indicators of potential contamination; absence from current databases does not mean absence of contamination.
- **Tribal and Military Records** / Properties near tribal lands or former military installations require additional searches of tribal environmental databases and Department of Defense records (FUDS — Formerly Used Defense Sites). / **Consequence**: Military contamination (unexplored ordnance, PFAS, TCE) can affect properties miles from the former installation; standard database searches may not capture these conditions.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Real Estate** — Environmental due diligence is a core component of real estate acquisition due diligence; Phase I is a standard closing condition
- **Environmental Liability** — Due diligence findings determine CERCLA defense eligibility and litigation exposure
- **Corporate** — M&A environmental due diligence for target company operations and real property portfolio
- **Insurance** — Environmental due diligence results drive insurance underwriting, coverage scope, and premium
- **Bankruptcy & Restructuring** — Environmental claims in bankruptcy require prior assessment of contamination scope and remediation costs

## Environmental Liability

# Environmental Liability

## Applicability

Applies when a matter involves potential liability under federal or state environmental statutes, including contamination, hazardous waste management, air emissions, water discharges, or regulatory enforcement. Critical in transactions involving industrial properties, manufacturing operations, and any entity that generates, transports, or disposes of hazardous substances.

## Key Requirements

### CERCLA (Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act)

- **Strict Liability** / Liability under Section 107 does not require proof of negligence or intent; mere ownership, operation, or arrangement for disposal is sufficient. / **Consequence**: Current owners/operators face full cleanup liability regardless of fault; contractual indemnities from sellers do not shield against EPA enforcement (EPA can pursue any PRP directly).
- **Joint and Several Liability** / Any single PRP may be held liable for the entire cost of remediation, even if multiple parties contributed. / **Consequence**: The most solvent or accessible PRP often bears disproportionate costs; contribution actions under Section 113(f) allow PRPs to seek allocation from other responsible parties, but only AFTER being sued or entering a settlement with the government.
- **Retroactive Liability** / Applies to contamination events occurring before CERCLA's enactment in 1980. / **Consequence**: A company that lawfully disposed of waste in the 1960s may be fully liable for cleanup costs decades later; no statute of limitations for initial cost recovery actions by the government.
- **PRP Categories** / Four categories: (1) current owners/operators, (2) owners/operators at time of disposal, (3) arrangers (generators), (4) transporters who selected the site. / **Consequence**: "Arranger" liability extends to parties who arranged for treatment or disposal even if they did not physically transport the waste; Burlington Northern (2009) narrowed but did not eliminate arranger liability.
- **Contribution and Allocation** / Section 113(f) allows PRPs to seek contribution using equitable factors (Gore factors): volume, toxicity, cooperation, ability to pay. / **Consequence**: Allocation is highly fact-intensive; parties settling with EPA receive contribution protection (Section 113(f)(2)), incentivizing early settlement.
- **Cleanup Cost Magnitude** / Superfund site cleanups average **$12-30 million**; complex sites can exceed **$500 million**. / **Consequence**: Environmental liability can dwarf the value of the underlying transaction; environmental insurance and indemnity escrows are essential risk management tools.

### RCRA (Resource Conservation and Recovery Act)

- **Cradle-to-Grave Tracking** / Generators, transporters, and treatment/storage/disposal facilities (TSDFs) must track hazardous waste from generation to disposal via manifest system. / **Consequence**: Generator liability does not end at disposal; improper disposal at a TSDF creates CERCLA liability for the generator as an "arranger."
- **Generator Categories** / Large Quantity Generators (LQGs: **1,000+ kg/month**), Small Quantity Generators (SQGs: **100-1,000 kg/month**), Very Small Quantity Generators (VSQGs: **<100 kg/month**). / **Consequence**: LQGs face the most stringent requirements — biennial reporting, personnel training, contingency plans, **90-day** maximum accumulation time. VSQGs have reduced requirements but are not exempt from CERCLA liability.
- **Corrective Action** / EPA can require facility-wide cleanup of all releases at RCRA-permitted facilities under Section 3008(h). / **Consequence**: Corrective action obligations can extend to contamination unrelated to the permitted operations; costs can rival Superfund cleanup costs.

### Clean Air Act (CAA)

- **Title V Operating Permits** / Major sources (emitting **100 tons/year** or more of any criteria pollutant, or **10 tons/year** of any HAP, or **25 tons/year** of combined HAPs) must obtain Title V permits. / **Consequence**: Operating without a Title V permit is a criminal offense; penalties up to **$100,000/day** per violation. Permit conditions become federally enforceable, allowing citizen suits.
- **New Source Review (NSR)** / New construction or major modifications at stationary sources in nonattainment areas require NSR permits with Lowest Achievable Emission Rate (LAER) technology. / **Consequence**: NSR can add **12-24 months** to project timelines; failure to obtain NSR permits before construction triggers enforcement, mandatory shutdown, and penalties.
- **GHG Reporting** / Facilities emitting **25,000+ metric tons CO2e/year** must report under EPA's Greenhouse Gas Reporting Program (40 CFR Part 98). / **Consequence**: Reported data is publicly available; feeds into climate disclosure requirements and enforcement actions. Inaccurate reporting triggers penalties.

### Clean Water Act (CWA)

- **NPDES Permits** / Any discharge of pollutants from a point source to waters of the United States requires a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System permit. / **Consequence**: Unpermitted discharges face penalties of up to **$64,618/day** per violation (as adjusted for inflation); both EPA and citizens can bring enforcement actions.
- **Stormwater Permits** / Construction activities disturbing **1+ acre** require a stormwater construction general permit; industrial facilities with specific SIC codes require multi-sector general permits. / **Consequence**: Failure to obtain stormwater permits is one of the most common CWA violations; penalties are substantial and citizen suit enforcement is active.
- **Wetlands (Section 404)** / Fill or dredge of wetlands requires a Section 404 permit from the Army Corps of Engineers. / **Consequence**: Unauthorized fill of wetlands triggers restoration obligations, penalties up to **$25,000/day**, and potential criminal prosecution for knowing violations. Sackett v. EPA (2023) narrowed the scope of "waters of the United States" but significant wetland protections remain.

### Penalty Framework

- **Civil Penalties** / EPA penalties across environmental statutes range from **$25,000 to $100,000+ per day** per violation, adjusted annually for inflation. / **Consequence**: Multi-year violations can result in penalties in the tens of millions; penalty calculations consider gravity, economic benefit of noncompliance, history of violations, and ability to pay.
- **Criminal Penalties** / Knowing violations of RCRA, CAA, and CWA carry criminal penalties including imprisonment (up to **15 years** for knowing endangerment under RCRA). / **Consequence**: Individual officers and managers face personal criminal liability; "responsible corporate officer" doctrine does not require proof that the individual personally committed the violation.
- **Supplemental Environmental Projects (SEPs)** / EPA may offset a portion of penalties through community benefit projects. / **Consequence**: SEPs can reduce cash penalty by up to **80%** of the mitigated amount; must have a nexus to the violation and benefit the affected community.

- **Real Estate** — CERCLA liability in property transactions, Phase I/II requirements, environmental covenants

- **Insurance** — Pollution exclusions in CGL policies, pollution legal liability policies, cleanup cost cap insurance
- **Bankruptcy & Restructuring** — Environmental claims in bankruptcy (government cleanup costs as administrative priority claims)

## Esg Reporting

# ESG Reporting

## Applicability

Applies when a company prepares, reviews, or is subject to environmental, social, and governance (ESG) reporting obligations or voluntary frameworks. Covers both mandatory disclosure regimes and voluntary standards that may create binding representations through repetition, incorporation into contracts, or reliance by investors.

## Key Requirements

### GRI (Global Reporting Initiative)

- **GRI Universal Standards** / GRI 1 (Foundation), GRI 2 (General Disclosures), GRI 3 (Material Topics). Required for any organization claiming GRI-conformant reporting. / **Consequence**: Claiming "in accordance with GRI" without meeting all requirements is misleading; partial use must be clearly labeled as "with reference to GRI." Stakeholder groups and regulators increasingly treat GRI reports as binding commitments.
- **Impact Materiality** / GRI assesses materiality based on the organization's impact on economy, environment, and people — not financial materiality to the organization. / **Consequence**: GRI's impact materiality can require disclosure of issues that are not financially material under SEC standards; creates broader disclosure obligations than investor-focused frameworks.
- **Sector Standards** / GRI is developing sector-specific standards (oil & gas, agriculture, mining, etc.) that supplement the universal standards. / **Consequence**: Sector standards introduce additional mandatory disclosures for organizations in covered sectors reporting under GRI.

### SASB (Sustainability Accounting Standards Board)

- **Industry-Specific Metrics** / SASB provides disclosure topics and metrics for **77 industries** grouped into **11 sectors**, focused on financially material sustainability issues. / **Consequence**: SASB metrics are designed for investor decision-making; now consolidated under the IFRS Foundation (ISSB), SASB standards continue as industry-specific guidance feeding into IFRS S1/S2.
- **Financial Materiality** / SASB applies a financial materiality lens — only issues reasonably likely to affect a company's financial condition or operating performance. / **Consequence**: Narrower than GRI's impact materiality; a company reporting under both SASB and GRI may need to disclose on different topics for the same subject area.

### TCFD / ISSB

- **Four Pillars** / TCFD framework (now subsumed into ISSB): Governance, Strategy, Risk Management, Metrics and Targets. / **Consequence**: TCFD has been adopted as the basis for mandatory reporting in the UK, EU (CSRD), Japan, Singapore, and California (SB 261). TCFD recommendations formally transferred to ISSB as of **October 2023**.
- **Scenario Analysis** / TCFD recommends climate scenario analysis (at minimum, a 2 degrees C or below scenario) to assess resilience of strategy. / **Consequence**: Scenario analysis is now mandatory under CSRD (ESRS E1) and California SB 261; SEC rules require qualitative discussion of scenario analysis if used internally.
- **ISSB Adoption Timeline** / IFRS S1 and S2 effective for periods beginning **January 1, 2024**; jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction adoption varies. / **Consequence**: Companies operating in multiple jurisdictions may face overlapping ISSB, CSRD, and SEC requirements with different scopes and timelines; convergence is incomplete.

### CSRD Double Materiality

- **Assessment Process** / Companies must conduct a double materiality assessment identifying topics that are material from both financial (outside-in) and impact (inside-out) perspectives. / **Consequence**: The double materiality assessment must be documented and forms the basis for determining which ESRS topics require disclosure; auditors will review the assessment methodology.
- **Mandatory Standards** / ESRS includes **12 standards** covering environment (5), social (4), and governance (1), plus cross-cutting standards (2). All are mandatory subject to materiality assessment. / **Consequence**: Even topics assessed as not material must include an explanation of why they were excluded; the "rebuttable presumption" for climate (ESRS E1) means climate is always material unless the company can prove otherwise.
- **Value Chain Scope** / CSRD requires reporting on the entire value chain, not just the reporting entity's own operations. / **Consequence**: Companies must collect data from suppliers, customers, and other value chain partners; **3-year transitional relief** for value chain data using estimates and sector averages.

### Mandatory vs. Voluntary Reporting

- **Mandatory Regimes** / SEC (U.S. public companies), CSRD (EU large companies + listed SMEs), California SB 253/261, UK Sustainability Disclosure Requirements. / **Consequence**: Mandatory disclosure carries regulatory enforcement, auditor attestation requirements, and securities liability for misstatements.
- **Voluntary Commitments** / Voluntary ESG reports, CDP disclosures, Science-Based Targets initiative (SBTi) commitments, RE100 pledges. / **Consequence**: Voluntary commitments can become legally binding through incorporation into contracts, reasonable investor reliance, or FTC/SEC enforcement if claims are materially misleading. **Once published, withdrawal creates its own reputational and legal risk.**
- **UN Global Compact / PRI** / Voluntary frameworks requiring annual communication on progress; PRI signatories must meet minimum reporting requirements. / **Consequence**: Failure to meet minimum requirements results in delisting (PRI has delisted **over 400 signatories** since 2020); delisting signals governance failure to institutional investors.

### Assurance Requirements

- **Limited vs. Reasonable Assurance** / Limited assurance (negative assurance — "nothing has come to our attention") vs. reasonable assurance (positive assurance — "in our opinion, the information is materially correct"). / **Consequence**: CSRD requires limited assurance initially, moving to reasonable assurance by **2028**. SEC climate rules require limited assurance progressing to reasonable assurance for LAFs. California SB 253 follows a similar phase-in.
- **Assurance Provider Qualifications** / CSRD: statutory auditors or accredited independent assurance services providers. SEC: registered public accounting firms or qualified attestation providers. / **Consequence**: Assurance market capacity is currently constrained; companies should engage assurance providers early to secure capacity and establish baseline processes.

### Greenwashing Enforcement

- **SEC Enforcement** / SEC has brought enforcement actions against investment advisers and companies for misleading ESG claims (e.g., DWS/BaFin investigation, Goldman ESG fund settlement). / **Consequence**: SEC penalties for ESG misrepresentation range from **$1.5 million to $19 million** in recent settlements; ESG task force within the Division of Enforcement is active.
- **EU Green Claims Directive** / Proposed directive requiring substantiation of environmental claims with recognized scientific evidence and lifecycle assessments before making public claims. / **Consequence**: Once adopted, blanket claims like "eco-friendly" or "sustainable" will require specific, verifiable substantiation; penalties determined by member states.
- **FTC Enforcement** / FTC pursues deceptive environmental marketing claims under Section 5 of the FTC Act, guided by the Green Guides. / **Consequence**: FTC enforcement includes consent orders, civil penalties for violation of consent orders (up to **$50,120 per violation**), and corrective advertising requirements.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Securities** — ESG disclosures in SEC filings, materiality determinations, safe harbor applicability
- **Consumer Protection** — Greenwashing claims in marketing, FTC Green Guides compliance
- **Data Privacy** — ESG data collection from employees and supply chain partners
- **Corporate** — Board ESG oversight, fiduciary duties, stakeholder engagement
- **Climate Disclosure** — ESG reporting frameworks feed into mandatory climate disclosure regimes

## Sustainability Representations

# Sustainability Representations

## Applicability

Applies when a company makes environmental or sustainability claims in marketing, advertising, product labeling, contracts, or public communications. Covers FTC Green Guides, EU Green Claims Directive, contractual sustainability warranties, and the legal risk of unsubstantiated environmental claims (greenwashing).

## Key Requirements

### FTC Green Guides

- **General Environmental Claims** / Unqualified claims like "eco-friendly," "green," or "environmentally safe" convey that the product has no negative environmental impact. / **Consequence**: Such broad claims are almost impossible to substantiate and are presumptively deceptive under the Green Guides. FTC enforcement results in consent orders requiring cessation, corrective advertising, and civil penalties up to **$50,120 per violation** of a consent order.
- **Carbon Neutral / Net Zero Claims** / Claims of carbon neutrality must be substantiated with reliable offset credits and transparent methodology. / **Consequence**: FTC is revising the Green Guides (expected **2025-2026**) to specifically address carbon offset and net-zero claims. Current guidance requires that offsets be real, additional, permanent, and verified. Unsubstantiated carbon neutral claims have triggered FTC investigations and state AG enforcement.
- **Recyclable Claims** / A product may only be labeled "recyclable" if it can be collected, separated, and recovered through an established recycling program available to a **substantial majority** (at least **60%**) of consumers or communities where the product is sold. / **Consequence**: If recycling access is limited, the claim must be qualified (e.g., "recyclable in communities where facilities exist"); unqualified claims when access is below 60% are deceptive.
- **Compostable Claims** / Product must completely break down into usable compost in a safe and timely manner in an appropriate composting facility or home compost. / **Consequence**: If the product requires industrial composting, the claim must be qualified; consumers assume "compostable" means home-compostable. Misleading compostable claims face the same enforcement as other deceptive environmental claims.
- **Made with Renewable Materials / Renewable Energy** / Claims must specify the percentage and type of renewable material or energy. / **Consequence**: "Made with renewable energy" when only **10%** of energy is renewable is deceptive without qualification; must disclose the actual percentage.
- **Degradable Claims** / Product must completely decompose within **1 year** after customary disposal. / **Consequence**: Landfill conditions generally prevent degradation; "degradable" claims for products typically disposed in landfills are deceptive.

### EU Green Claims Directive (Proposed)

- **Substantiation Requirement** / Before making any environmental claim, companies must conduct a science-based assessment using recognized methodologies (e.g., PEF, OEF). / **Consequence**: Claims must be verified by an accredited third-party verifier before publication; unsubstantiated claims are prohibited. Penalties set by member states, expected to include fines of at least **4% of annual turnover**.
- **Comparative Claims** / Environmental comparisons must use equivalent methodologies, cover the same lifecycle stages, and use up-to-date data. / **Consequence**: Selective comparisons (e.g., comparing only one environmental attribute while ignoring others where the product performs worse) are prohibited.
- **Offset-Based Claims** / Claims like "climate neutral" or "carbon compensated" based solely on carbon offsets are effectively prohibited. / **Consequence**: Companies cannot use offsets to claim environmental neutrality; offsets must be supplementary to actual emissions reductions, and this must be clearly communicated.
- **Sustainability Labels** / Only labels based on official certification schemes or established by public authorities will be permitted; new private label schemes require government approval. / **Consequence**: Proliferation of private eco-labels will be curtailed; existing labels must meet transparency and third-party verification requirements.

### Substantiation Requirements

- **Competent and Reliable Scientific Evidence** / FTC standard: tests, analyses, research, or studies conducted and evaluated by qualified professionals using accepted methods. / **Consequence**: Anecdotal evidence, internal assessments without methodology, and outdated studies do not constitute adequate substantiation; the burden of proof is on the claim-maker.
- **Lifecycle Assessment (LCA)** / Comprehensive environmental assessment from raw material extraction through disposal. ISO 14040/14044 standards. / **Consequence**: Full LCA is the gold standard for substantiation but costs **$50,000-$200,000** and takes **6-12 months**; streamlined LCA may be acceptable for specific claims.
- **Pre-Claim Substantiation** / Substantiation must exist BEFORE the claim is made, not developed after an enforcement inquiry. / **Consequence**: Retroactive substantiation is a red flag for enforcers; companies should maintain a substantiation file for every environmental claim.

### Contract Clauses — Sustainability Representations

- **Supplier Sustainability Warranties** / Contractual representations that products meet specified environmental standards (carbon footprint, recycled content, chemical restrictions). / **Consequence**: Breach of sustainability warranty creates contractual liability; damages may include cost of recall, replacement, regulatory penalties, and reputational harm. Warranty survival periods of **2-3 years** are typical.
- **Supply Chain Compliance** / Representations that the supply chain complies with specified environmental and social standards (e.g., no deforestation, fair labor). / **Consequence**: EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) will require companies to conduct and disclose supply chain due diligence; contractual flow-down provisions are essential.
- **Environmental Indemnity for Claims** / Indemnification from supplier if sustainability representations prove false and buyer faces regulatory action or third-party claims. / **Consequence**: Indemnity should cover defense costs, penalties, corrective advertising, and consequential damages (product recall, lost sales); caps should be sized to the exposure.
- **Audit and Verification Rights** / Buyer right to audit supplier's sustainability claims and supporting documentation, typically with **30 days** notice. / **Consequence**: Without audit rights, sustainability representations are unverifiable; audit rights should include access to sub-suppliers for supply chain claims.

### Enforcement Trends

- **FTC Actions** / FTC has increased greenwashing enforcement, targeting both large corporations and smaller companies making unsubstantiated claims. / **Consequence**: Recent actions include cases involving plastic recyclability claims, "bamboo" textile labeling (actually rayon), and biodegradable claims for products disposed in landfills.
- **State AG Actions** / State attorneys general, particularly in New York, California, and Massachusetts, actively pursue greenwashing under state consumer protection statutes. / **Consequence**: State actions can proceed in parallel with FTC enforcement; state penalties can be substantial (New York: up to **$5,000 per violation** under GBL Section 350).
- **Class Action Litigation** / Private plaintiffs increasingly file class actions alleging deceptive environmental marketing under state consumer protection statutes and common law fraud. / **Consequence**: Class action settlements for greenwashing claims have ranged from **$5 million to $50 million+**; even unsuccessful claims impose significant defense costs.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection** — Greenwashing enforcement under FTC Act and state UDAP statutes
- **ESG Reporting** — Sustainability claims in reports must be consistent with marketing claims
- **Climate Disclosure** — Carbon neutral claims must align with formal emissions disclosures
- **IP and Technology** — Sustainability certifications and eco-label trademarks
- **International Trade** — EU Green Claims Directive and Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) affect imported goods
