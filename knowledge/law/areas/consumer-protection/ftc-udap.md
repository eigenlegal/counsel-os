# FTC Act Section 5 and UDAP

## Overview
Section 5 of the Federal Trade Commission Act (15 U.S.C. 45) prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce. The FTC enforces this provision through administrative proceedings, consent orders, and civil penalty actions, and has developed extensive guidance through enforcement actions, policy statements, and industry guides.

## Key Requirements
- **Deception standard:** A representation, omission, or practice is deceptive if it is likely to mislead consumers acting reasonably under the circumstances, and the representation, omission, or practice is material (i.e., likely to affect the consumer's conduct or decision regarding a product or service).
- **Unfairness standard:** An act or practice is unfair if it causes or is likely to cause substantial injury to consumers, is not reasonably avoidable by consumers, and is not outweighed by countervailing benefits to consumers or competition.
- **Advertising substantiation:** Advertisers must have a reasonable basis for objective claims before disseminating them. Health and safety claims require competent and reliable scientific evidence.
- **Endorsement and testimonial guidelines (16 C.F.R. Part 255):** Material connections between endorsers and advertisers must be clearly disclosed. Endorsements must reflect honest opinions and typical consumer experience or clearly disclose atypical results.
- **Native advertising and influencer marketing:** Sponsored content must be clearly identifiable as advertising. Influencer posts require conspicuous disclosure of material relationships (#ad, #sponsored).
- **Made in USA claims:** Products advertised as "Made in USA" must be "all or virtually all" made in the US. Qualified claims must be truthful and substantiated. FTC Made in USA Rule (16 C.F.R. Part 323) requires country-of-origin markings.
- **Negative option marketing (FTC Negative Option Rule, ROSCA, proposed Click-to-Cancel rule):** Pre-sale disclosures of material terms, informed affirmative consent, and simple cancellation mechanisms are required.
- **Health and environmental claims:** Green Guides (16 C.F.R. Part 260) govern environmental marketing claims. Health claims require substantiation. COVID-19 and similar health emergency claims are subject to heightened enforcement.
- **Dark patterns:** The FTC has increasingly targeted manipulative design practices that trick or trap consumers into unintended actions, including misleading interfaces, hidden charges, and obstruction of cancellation.

## Common Contract Issues
- Marketing and advertising agreements that fail to require substantiation of claims or compliance with FTC guides.
- Influencer and affiliate agreements lacking adequate disclosure requirements or compliance monitoring provisions.
- Terms of service containing misleading descriptions of product capabilities, pricing, or limitations.
- Subscription and billing terms that obscure material costs or make cancellation unduly burdensome.
- Warranty and guarantee representations in marketing materials that conflict with contractual warranty limitations.
- Indemnification provisions in advertising and marketing vendor agreements that allocate FTC enforcement risk.
- Data collection and use practices described in privacy policies that do not match actual practices (FTC treats this as deceptive).

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **Data Privacy:** The FTC actively enforces data privacy and security practices under Section 5 unfairness and deception authority, overlapping with CCPA and state privacy law requirements.
- **Consumer Protection (State):** State UDAP statutes often provide broader protections, private rights of action, and treble damages not available under the FTC Act (which has no private right of action).
- **Consumer Protection (Auto-Renewal):** ROSCA and FTC negative option rules directly govern subscription and auto-renewal practices.
- **IP and Technology:** FTC Endorsement Guides apply to technology reviews, app store descriptions, and SaaS marketing claims.
- **Securities:** Advertising and marketing claims about investment products or financial performance may trigger both FTC and SEC scrutiny.
