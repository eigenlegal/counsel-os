# Subpoenas

## Overview
Subpoenas are court-ordered directives requiring the production of documents (subpoena duces tecum), testimony (subpoena ad testificandum), or both. Subpoenas may be issued in civil litigation, criminal proceedings, arbitration, or regulatory investigations. Proper response requires prompt analysis of obligations, objections, and privilege issues within strict compliance deadlines.

## Key Requirements
- **Types of subpoenas:** Subpoena ad testificandum (compels testimony at a deposition, hearing, or trial), subpoena duces tecum (compels production of documents or tangible things), and combined subpoenas (testimony + documents). Federal subpoenas under FRCP Rule 45; state subpoenas under applicable state procedural rules.
- **Service and jurisdiction:** Federal subpoenas may be served anywhere in the United States (FRCP Rule 45(b)(2)). State court subpoena reach varies by state. Subpoenas must be properly served; defective service may be grounds for non-compliance. Personal service is generally required for testimony subpoenas.
- **Compliance timeline:** Federal subpoenas require production on the date specified (must allow reasonable time). Objections to federal subpoenas must be served in writing before the earlier of the compliance date or 14 days after service (FRCP Rule 45(d)(2)(B)). State timelines vary. Immediate action upon receipt is essential.
- **Objections and motions to quash/modify:** Grounds for objection include: undue burden or expense, privilege (attorney-client, work product, trade secrets), overbreadth, lack of relevance, improper service, compliance would require travel beyond 100 miles (FRCP Rule 45), and conflict with protective orders. Motion to quash or modify must be filed with the issuing court before the compliance deadline.
- **Privilege review:** All responsive documents must be reviewed for privileged material before production. Privilege logs must be prepared identifying withheld documents by date, author, recipient, subject matter, and privilege claimed. FRE 502(d) orders and claw-back agreements provide protection against inadvertent privilege waiver.
- **Third-party subpoenas (non-party compliance):** Non-parties may be subpoenaed for documents and testimony. Non-parties are entitled to heightened protection from burden and may recover reasonable costs of compliance. Non-parties should notify their customers/clients if subpoenaed for their records (unless prohibited by court order or applicable law).
- **Protective orders:** Confidential information produced in response to subpoenas should be subject to a protective order limiting use and disclosure. Negotiate a stipulated protective order or seek court-ordered protection before producing sensitive materials.
- **Criminal and grand jury subpoenas:** Criminal subpoenas and grand jury subpoenas carry unique obligations and constraints. Grand jury proceedings are secret (FRCP Criminal Rule 6(e)). Fifth Amendment privilege against self-incrimination may apply. Immediate engagement of experienced criminal counsel is essential.

## Common Contract Issues
- Confidentiality agreements that do not address compelled disclosure — standard NDAs should include a carve-out permitting disclosure pursuant to subpoena or court order, with notice to the disclosing party and efforts to obtain a protective order.
- Contracts with third-party document retention and production obligations that are relevant when the company is subpoenaed for records it does not directly hold.
- Indemnification provisions that cover costs of responding to third-party subpoenas related to the indemnified party's conduct.
- Data processing agreements that address the processor's obligations when it receives a subpoena for the controller's data.
- Vendor agreements that require the vendor to notify the customer promptly upon receiving a subpoena for the customer's data (consistent with applicable law and court orders).
- Arbitration agreements with provisions for subpoena authority — arbitrators' subpoena power varies by jurisdiction and arbitration rules.

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **Litigation (Litigation Holds):** Receipt of a subpoena triggers an immediate obligation to preserve all potentially responsive materials.
- **Litigation (Regulatory Inquiries):** Regulatory subpoenas and civil investigative demands (CIDs) have distinct procedural requirements from judicial subpoenas.
- **Data Privacy:** Subpoenas for personal data may implicate data privacy obligations. GDPR and other privacy laws may restrict disclosure of personal data in response to foreign legal process.
- **IP and Technology (Trade Secrets):** Trade secrets produced in response to subpoenas must be protected by stipulated protective orders to maintain their trade secret status.
- **Employment:** Employee records subpoenaed in litigation require review for privacy-protected information and compliance with state employee privacy laws.
- **Insurance:** Subpoenas received in connection with insured matters should be tendered to insurers with notice of potential defense obligations.
