# US State Privacy Laws (Non-California)

## Overview
A growing number of US states have enacted comprehensive consumer privacy laws modeled on elements of the GDPR and CCPA. These laws vary in scope, consumer rights, business obligations, and enforcement mechanisms, creating a patchwork of requirements for companies operating across multiple states.

## Key Requirements
- **Virginia (VCDPA, effective Jan 2023):** Applies to entities conducting business in Virginia or targeting Virginia residents that process PI of 100,000+ consumers or 25,000+ consumers while deriving 50%+ revenue from PI sales. Provides rights to access, correct, delete, portability, and opt out of targeted advertising, sale, and profiling. Requires data protection assessments for targeted advertising, sale of PI, profiling, sensitive data processing, and any processing presenting heightened risk. No private right of action; AG enforcement only.
- **Colorado (CPA, effective Jul 2023):** Similar thresholds and rights to VCDPA. Requires honoring universal opt-out mechanisms. Data protection assessments required. AG and DA enforcement.
- **Connecticut (CTDPA, effective Jul 2023):** Similar to VCDPA with broader consent requirements for sensitive data. Must recognize universal opt-out mechanisms. AG enforcement only.
- **Utah (UCPA, effective Dec 2023):** Higher applicability threshold ($25M revenue AND meets consumer number thresholds). Narrower consumer rights (no right to correct). No data protection assessment requirement. AG enforcement only.
- **Texas (TDPSA, effective Jul 2024):** No revenue threshold — applies to any entity doing business in Texas that processes PI and is not a small business under SBA standards. Broad definition of "sale." AG enforcement with 30-day cure period.
- **Oregon (OCPA, effective Jul 2024):** Applies to entities doing business in Oregon or targeting Oregon residents. Includes non-profit organizations (unlike most state laws). No revenue threshold. AG enforcement.
- **Montana, Iowa, Indiana, Tennessee:** Each with varying effective dates, thresholds, and nuances on consumer rights and enforcement.
- **Emerging states:** New laws continue to be enacted; counsel must monitor legislative developments in states where the company operates or targets residents.
- **Common requirements across most states:** Right to access, delete, portability, opt out of targeted advertising and sale; consent for sensitive data; reasonable data security; processor contracts with restrictive terms.

## Common Contract Issues
- Vendor/processor agreements that satisfy only CCPA or GDPR but omit requirements specific to other state laws (e.g., data protection assessment cooperation, universal opt-out signal compliance).
- Failure to account for varying definitions of "sale" across states — some laws define sale more broadly than others, potentially capturing data sharing arrangements not considered a sale in California.
- Inconsistent cure period provisions — some state laws require a right-to-cure period before enforcement actions; contracts should reflect awareness of these timelines.
- Missing or inadequate sensitive data consent mechanisms — most state laws require opt-in consent for sensitive data, but categories of sensitive data vary by state.
- Advertising and analytics arrangements that trigger "targeted advertising" opt-out requirements under state laws even when structured to avoid CCPA "sharing."
- Sub-processor flow-down requirements vary; contracts must ensure compliance with the most restrictive applicable state law.

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **Data Privacy (CCPA/CPRA, GDPR):** Companies subject to multiple state laws plus CCPA and GDPR must build compliance programs that satisfy all simultaneously; contracts should be drafted to the most restrictive standard.
- **Consumer Protection:** State privacy laws supplement existing state consumer protection statutes; AG enforcement may draw on both privacy-specific and general consumer protection authority.
- **Employment:** Most state privacy laws exempt employee data or have delayed applicability to HR data; however, this varies by state and counsel must verify exemptions.
- **IP and Technology:** Technology vendor and SaaS agreements must be updated to reflect state-specific processor requirements, including data protection assessment obligations and universal opt-out signal support.
