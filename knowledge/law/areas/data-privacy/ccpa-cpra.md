# CCPA/CPRA (California Consumer Privacy Act / California Privacy Rights Act)

## Overview
The California Consumer Privacy Act (Cal. Civ. Code 1798.100 et seq.), as amended by the California Privacy Rights Act (Proposition 24, effective January 1, 2023), grants California residents broad rights over their personal information and imposes obligations on businesses that collect, sell, or share that information. Enforcement is handled by the California Attorney General and the California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA).

## Key Requirements
- **Applicability thresholds:** Applies to for-profit entities doing business in California that meet any of: (a) annual gross revenue exceeding $25 million, (b) annually buying, selling, or sharing personal information of 100,000+ California residents/households/devices, or (c) deriving 50%+ of annual revenue from selling or sharing personal information.
- **Right to know/access:** Consumers can request disclosure of specific pieces and categories of personal information collected, sources, purposes, and third parties with whom information is shared.
- **Right to delete:** Consumers can request deletion of personal information, subject to enumerated exceptions.
- **Right to opt out of sale/sharing:** Businesses must provide a "Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information" link and honor opt-out requests, including signals like Global Privacy Control (GPC).
- **Right to correct:** Consumers can request correction of inaccurate personal information (CPRA addition).
- **Right to limit use of sensitive personal information:** Consumers can direct businesses to limit use of sensitive PI to purposes necessary for providing requested goods/services (CPRA addition).
- **Service provider/contractor contracts:** Written agreements required with service providers and contractors that restrict use of personal information to specified business purposes and prohibit selling/sharing.
- **Privacy notice requirements:** Must disclose categories of PI collected, purposes, categories sold/shared, and retention periods at or before the point of collection.
- **Data minimization:** Collection, use, retention, and sharing must be reasonably necessary and proportionate to disclosed purposes (CPRA addition).
- **No discrimination:** Businesses cannot discriminate against consumers who exercise their privacy rights, though financial incentive programs are permitted with notice and consent.

## Common Contract Issues
- Service provider agreements lacking CCPA-required restrictive language — contracts must explicitly restrict the service provider from retaining, using, or disclosing personal information beyond the contracted services.
- Failure to distinguish between "sale," "sharing," and "disclosure for a business purpose" — each has different legal requirements and contractual implications.
- Missing contractor certification language under CPRA — contractors must certify they understand and will comply with CCPA/CPRA restrictions.
- Inadequate flow-down provisions for sub-service-providers and subcontractors.
- Data retention provisions that lack specific timeframes or criteria, violating CPRA's retention period disclosure requirement.
- Advertising and analytics arrangements that constitute "sharing" for cross-context behavioral advertising without appropriate disclosures and opt-out mechanisms.
- Indemnification gaps for regulatory enforcement actions and private right of action claims (limited to data breaches involving unencrypted/unredacted PI due to failure to maintain reasonable security).
- Audit and compliance verification rights — necessary for demonstrating compliance but often resisted by service providers.

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **Data Privacy (GDPR):** Companies subject to both GDPR and CCPA/CPRA must harmonize data processing agreements, privacy notices, and rights fulfillment processes; GDPR's DPA and CCPA's service provider agreement can be combined but must satisfy both regimes.
- **Consumer Protection:** CCPA's private right of action for data breaches intersects with broader consumer protection statutes; FTC enforcement on data security overlaps with CCPA reasonable security requirements.
- **Employment:** CCPA applies to employee and job applicant personal information (post-exemption expiration on January 1, 2023); HR data handling must comply with notice, access, and deletion rights.
- **IP and Technology:** SaaS and technology vendor agreements must address CCPA service provider requirements, including restrictions on using customer data for product improvement or model training.
- **Financial Services:** GLBA-covered financial institutions are partially exempt from CCPA, but the exemption applies at the data level, not entity level, requiring careful analysis.
