# GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

## Overview
The EU General Data Protection Regulation (Regulation 2016/679) is the primary data protection law governing the processing of personal data of individuals in the European Economic Area. It applies to any organization worldwide that processes EU/EEA residents' personal data, regardless of where the organization is established.

## Key Requirements
- **Lawful basis required:** Every processing activity must have one of six lawful bases: consent, contract performance, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, or legitimate interests (Article 6).
- **Data Processing Agreements:** Controllers must have written DPAs with all processors, including mandatory clauses per Article 28(3): subject matter/duration, nature/purpose of processing, type of personal data, categories of data subjects, and controller obligations/rights.
- **Data Protection Impact Assessments:** Required for processing likely to result in high risk to individuals, including systematic profiling, large-scale processing of special categories, and large-scale public monitoring (Article 35).
- **Data breach notification:** Controllers must notify supervisory authorities within 72 hours of becoming aware of a personal data breach, and affected data subjects without undue delay when the breach poses high risk (Articles 33-34).
- **Data subject rights:** Right of access (Art. 15), rectification (Art. 16), erasure (Art. 17), restriction (Art. 18), portability (Art. 20), objection (Art. 21), and rights related to automated decision-making (Art. 22).
- **Cross-border transfers:** Transfers outside the EEA require an adequacy decision, appropriate safeguards (SCCs, BCRs), or a derogation under Article 49.
- **Data Protection Officer:** Required for public authorities, organizations conducting regular and systematic monitoring at scale, or large-scale processing of special categories (Article 37).
- **Records of processing activities:** Controllers with 250+ employees (or engaging in non-occasional processing) must maintain written records (Article 30).
- **Privacy by design and by default:** Data protection must be integrated into processing activities from the design stage (Article 25).

## Common Contract Issues
- Missing or incomplete DPA clauses — Article 28(3) requires specific mandatory provisions; omitting any is non-compliant.
- Vague descriptions of processing activities — the DPA must specify the subject matter, duration, nature, purpose, data types, and data subject categories.
- Sub-processor controls — contracts must require prior written authorization for sub-processors and impose equivalent data protection obligations.
- Cross-border transfer mechanisms — Standard Contractual Clauses must be the correct module (controller-to-processor, controller-to-controller, processor-to-processor, or processor-to-controller) and may require supplementary measures per Schrems II.
- Audit rights — controllers must have the right to audit processor compliance; contracts that eliminate or unreasonably restrict this right are problematic.
- Data retention clauses that conflict with storage limitation principle (Article 5(1)(e)).
- Indemnification and liability allocation for data breaches and regulatory fines.
- Lack of clear data return/deletion obligations at contract termination.

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **Consumer Protection:** GDPR consent and transparency requirements overlap with unfair commercial practices when consumer data is involved in marketing or sales.
- **Employment:** Employee data processing triggers GDPR obligations, including lawful basis for HR processing, employee monitoring rules, and works council consultation requirements in some member states.
- **IP and Technology:** SaaS and technology agreements almost always involve personal data processing, requiring DPA attachments and technical security measures.
- **International Trade:** Data localization requirements and cross-border transfer restrictions may conflict with data flow provisions in trade agreements.
- **Financial Services:** GDPR interacts with financial services regulations (PSD2, AML directives) where both regimes apply to the same data.
