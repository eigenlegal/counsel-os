# Securities Registration Exemptions

## Overview
The Securities Act of 1933 requires registration of all securities offerings unless an exemption applies. Most startup and private company fundraising relies on Regulation D exemptions, particularly Rule 506(b) and Rule 506(c). Other exemptions include Regulation A, Regulation CF (crowdfunding), Regulation S (offshore), and Section 4(a)(2) (private placement). Proper reliance on an exemption requires strict compliance with its conditions.

## Key Requirements
- **Rule 506(b) (most common for startups):** No limit on capital raised. Up to 35 non-accredited investors (but practically used only with accredited investors to avoid information delivery requirements). No general solicitation or advertising permitted. Accredited investor status may be based on self-certification/questionnaire. SEC Form D filing required. State blue sky filings required but states cannot impose merit review (federal preemption under NSMIA).
- **Rule 506(c) (general solicitation permitted):** No limit on capital raised. ALL investors must be accredited investors. General solicitation and advertising are permitted. Issuer must take "reasonable steps to verify" accredited investor status — cannot rely solely on self-certification. Acceptable verification methods include reviewing tax returns, bank statements, or obtaining written confirmation from a registered broker-dealer, attorney, or CPA.
- **Regulation A (Mini-IPO):** Tier 1: up to $20M in a 12-month period, with state blue sky compliance required. Tier 2: up to $75M in a 12-month period, with state blue sky preemption but ongoing SEC reporting obligations (annual, semiannual, current reports). Both tiers require SEC qualification of an offering statement. Available to US and Canadian companies.
- **Regulation CF (crowdfunding):** Up to $5M in a 12-month period. Must be conducted through a registered funding portal or broker-dealer. Individual investment limits based on income/net worth (greater of $2,500 or 5% of income/net worth if either is under $124K; 10% of lesser of income or net worth if both exceed $124K). Annual report filing required.
- **Regulation S (offshore offerings):** Exempts offers and sales made outside the United States to non-US persons. No directed selling efforts in the US. Category 1 (non-reporting foreign issuers), Category 2, and Category 3 (US reporting issuers) have different distribution compliance period requirements (40 days to 1 year).
- **Rule 701 (compensatory issuances):** Exempts securities issued under written compensatory plans to employees, directors, consultants, and advisors. Limited to the greater of $1M, 15% of total assets, or 15% of outstanding securities in a 12-month period. Enhanced disclosure required if aggregate sales exceed $10M.
- **Section 4(a)(2) (statutory private placement):** The statutory exemption for "transactions by an issuer not involving any public offering." No specific safe harbor requirements, but relies on common-law factors (number and sophistication of offerees, manner of offering, availability of information). Regulation D provides a safe harbor under this section.
- **Integration doctrine:** The SEC may integrate multiple offerings conducted close in time if they are considered part of a single plan of financing, potentially disqualifying exemption reliance. Rule 152 provides safe harbors from integration for offerings more than 30 days apart and for certain concurrent offerings.

## Common Contract Issues
- Subscription agreements that fail to include adequate accredited investor representations and warranties, or that do not require information sufficient for Rule 506(c) verification.
- Convertible notes and SAFEs that do not address securities law compliance at conversion — the conversion event is a separate securities issuance requiring its own exemption analysis.
- Offering materials containing material misstatements or omissions that expose the issuer to anti-fraud liability regardless of exemption compliance.
- Failure to file Form D within the 15-day deadline or to make required state blue sky notice filings.
- General solicitation activities (pitch events, social media posts, website announcements) that disqualify Rule 506(b) reliance.
- Transfer restriction provisions and legends that are missing, inadequate, or inconsistent with applicable holding period requirements.
- Side letters or special arrangements with individual investors that create different terms potentially requiring separate exemption analysis.
- Finder's fee arrangements with unregistered broker-dealers that may violate broker-dealer registration requirements and jeopardize the exemption.

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **Securities (Equity Issuance):** Equity compensation plans must comply with Rule 701 or another exemption; options and RSUs are securities subject to registration or exemption requirements.
- **Corporate (M&A):** Equity issued as acquisition consideration must be registered or exempt. Rollover equity arrangements in M&A transactions require securities analysis.
- **Corporate (Governance):** Investor rights negotiated in exempt offerings (board seats, protective provisions, information rights) create ongoing governance obligations.
- **Employment:** Equity compensation issued under Rule 701 must comply with plan requirements, Form S-8 considerations for public companies, and Section 409A for deferred compensation.
- **Financial Services:** Finder's fee and broker-dealer registration issues arise frequently in private placements; compliance with Investment Company Act exclusions may be necessary for certain fund-like structures.
- **International Trade:** Regulation S offerings require careful analysis of selling restrictions, distribution compliance periods, and coordination with local securities laws in the jurisdictions where offerings are made.
