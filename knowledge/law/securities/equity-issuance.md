# Equity Compensation and Capitalization

## Overview
Equity compensation is a critical tool for attracting, retaining, and incentivizing employees, directors, and consultants, particularly in startups and growth-stage companies. Proper structuring requires navigating securities law, tax law (including Section 409A), corporate law, and accounting considerations. Capitalization management ensures accurate tracking of ownership and dilution.

## Key Requirements
- **Stock option plans:** Equity incentive plans (EIPs) authorize the board to grant stock options, restricted stock, RSUs, and other equity awards. Plans must be approved by the board and typically by stockholders. Plans define the option pool size, eligible participants, types of awards, vesting schedules, exercise procedures, and plan administration.
- **Incentive Stock Options (ISOs, Section 422):** Favorable tax treatment for employees (no taxable income at exercise if holding period requirements are met). $100K annual vesting limit. Must be granted at or above FMV. 10-year maximum term. Only available to employees (not contractors or directors). Company does not receive a tax deduction.
- **Non-Qualified Stock Options (NQSOs):** Taxable as ordinary income at exercise (spread between exercise price and FMV). Available to employees, contractors, and directors. No $100K limit. Company receives a tax deduction equal to the employee's income.
- **Section 409A compliance:** Stock options with an exercise price below FMV on the grant date are treated as deferred compensation subject to Section 409A, resulting in immediate taxation, a 20% additional tax, and interest penalty. Requires a "409A valuation" — an independent appraisal of FMV or use of safe harbor methods (qualified independent appraisal, illiquid startup formula).
- **409A valuations:** Must be performed by a qualified appraiser using reasonable valuation methods. Valuations are generally valid for 12 months unless a material event occurs (new funding round, significant revenue change, M&A activity). Common methods include market approach (comparable companies), income approach (DCF), and asset approach.
- **Restricted Stock Awards (RSAs):** Actual shares issued subject to vesting and repurchase rights. Section 83(b) election allows recipient to pay tax on the grant date value (before appreciation), converting future gains to capital gains. Election must be filed with the IRS within 30 days of grant — this deadline is absolute and cannot be extended.
- **Restricted Stock Units (RSUs):** A promise to deliver shares upon vesting. No shares issued until vesting (or later settlement date). Taxed as ordinary income at delivery. Subject to Section 409A unless settled within the "short-term deferral" period (by March 15 of the year following vesting). Common in later-stage and public companies due to no exercise price and no Section 83(b) election issues.
- **QSBS (Qualified Small Business Stock, Section 1202):** Stockholders who hold QSBS for more than 5 years may exclude up to $10M (or 10x basis) in capital gains from federal taxation. Requirements: C corporation, aggregate gross assets not exceeding $50M at issuance, active business requirement (80%+ of assets used in qualified trades or businesses), and certain excluded industries (professional services, banking, hospitality).
- **Cap table management:** Accurate tracking of all outstanding equity (common stock, preferred stock, options, warrants, convertible notes, SAFEs) on a fully diluted basis. Critical for financing rounds, M&A, and corporate governance decisions. Must account for all conversions, exercises, forfeitures, and transfers.
- **Rule 701 compliance:** Compensatory equity issuances must comply with Rule 701 limits (greater of $1M, 15% of total assets, or 15% of outstanding in 12 months). Enhanced disclosure required above $10M threshold.

## Common Contract Issues
- Equity incentive plans with insufficient share reserves, requiring frequent stockholder approval for increases.
- Option agreements with exercise prices below 409A FMV, creating deferred compensation tax exposure for the recipient.
- Missing or late Section 83(b) elections for restricted stock grants — the 30-day deadline is a hard deadline with no remedy for missed filings.
- Vesting schedules that do not address acceleration upon termination or change of control (single trigger vs. double trigger).
- Repurchase rights on unvested shares that fail to specify exercise mechanics, timing, and price (cost vs. FMV).
- Clawback provisions that are overly broad or conflict with state law limitations.
- Failure to update 409A valuations after material events, creating risk that subsequent option grants are mispriced.
- Convertible note and SAFE conversion mechanics that do not clearly specify pre-money vs. post-money treatment of the option pool.
- Cap table errors caused by failure to track option exercises, forfeitures, early exercises, and note/SAFE conversions.
- Transfer restrictions on equity awards that are inconsistent with the company's charter, stockholders agreement, or applicable securities laws.

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **Securities (Exemptions):** All equity issuances are securities transactions requiring registration or exemption analysis (typically Rule 701 for compensatory issuances, Regulation D for investment rounds).
- **Corporate (Governance):** Board and stockholder approval is required for equity incentive plans. Protective provisions in investor agreements may give investors a veto over option pool increases.
- **Corporate (M&A):** Treatment of outstanding options, RSUs, and other equity awards in M&A transactions (assumption, substitution, acceleration, cash-out) is a heavily negotiated deal term.
- **Employment:** Equity compensation is a key component of offer letters and employment agreements. Acceleration provisions, post-termination exercise periods, and clawback terms must be coordinated with severance and termination provisions.
- **Data Privacy:** Cap table management platforms contain sensitive personal and financial information subject to data protection requirements.
- **Tax:** Section 409A, Section 83(b), ISO/NQSO treatment, QSBS eligibility, and state tax implications are integral to equity compensation structuring. International employees may trigger additional tax complexity.
