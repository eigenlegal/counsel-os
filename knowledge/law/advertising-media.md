## Advertising Standards

# Advertising Standards

## Applicability

Applies to all commercial advertising and promotional claims, including traditional media, digital advertising, social media, direct mail, packaging, and point-of-sale materials. Governed primarily by FTC Act Section 5 (unfair or deceptive acts or practices), Lanham Act Section 43(a) (false advertising between competitors), and state consumer protection statutes.

## Key Requirements

### Substantiation Doctrine (FTC Reasonable Basis)

- **What**: Advertisers must possess a "reasonable basis" for objective claims before making them; the level of substantiation required depends on the nature of the claim
- **Threshold/Timeline**: FTC Pfizer factors for determining adequate substantiation: (1) type of claim, (2) product type, (3) consequences of false claim, (4) benefits of truthful claim, (5) cost of developing substantiation, (6) amount of substantiation experts in the field would consider reasonable; health/safety claims generally require "competent and reliable scientific evidence" (at least one well-designed, controlled clinical study); establishment claims ("studies show...") require the specific level of proof stated
- **Consequence**: FTC enforcement includes cease and desist orders, civil penalties up to $50,120 per violation for knowing violations of FTC rules, monetary redress/disgorgement (AMG Capital Management v. FTC, 2021 limited FTC's Section 13(b) authority — Congress restored through new legislation); state AG enforcement under state UDAP statutes; competitor Lanham Act suits provide injunctive relief and damages

### Comparative Advertising (Lanham Act Section 43(a))

- **What**: Claims that explicitly or impliedly compare the advertiser's product/service to a competitor's; permissible if truthful and non-misleading, but subject to heightened scrutiny
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Two theories of liability: (1) literal falsity — claim is demonstrably false on its face (no consumer survey needed); (2) misleading — literally true but conveys false message to consumers (consumer survey evidence required to show deception); tests/surveys supporting comparative claims must be scientifically sound and representative; puffery defense available for vague, unverifiable claims of superiority ("best in class")
- **Consequence**: Lanham Act provides: injunctive relief (most common remedy), actual damages, defendant's profits, costs, and reasonable attorney fees in exceptional cases; no criminal liability; comparative advertising must not create false association or confusion with competitor's brand; use of competitor's trademark in comparative ads is permissible nominative fair use if truthful

### Native Advertising (Deceptive Format)

- **What**: Advertising formatted to resemble editorial content, news articles, social media posts, or other non-advertising content; must be clearly identifiable as advertising to avoid deception
- **Threshold/Timeline**: FTC Enforcement Policy Statement on Deceptively Formatted Advertising (2015) and Native Advertising Guide require disclosures that are "clear and conspicuous" — meaning: clear and unambiguous language, prominent placement, proximate to the claim, unavoidable by consumer; "Sponsored," "Ad," "Paid Advertisement" are acceptable labels; "Promoted" alone may be insufficient
- **Consequence**: FTC has brought enforcement actions against both advertisers and publishers/platforms for deceptive native advertising; Lord & Taylor (2016) was first FTC native advertising case (paid Instagram posts without disclosure); media companies have independent obligation to distinguish advertising from editorial content

### Testimonials and Endorsements

- **What**: Advertising featuring consumer experiences, expert opinions, or celebrity endorsements must reflect honest opinions, actual experience, and typical results
- **Threshold/Timeline**: FTC 2023 Endorsement Guides: endorsements must reflect endorser's genuine experience and current opinion; atypical results must be clearly disclosed — cannot use "results not typical" as safe harbor without disclosing typical results; material connections between endorser and advertiser must be disclosed; expert endorsements must be supported by actual expertise and adequate evaluation
- **Consequence**: Advertiser liable for endorser's claims if advertiser provided misleading information or knew/should have known claims were false; "results not typical" disclaimers insufficient — must disclose what consumers can generally expect; FTC may pursue both advertiser and endorser individually

### Made in USA Claims

- **What**: Products claimed to be "Made in USA" must be "all or virtually all" made in the United States under FTC's Enforcement Policy Statement; FTC Made in USA Rule (2021) codified this standard
- **Threshold/Timeline**: "All or virtually all" means: final assembly in the US, all significant processing in the US, all or virtually all ingredients/components from the US; qualified claims ("Made in USA with imported components") must be truthful and substantiated; FTC Made in USA Rule provides for civil penalties for unqualified "Made in USA" claims that fail the "all or virtually all" standard
- **Consequence**: Civil penalties up to $50,120 per violation under the Rule; FTC has brought numerous enforcement actions; state laws may impose additional requirements (California requires 100% domestic content for unqualified claims under Bus. & Prof. Code 17533.7); Customs marking requirements (19 U.S.C. 1304) are separate from FTC standards

### Environmental Marketing Claims (Green Guides)

- **What**: FTC Green Guides (16 CFR Part 260) address claims such as "recyclable," "biodegradable," "compostable," "carbon neutral," and "sustainable"
- **Threshold/Timeline**: "Recyclable" — available to substantial majority of consumers (60%+ access); "biodegradable" — complete decomposition within reasonably short period (1 year) after customary disposal; carbon offset claims must be specific about methodology and certification; general "eco-friendly" or "green" claims are likely deceptive because they are too vague to substantiate
- **Consequence**: FTC enforcement through consent orders and penalties; class action litigation risk under state consumer protection statutes; SEC climate disclosure rules (pending) create parallel obligations for public companies; California, New York, and other states have specific greenwashing laws with private rights of action

### Pricing Claims

- **What**: Representations about pricing, discounts, and savings must be truthful; former price comparisons, MSRP references, and percentage-off claims must reflect genuine prior prices or valid reference points
- **Threshold/Timeline**: FTC Guides Against Deceptive Pricing (16 CFR Part 233): former price must be genuine price at which item was openly and actively offered for a reasonably substantial period; "free" offers must not increase the regular price of the required purchase; MSRP comparisons valid only if MSRP is price at which product is generally sold in the area
- **Consequence**: Deceptive pricing claims subject to FTC enforcement, state AG actions, and class action litigation; particular enforcement focus on e-commerce dynamic pricing and artificial reference prices; California, New York, and other states have specific false advertising statutes with statutory damages

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **consumer-protection/**: FTC Act Section 5 is the primary federal authority for advertising enforcement; state UDAP statutes provide parallel remedies; auto-renewal and negative option rules apply to subscription advertising
- **influencer-disclosures/**: FTC 2023 Endorsement Guides apply to both traditional endorsements and influencer marketing; substantiation requirements apply to claims made through endorsers
- **ip-and-technology/**: Trademark law limits comparative advertising; copyright clearance required for third-party content in ads; trade dress claims may arise from look-and-feel similarities

## Content Licensing

# Content Licensing

## Applicability

Applies to agreements involving the licensing, distribution, and use of creative content including video, audio, images, text, music, and user-generated content. Covers rights grants, music licensing frameworks, talent/union requirements, and platform licensing structures.

## Key Requirements

### Rights Grants Structure

- **What**: Content licenses define the scope of permitted use through several key dimensions that must be clearly specified:
  - **Exclusivity**: Exclusive (licensee only), sole (licensor + one licensee), or non-exclusive (multiple licensees)
  - **Territory**: Geographic scope (worldwide, North America, specific countries)
  - **Term**: Duration (perpetual, fixed term, renewable)
  - **Media/Platform**: Channels of distribution (all media, digital only, specific platforms)
  - **Purpose**: Permitted uses (advertising, editorial, internal, resale)
- **Threshold/Timeline**: License scope should be negotiated before content creation; work-for-hire requires written agreement signed before creation (17 U.S.C. 101); assignment of copyright must be in writing (17 U.S.C. 204); exclusive licenses must be in writing to be enforceable; termination rights under 17 U.S.C. 203 allow authors to terminate grants after 35 years
- **Consequence**: Ambiguous rights grants construed against the drafter; use outside licensed scope constitutes copyright infringement; statutory damages for registered works: $750-$30,000 per work ($150,000 for willful infringement); moral rights (VARA, 17 U.S.C. 106A) apply to works of visual art

### Music Licensing Framework

- **What**: Music use requires clearing multiple rights depending on the type of use:
  - **Synchronization License**: Right to synchronize musical composition with visual media (negotiated directly with publisher or songwriter)
  - **Master Use License**: Right to use specific sound recording (negotiated with record label or recording owner)
  - **Mechanical License**: Right to reproduce and distribute musical composition in audio format (compulsory license available under 17 U.S.C. 115; administered through Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC) for digital uses post-Music Modernization Act 2018)
  - **Public Performance License**: Right to publicly perform musical composition (obtained through PROs: ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, GMR); blanket licenses cover repertoire
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Sync license fees range widely based on use (major label: $15K-$500K+ for advertising, less for indie/emerging); mechanical royalty rate set by Copyright Royalty Board (currently $0.12 per copy or $0.0231 per minute for physical/download); streaming mechanical rates set through CRB proceedings; public performance blanket license fees based on venue/audience size
- **Consequence**: Using music without proper licenses constitutes copyright infringement for both composition and recording; statutory damages per work infringed; PROs actively monitor and enforce; DMCA takedown for unauthorized digital use; Music Modernization Act (2018) created blanket mechanical license for digital music services through the MLC

### Union and Guild Requirements (SAG-AFTRA)

- **What**: SAG-AFTRA (Screen Actors Guild - American Federation of Television and Radio Artists) agreements govern terms for performers in covered productions including commercials, new media, television, and film
- **Threshold/Timeline**: SAG-AFTRA Commercials Contract: session fees (minimum $720.25/day for on-camera broadcast, 2024 rates); use fees/residuals based on media type and market; New Media Agreement covers content for internet/new media platforms — tiered based on budget (under/over $50K per episode); waiver process available for low-budget productions; 13-week maximum use cycle for broadcast commercials before renegotiation
- **Consequence**: Using SAG-AFTRA talent without signatory agreement subjects producer to Global Rule One enforcement; financial core (fi-core) performers may work non-union but lose membership benefits; non-union productions may face organizing campaigns; pension and health contribution obligations (current rate ~19% of gross compensation); late payment penalties

### User-Generated Content (UGC)

- **What**: Content created by users/consumers rather than the brand; use in commercial contexts requires appropriate rights clearance
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Platform terms of service typically grant platform a broad license but do not extend to third parties; brands must obtain separate permission from content creators; FTC disclosure requirements apply when UGC is solicited, incentivized, or curated by brand; DMCA safe harbor (17 U.S.C. 512) protects platforms that comply with notice-and-takedown but does not protect brands that affirmatively use infringing UGC
- **Consequence**: Using UGC without permission constitutes copyright infringement regardless of platform terms; right of publicity claims if individual's likeness used commercially; brands may be liable for UGC claims if they materially contributed to or directed the content; terms and conditions for UGC campaigns should include broad rights grant, representations, and indemnification

### Platform Licensing and Distribution

- **What**: Distribution agreements with digital platforms (streaming services, social media, app stores) involve platform-specific licensing terms, revenue sharing, and content requirements
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Major platform standard terms: YouTube (45/55 revenue share for Partner Program, $500 minimum payout); App Store/Google Play (70/30 revenue share, reduced to 85/15 for subscriptions after year 1 and small developers under $1M); Spotify (per-stream rates approximately $0.003-$0.005); most platforms require worldwide, non-exclusive license with broad sublicensing rights
- **Consequence**: Platform terms are generally non-negotiable for smaller creators; content removal risk for policy violations; platform algorithm changes may dramatically affect visibility/revenue; exclusivity requirements may limit multi-platform distribution; DMCA compliance obligations for platforms hosting third-party content

### Residuals and Reuse Fees

- **What**: Ongoing payments to talent, creators, or rights holders based on continued or expanded use of content beyond initial license scope
- **Threshold/Timeline**: SAG-AFTRA residuals calculated based on: media type, market, viewership/impressions; broadcast residuals decline over 13-week cycles; streaming residuals based on subscriber count tiers; music re-use requires fresh licenses if original term expires; stock footage/photography licenses may be royalty-free (one-time fee) or rights-managed (per-use fee)
- **Consequence**: Failure to pay residuals triggers union grievance process and potential strike authorization; unpaid residuals accrue interest; talent may have right to terminate license for non-payment; audit rights standard in residual-bearing agreements; residuals are a significant ongoing cost that must be budgeted

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **ip-and-technology/**: Copyright ownership, fair use defense, DMCA safe harbors, and trademark licensing intersect with content licensing; work-for-hire analysis critical for commissioned content
- **right-of-publicity/**: Content featuring identifiable individuals requires publicity rights clearance in addition to copyright licenses
- **employment/**: Talent classification (employee vs. independent contractor) affects ownership, union obligations, and tax treatment

## Defamation

# Defamation & Section 230

## Applicability

Applies to claims involving false statements of fact that damage reputation, including libel (written/published defamation), slander (spoken defamation), false light invasion of privacy, and related claims. Also covers Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act, which provides immunity for online platforms, and anti-SLAPP statutes that protect speech on matters of public concern.

## Key Requirements

### Elements of Defamation

- **What**: A plaintiff must prove: (1) a false statement of fact (not opinion), (2) published to a third party, (3) with the requisite degree of fault, and (4) damages (presumed for libel per se and slander per se in many jurisdictions; actual damages required otherwise)
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Statutes of limitations vary by state: typically 1-3 years from publication (1 year in CA, NY, TX; 2 years in IL, PA; 3 years in some states); single publication rule — statute runs from first publication (republication may restart); discovery rule may toll limitations in some jurisdictions; retraction demand may be a prerequisite to suit (approximately 33 states have retraction statutes)
- **Consequence**: Damages include compensatory (lost income, emotional distress, reputational harm), special damages (specific financial losses — required for slander per quod), and punitive damages (if actual malice proven); injunctions against speech are disfavored as prior restraints; per se categories (crime, loathsome disease, business unfitness, sexual misconduct) allow presumed damages without proof of specific harm

### Public Figure vs. Private Figure (Fault Standards)

- **What**: The First Amendment imposes different fault requirements depending on the plaintiff's public or private status:
  - **Public officials/figures**: Must prove "actual malice" — knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard for truth or falsity (New York Times v. Sullivan, 376 U.S. 254 (1964))
  - **Limited purpose public figures**: Actual malice required only for statements related to the particular controversy that made them a public figure
  - **Private figures**: Must prove at least negligence (Gertz v. Robert Welch, Inc., 418 U.S. 323 (1974)); states may impose higher standards; presumed/punitive damages still require actual malice proof
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Public figure status is a question of law for the court; factors: general fame/notoriety, voluntary injection into public controversy, access to media to counter claims; involuntary public figures are rare; corporations may be public figures for defamation purposes
- **Consequence**: Actual malice standard is extremely difficult to meet — plaintiff must show subjective awareness of probable falsity by clear and convincing evidence; summary judgment frequently granted to defendants in public figure cases; discovery into editorial processes and journalist state of mind is permitted (Herbert v. Lando)

### Defenses

- **What**: Key defenses to defamation claims:
  - **Truth**: Complete defense in all jurisdictions; defendant need not prove truth — plaintiff must prove falsity (for public figures; varies for private figures)
  - **Opinion**: Pure opinion is not actionable; the "totality of circumstances" test (Milkovich v. Lorain Journal, 1990) — a statement is opinion if it cannot be proven true or false; context matters (op-ed page vs. news story)
  - **Absolute Privilege**: Statements in judicial proceedings, legislative proceedings, executive communications, and spousal communications are absolutely privileged
  - **Qualified Privilege**: Good faith statements on matters of legitimate interest (employer references, credit reports, self-defense/defense of others); defeated by actual malice
  - **Fair Report Privilege**: Accurate reporting of official government proceedings, records, and reports
  - **Wire Service Defense**: Republication of wire service reports without knowledge of falsity (recognized in some jurisdictions)
  - **Neutral Reportage**: Newsworthy reporting of defamatory allegations by public figures against other public figures (limited recognition — not universally accepted)
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Privilege defenses are raised in motion to dismiss or summary judgment; burden on plaintiff to show abuse of privilege; qualified privilege lost upon showing of actual malice or excessive publication
- **Consequence**: Successful assertion of privilege results in dismissal; attorney fees may be recoverable under anti-SLAPP statutes; truth defense eliminates all forms of defamation liability; opinion defense requires court determination of whether statement is provably false

### Section 230 (47 U.S.C. 230)

- **What**: Section 230(c)(1) provides that "no provider or user of an interactive computer service shall be treated as the publisher or speaker of any information provided by another information content provider" — immunity for platforms hosting third-party content
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Three requirements for immunity: (1) defendant is a provider or user of an interactive computer service, (2) the claim treats defendant as publisher/speaker, (3) the information was provided by another information content provider (not created or materially contributed to by the platform); "interactive computer service" broadly interpreted to include websites, social media platforms, review sites, email providers, and app stores
- **Consequence**: Section 230 provides broad immunity from state and federal claims based on third-party content, including defamation, negligence, and state consumer protection claims; does NOT protect against: federal criminal law, intellectual property claims, CSAM (FOSTA-SESTA carve-out for sex trafficking), or content the platform itself creates/develops; platforms retain immunity even when they moderate content (Section 230(c)(2) — "good Samaritan" provision)

### Section 230 Limitations

- **What**: Section 230 immunity does not apply when the platform is an "information content provider" — i.e., it creates or develops the content at issue
- **Threshold/Timeline**: "Development" means materially contributing to the alleged illegality of the content (Fair Housing Council v. Roommates.com, 2008 — requiring users to provide discriminatory preferences made platform a content developer); merely providing neutral tools (search, algorithms, recommendation engines) generally does not constitute development; editing, selecting, or organizing third-party content generally protected; generative AI outputs by platforms may not be protected (emerging area)
- **Consequence**: Platforms that substantially alter or contribute to unlawful content lose immunity for that content; platform policies encouraging or soliciting illegal content may create developer liability; state attempts to impose platform liability (e.g., Texas HB 20, Florida SB 7072) have faced First Amendment challenges (Moody v. NetChoice, 2024 — Supreme Court remanded for further analysis)

### Anti-SLAPP Statutes

- **What**: Strategic Lawsuits Against Public Participation (SLAPP) statutes provide expedited mechanism to dismiss meritless claims targeting protected speech, typically with fee-shifting to deter abusive litigation
- **Threshold/Timeline**: 28+ states and D.C. have anti-SLAPP statutes; California (CCP 425.16) is the strongest — applies to statements in connection with public issue or issue of public interest; motion filed within 60 days of service; discovery stayed pending motion resolution; two-step analysis: (1) defendant shows speech on matter of public concern, (2) burden shifts to plaintiff to show probability of prevailing
- **Consequence**: Successful anti-SLAPP motion results in dismissal and mandatory attorney fees to defendant; immediate right to appeal denial of anti-SLAPP motion in most jurisdictions (California, Texas, D.C.); no federal anti-SLAPP statute (though legislation proposed); federal courts in diversity cases may or may not apply state anti-SLAPP laws (circuit split — Ninth Circuit applies, some circuits do not)

### Online Defamation Specifics

- **What**: Defamation claims arising from online publications (social media posts, reviews, blog posts, comments, forum posts) involve specific procedural and jurisdictional considerations
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Anonymity: John Doe subpoenas to platforms to identify anonymous speakers (courts apply balancing tests — Dendrite, Cahill — requiring showing of prima facie case before unmasking); jurisdictional reach — Calder "effects test" for personal jurisdiction (intentional conduct aimed at forum state); SPEECH Act (28 U.S.C. 4101-4105) — foreign defamation judgments not enforceable in US unless consistent with First Amendment
- **Consequence**: Online publication may trigger claims in multiple jurisdictions (each download/view is a publication); Internet Archive/cached versions create permanence concerns; DMCA is not a remedy for defamation (only for copyright infringement); de-indexing requests to search engines are an extra-legal remedy with limited effectiveness; online reputation management firms may create additional legal exposure

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **right-of-publicity/**: False endorsement claims (Lanham Act 43(a)) and right of publicity claims may overlap with defamation when unauthorized use implies association or endorsement
- **advertising-standards/**: Comparative advertising that makes false factual claims about competitors may give rise to both Lanham Act and defamation claims
- **consumer-protection/**: Consumer review protections (Consumer Review Fairness Act) prevent businesses from suppressing negative reviews through contract terms; FTC enforcement for fake reviews

## Influencer Disclosures

# Influencer Disclosures

## Applicability

Applies to any arrangement where a brand compensates or provides something of value to an individual who then promotes the brand's products or services through social media, blogs, podcasts, videos, or other content. Governed by FTC Endorsement Guides (16 CFR Part 255), FTC Act Section 5, and increasingly by platform-specific and state-level requirements.

## Key Requirements

### FTC 2023 Endorsement Guides (Revised)

- **What**: Updated Guides substantially expand FTC expectations for endorsement disclosures, defining when and how material connections must be disclosed; apply to endorsers, advertisers, and intermediaries
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Key 2023 changes: (1) endorsements by virtual influencers (AI-generated personas) require same disclosures; (2) brands cannot use endorsements they know or should know are deceptive; (3) procured reviews must be disclosed; (4) "results not typical" disclaimers without disclosing typical results are insufficient; (5) children and teens require heightened protections; (6) advertiser liability for endorser non-compliance is explicit
- **Consequence**: FTC can pursue enforcement against advertisers, endorsers, PR agencies, and affiliate networks; civil penalties up to $50,120 per violation for knowing violations of FTC rules; Section 5 enforcement through consent orders; recent FTC actions have included monetary penalties ranging from $4.2M (Fashion Nova, 2022) to $600M (Epic Games/Fortnite, 2022, which included dark patterns)

### Material Connection Definition

- **What**: A "material connection" is any relationship between an endorser and the brand that might affect the credibility a consumer gives the endorsement; must be disclosed if not reasonably expected by the audience
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Material connections include: monetary payment (any amount), free or discounted products/services, gifts, affiliate commissions, employment relationship, family relationship, business partnership/ownership, contest/sweepstakes incentive, early access to products; one-time gifts create ongoing disclosure obligation for the period the endorser continues to promote
- **Consequence**: Failure to disclose material connection renders the endorsement deceptive under FTC Act Section 5; FTC has brought actions for undisclosed connections as small as free product samples; brands must have reasonable programs to monitor and ensure influencer compliance; contracts should require disclosure compliance as a material term

### Clear and Conspicuous Standard

- **What**: Disclosures must be "clear and conspicuous" — unavoidable, understandable, and placed where consumers will notice them before acting on the endorsement
- **Threshold/Timeline**: FTC-approved approaches:
  - **Acceptable**: "#ad" or "Ad" at the beginning of a post; "Paid partnership with [Brand]" prominently displayed; verbal disclosure at the start of a video; integrated disclosure ("Brand X gave me this product to try...")
  - **Not acceptable**: "#ad" buried among many hashtags; disclosure only in "About" section or link in bio; disclosure only at end of long video; ambiguous terms ("#sp," "#collab," "#ambassador" alone); disclosure visible only after "See More" click; small text overlaid on busy image
- **Consequence**: Each insufficiently disclosed post is a potential separate violation; FTC evaluates from the perspective of a reasonable consumer in the target audience; visual and audio disclosures must be simultaneous with the endorsement claim; stories/ephemeral content requires disclosure in each story frame, not just the first

### Platform-Specific Rules

- **What**: Major social media platforms have their own branded content/disclosure tools and policies in addition to FTC requirements:
  - **Instagram**: Paid Partnership label (branded content tag); required for sponsored posts; provides transparency to consumers and analytics to brands
  - **TikTok**: Branded Content toggle; disclosure required in caption and/or via platform tool; branded content policy requires commercial intent disclosure
  - **YouTube**: "Includes paid promotion" checkbox; disclosure required in video and description; YouTube-specific advertising policies for product placements
  - **Twitter/X**: "Paid" label available; FTC disclosure still required in tweet text
  - **Podcasts**: Verbal disclosure required at beginning of episode or segment; FTC guidance applies to audio endorsements
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Platform tools supplement but do not replace FTC disclosure requirements; using a platform's branded content tool alone may be insufficient if the disclosure is not clear and conspicuous under FTC standards; platform policy violations may result in content removal, reduced distribution, or account restrictions
- **Consequence**: Platform penalties are separate from FTC enforcement; brands should require use of both FTC-compliant disclosures and platform tools; platform advertising policies may be stricter than FTC minimums in some respects

### Brand Liability for Influencer Non-Compliance

- **What**: Brands are responsible for ensuring their influencers comply with disclosure requirements; cannot delegate compliance away through contracts alone
- **Threshold/Timeline**: FTC expects brands to: (1) clearly communicate disclosure requirements to influencers in writing, (2) provide specific guidance on how to disclose (not just "comply with FTC guidelines"), (3) monitor influencer posts for compliance, (4) follow up and enforce when non-compliance is detected, (5) terminate non-compliant influencers if necessary; a single contract clause requiring FTC compliance is necessary but not sufficient
- **Consequence**: FTC has pursued brands (not just influencers) for disclosure failures; Fashion Nova ($4.2M penalty for suppressing negative reviews); Lord & Taylor (2016 consent order for paid Instagram campaign without disclosures); Sunday Riley (employee fake reviews); reasonable monitoring program is a practical requirement; compliance training and written guidelines should be provided to all influencer partners

### Affiliate Marketing Disclosures

- **What**: Affiliate marketers who earn commissions from product links or referral codes have a material connection that must be disclosed; applies to bloggers, social media influencers, review sites, and comparison platforms
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Commission-based links must be disclosed before the consumer clicks; "As an Amazon Associate I earn from qualifying purchases" or similar disclosure must be clearly visible on the page containing affiliate links; each post/page with affiliate links needs its own disclosure (site-wide disclaimer alone is insufficient)
- **Consequence**: FTC enforcement extends to affiliate networks and publishers; review sites ranking products with undisclosed affiliate relationships face heightened scrutiny; Google and search engines may also penalize undisclosed affiliate content through search quality guidelines

### Reviews and Testimonials

- **What**: Online reviews are endorsements under FTC Guides; fake reviews, suppressed negative reviews, and incentivized reviews without disclosure are deceptive practices
- **Threshold/Timeline**: FTC 2023 proposed rule on fake reviews and testimonials: prohibits fake reviews, incentivized reviews without disclosure, insider reviews without disclosure, company-controlled review websites presented as independent, and review suppression; businesses may not condition transactions on leaving positive reviews or imply legal threats against negative reviewers (Consumer Review Fairness Act, 15 U.S.C. 45b)
- **Consequence**: FTC penalties for fake review schemes; Consumer Review Fairness Act voids contract provisions restricting honest reviews ($50,120 civil penalty per violation); state AG enforcement under UDAP statutes; class action risk for review manipulation; platform policies independently prohibit fake reviews with account-level sanctions

### Children and Teens

- **What**: Endorsements and advertising directed at children and adolescents receive heightened scrutiny; FTC, COPPA, and CARU (Children's Advertising Review Unit) guidelines apply
- **Threshold/Timeline**: COPPA applies to collection of personal information from children under 13 in connection with endorsement campaigns; CARU Self-Regulatory Guidelines recommend that advertising directed to children be clearly distinguishable from content; FTC has expressed concern about "kidfluencers" whose content blurs advertising and entertainment; influencer campaigns targeting minors must ensure disclosures are understandable by the target age group
- **Consequence**: COPPA violations up to $50,120 per violation; FTC has signaled increased enforcement focus on advertising to children through influencers; platforms may restrict branded content involving minors; state laws (e.g., Illinois, California) impose specific requirements for minor influencers including trust accounts (similar to Coogan Law)

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **advertising-standards/**: Substantiation requirements apply to claims made through influencer endorsements; brands responsible for ensuring influencer claims are truthful and substantiated
- **right-of-publicity/**: Influencer agreements should include publicity rights license for brand use of influencer content and likeness
- **data-privacy/**: Influencer campaigns collecting consumer data (contests, surveys, tracking links) must comply with applicable privacy laws (CCPA, GDPR for EU-targeting campaigns)

## Right Of Publicity

# Right of Publicity

## Applicability

Applies when a person's name, image, likeness, voice, or other identifying characteristics are used for commercial purposes. The right of publicity is primarily a state law right with significant variation across jurisdictions. Increasingly relevant with AI-generated likenesses and deepfakes.

## Key Requirements

### State-by-State Framework

- **What**: The right of publicity protects individuals from unauthorized commercial use of their identity; recognized in 35+ states through statute, common law, or both; no federal right of publicity statute exists
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Elements generally required: (1) defendant used plaintiff's identity, (2) appropriation of the commercial value of the identity, (3) without consent, (4) resulting in injury; some states require proof of commercial purpose, others extend to any unauthorized use; applicable law typically where the unauthorized use occurred or where the individual is domiciled
- **Consequence**: Remedies vary by state: compensatory damages (lost licensing revenue, unjust enrichment), statutory damages where available, injunctive relief, punitive damages in some jurisdictions, and attorney fees; statutes of limitations typically 1-3 years from discovery of unauthorized use

### California (Civil Code Section 3344)

- **What**: Strongest statutory right of publicity; prohibits knowing use of another's name, voice, signature, photograph, or likeness for advertising, selling, or soliciting purposes without prior consent
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Statutory minimum damages of $750 per unauthorized use even without proof of actual damages; actual damages, profits attributable to the unauthorized use, punitive damages, and attorney fees also recoverable; 2-year statute of limitations; common law right of publicity also recognized (broader, covering any indicia of identity)
- **Consequence**: California's broad protection extends beyond traditional name/likeness to "any attribute" of identity under common law (Carson v. Here's Johnny, analogous); Section 3344 applies to use in California regardless of where defendant is located; consent must be specific to the use (general release may be insufficient for materially different use)

### New York (Civil Rights Law Sections 50/51)

- **What**: Statutory right of privacy (not publicity) prohibiting use of name, portrait, picture, or voice for advertising or trade purposes without written consent; narrower than California
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Section 50 (misdemeanor — criminal); Section 51 (civil cause of action); requires use for "advertising purposes" or "purposes of trade"; newsworthy use and public interest exceptions are broad; no post-mortem protection under current statute (legislation proposed but not enacted as of 2025)
- **Consequence**: Compensatory damages, injunctive relief, and punitive damages available; "incidental use" doctrine permits use in news, entertainment, and creative works with public interest connection; fictional works receive broader protection than commercial advertising

### Deceased Personality Rights

- **What**: Post-mortem right of publicity varies dramatically by state; some states provide statutory protection for decades after death, others provide none
- **Threshold/Timeline**:
  - **California**: 70 years post-mortem (Civil Code 3344.1); applies to personality whose likeness has commercial value at death; transferable/devisable
  - **Indiana**: 100 years post-mortem (IC 32-36); broadest duration
  - **Tennessee**: 10 years post-mortem, unlimited if continuously exploited (Personal Rights Protection Act)
  - **New York**: No post-mortem statutory protection (common law claim dies with person)
  - **Many states**: No post-mortem protection at all
- **Consequence**: Choice of law is critical — estate should consider which state's law provides strongest protection; post-mortem rights are property rights (transferable, licensable, devisable); estates of major personalities actively license and enforce (e.g., estate of Marilyn Monroe, Elvis Presley, MLK Jr.)

### AI-Generated Likenesses

- **What**: Emerging area addressing use of artificial intelligence to generate realistic depictions of individuals' faces, voices, and mannerisms without consent; deepfakes and synthetic media create new enforcement challenges
- **Threshold/Timeline**: NO FAKES Act (Nurture Originals, Foster Art, and Keep Entertainment Safe Act) — federal legislation introduced but not yet enacted as of early 2026; would create federal right to control digital replicas of voice and visual likeness; Tennessee ELVIS Act (2024) — first state to explicitly protect voice from AI replication; California AB 2602 and AB 1836 (2024) — requirements for consent to digital replicas in entertainment contracts and post-mortem digital replica protection; FTC proposed rule on AI impersonation (2024)
- **Consequence**: Existing right of publicity statutes apply to AI-generated likenesses in most jurisdictions even without AI-specific legislation; platforms implementing AI tools face contributory liability risk; consent for AI training on likeness should be explicitly addressed in talent agreements; "digital replica" provisions increasingly standard in SAG-AFTRA and entertainment contracts following 2023 strike

### First Amendment Limits

- **What**: The First Amendment limits right of publicity claims when the use of identity serves expressive, newsworthy, or creative purposes rather than purely commercial exploitation
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Three primary frameworks courts use to balance publicity rights against First Amendment:
  - **Transformative Use Test** (California — Comedy III v. Saderup): Does the work add significant creative elements so it is "transformed" beyond mere celebrity likeness? If sufficiently transformative, First Amendment prevails
  - **Rogers Test** (Second Circuit — Rogers v. Grimaldi): Does the use of identity have artistic relevance to the work and not explicitly mislead as to source/endorsement? Used primarily for titles and content of expressive works
  - **Predominant Purpose Test** (Missouri — Doe v. TCI Cablevision): Is the predominant purpose commercial exploitation or expression?
- **Consequence**: News reporting, commentary, satire, and parody receive strong First Amendment protection; commercial advertising receives less protection; fine art and entertainment fall in between; social media posts may be commercial or expressive depending on context

### Consent and Releases

- **What**: Written releases should specify the scope of permitted use of an individual's identity with sufficient particularity to avoid disputes
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Key release terms: specific uses permitted, media/platforms, territory, duration (perpetual vs. term), right to modify/alter likeness, compensation terms, exclusivity, right to approve final use; blanket releases are disfavored — courts may limit overly broad releases to reasonably contemplated uses; minors require parental/guardian consent (state-specific requirements); revocation rights may exist under state law even with signed release
- **Consequence**: Insufficient release language exposes user to full publicity rights liability; model releases for stock photography typically grant broad rights but may not cover sensitive contexts (e.g., adult content, political endorsement, pharmaceutical advertising) without specific consent; digital replica/AI training rights should be separately and explicitly addressed

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **defamation/**: Unauthorized use of likeness may also give rise to false endorsement (Lanham Act 43(a)) or false light claims if the use implies association or endorsement
- **content-licensing/**: Publicity rights clearance is separate from and in addition to copyright clearance; photograph copyright owner and photograph subject may be different parties
- **influencer-disclosures/**: Influencer agreements should include publicity rights license with specified scope; social media use of third-party likenesses requires consent
- **consumer-protection/**: False endorsement claims under Lanham Act and state consumer protection statutes overlap with right of publicity. Cross-ref: `consumer-protection/endorsements-testimonials.md`
