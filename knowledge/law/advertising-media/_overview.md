## Key Constraints

These are non-overridable legal requirements from this area. They cannot be modified by practice/ or matters/ overrides.

- Objective advertising claims must be substantiated BEFORE they are made — a "reasonable basis," and for health/safety claims competent and reliable scientific evidence (FTC Act Section 5); disclaimers cannot cure a deceptive net impression.
- Material connections between an advertiser and any endorser (payment, free product, employment, family relationship, affiliate commission) must be clearly and conspicuously disclosed (16 CFR Part 255); the advertiser remains liable for endorser non-compliance and cannot delegate compliance away by contract.
- Fake reviews, sentiment-conditioned incentivized reviews, undisclosed insider reviews, and review suppression are prohibited by trade regulation rule (16 CFR Part 465), carrying per-violation civil penalties.
- Unqualified "Made in USA" claims require the product to be "all or virtually all" made in the United States (16 CFR Part 323); California imposes its own stricter statutory tolerances.
- A person's name, image, likeness, or voice cannot be used for commercial purposes without consent — state right-of-publicity law applies equally to AI-generated replicas, and post-mortem rights persist for decades in many states (California 70 years; New York 40 years).
- Music use requires clearing BOTH the musical composition (sync license) and the sound recording (master use license); no single license covers both unless it expressly says so.
- Copyright clearance and publicity-rights clearance are independent — a license from the photographer/creator does not clear the depicted person's rights, and platform terms of service do not grant brands rights to reuse user-generated content.
- Work-for-hire status for commissioned works requires a signed writing before creation; copyright assignments and exclusive licenses must be in writing (17 U.S.C. 101, 204).
- Section 230 protects platforms only for content provided by others — never for content the company itself creates or materially develops, and never against intellectual property claims or federal criminal law.
- Statements of fact that disparage identifiable persons or competitors must be true and provable; literally false comparative advertising creates Lanham Act liability without proof of consumer confusion.
