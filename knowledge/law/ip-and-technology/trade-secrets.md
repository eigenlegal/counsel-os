# Trade Secrets

## Overview
Trade secret law protects confidential business information that derives independent economic value from not being generally known or readily ascertainable, provided the owner takes reasonable measures to maintain its secrecy. The Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA) provides a federal cause of action, while the Uniform Trade Secrets Act (UTSA) has been adopted in nearly all states.

## Key Requirements
- **Definition:** A trade secret is information (including formulas, patterns, compilations, programs, devices, methods, techniques, or processes) that derives independent economic value from not being generally known or readily ascertainable by proper means, and is subject to reasonable efforts to maintain its secrecy (UTSA/DTSA).
- **Reasonable measures to maintain secrecy:** The trade secret holder must take affirmative steps to protect confidentiality, including: NDAs with employees and business partners, access controls (physical and digital), marking documents as confidential, need-to-know access policies, exit interviews and return-of-materials procedures, and employee training.
- **Misappropriation:** Acquisition through improper means (theft, bribery, breach of duty, espionage, electronic intrusion) or disclosure/use without consent by someone who acquired it through improper means or under a duty of confidentiality.
- **DTSA federal cause of action (18 U.S.C. 1836):** Provides civil remedies including injunctive relief, damages (actual loss + unjust enrichment, or reasonable royalty), and exemplary damages (up to 2x) and attorney's fees for willful and malicious misappropriation. Requires the trade secret to be related to a product or service used in, or intended for use in, interstate or foreign commerce.
- **DTSA ex parte seizure (18 U.S.C. 1836(b)(2)):** In extraordinary circumstances, courts may order seizure of property to prevent propagation or dissemination of a trade secret. Strict requirements include showing other remedies are inadequate.
- **DTSA whistleblower immunity (18 U.S.C. 1833(b)):** Individuals may disclose trade secrets to government officials or in court filings under seal for the purpose of reporting a suspected violation of law. Employers must provide notice of this immunity in NDAs and employment agreements; failure to do so prevents recovery of exemplary damages and attorney's fees.
- **Inevitable disclosure doctrine:** Some jurisdictions permit injunctive relief when a former employee with knowledge of trade secrets takes a position with a competitor where disclosure is "inevitable," even without evidence of actual or threatened misappropriation. The doctrine is controversial and rejected in some states (California).
- **Reverse engineering and independent development:** Reverse engineering of a lawfully acquired product and independent development are legitimate means of discovering trade secrets and do not constitute misappropriation.

## Common Contract Issues
- NDAs with overly broad definitions of confidential information that include general skills and knowledge (unenforceable and may indicate overreach).
- NDAs that fail to include the DTSA whistleblower immunity notice — the employer forfeits the right to exemplary damages and attorney's fees in DTSA claims.
- Absence of post-employment obligations regarding return of materials, certification of non-retention, and reminder of continuing confidentiality obligations.
- Joint development or collaboration agreements that do not clearly delineate each party's pre-existing confidential information and newly created information.
- Vendor and service provider agreements lacking adequate confidentiality protections for shared trade secrets.
- Residual knowledge clauses that may inadvertently waive trade secret protection by permitting use of information retained in unaided memory.
- Missing or inadequate provisions for return or destruction of confidential information upon termination of the business relationship.
- Confidentiality provisions with unreasonably short duration (trade secret protection can last indefinitely as long as secrecy is maintained; contractual confidentiality obligations should match).

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **IP and Technology (Patents):** Filing a patent requires public disclosure that destroys trade secret status. Companies must strategically choose between patent and trade secret protection.
- **IP and Technology (SaaS/Technology):** Technology vendor agreements must protect proprietary algorithms, source code, and business logic as trade secrets through appropriate contractual and technical measures.
- **Employment:** Employee NDAs, PIIAs, exit procedures, and garden leave provisions are primary tools for trade secret protection. Non-competes may be used (where enforceable) to supplement trade secret protection.
- **Employment (Contractor Classification):** Independent contractor agreements must include confidentiality provisions; contractors' access to trade secrets should be limited and documented.
- **Litigation:** Trade secret misappropriation claims often involve expedited proceedings for temporary restraining orders and preliminary injunctions, forensic investigation of data exfiltration, and coordination with criminal referrals for egregious cases (Economic Espionage Act, 18 U.S.C. 1831-1839).
- **International Trade:** Trade secrets are protected under various international frameworks; the EU Trade Secrets Directive (2016/943) provides harmonized protection in the EU.
