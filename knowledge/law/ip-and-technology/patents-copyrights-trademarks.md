# Patents, Copyrights, and Trademarks

## Overview
Patents, copyrights, and trademarks are the three primary forms of registered intellectual property, each protecting different aspects of innovation and creative works. Understanding the scope, requirements, and limitations of each is essential for IP portfolio management, licensing, enforcement, and transactional due diligence.

## Key Requirements
- **Patents (35 U.S.C.):**
  - Utility patents protect new, useful, and non-obvious inventions and processes for 20 years from the filing date.
  - Design patents protect ornamental designs for articles of manufacture for 15 years from grant date.
  - Patent prosecution requires filing with the USPTO, examination, and allowance; international filings under the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) or direct national filings.
  - First-inventor-to-file system (post-America Invents Act): priority generally goes to the first to file, not the first to invent.
  - Patent claims define the scope of protection; claim construction is central to infringement and validity analysis.
  - Patentable subject matter excludes abstract ideas, laws of nature, and natural phenomena (Alice/Mayo framework). Software and AI-related patents face heightened Section 101 scrutiny.
  - AI-generated inventions: USPTO has taken the position that only natural persons can be named as inventors; AI systems cannot be listed as inventors (Thaler v. Vidal).
  - Maintenance fees required at 3.5, 7.5, and 11.5 years after grant to keep utility patents in force.

- **Copyrights (17 U.S.C.):**
  - Protects original works of authorship fixed in a tangible medium, including literary works, software code, visual art, music, and architectural works.
  - Protection is automatic upon creation and fixation; registration with the US Copyright Office provides additional benefits (statutory damages, attorney's fees, prerequisite to infringement suit for US works).
  - Duration: life of the author plus 70 years (individual works); 95 years from publication or 120 years from creation for works made for hire.
  - Fair use (Section 107): four-factor balancing test allows limited use without permission for purposes such as criticism, commentary, news reporting, teaching, and research.
  - AI and copyright: the US Copyright Office requires human authorship; purely AI-generated content is not copyrightable. Works with AI assistance may be copyrightable to the extent of human creative contribution.
  - DMCA provisions: safe harbors for online service providers (Section 512), anti-circumvention provisions (Section 1201).

- **Trademarks (Lanham Act, 15 U.S.C. 1051 et seq.):**
  - Protects words, names, symbols, sounds, colors, and trade dress that identify and distinguish goods or services.
  - Rights arise from use in commerce; federal registration on the Principal Register provides nationwide constructive notice, presumption of validity, and access to federal courts.
  - Registration requires use in commerce (or bona fide intent to use with later filing of statement of use).
  - Trademark strength spectrum: fanciful > arbitrary > suggestive > descriptive (registrable only with secondary meaning) > generic (not protectable).
  - Maintenance: declaration of continued use (Section 8) and renewal (Section 9) required at specified intervals (between 5th and 6th year, then every 10 years).
  - Opposition and cancellation proceedings before the TTAB.
  - Likelihood of confusion is the primary test for infringement.

## Common Contract Issues
- IP assignment provisions that fail to convey all necessary rights (e.g., assigning patents but not related copyrights, trade secrets, or know-how).
- License scope ambiguity — unclear whether a license is exclusive or non-exclusive, perpetual or term-limited, worldwide or territory-restricted, and whether sublicensing is permitted.
- IP representations and warranties that omit key issues (freedom to operate, non-infringement, ownership of all necessary rights, absence of encumbrances).
- Inadequate IP indemnification — missing caps, exclusions, or control-of-defense provisions; failure to address remedies if the indemnifying party cannot cure infringement.
- Joint ownership of IP without clear governance — co-owners of patents can independently exploit and license without accounting to the other, creating unpredictable outcomes.
- Trademark license agreements lacking quality control provisions, risking "naked license" and potential abandonment of trademark rights.
- Failure to address improvements, enhancements, and derivative works ownership in license and development agreements.

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **IP and Technology (Trade Secrets):** Patent filing requires public disclosure, which can destroy trade secret protection. Companies must make strategic decisions about patent vs. trade secret protection.
- **IP and Technology (Open Source):** Open source license terms may conflict with patent claims if the open source license includes patent grants or retaliation clauses.
- **Employment:** Invention assignment agreements must comply with state law limitations. Copyright work-for-hire doctrine applies differently to employees and contractors.
- **Corporate (M&A):** IP due diligence is critical in technology M&A; IP representations and warranties are heavily negotiated; IP transfer and licensing are central to deal structure.
- **International Trade:** Patent and trademark rights are territorial; international IP portfolios require country-by-country prosecution strategies. Import restrictions (ITC Section 337) can be used to enforce IP against infringing imports.
- **Antitrust:** Patent licensing practices (tying, exclusive dealing, FRAND commitments for standard-essential patents) may raise antitrust concerns.
