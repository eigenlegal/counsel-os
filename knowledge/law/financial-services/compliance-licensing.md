# Financial Services Compliance and Licensing

## Overview
Financial services activities are subject to extensive federal and state licensing requirements and ongoing compliance obligations. The regulatory framework varies by activity type (lending, money transmission, securities, insurance, banking) and by the entity's charter type (national bank, state bank, non-bank). Fintech companies must carefully analyze which activities require licensing and whether bank partnerships or other structures can satisfy regulatory requirements.

## Key Requirements
- **State lending licenses:** Consumer lending, commercial lending, mortgage lending, and loan servicing each require separate state licenses in most jurisdictions. Requirements include net worth minimums, surety bonds, background checks, and annual reporting. NMLS centralizes multi-state applications.
- **State usury laws:** Each state sets maximum permissible interest rates for various types of loans. National banks may "export" their home state's interest rate to borrowers in other states under federal preemption (12 U.S.C. 85). The Madden v. Midland Funding issue (Second Circuit) created uncertainty about whether non-bank assignees of bank-originated loans retain rate exportation benefits; the OCC/FDIC "valid when made" rules and state responses continue to evolve.
- **Bank partnership models (BaaS/rent-a-charter):** Fintech companies partner with chartered banks to offer banking products (lending, deposits, payments) under the bank's charter and regulatory authority. Regulators (OCC, FDIC, Federal Reserve, state banking departments) scrutinize these relationships through third-party risk management guidance. The bank must maintain meaningful oversight and cannot be a "passive participant."
- **Third-party risk management:** Federal banking regulators (OCC Bulletin 2023-17, FDIC FIL-44-2008, Federal Reserve SR 13-19) require banks to manage risks from third-party relationships through due diligence, contract structuring, ongoing monitoring, and contingency planning. Fintech partners are subject to regulatory examination through the bank.
- **Consumer compliance framework:** TILA/Regulation Z (credit disclosures), ECOA/Regulation B (fair lending, non-discrimination), FCRA/Regulation V (credit reporting), EFTA/Regulation E (electronic fund transfers), FDCPA (debt collection), HMDA (mortgage data reporting), RESPA (real estate settlement), and state equivalents.
- **CFPB supervisory and enforcement authority:** The CFPB has supervisory authority over banks with assets exceeding $10B and over non-bank entities in certain markets (mortgage, payday lending, private education lending, and larger participants in other consumer financial markets). UDAAP authority prohibits unfair, deceptive, or abusive acts or practices.
- **State regulatory authority:** State banking departments, DFS (New York), and other state regulators have examination and enforcement authority over state-chartered banks and state-licensed non-bank financial services providers.
- **Regulatory change management:** Financial services companies must monitor and adapt to regulatory changes including new rulemakings, enforcement actions, guidance, and no-action letters from federal and state regulators.

## Common Contract Issues
- BaaS/bank partnership agreements that do not adequately address regulatory examination rights, audit provisions, and compliance oversight responsibilities.
- Lending program agreements that fail to clearly allocate the "true lender" determination — if the non-bank fintech is deemed the true lender, it must hold its own lending licenses and cannot rely on the bank's charter.
- Third-party vendor agreements with financial institutions that do not meet regulatory expectations for due diligence, performance standards, compliance requirements, and contingency planning.
- Compliance representation and warranty provisions that are overly broad or that shift compliance responsibility in ways regulators do not recognize.
- Missing or inadequate business continuity and disaster recovery provisions required by financial regulators.
- Subcontractor provisions that do not provide the financial institution with adequate oversight of fourth-party risks.
- Data and technology provisions that do not address regulatory data access, recordkeeping, and examination requirements.
- Termination provisions that do not account for wind-down requirements, customer notification obligations, and regulatory approval for material relationship changes.

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **Financial Services (Payments):** Money transmission licensing is separate from lending licensing; companies offering both payment and credit products need multiple licenses.
- **Financial Services (KYC/AML):** BSA/AML compliance obligations apply to all financial institutions and must be integrated into licensing and compliance programs.
- **Consumer Protection:** Consumer financial services are subject to both CFPB UDAAP authority and state consumer protection statutes; compliance programs must address both.
- **Data Privacy:** Financial institutions are subject to GLBA privacy and safeguarding requirements in addition to general data privacy laws (CCPA, state privacy laws).
- **Corporate:** Financial services licensing may restrict corporate restructuring options (change of control approvals, entity conversions) and impose ongoing reporting obligations.
- **Securities:** Some fintech products (investment platforms, robo-advisers, fractional shares) may require broker-dealer or investment adviser registration in addition to financial services licenses.
