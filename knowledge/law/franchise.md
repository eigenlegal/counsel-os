## Franchise Agreements

# Franchise Agreements

## Applicability

Applies to the negotiation, drafting, and review of franchise agreements, area development agreements, and related contracts between franchisors and franchisees. Covers key commercial terms including territory, fees, non-compete, transfer, renewal, system standards, and termination provisions.

## Key Requirements

### Territory

- **Exclusive Territory** / Defined geographic area in which franchisor agrees not to establish competing units or grant additional franchises / **Threshold**: Exclusivity may be conditioned on performance requirements (minimum revenue, development schedule, customer satisfaction scores) / **Consequence**: Loss of exclusivity for failure to meet performance benchmarks; must ensure benchmarks are clearly defined and achievable
- **Non-Exclusive Territory** / Franchisee has right to operate but franchisor retains right to operate or franchise in the same area / **Consequence**: Franchisee has no protection against cannibalization; market value of franchise reduced
- **Protected vs. Non-Protected** / Some agreements provide "protected" area (no other franchised units) but reserve right for corporate-owned units, alternative channels (online, delivery-only, non-traditional venues) / **Consequence**: Erosion of territory protection through reserved channels; evaluate breadth of exceptions
- **Area Development** / Agreement to develop multiple units in a defined territory on a specified schedule / **Threshold**: Development schedule with specific opening deadlines (e.g., 3 units in 3 years) / **Consequence**: Failure to meet development schedule = loss of development rights; may trigger termination of entire development agreement

### Fees

- **Initial Franchise Fee** / One-time fee for right to operate franchise / **Threshold**: Typical range $20,000-$50,000; some systems significantly higher ($50K-$100K+) / **Consequence**: Non-refundable in most agreements; state laws may provide rescission rights
- **Ongoing Royalties** / Continuing payments, typically percentage of gross sales / **Threshold**: Typical range 4-8% of gross sales (some systems 2-12%); alternative structures include flat fees or tiered percentages / **Consequence**: Royalty base (gross vs. net) is critical; gross sales includes all revenue before deductions
- **Advertising/Marketing Fees** / Contributions to national/regional advertising fund and/or local advertising requirement / **Threshold**: Typical range 1-4% of gross sales for fund contribution; additional 1-3% for local advertising / **Consequence**: Franchisor typically has discretion over fund expenditure; franchisee has limited control; some states require accounting
- **Technology Fees** / Fees for required technology systems (POS, CRM, software platforms) / **Consequence**: Technology mandates create ongoing cost obligation; review whether technology fees are capped and whether franchisor controls pricing
- **Transfer Fees** / Fee paid upon transfer of franchise to new owner / **Threshold**: Typically 25-50% of then-current initial franchise fee, or fixed amount ($5K-$15K) / **Consequence**: Transfer fee is cost of exit; affects franchise resale value
- **Renewal Fees** / Fee paid upon renewal of franchise agreement / **Threshold**: Typically 10-50% of then-current initial franchise fee / **Consequence**: Must budget for renewal cost; some agreements require payment of "then-current" full franchise fee

### Non-Compete Provisions

- **In-Term Non-Compete** / Franchisee prohibited from operating competing business during term of franchise agreement / **Consequence**: Generally enforceable; prevents diversion of resources and trade secrets
- **Post-Term Non-Compete** / Restriction on competing business after termination or expiration / **Threshold**: Typical duration 1-2 years post-termination; geographic scope typically within franchise territory plus radius (15-25 miles for retail, larger for service) / **Consequence**: Enforceability varies by state; overbroad restrictions may be modified (blue-pencil) or voided; FTC has proposed (but not finalized) broader non-compete restrictions that may exempt franchise non-competes
- **Business Type Restriction** / Non-compete typically covers "same or similar" business; scope of "similar" frequently litigated / **Consequence**: Narrow definition preferred by franchisee; broad definition preferred by franchisor; ambiguity invites litigation

### Transfer Provisions

- **Consent Requirement** / Franchise agreements typically require franchisor consent to transfer; standard: consent "not unreasonably withheld" / **Consequence**: Franchisor's ability to withhold consent effectively controls exit; "reasonable" standard varies by jurisdiction
- **Conditions of Transfer** / Typical conditions: (1) transferee meets franchisor's qualifications; (2) transferee completes training; (3) transfer fee paid; (4) all outstanding obligations current; (5) transferee signs then-current form of franchise agreement; (6) franchisee signs general release / **Consequence**: Requirement to sign "then-current" agreement may impose materially different terms; release may extinguish existing claims
- **Right of First Refusal (ROFR)** / Franchisor right to match bona fide third-party offer and acquire the franchise on same terms / **Consequence**: ROFR can deter potential buyers; creates delay in transfer process; franchisee should negotiate time limits on ROFR exercise (30-60 days typical)
- **Transfer to Family/Entity** / Many agreements distinguish between arms-length transfers and internal transfers (to family members, estate, or controlled entity) / **Consequence**: Internal transfers may have reduced conditions; but franchisor approval still typically required

### Renewal

- **Renewal Rights** / Most franchise agreements grant one or more renewal terms (typically 5-10 years each) subject to conditions / **Consequence**: No guaranteed right to renewal unless specified; state relationship laws may provide additional protections
- **Renewal Conditions** / Typical conditions: (1) timely notice of intent to renew (6-12 months before expiration); (2) not in default; (3) completed renovation/upgrade requirements; (4) signed then-current form of agreement; (5) paid renewal fee / **Consequence**: Renovation requirements at renewal can be substantial ($100K-$500K+); then-current agreement may change material terms
- **Good Faith Requirement** / Some state relationship laws impose good faith requirement on renewal decisions / **Consequence**: See `relationship-laws.md` for state-specific renewal protections

### System Standards

- **Operations Manual** / Franchisor maintains confidential operations manual setting standards for all aspects of operations; manual may be modified during the term / **Consequence**: Manual modifications effectively change franchise terms; review whether agreement limits changes that materially increase cost or fundamentally alter business model
- **Technology Requirements** / Required POS systems, software, hardware, telecommunications; franchisor may mandate specific vendors / **Consequence**: Technology mandates create cost obligations; proprietary systems may limit franchisee flexibility
- **Approved Suppliers** / Franchisor may require use of approved suppliers for products, ingredients, equipment, signage / **Consequence**: Approved supplier requirements may increase costs above market rate; review whether franchisor or affiliates receive rebates from suppliers
- **Quality Standards** / Product/service quality standards, customer experience requirements, mystery shopper programs / **Consequence**: Failure to meet quality standards = default; may trigger cure period or termination depending on severity

### Termination

- **Termination for Cause** / Franchisor may terminate for specified defaults: non-payment, abandonment, bankruptcy, criminal conduct, health/safety violations, repeated defaults, unauthorized transfer / **Consequence**: Defaults typically classified as curable (with notice and cure period) or incurable (immediate termination)
- **Cure Period** / Most defaults require notice and opportunity to cure / **Threshold**: Typical cure periods: 10-30 days for monetary defaults; 30-60 days for operational defaults / **Consequence**: State relationship laws may impose minimum cure periods; see `relationship-laws.md`
- **Post-Termination Obligations** / Upon termination: cease use of marks, de-identification of premises, return of confidential information, payment of all outstanding obligations, non-compete effective / **Consequence**: De-identification costs borne by franchisee; timeline for completion typically 30-60 days; failure to de-identify = trademark infringement

### Dispute Resolution

- **Mandatory Arbitration** / Many franchise agreements require arbitration of disputes / **Consequence**: Arbitration typically limits discovery and appeal rights; some states restrict mandatory arbitration in franchise context
- **Venue Selection** / Agreements typically specify franchisor's home jurisdiction for dispute resolution / **Consequence**: Franchisee forced to litigate in distant forum; some state relationship laws prohibit out-of-state venue requirements
- **Waiver of Jury Trial** / Common in franchise agreements / **Consequence**: Enforceability varies by state; some states prohibit prospective jury waiver

- **IP and Technology** (`ip-and-technology/`): trademark licensing, trade secrets, technology mandates
- **Employment** (`employment/`): joint employer liability for franchisee employees, non-compete enforceability

## Ftc Franchise Rule

# FTC Franchise Rule

## Applicability

Applies to all franchisors offering or selling franchises in the United States. The FTC Franchise Rule (16 CFR Part 436) requires franchisors to provide prospective franchisees with a Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD) containing 23 specific items of disclosure before any sale. The FTC does not require registration but enforces disclosure requirements nationwide.

## Key Requirements

### Definition of Franchise

- **Three-Element Test** / A franchise exists when: (1) the franchisee is granted the right to operate a business identified or associated with the franchisor's trademark; (2) the franchisor exerts or has authority to exert significant control over, or provides significant assistance in, the franchisee's method of operation; (3) the franchisee is required to make a payment of $615 or more (adjusted periodically) to the franchisor or its affiliate before or within 6 months after commencing operations / **Consequence**: Meeting all three elements = franchise; must comply with FTC Rule regardless of what parties call the relationship
- **Business Opportunity Variant** / Where there is no trademark but franchisor makes express or implied representations about income/sales, the relationship may still be covered / **Consequence**: Must analyze whether arrangement is a franchise or business opportunity under FTC and state law

### Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD) — 23 Items

- **Item 1** / The Franchisor and any Parents, Predecessors, and Affiliates
- **Item 2** / Business Experience (of directors, officers, and franchise executives)
- **Item 3** / Litigation history (10-year lookback for material civil actions; any criminal/regulatory actions)
- **Item 4** / Bankruptcy history (10-year lookback for directors, officers, predecessors, affiliates)
- **Item 5** / Initial Fees (all fees paid before operations begin)
- **Item 6** / Other Fees (ongoing fees: royalties, advertising, technology, renewal, transfer)
- **Item 7** / Estimated Initial Investment (total range, itemized)
- **Item 8** / Restrictions on Sources of Products and Services (approved suppliers, rebates)
- **Item 9** / Franchisee's Obligations (cross-reference table to agreement)
- **Item 10** / Financing arrangements offered by franchisor
- **Item 11** / Franchisor's Assistance, Advertising, Computer Systems, and Training
- **Item 12** / Territory (exclusive, non-exclusive, conditions, reservations)
- **Item 13** / Trademarks (registrations, agreements, disputes)
- **Item 14** / Patents, Copyrights, and Proprietary Information
- **Item 15** / Obligation to Participate in Actual Operation
- **Item 16** / Restrictions on What the Franchisee May Sell
- **Item 17** / Renewal, Termination, Transfer, and Dispute Resolution
- **Item 18** / Public Figures (endorsements, involvement)
- **Item 19** / Financial Performance Representations (optional but must comply with strict format if made; cannot make earnings claims outside FDD)
- **Item 20** / Outlets and Franchisee Information (system size, openings, closings, transfers, contact information for current and former franchisees)
- **Item 21** / Financial Statements (audited for 3 fiscal years; GAAP)
- **Item 22** / Contracts (copies of all agreements franchisee will sign)
- **Item 23** / Receipts (detachable, signed by franchisee)
- **Consequence**: Incomplete, inaccurate, or outdated FDD = FTC Rule violation; state registration may also be denied

### Timing Requirements

- **14-Day Waiting Period** / Franchisor must provide FDD at least 14 calendar days before the prospective franchisee: (1) signs a binding agreement, or (2) makes any payment to the franchisor or its affiliates / **Consequence**: Franchise agreement signed before 14-day period = voidable; franchisor liable for rescission and damages
- **Material Change Disclosure** / If FDD is materially changed after delivery but before signing, franchisee must receive updated disclosure and a new 14-day period begins for material changes to Items 5, 6, or 7 (or 7 days for other material changes) / **Consequence**: Failure to update = disclosure violation; material changes include fee increases, litigation developments, financial statement changes
- **Annual Update** / FDD must be updated within 120 days after the franchisor's fiscal year end / **Consequence**: Offering franchises with stale FDD = violation; must also update for material changes within the year

### Financial Performance Representations (Item 19)

- **Optional but Regulated** / Franchisors are not required to make financial performance representations, but if they do, must comply with Item 19 format / **Consequence**: Making earnings claims outside the FDD (including verbal representations by sales staff) violates the FTC Rule
- **Required Content** / Must include: (1) reasonable basis and written substantiation; (2) assumptions underlying the representation; (3) number and percentage of existing outlets that achieved the stated results; (4) clear identification of geographic scope and time period / **Consequence**: Unsupported earnings claims = FTC enforcement; state enforcement; franchisee rescission claims
- **No Earnings Claims** / If Item 19 is not used, FDD must contain disclaimer stating franchisor does not make and does not authorize others to make financial performance representations / **Consequence**: Sales personnel making informal earnings claims despite disclaimer = franchisor liability

### Exemptions

- **Minimum Investment Exemption** / Franchise not covered if franchisee's total investment (minus cost of unimproved land and any franchisor financing) exceeds a specified threshold / **Threshold**: Currently evaluated case-by-case; historically very high thresholds / **Consequence**: Exemption is narrow; most franchises are covered
- **Fractional Franchise Exemption** / Large businesses adding a franchise component to existing operations (franchisee already in same type of business, franchise is no more than 20% of sales) / **Consequence**: Exemption reduces compliance burden for supplemental product/service offerings
- **Leased Department Exemption** / Arrangements to operate a department within a retailer's store / **Consequence**: Must meet specific criteria; does not apply to most franchise-like arrangements
- **Large Franchisee Exemption** / Some states exempt sales to experienced, sophisticated franchisees (varies by state) / **Consequence**: Federal FTC Rule does not have this exemption; state-only benefit

### Penalties and Enforcement

- **FTC Enforcement** / FTC may bring enforcement action for Rule violations; penalties up to $50,120 per violation (adjusted for inflation); injunctive relief; rescission / **Consequence**: Each franchise sold in violation = separate violation; penalties compound rapidly
- **No Private Right of Action** / FTC Rule does not create a federal private right of action; franchisees must rely on state law claims / **Consequence**: Franchisees sue under state franchise statutes, state UDAP laws, or common law fraud; FTC Rule violations are evidence in state proceedings
- **State Enforcement** / State regulators independently enforce registration and disclosure requirements; may issue stop orders, impose penalties, deny registration / **Consequence**: Multi-state enforcement exposure; must comply with both FTC and state requirements

## Relationship Laws

# Franchise Relationship Laws

## Applicability

Applies to the ongoing franchise relationship, particularly termination, non-renewal, encroachment, sourcing, and transfer disputes. Over 20 states have enacted franchise relationship laws that impose requirements beyond those in the franchise agreement, providing franchisees with statutory protections that cannot be waived by contract.

## Key Requirements

### Good Cause Termination

- **Requirement** / Over 20 states require franchisors to have "good cause" before terminating a franchise agreement / **Consequence**: Termination without good cause = wrongful termination; franchisee entitled to damages, injunctive relief, and/or rescission
- **Good Cause Defined** / Typically includes: material breach of agreement, failure to comply with lawful requirements, bankruptcy/insolvency, criminal conduct, repeated violations after notice and cure, abandonment, health/safety violations, loss of right to occupy premises / **Consequence**: Definition varies by state; some states enumerate specific grounds; others use general standard
- **States with Good Cause Requirement** / Arkansas, California, Connecticut, Delaware, Hawaii, Idaho, Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Michigan, Minnesota, Mississippi, Missouri, Nebraska, New Jersey, Virginia, Washington, Wisconsin (partial list — requirements and scope vary) / **Consequence**: Must analyze each state where franchises operate; franchise agreement terms less protective than state law are preempted

### Notice and Cure Requirements

- **Notice Before Termination** / Most relationship laws require written notice specifying the default and providing opportunity to cure before termination / **Timeline**: Typical cure periods: 30 days (most common minimum), 60 days (some states), 90 days (limited states for certain defaults) / **Consequence**: Termination without proper notice and cure period = wrongful termination; even if good cause exists
- **Incurable Defaults** / Some defaults are incurable under state law (bankruptcy, conviction of felony, repeated same default, abandonment) and permit immediate termination / **Consequence**: Even for incurable defaults, most states require written notice (though no cure period); must confirm state-specific requirements
- **Reasonable Time to Cure** / Some states require "reasonable" cure period rather than a specified number of days / **Consequence**: Reasonableness is fact-specific; may exceed contractual cure period

### Non-Renewal Protections

- **Notice of Non-Renewal** / Many states require advance notice if franchisor intends not to renew / **Timeline**: Typical notice requirements: 90 days to 180 days before expiration (varies by state) / **Consequence**: Failure to provide timely notice may result in automatic renewal or liability for damages
- **Good Cause for Non-Renewal** / Some states require good cause for non-renewal (same standard as termination); others require only reasonable notice / **Consequence**: Non-renewal without good cause in protected states = wrongful non-renewal; franchisee may seek injunctive relief compelling renewal
- **Repurchase Obligation** / Limited states require franchisor to repurchase franchisee's inventory, equipment, or supplies at fair market value upon non-renewal / **Consequence**: Creates financial obligation for franchisor at non-renewal; may affect decision to non-renew

### Encroachment and Impact Protection

- **Encroachment** / Placing a new franchise or company-owned unit unreasonably close to an existing franchisee's location, harming the existing franchisee's business / **Consequence**: Some states prohibit encroachment; others address through implied covenant of good faith; remedies include injunction and damages
- **Impact Assessment** / Some franchise systems require impact studies before placing new units near existing franchisees / **Consequence**: Failure to conduct impact assessment when required may constitute bad faith
- **Alternative Channel Encroachment** / Franchisor's expansion into online sales, delivery-only, or non-traditional venues may compete with existing franchisees / **Consequence**: Growing area of dispute; franchise agreements may address or reserve these rights; implied good faith obligations may apply

### Sourcing Requirements

- **Approved Supplier Requirements** / Franchisors may require use of approved suppliers; some states regulate this requirement / **Consequence**: Sourcing requirements that are pretextual (designed to generate rebates rather than maintain quality) may violate relationship laws and antitrust principles
- **Competitive Pricing** / Some states require that approved supplier pricing be competitive with comparable market alternatives / **Consequence**: Excessive pricing through mandated suppliers = potential UDAP violation; franchisee may seek damages
- **Supplier Rebates** / If franchisor or affiliates receive rebates from designated suppliers, disclosure may be required / **Consequence**: Undisclosed rebates = potential fraud; must be disclosed in FDD Item 8; see `ftc-franchise-rule.md`

### Churning Prohibition

- **Definition** / Churning occurs when a franchisor terminates or refuses to renew a franchise in order to acquire the location or customer base for a company-owned unit or new franchisee / **Consequence**: Prohibited under relationship laws of several states; considered bad faith termination
- **Evidence** / Franchisor opens company-owned or new franchise unit in same territory shortly after termination/non-renewal / **Consequence**: Timing proximity creates inference of churning; franchisor bears burden of showing legitimate cause

### Key State Relationship Laws

#### California — Franchise Relations Act (Bus & Prof Code 20000-20043)
- **Good Cause** / Required for termination and non-renewal
- **Notice** / Written notice specifying grounds; reasonable opportunity to cure
- **Transfer** / Cannot unreasonably withhold consent to transfer
- **Consequence**: Strong franchisee protections; active enforcement

#### Iowa — Iowa Franchise Act (Iowa Code 523H)
- **Good Cause** / Required for termination
- **Notice** / 30 days minimum; 90 days for non-renewal
- **Encroachment** / Prohibits unreasonable encroachment
- **Consequence**: Among the most comprehensive state franchise relationship laws

#### New Jersey — Franchise Practices Act (N.J.S.A. 56:10-1 et seq.)
- **Good Cause** / Required for termination, non-renewal, and transfer denial
- **Notice** / 60 days written notice
- **Repurchase** / Fair market value repurchase obligation
- **Consequence**: Powerful franchisee protections; frequently litigated

#### Minnesota — Franchise Act (Minn. Stat. 80C)
- **Good Cause** / Required for termination
- **Notice** / 90 days; 180 days for non-renewal
- **Transfer** / Cannot unreasonably withhold
- **Venue** / Prohibits out-of-state venue requirements for Minnesota franchisees
- **Consequence**: Most favorable to franchisees; restricts many standard franchise agreement provisions

### Contractual Provisions vs. State Law

- **Supremacy of State Law** / State relationship law requirements cannot be waived by contract; contractual provisions less protective than state law are void / **Consequence**: Franchise agreement that purports to allow termination without cause is unenforceable in good-cause states
- **Choice of Law** / Franchise agreements typically select franchisor's home state law; many relationship law states prohibit choice-of-law provisions that would deprive franchisee of state protections / **Consequence**: Choice-of-law clause may be unenforceable as to franchisees in states that prohibit waiver; must apply state law where franchise is located
- **Merger/Integration Clause** / Does not prevent application of state relationship law protections / **Consequence**: Statutory rights exist independent of contract; integration clause cannot exclude them

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Antitrust** (`antitrust/`): encroachment and sourcing restrictions may implicate competition law
- **Employment** (`employment/`): post-termination non-compete enforceability; employee transition upon termination

## State Franchise Laws

# State Franchise Laws

## Applicability

Applies to franchisors offering or selling franchises in states that require franchise registration, impose additional disclosure requirements, or regulate business opportunities more broadly. While the FTC Franchise Rule applies nationwide, state franchise laws add registration requirements, merit review, and enforcement mechanisms that vary significantly by jurisdiction.

## Key Requirements

### Registration States

- **15 Registration Jurisdictions** / California, Hawaii, Illinois, Indiana, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota, Rhode Island, South Dakota, Virginia, Washington, Wisconsin, and the District of Columbia require franchise registration before offering or selling franchises / **Consequence**: Offering or selling a franchise without registration = violation; state may impose stop orders, rescission, penalties; franchisor and its officers may be personally liable
- **Filing Review** / State examiners review FDD for completeness, accuracy, and compliance; may issue comment letters requiring amendments / **Timeline**: Initial review typically 30-90 days; comment responses extend timeline; some states require pre-effective review / **Consequence**: Cannot sell in the state until registration is effective; selling during review period = violation
- **Annual Renewal** / Most states require annual renewal of registration, typically 30-120 days after fiscal year end (concurrent with FDD update) / **Consequence**: Lapsed registration = cannot sell; must file renewal before expiration or cease sales
- **Effectiveness** / Registration effective upon state approval (or deemed effective after review period in some states); some states allow registration by coordination with other states / **Consequence**: Must track effectiveness dates; offering before effective date = violation

### Material Change Amendments

- **Trigger** / Any material change to FDD items requires amendment filing within prescribed period / **Timeline**: Most states require filing within 30 days of material change; some require pre-sale filing before using amended FDD / **Consequence**: Selling with outdated FDD = violation; even if FTC-compliant, state amendment timing may differ
- **Material Changes** / Changes to: fees, litigation, bankruptcy, financial statements, officers, business model, territory, and any information that would reasonably affect a prospective franchisee's decision / **Consequence**: Failure to identify material change = continuing violation for each franchise sold with stale disclosure

### State-Specific Requirements

#### California
- **Registration** / Department of Financial Protection and Innovation (DFPI); California Franchise Investment Law (Corp Code 31000-31516) / **Consequence**: Among the most rigorous review processes; examiners may require substantive changes to FDD
- **Franchise Relations** / California Franchise Relations Act (Bus & Prof Code 20000-20043) provides relationship protections (see `relationship-laws.md`) / **Consequence**: Cannot terminate, refuse to renew, or restrict transfer without good cause and proper notice

#### New York
- **Registration** / Department of Law, Investor Protection Bureau / **Consequence**: Detailed review; frequently issues comment letters on Item 19 and financial statement issues
- **Surety Bond** / May require surety bond as condition of registration if franchisor's financial condition is weak / **Consequence**: Bond requirement can be substantial; failure to post = registration denied

#### Illinois
- **Registration** / Office of Attorney General; Illinois Franchise Disclosure Act (815 ILCS 705) / **Consequence**: Broad definition of franchise; captures arrangements some other states might not

#### Minnesota
- **Registration** / Department of Commerce; Minnesota Franchise Act (Minn. Stat. 80C) / **Consequence**: Extensive relationship protections; restricts certain franchise agreement terms (see `relationship-laws.md`)

### Filing Fee States (Non-Registration)

- **States with Filing Requirements** / Several states require notice filings or exemption filings without full registration review (e.g., Connecticut, Florida, Kentucky, Nebraska, Oregon, Texas, Utah) / **Consequence**: Filing requirements vary; some require filing before first sale; others require annual notice; failure to file = potential violation
- **Business Opportunity States** / Some states regulate franchises through broader business opportunity statutes rather than dedicated franchise laws (e.g., Connecticut, Florida, Maine, Nebraska) / **Consequence**: Business opportunity laws may have different definitions and requirements; must analyze whether arrangement triggers both franchise and business opportunity coverage

### Exemptions by State

- **Large Franchisee/Sophisticated Investor** / Many states exempt sales to experienced, well-capitalized franchisees (e.g., net worth >$5M, entity >5 years in business) / **Consequence**: Exemption must be properly claimed; reliance on exemption without meeting criteria = violation
- **Existing Franchisee** / Sales to existing franchisees for additional units or renewal may be exempt in some states / **Consequence**: Exemption conditions vary; must confirm applicability in each state
- **Minimal Sales** / Some states exempt first sale or very limited number of sales in the state / **Consequence**: Very narrow; not practical for franchise expansion
- **Large Investment** / Some states exempt transactions where the franchisee's initial investment exceeds a high threshold / **Consequence**: Thresholds vary by state; must confirm current amounts

### Franchise Broker Registration

- **State Requirements** / Several states require franchise brokers (persons who sell franchises on behalf of the franchisor) to register or be identified in the FDD / **Consequence**: Using unregistered brokers in states requiring registration = violation; franchisor may be liable for broker's actions
- **FDD Disclosure** / FTC Rule requires disclosure of franchise brokers and their compensation in the FDD / **Consequence**: Failure to disclose broker relationships = FDD deficiency

### State Enforcement and Remedies

- **State AG/Regulator Authority** / State franchise administrators may: issue stop orders, revoke/suspend registration, deny renewal, impose civil penalties, seek injunctive relief / **Consequence**: State enforcement is independent of FTC; states may be more aggressive on relationship issues
- **Private Right of Action** / Most state franchise statutes provide franchisees with private right of action for disclosure violations: rescission, damages, attorney's fees / **Consequence**: Franchisee can seek rescission of franchise agreement and recovery of investment if disclosure requirements were violated
- **Personal Liability** / Many states impose personal liability on franchisor officers and directors who authorize or participate in violations / **Consequence**: Individual liability creates strong incentive for compliance; D&O insurance may not cover franchise regulation violations
