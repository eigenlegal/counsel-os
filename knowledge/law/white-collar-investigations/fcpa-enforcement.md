# FCPA Enforcement

## Applicability

Applies to organizations and individuals subject to the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act, which prohibits bribing foreign government officials and requires accurate books and records for issuers. Covers dual DOJ/SEC enforcement tracks, resolution types, declination factors, disgorgement, and coordination with international enforcement authorities.

## Key Requirements

### FCPA Structure — Dual Track

- **Anti-Bribery Provisions (DOJ)** / Prohibit corruptly offering, paying, promising, or authorizing payment of anything of value to a foreign official to obtain or retain business / **Jurisdiction**: (1) Domestic concerns (US persons and entities); (2) issuers (companies listed on US exchanges); (3) any person acting within US territory / **Consequence**: Criminal penalties: individuals up to $250K and 5 years imprisonment; entities up to $2M per violation; alternative fines of 2x gain/loss
- **Books and Records / Internal Controls (SEC)** / Issuers must: (1) make and keep accurate books, records, and accounts (Section 13(b)(2)(A)); (2) maintain adequate internal accounting controls (Section 13(b)(2)(B)) / **Consequence**: Civil and criminal liability; no materiality threshold for books and records violations; strict liability for failure to maintain controls
- **DOJ Criminal Enforcement** / DOJ Criminal Division, Fraud Section prosecutes anti-bribery violations and willful books-and-records violations / **Consequence**: Criminal conviction; imprisonment; criminal fines; debarment from government contracting
- **SEC Civil Enforcement** / SEC Division of Enforcement pursues civil anti-bribery violations by issuers and books-and-records/internal-controls violations / **Consequence**: Civil penalties; disgorgement of profits; injunctive relief; officer/director bars

### Resolution Types

- **Guilty Plea** / Company or individual pleads guilty to FCPA charges / **Consequence**: Most severe; conviction triggers collateral consequences; reserved for most egregious cases or repeat offenders
- **Deferred Prosecution Agreement (DPA)** / Charges filed but deferred; dismissed upon compliance with terms / **Timeline**: Typically 3 years / **Consequence**: Public filing; financial penalties; compliance and cooperation obligations; monitor may be imposed
- **Non-Prosecution Agreement (NPA)** / Agreement not to prosecute; less formal than DPA / **Timeline**: Typically 3 years / **Consequence**: No charges filed; less public stigma than DPA; still involves financial penalties and compliance commitments
- **Declination** / DOJ declines to prosecute / **Consequence**: Best outcome; DOJ Corporate Enforcement Policy creates presumption of declination for voluntary self-disclosure + full cooperation + timely remediation; may require disgorgement
- **SEC Administrative Orders** / Cease-and-desist orders for books-and-records and internal-controls violations / **Consequence**: Civil penalties; disgorgement; public order; may require compliance undertakings

### Declination Factors

- **Voluntary Self-Disclosure** / Company identified and disclosed misconduct before government discovery / **Consequence**: Single most important factor; creates presumption of declination under Corporate Enforcement Policy
- **Full Cooperation** / Timely preservation and production of evidence; identification of individuals; making witnesses available; no selective disclosure / **Consequence**: Cooperation must be genuine, proactive, and complete; partial cooperation = reduced credit
- **Timely Remediation** / Root cause analysis; disciplinary action against responsible individuals; compliance program enhancements; termination of corrupt relationships / **Consequence**: Must demonstrate concrete remedial steps, not just promises; remediation should address systemic issues
- **Disgorgement** / Even in declinations, DOJ may require disgorgement of profits attributable to misconduct / **Consequence**: Declination is not cost-free; company must disgorge ill-gotten gains

### Disgorgement and Penalties

- **Disgorgement** / Return of profits obtained through corrupt conduct; calculated as net profit (revenue minus direct costs) / **Threshold**: SEC disgorgement limited by Kokesh v. SEC (5-year statute of limitations) and Liu v. SEC (cannot exceed net profits) / **Consequence**: Disgorgement can be substantial; multi-million or billion-dollar amounts in major cases
- **Criminal Fines** / Statutory: up to $2M per violation for entities; up to $250K per violation for individuals; alternative: 2x pecuniary gain or loss / **Consequence**: DOJ typically seeks penalties above statutory maximum using alternative fine provision
- **Civil Penalties** / SEC civil penalties tiered: Tier 1 ($10K-$100K), Tier 2 ($50K-$500K for fraud/deceit), Tier 3 ($100K-$1M for fraud + substantial losses) per violation / **Consequence**: Multiple violations compound rapidly
- **Credit for Parallel Payments** / DOJ and SEC coordinate to avoid duplicative penalties; credit given for payments to other authorities (foreign and domestic) / **Consequence**: Must negotiate credit across enforcement authorities; total resolution may involve 3-5+ agencies

### International Enforcement Coordination

- **Multi-Jurisdictional Investigations** / Increasing coordination between DOJ/SEC and foreign authorities (UK SFO, French PNF, Brazilian MPF, Dutch OM, Swiss OAG) / **Consequence**: Single corrupt scheme may trigger enforcement in 3-5+ jurisdictions; must coordinate defense strategy across jurisdictions
- **UK Bribery Act** / Broader than FCPA: covers commercial bribery, no facilitating payments exception, strict liability for corporate failure to prevent bribery / **Consequence**: UK nexus (even minimal) can create parallel liability; "adequate procedures" defense requires robust compliance program
- **Global Settlement Coordination** / Authorities coordinate total penalty amount and allocate credit for payments across jurisdictions / **Consequence**: Total global resolution may exceed $1B for major cases (e.g., Airbus: $3.9B; Ericsson: $1.06B)

### Individual Prosecution

- **Emphasis on Individuals** / DOJ prioritizes individual accountability; company cooperation credit requires identification of all individuals involved / **Consequence**: Corporate resolution does not protect individuals; DOJ may prosecute individuals even while company receives declination
- **Travel Act and Money Laundering** / DOJ may use Travel Act (18 U.S.C. 1952), money laundering (18 U.S.C. 1956-1957), and wire fraud (18 U.S.C. 1343) as additional charges / **Consequence**: Additional charges expand sentencing exposure; money laundering carries up to 20 years imprisonment
- **Statute of Limitations** / 5 years for FCPA anti-bribery; 5 years for books and records; conspiracy and money laundering may extend exposure / **Consequence**: Statute may be tolled by tolling agreements during cooperation; DOJ frequently obtains tolling agreements as condition of cooperation

### FCPA Compliance Program Elements

- **Tone at the Top** / Senior management commitment to anti-corruption compliance / **Consequence**: DOJ evaluates leadership's genuine commitment, not just statements
- **Risk Assessment** / Country risk, industry risk, third-party risk, transaction risk / **Consequence**: Must be specific and updated; generic assessment is insufficient
- **Third-Party Due Diligence** / Risk-based diligence on agents, consultants, JV partners, distributors / **Threshold**: Enhanced diligence for high-risk third parties (government connections, high-corruption jurisdictions, unusual payment terms) / **Consequence**: Third-party misconduct is the primary source of FCPA liability; inadequate diligence = corporate liability
- **Financial Controls** / Review and approval processes for: gifts and hospitality, travel expenses, charitable donations, political contributions, facilitation payments / **Consequence**: Corrupt payments often disguised in these categories; controls must detect anomalies
