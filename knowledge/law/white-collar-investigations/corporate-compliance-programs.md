## Corporate Compliance Programs

# Corporate Compliance Programs

## Applicability

Applies to organizations designing, implementing, or evaluating corporate compliance programs. Centers on the DOJ Evaluation of Corporate Compliance Programs (June 2020 + updates), which provides the definitive framework prosecutors use to assess program effectiveness. Relevant to sentencing mitigation, enforcement negotiations, and proactive compliance.

## Key Requirements

### DOJ Evaluation Framework — Three Fundamental Questions

#### Question 1: Is the Compliance Program Well-Designed?

- **Risk Assessment** / Company must conduct periodic, comprehensive risk assessment tailored to its specific business, industry, geography, and regulatory environment / **Threshold**: Assessment must be updated to reflect current risks (not a stale document); must cover all compliance risk areas / **Consequence**: No risk assessment = program is per se deficient; generic risk assessment not tailored to company = inadequate
- **Policies and Procedures** / Written policies that address identified risks; must be accessible, understandable, and operationalized (not just "paper" compliance) / **Consequence**: Policies that exist but are not followed = worse than no policies (shows awareness without action)
- **Training and Communication** / Risk-based training program: frequency (at minimum annual; more frequent for high-risk roles), format (interactive preferred over passive), tracking (completion rates, comprehension testing), tailored content by function and risk level / **Consequence**: 100% completion target; training must be in languages employees understand; failure to train = individual and corporate liability exposure
- **Confidential Reporting and Investigation** / Anonymous reporting mechanism (hotline, web portal); no retaliation; prompt and thorough investigation of all reports; tracking and trend analysis / **Consequence**: Underutilized hotline raises concern about awareness or trust; failure to investigate reports = evidence of program failure
- **Third-Party Management** / Risk-based due diligence on third parties (agents, distributors, joint venture partners, vendors); ongoing monitoring; contractual compliance provisions / **Consequence**: Third-party misconduct attributed to company if due diligence inadequate; high-risk jurisdictions and relationships require enhanced diligence
- **Mergers and Acquisitions** / Pre-acquisition due diligence for compliance risks; post-acquisition integration of acquired entity into compliance program / **Timeline**: Compliance integration should begin immediately post-closing; full integration within reasonable time (6-12 months typical) / **Consequence**: Acquiring company inherits liability for pre-acquisition misconduct if it knew or should have known; DOJ expects compliance diligence in M&A

#### Question 2: Is the Program Adequately Resourced and Empowered?

- **CCO Authority and Stature** / Chief Compliance Officer (or equivalent) must have: (1) sufficient seniority and authority; (2) direct access to the board or audit committee; (3) autonomy from management (especially from business functions that create compliance risk) / **Consequence**: CCO who reports only to General Counsel without board access = structural deficiency; CCO must be empowered to escalate without fear of retaliation
- **Budget and Resources** / Compliance function must have adequate budget, staffing, and technology to fulfill its mandate / **Threshold**: No specific dollar amount; DOJ assesses proportionality to company size, risk, and complexity / **Consequence**: Underfunded compliance department = evidence that company does not prioritize compliance; budget cuts following misconduct = aggravating factor
- **Board and Senior Management Engagement** / Board (or audit committee) must exercise meaningful oversight of compliance program; regular reporting from CCO; board should ask probing questions, not rubber-stamp / **Consequence**: Passive board oversight = governance failure; DOJ evaluates quality of board engagement, not just frequency of reports
- **Data and Technology** / Compliance function should have access to relevant data and analytics to detect and prevent misconduct / **Consequence**: Company that has data capabilities but does not apply them to compliance = missed opportunity that DOJ will note

#### Question 3: Does the Program Work in Practice?

- **Testing and Monitoring** / Compliance program must be tested through audits, control testing, and continuous monitoring (not just self-assessment) / **Consequence**: Untested program = no evidence it works; DOJ wants to see proactive testing, not just reactive response to problems
- **Investigation Quality** / Internal investigations must be prompt, thorough, independent, and properly scoped; root cause analysis required / **Consequence**: Superficial or outcome-oriented investigations = program failure; must demonstrate genuine truth-seeking
- **Remediation** / Misconduct must result in meaningful remediation: disciplinary action (consistently applied), control enhancements, root cause correction / **Consequence**: Failure to discipline senior employees = evidence of selective enforcement; remediation must address systemic issues, not just individual conduct
- **Continuous Improvement** / Program must evolve based on lessons learned from investigations, audits, regulatory developments, and industry trends / **Consequence**: Static program = stale program; DOJ evaluates whether program has improved over time
- **Incentive Structure** / Compensation and promotion criteria should incorporate compliance performance; DOJ 2023 update specifically evaluates: (1) compliance incorporated in compensation decisions, (2) clawback provisions for misconduct, (3) prohibition on bonuses/raises during investigation of misconduct / **Consequence**: Compensating employees despite compliance failures = undermining program; financial incentives must align with compliance expectations

### Culture of Compliance

- **Tone at the Top** / Senior leadership must visibly and consistently support compliance program; personal conduct must model expected behavior / **Consequence**: Leadership that tolerates "gray areas" or prioritizes revenue over compliance = culture problem; DOJ evaluates whether leadership walked the talk
- **Tone at the Middle** / Middle management must translate senior leadership's message into day-to-day operational reality / **Consequence**: Gap between executive statements and middle management behavior = ineffective program; training and accountability for middle managers is critical
- **Speak-Up Culture** / Employees must feel safe raising concerns without fear of retaliation; measured through surveys, hotline utilization, exit interview data / **Consequence**: Fear of retaliation = chilling effect; DOJ evaluates whether employees actually use reporting mechanisms

### USSG Chapter 8 — Compliance as Sentencing Factor

- **Effective Program** / Organization with effective compliance program at time of offense may receive reduced culpability score (3-point reduction) / **Threshold**: Program must have been in place and functioning (not adopted after the fact); high-level personnel must not have participated in or condoned the offense / **Consequence**: Culpability score reduction translates to significantly lower fine range (multiplier can drop from 2.0 to 0.4)
- **Seven Minimum Requirements** / (1) Compliance standards and procedures; (2) specific high-level oversight; (3) due care in delegating authority; (4) effective communication and training; (5) monitoring, auditing, and reporting; (6) consistent enforcement and discipline; (7) response and remediation after detection / **Consequence**: Must meet all seven; failure on any = program not "effective" for sentencing purposes
- **Post-Offense Conduct** / Even if program was not effective at time of offense, post-offense adoption of effective program and cooperation = additional sentencing credit / **Consequence**: Remediation after detection is valuable but less powerful than pre-existing effective program
