## Whistleblower

# Whistleblower Protections and Programs

## Applicability

Applies to organizations subject to federal and state whistleblower protection statutes, including public companies, government contractors, healthcare providers, and financial institutions. Covers Sarbanes-Oxley Section 806, Dodd-Frank SEC Whistleblower Program, False Claims Act qui tam provisions, and state whistleblower laws.

## Key Requirements

### Sarbanes-Oxley Section 806

- **Covered Employees** / Employees of publicly traded companies and their subsidiaries, contractors, subcontractors, and agents / **Consequence**: Broad coverage; includes employees of private subsidiaries of public companies
- **Protected Activity** / Reporting fraud against shareholders, securities violations, wire/mail/bank fraud, or any federal offense relating to fraud against shareholders; filing, testifying, or participating in SEC proceedings / **Consequence**: Protection extends to internal reporting (supervisor, compliance, audit committee) and external reporting (federal agencies, Congress, supervisors)
- **Filing Requirement** / Complaint must be filed with OSHA within 180 days of the adverse action / **Timeline**: 180 calendar days — strict statute of limitations / **Consequence**: Late filing = claim dismissed; no tolling for continuing violations in most circuits
- **OSHA Process** / OSHA investigates; preliminary order within 60 days; either party may request ALJ hearing within 30 days of OSHA findings / **Consequence**: OSHA can order preliminary reinstatement while case is adjudicated
- **Remedies** / Reinstatement, back pay with interest, compensatory damages (including attorney fees, litigation costs, expert witness fees), special damages / **Consequence**: No punitive damages under SOX 806; but compensatory damages can include emotional distress
- **Criminal Prohibition** / SOX Section 1107: criminal penalties for retaliation against any person who provides truthful information to law enforcement about a federal offense / **Consequence**: Fines and/or up to 10 years imprisonment; applies to all entities, not just public companies

### Dodd-Frank SEC Whistleblower Program

- **Eligibility** / Any individual who voluntarily provides original information to the SEC that leads to successful enforcement action with sanctions exceeding $1 million / **Consequence**: Only individuals (not companies) eligible; information must be "original" (derived from independent knowledge or analysis)
- **Award Amount** / 10-30% of monetary sanctions collected in SEC enforcement action exceeding $1 million / **Threshold**: $1M minimum sanctions threshold; SEC has discretion on percentage within 10-30% range based on significance of information, cooperation, programmatic interest / **Consequence**: Awards have exceeded $100M for single whistleblowers; program has awarded over $1 billion total through 2024
- **Anti-Retaliation** / Prohibits retaliation by employers against individuals who: (1) provide information to SEC, (2) initiate or assist in SEC investigation, (3) make disclosures required by SOX, securities laws, or other laws under SEC jurisdiction / **Consequence**: Private right of action in federal court (no OSHA filing required); 2x back pay, reinstatement, attorney fees; 6-year statute of limitations (3 years from when facts known, but no more than 6 years from violation)
- **Internal Reporting Credit** / SEC considers whether whistleblower reported internally first (120-day safe harbor: report internally, then to SEC within 120 days and receive credit as of internal reporting date) / **Consequence**: Internal reporting encouraged but not required; organizations benefit from effective internal reporting channels
- **Confidentiality** / SEC prohibited from disclosing whistleblower identity except as required by court or in connection with public proceeding / **Consequence**: Whistleblower identity protected; but practical identification may occur through nature of information disclosed

### False Claims Act (FCA) — Qui Tam Provisions

- **Scope** / Covers fraud against the federal government: false claims for payment, false statements, reverse false claims (concealing obligation to pay government), conspiracy / **Consequence**: Applies to government contractors, healthcare providers (Medicare/Medicaid), grant recipients, and any entity submitting claims to the government
- **Qui Tam Action** / Private individual (relator) files lawsuit on behalf of the government under seal; DOJ has 60 days (extendable) to investigate and decide whether to intervene / **Consequence**: Complaint remains under seal during government investigation; relator must serve government but not defendant during seal period
- **Relator's Share — Government Intervenes** / 15-25% of recovery if the government intervenes and proceeds with the action / **Consequence**: Government typically intervenes in cases with strong evidence; intervention significantly increases recovery likelihood
- **Relator's Share — Government Declines** / 25-30% of recovery if the government declines to intervene and relator proceeds independently / **Consequence**: Relator bears litigation costs and risk; lower success rate without government support
- **Damages and Penalties** / Treble damages (3x government's loss) plus civil penalties of $13,946-$27,894 per false claim (adjusted annually for inflation) / **Consequence**: Penalties compound rapidly with volume of false claims; single government contract with repeated false billing = massive exposure
- **Public Disclosure Bar** / Qui tam action barred if substantially based on publicly disclosed information unless relator is an "original source" (direct and independent knowledge, voluntarily disclosed to government before public disclosure) / **Consequence**: Prior government investigation, media reports, or audit findings may bar qui tam claim
- **Retaliation Protection** / FCA Section 3730(h): protection for employees who investigate, initiate, testify, or assist in FCA action / **Consequence**: 2x back pay, reinstatement, special damages, attorney fees; 3-year statute of limitations

### State Whistleblower Laws

- **Scope** / Most states have whistleblower protection statutes covering public and/or private sector employees / **Consequence**: May provide broader protection than federal statutes; different filing requirements, statutes of limitations, and remedies
- **Key State Programs** / California (Labor Code 1102.5 — broad protection, punitive damages available), New York (Labor Law 740 — 2 years SOL, reinstatement + back pay + front pay + attorney fees + $10K civil penalty), Illinois (Whistleblower Act — 740 ILCS 174), New Jersey (Conscientious Employee Protection Act — broad "reasonable belief" standard) / **Consequence**: Must analyze state law in each relevant jurisdiction; state protections often run parallel to federal
- **State False Claims Acts** / 30+ states and DC have enacted state false claims acts modeled on federal FCA, covering state Medicaid funds / **Consequence**: Parallel state and federal qui tam actions possible; relator may recover under both

### Organizational Best Practices

- **Internal Reporting Channels** / Establish anonymous hotline/reporting system; communicate availability regularly; ensure reports reach appropriate level (compliance, audit committee) / **Consequence**: Effective internal channels may: (1) resolve issues before government involvement, (2) demonstrate compliance culture, (3) qualify for cooperation credit
- **Non-Retaliation Policy** / Explicit written policy prohibiting retaliation; train managers on compliance; investigate and address any suspected retaliation immediately / **Consequence**: Policy alone is insufficient — must be enforced; single retaliation incident undermines entire compliance program credibility
- **Investigation Protocol** / All whistleblower reports investigated promptly and documented; findings reported to appropriate governance body / **Consequence**: Failure to investigate = evidence of indifference; regulators and courts view unfavorably
