# US and International Sanctions

## Overview
The US maintains comprehensive and targeted economic sanctions programs administered by the Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) within the US Department of the Treasury. Sanctions restrict or prohibit transactions with specified countries, regions, entities, and individuals. Violations carry severe civil and criminal penalties, and sanctions compliance is a strict liability regime — violations occur regardless of intent or knowledge.

## Key Requirements
- **Comprehensive sanctions programs:** Prohibit virtually all transactions and dealings with the sanctioned country/region, including Cuba, Iran, North Korea, Syria, and the Crimea/Donetsk/Luhansk regions of Ukraine. Specific prohibitions include imports, exports, financial transactions, investment, and provision of services.
- **Targeted/list-based sanctions:** Block the property and interests in property of designated persons and entities on the SDN (Specially Designated Nationals and Blocked Persons) List. Any transaction with an SDN or entity 50%+ owned by one or more SDNs is prohibited. Other restrictive lists include the Sectoral Sanctions Identifications (SSI) List, Non-SDN Menu-Based Sanctions List, and Foreign Sanctions Evaders List.
- **50% Rule:** An entity that is 50% or more owned, directly or indirectly, by one or more blocked persons is itself considered blocked, even if the entity is not separately listed on the SDN List. Compliance programs must trace ownership to identify blocked entities.
- **Secondary sanctions:** Some programs impose sanctions on non-US persons who engage in certain transactions with sanctioned targets. These can affect non-US entities' access to the US financial system.
- **OFAC licensing:** Specific and general licenses authorize transactions that would otherwise be prohibited. General licenses authorize categories of transactions without requiring individual application. Specific licenses are granted case-by-case upon application to OFAC.
- **Screening obligations:** There is no explicit regulatory requirement to screen, but OFAC's strict liability regime creates a de facto obligation. Compliance programs must screen all counterparties, customers, vendors, and beneficial owners against OFAC lists. Screening should occur at onboarding, periodically during the relationship, and whenever lists are updated.
- **Voluntary self-disclosure:** OFAC encourages voluntary self-disclosure of apparent violations. Self-disclosure is a significant mitigating factor in penalty determinations and can reduce civil monetary penalties by up to 50%.
- **EU, UK, and multilateral sanctions:** Companies with international operations must also comply with EU sanctions regulations (administered by member state authorities), UK sanctions (OFSI), UN Security Council sanctions, and other jurisdictions' sanctions regimes. These may differ from US sanctions in scope and application.

## Common Contract Issues
- Sanctions compliance representations and warranties — counterparties should represent that they are not SDNs, 50%-or-more owned by SDNs, located in comprehensively sanctioned jurisdictions, or acting on behalf of sanctioned persons.
- Anti-diversion and end-use clauses ensuring that goods, technology, or services will not be diverted to sanctioned destinations or persons.
- Sanctions compliance obligations requiring counterparties to maintain screening procedures and notify of any sanctions exposure.
- Payment terms specifying that all payments must be made through the US financial system and in compliance with OFAC regulations; blocked transactions must be reported.
- Force majeure clauses that address sanctions-related performance prohibitions — sanctions compliance is a legal obligation, not merely a force majeure event.
- Termination rights triggered by sanctions designation of a counterparty or changes in sanctions programs affecting the transaction.
- Sanctions carve-outs in indemnification provisions recognizing that compliance with sanctions is a legal obligation that cannot be indemnified against.
- International contracts that must address which sanctions regimes apply (US, EU, UK) and how conflicts between regimes are resolved.

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **International Trade (Export Controls):** Sanctions and export controls are distinct regimes but often overlap. A transaction may require analysis under both OFAC sanctions and EAR/ITAR export controls.
- **Financial Services (KYC/AML):** OFAC screening is a component of BSA/AML compliance. Financial institutions must integrate sanctions screening into customer onboarding and transaction monitoring.
- **Corporate (M&A):** Sanctions due diligence is critical in cross-border M&A. Target company's historical and ongoing dealings with sanctioned jurisdictions and persons must be thoroughly investigated.
- **Data Privacy:** Sanctions screening involves processing personal data of counterparties, which must comply with applicable data privacy laws (particularly GDPR when screening EU persons).
- **Employment:** Foreign national employees from sanctioned jurisdictions may require OFAC license for employment in certain circumstances. Deemed export analysis is also required for access to controlled technology.
- **IP and Technology:** Technology licensing and SaaS agreements must include sanctions compliance provisions and may require geo-blocking for comprehensively sanctioned jurisdictions.
