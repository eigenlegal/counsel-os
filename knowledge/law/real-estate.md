## Commercial Leases

# Commercial Leases

## Applicability

Applies to any lease of commercial real property, including office, retail, industrial, and mixed-use spaces. Covers lease negotiation, administration, assignment, subletting, default, and termination.

## Key Requirements

### Lease Structures

- **Triple Net (NNN)** / Tenant pays base rent plus all operating expenses (taxes, insurance, CAM). Dominant in single-tenant retail and industrial. / **Consequence**: Tenant bears full cost volatility; must audit CAM reconciliations annually or risk overpayment.
- **Gross Lease** / Landlord includes operating expenses in base rent, often with a base-year stop. / **Consequence**: Tenant protected from cost increases only up to the base-year amount; expenses above the stop pass through.
- **Modified Gross** / Hybrid — landlord and tenant split operating expenses per the lease terms. / **Consequence**: Allocation must be explicit; ambiguous language defaults to local custom, creating dispute risk.

### CAM (Common Area Maintenance)

- **CAM Reconciliation** / Landlord must provide annual reconciliation within **90-120 days** after calendar year end (per most leases). / **Consequence**: Failure to reconcile timely may waive right to collect shortfalls in some jurisdictions.
- **CAM Cap** / Negotiable annual cap on controllable CAM increases, typically **3-5% per year**. / **Consequence**: Without a cap, tenant exposure to cost increases is unlimited.
- **Audit Rights** / Tenant right to audit landlord's CAM books, typically within **12-24 months** of reconciliation. / **Consequence**: Failure to exercise audit right within the window waives the right for that year.

### Tenant Improvement (TI) Allowances

- **TI Allowance** / Landlord contribution toward build-out, typically **$30-$80/SF** for office (market-dependent). / **Consequence**: Unused TI typically cannot be applied to rent; use-it-or-lose-it deadlines of **12-18 months** are common.
- **Ownership of Improvements** / Improvements generally become landlord's property upon installation unless lease specifies otherwise. / **Consequence**: Tenant may be required to remove improvements and restore premises at lease end, at significant cost.

### Rent Escalation

- **Fixed Increases** / Annual bumps of **2-3%** per year, compounding. / **Consequence**: Predictable for both parties; tenant locked in regardless of market movement.
- **CPI Escalation** / Tied to Consumer Price Index, sometimes with a floor (e.g., **2%**) and cap (e.g., **5%**). / **Consequence**: In high-inflation periods, CPI escalation without a cap can dramatically increase rent obligations.
- **Fair Market Value Reset** / Rent resets to FMV at option renewal, often with appraisal mechanism. / **Consequence**: Dispute-prone without clear appraisal procedures and timeline (typically **60-90 days** before option exercise).

### Assignment and Subletting

- **Landlord Consent** / Most commercial leases require landlord consent, not to be unreasonably withheld. / **Consequence**: Unreasonable withholding may constitute breach; "reasonable" is fact-specific and jurisdiction-dependent.
- **Recapture Rights** / Landlord may recapture the space upon assignment/subletting request. / **Consequence**: Tenant loses the space entirely; effectively a termination right for landlord.
- **Profit Sharing** / Landlord often entitled to **50%** of sublease profits (excess over base rent). / **Consequence**: Reduces tenant's economic incentive to sublease.

### Default and Cure

- **Monetary Default** / Tenant typically has **5-10 business days** to cure after notice. / **Consequence**: Failure to cure triggers landlord's remedies including lease termination and damages.
- **Non-Monetary Default** / Cure period of **30 days** typical, extendable if tenant is diligently pursuing cure. / **Consequence**: Some defaults (bankruptcy filing, assignment for benefit of creditors) may be incurable.
- **Landlord Default** / Tenant must provide notice; cure period of **30-60 days** typical. / **Consequence**: Tenant remedies often limited to offset against rent or self-help; lease termination for landlord default is rare.

### Holdover

- **Holdover Rate** / Typically **150-200%** of the last month's rent during holdover period. / **Consequence**: Creates strong economic incentive to vacate; holdover tenant may also be liable for consequential damages to landlord.

### Specialty Retail Provisions

- **Co-Tenancy** / Tenant's rent obligation may be reduced or terminated if anchor tenant vacates. / **Consequence**: Landlord faces cascading vacancy risk; triggers often require **60-90 day** cure periods.
- **Radius Restrictions** / Tenant prohibited from operating competing store within **3-5 mile** radius. / **Consequence**: Violation may convert to percentage rent or constitute default.
- **Percentage Rent** / Tenant pays additional rent based on gross sales exceeding a breakpoint. / **Consequence**: Tenant must maintain accurate sales records; landlord audit rights apply.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Environmental & ESG** — Environmental conditions affecting leased premises (hazardous materials, indoor air quality)
- **Data Privacy** — Tenant data in smart buildings, security camera footage
- **Construction** — Build-out of tenant improvements, mechanic's lien exposure for landlord
- **Insurance** — Tenant's insurance requirements, waiver of subrogation, landlord as additional insured

## Construction

# Construction Law

## Applicability

Applies when a matter involves construction contracts, mechanic's liens, payment or performance bonds, delay claims, change orders, or construction disputes. Covers both private and public construction projects.

## Key Requirements

### Mechanic's Liens

- **Preliminary Notice** / Most states require a preliminary notice from subcontractors and suppliers within **20-30 days** of first furnishing labor or materials. / **Consequence**: Failure to serve timely preliminary notice extinguishes lien rights entirely in most states (California requires 20 days; Texas requires notice by the 15th of the second month).
- **Filing Deadlines** / Lien must be recorded within **30-120 days** of completion or last furnishing, depending on state. / **Consequence**: California: **90 days** after completion; New York: **8 months** (private) / **30 days** (public improvement); Texas: **15th of the 4th month** after last furnishing. Missing the deadline permanently waives the lien.
- **Lien Priority** / Mechanic's liens generally relate back to the date of first visible work on the project. / **Consequence**: In many states, mechanic's liens prime subsequently recorded mortgages; lenders require lien waivers to protect priority.
- **Enforcement** / Lien must be enforced by filing suit within **1-2 years** of recording (varies by state). / **Consequence**: Failure to timely enforce releases the lien; frivolous lien claims may expose the claimant to penalties.
- **Lien Waivers** / Conditional waivers (effective upon payment) and unconditional waivers (effective immediately). / **Consequence**: Many states prescribe statutory waiver forms (e.g., California Civil Code Section 8132-8138); non-compliant forms may be unenforceable.

### Miller Act (Federal Projects)

- **Payment Bond** / Required for all federal construction contracts exceeding **$35,000**. / **Consequence**: Subcontractors and suppliers may assert claims against the payment bond in lieu of lien rights (federal property cannot be liened).
- **Performance Bond** / Required for federal contracts exceeding **$150,000**. / **Consequence**: Surety must complete the project or pay damages if the principal contractor defaults.
- **Notice Requirements** / Claimants without a direct contract with the prime contractor must give written notice to the prime within **90 days** of last furnishing. / **Consequence**: Failure to provide timely notice bars the bond claim.
- **Suit Deadline** / Bond claim suit must be filed within **1 year** after last furnishing but not before **90 days** after notice. / **Consequence**: Missing either the floor or ceiling deadline bars recovery.

### AIA Contract Forms

- **A101** / Standard owner-contractor agreement (stipulated sum). Most widely used private construction contract in the U.S. / **Consequence**: Incorporates A201 General Conditions by reference; modifying A101 without conforming A201 creates internal conflicts.
- **A201** / General conditions of the contract for construction. Governs administration, payment, changes, claims, and termination. / **Consequence**: Section 15 (claims and disputes) requires initial decision by the architect before arbitration/litigation; bypassing the process may waive claims.
- **Retainage** / Typically **5-10%** of each payment withheld until substantial completion. / **Consequence**: Some states cap retainage (e.g., **5%** in many states) and require prompt release after completion; withholding beyond statutory limits triggers penalties and interest.

### Delay Claims

- **Excusable Delay** / Caused by events beyond the contractor's control (weather, force majeure, unforeseen conditions). / **Consequence**: Entitles contractor to time extension but generally NOT additional compensation unless the delay is also compensable.
- **Compensable Delay** / Caused by the owner or owner's agents (design errors, change orders, access delays). / **Consequence**: Entitles contractor to both time extension AND additional compensation (extended general conditions, escalation costs).
- **Concurrent Delay** / Both owner and contractor contribute to the same delay period. / **Consequence**: Most jurisdictions award time extension but no additional compensation; some jurisdictions apportion fault.
- **No-Damages-for-Delay Clauses** / Contract provision barring monetary recovery for delays. / **Consequence**: Enforceable in most states but subject to exceptions: active interference, bad faith, delays not contemplated by the parties, and delays of unreasonable duration.
- **Float** / Schedule slack between activities. / **Consequence**: Ownership of float is contested; best practice is to address float ownership explicitly in the contract (owner-owned, contractor-owned, or shared).

### Change Orders

- **Written Requirement** / Most construction contracts require changes to be authorized in writing before work begins. / **Consequence**: Oral changes may be unenforceable under the contract's changes clause, but constructive change doctrine may provide relief.
- **Pricing** / Change order pricing methods: agreed lump sum, unit prices, or cost-plus with a markup of **10-15%** (overhead and profit). / **Consequence**: Failure to agree on price before performing changed work creates dispute risk; contractor should reserve rights in writing.
- **Constructive Change** / Owner conduct that effectively requires extra work without a formal change order (defective specifications, acceleration). / **Consequence**: Contractor may recover if it can show the work was beyond the original scope and was directed or necessitated by the owner.

### Substantial Completion

- **Definition** / The stage when the work is sufficiently complete that the owner can occupy or use it for its intended purpose. / **Consequence**: Triggers multiple contractual events: commencement of warranty period (typically **1 year**), release of retainage, shift of risk of loss, and start of certain limitation periods.
- **Punch List** / List of minor items remaining after substantial completion, typically completed within **30-60 days**. / **Consequence**: Owner may withhold **150-200%** of the estimated punch list value until completion; unreasonable punch lists may constitute bad faith.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Real Estate** — Construction liens encumber the real property; lien priority affects mortgage lenders
- **Environmental & ESG** — Construction activities trigger environmental permits (stormwater, dust, hazardous materials)
- **Insurance** — Builder's risk insurance, contractor liability insurance, surety bonds
- **Employment** — Prevailing wage requirements on public projects (Davis-Bacon Act for federal, state equivalents)

## Easements Encumbrances

# Easements and Encumbrances

## Applicability

Applies when a real property matter involves easements (creation, scope, termination), encumbrances on title, encroachments, covenants, conditions, and restrictions (CC&Rs), or title exceptions. Critical in acquisitions, development, and financing of real property.

## Key Requirements

### Easement Types

- **Appurtenant Easement** / Benefits a specific parcel (dominant estate) by burdening another parcel (servient estate); runs with the land. / **Consequence**: Transfers automatically with the dominant estate; buyer of the servient estate takes subject to the easement even without actual notice if properly recorded.
- **Easement in Gross** / Benefits a person or entity rather than a parcel (e.g., utility easements). / **Consequence**: Traditional rule: not assignable. Modern rule: commercial easements in gross ARE assignable (Restatement Third); personal easements in gross generally are not.
- **Negative Easement** / Right to prevent the servient estate owner from doing something (e.g., blocking light, view, or air). / **Consequence**: Disfavored at common law; limited to light, air, support, and stream flow. Conservation easements are the primary modern expansion.

### Creation Methods

- **Express Easement** / Created by written agreement or deed; must satisfy the Statute of Frauds. / **Consequence**: Must be recorded to provide constructive notice to subsequent purchasers; unrecorded express easements may be defeated by a bona fide purchaser.
- **Implied Easement** / Arises from prior use at the time of severance when use was apparent, continuous, and reasonably necessary. / **Consequence**: Does not appear in the deed or on record; title insurance may or may not cover losses from implied easements depending on the policy.
- **Prescriptive Easement** / Acquired through open, notorious, continuous, hostile use for the statutory period (typically **5-20 years** depending on state). / **Consequence**: California: **5 years**; New York: **10 years**; Texas: **10 years**. Once established, has the same effect as an express easement; can be prevented by permission, posting, or legal action during the prescriptive period.
- **Easement by Necessity** / Arises when a parcel is landlocked and has no access to a public road; requires common grantor. / **Consequence**: Automatically created upon severance of the landlocked parcel; scope is limited to the necessity (access only); terminates when necessity ends.

### Termination of Easements

- **Release** / Written release from the easement holder, must satisfy the Statute of Frauds. / **Consequence**: Must be recorded to clear title; failure to record leaves the easement of record even if released.
- **Abandonment** / Requires affirmative act demonstrating intent to abandon (nonuse alone is insufficient in most jurisdictions). / **Consequence**: Difficult to prove; mere nonuse for **10-20 years** without affirmative act is generally insufficient.
- **Merger** / Same party acquires both the dominant and servient estates. / **Consequence**: Easement is extinguished by merger; if the properties are later separated, the easement does not automatically revive.
- **Prescription** / Servient owner blocks the easement holder's use openly and continuously for the prescriptive period. / **Consequence**: Same statutory periods as prescriptive easement creation apply; hostile obstruction must be continuous.

### Utility Easements

- **Scope** / Typically **10-20 feet** wide for underground utilities; **50-100 feet** or more for overhead transmission lines. / **Consequence**: Development within utility easement areas is prohibited or severely restricted; encroachment into utility easements may require relocation at the property owner's expense (**$50,000-$500,000+** for major utilities).
- **Relocation** / Easement holder generally has the right to access the easement for maintenance and repair. / **Consequence**: Property owner cannot interfere with utility access; planned development must accommodate existing utility easements or negotiate relocation.

### Title Exceptions

- **Standard Exceptions** / Title insurance standard exceptions: survey matters, easements not of record, parties in possession, mechanic's liens, taxes not yet due. / **Consequence**: Standard exceptions can be removed by providing a survey (ALTA/NSPS), seller's affidavit, and tax certificates; failure to remove creates coverage gaps.
- **Special Exceptions** / Specific recorded instruments that encumber the property (recorded easements, CC&Rs, mortgages, judgments). / **Consequence**: Each special exception must be reviewed for impact on intended use; unacceptable exceptions should be cleared before closing or addressed through indemnification.

### Encroachments

- **Discovery** / Encroachments identified through ALTA/NSPS survey comparing improvements to boundary lines and easements. / **Consequence**: Encroachment by the subject property onto adjacent property creates trespass liability; encroachment by adjacent property may ripen into prescriptive easement if not addressed within the statutory period.
- **Resolution** / Options: removal, purchase of encroached area, license agreement, or easement grant. / **Consequence**: Forced removal may be ordered by court if encroachment is intentional; courts may deny removal and award damages if removal would be inequitable (relative hardship doctrine).

### CC&Rs (Covenants, Conditions, and Restrictions)

- **Enforcement** / CC&Rs are enforced by the HOA, declarant, or any lot owner within the development. / **Consequence**: Violation of CC&Rs may result in injunctive relief, fines, or liens on the property; some CC&Rs include attorney's fees provisions for enforcement.
- **Amendment** / Typically requires approval of **67-75%** of lot owners; some provisions may require unanimous consent. / **Consequence**: Amendment thresholds vary by state law and the CC&R document; certain provisions (e.g., anti-discrimination) cannot be enforced even if in the recorded CC&Rs.
- **Duration** / CC&Rs typically run for **20-30 years** with automatic renewal periods of **10 years** unless amended or terminated. / **Consequence**: CC&Rs from decades-old developments may contain outdated or unenforceable provisions; review for current compliance and enforceability.
- **Reasonableness** / Courts apply a reasonableness standard to CC&R enforcement; restrictions must bear a rational relationship to property values or community welfare. / **Consequence**: Unreasonable restrictions may be struck down; racial, religious, and other discriminatory covenants are unenforceable under the Fair Housing Act.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Real Estate Transactions** — Title examination, survey review, exception clearance before closing
- **Construction** — Utility easement conflicts with construction plans, mechanic's lien priority vs. recorded easements
- **Zoning and Land Use** — Easement restrictions may limit development beyond zoning allowances
- **Environmental & ESG** — Environmental easements, conservation easements, deed restrictions related to contamination

## Environmental Covenants

# Environmental Covenants in Real Estate

## Applicability

Applies when a real property transaction involves potential environmental contamination, remediation obligations, Phase I or Phase II environmental site assessments, brownfield redevelopment, or environmental indemnities. Critical for any acquisition, lease, or financing of commercial or industrial property.

## Key Requirements

### CERCLA Liability Framework

- **Strict Liability** / Liability under CERCLA Section 107 does not require fault, negligence, or intent. / **Consequence**: A current owner may be liable for contamination caused decades ago by a prior owner; "I didn't cause it" is not a defense.
- **Joint and Several Liability** / Any single potentially responsible party (PRP) may be held liable for the entire cleanup cost. / **Consequence**: EPA may pursue the most solvent PRP for full remediation costs, even if that party contributed only a fraction of the contamination; contribution actions under Section 113(f) are the only recourse.
- **Retroactive Liability** / CERCLA applies to contamination that occurred before the statute's 1980 enactment. / **Consequence**: Pre-1980 industrial activities can trigger full cleanup liability for current owners; historical use review is essential in due diligence.
- **PRP Categories** / Current owners/operators, prior owners/operators at time of disposal, generators who arranged for disposal, transporters who selected the disposal site. / **Consequence**: All four categories face joint and several liability; lenders may be treated as "owners" if they participate in management (secured creditor exemption under Section 101(20)(E) is narrow).

### Phase I Environmental Site Assessment

- **ASTM E1527-21 Standard** / The current standard for All Appropriate Inquiry (AAI) Phase I ESAs, required for the innocent purchaser defense. / **Consequence**: A Phase I conducted under the superseded E1527-13 standard after **February 13, 2024** does not satisfy AAI; the innocent purchaser defense is unavailable.
- **Shelf Life** / Phase I reports are valid for **180 days** from the date of the site visit; certain components must be updated if the report is **90 days** old. / **Consequence**: Using an expired Phase I eliminates the innocent purchaser defense; must update or commission a new Phase I before closing.
- **Recognized Environmental Conditions (RECs)** / Phase I identifies RECs, Controlled RECs (CRECs), and Historical RECs (HRECs) that indicate potential contamination. / **Consequence**: A REC typically triggers recommendation for Phase II investigation; proceeding without Phase II when RECs are identified undermines the innocent purchaser defense.
- **Scope Limitations** / Standard Phase I does not cover asbestos, lead-based paint, radon, mold, or wetlands unless specifically requested. / **Consequence**: Additional assessments for non-scope items must be separately contracted; their absence does not eliminate liability for those conditions.

### Phase II Environmental Site Assessment

- **Sampling and Analysis** / Phase II involves subsurface investigation — soil borings, groundwater monitoring wells, soil vapor sampling — to confirm or deny the presence of contamination. / **Consequence**: Phase II costs range from **$10,000 to $100,000+** depending on scope; results determine whether remediation is required and estimated costs.
- **Regulatory Thresholds** / Contamination compared against federal (EPA RSLs) and state cleanup standards. / **Consequence**: Exceedance of applicable screening levels triggers reporting obligations in most states within **24 hours to 30 days** of discovery; failure to report is independently actionable.

### Brownfield Redevelopment

- **Voluntary Cleanup Programs (VCPs)** / State programs allowing prospective purchasers to remediate and obtain liability protection (comfort letters, no-further-action letters, covenants not to sue). / **Consequence**: VCP enrollment must occur BEFORE or concurrent with acquisition; post-acquisition enrollment may not provide retroactive protection.
- **Brownfield Tax Incentives** / Federal tax deduction under IRC Section 198 for environmental remediation expenditures at qualified contaminated sites (subject to periodic reauthorization). / **Consequence**: Deduction availability depends on congressional reauthorization; verify current status before relying on the incentive in transaction economics.

### Environmental Indemnities

- **Seller Indemnification** / Seller indemnifies buyer for pre-existing environmental conditions, typically with a **survival period of 5-10 years** or aligned with the applicable statute of limitations. / **Consequence**: Indemnities are only as valuable as the indemnitor's financial capacity; environmental insurance should be considered for high-risk transactions.
- **Indemnity Scope** / Must cover investigation costs, remediation costs, regulatory penalties, third-party claims, diminution in value, and natural resource damages. / **Consequence**: Narrow indemnities that exclude categories (e.g., "remediation only") leave buyer exposed to regulatory fines, third-party tort claims, and natural resource damages.
- **Escrow / Holdback** / Portion of purchase price held in escrow (typically **10-15%**) pending completion of environmental investigation or remediation. / **Consequence**: Escrow period must align with anticipated remediation timeline; premature release eliminates buyer's recourse if additional contamination is discovered.

### Innocent Purchaser Defense

- **Requirements** / All Appropriate Inquiry (AAI) before acquisition, no knowledge of contamination at time of purchase, exercise of due care after discovery, cooperation with response actions. / **Consequence**: Defense is affirmative — buyer bears the burden of proof; any gap in the AAI process (expired Phase I, failure to investigate RECs) defeats the defense entirely.
- **Continuing Obligations** / After discovery of contamination, innocent purchaser must take reasonable steps to stop continuing releases, prevent exposure, and cooperate with EPA. / **Consequence**: Failure to meet continuing obligations forfeits innocent purchaser status, exposing the buyer to full CERCLA liability.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Environmental & ESG** — CERCLA liability framework, regulatory compliance, climate disclosure for contaminated assets
- **Real Estate Transactions** — Environmental representations and warranties, closing conditions, title exceptions for environmental liens
- **Insurance** — Environmental insurance policies (pollution legal liability, cleanup cost cap) as supplement to contractual indemnities
- **Financial Services** — Lender liability under CERCLA, secured creditor exemption, environmental due diligence for loan underwriting

## Real Estate Transactions

# Real Estate Transactions

## Applicability

Applies to the acquisition, disposition, and financing of real property, including purchase and sale agreements, title insurance, surveys, due diligence, closing procedures, and recording. Covers both commercial and residential transactions where legal counsel is involved.

## Key Requirements

### Title Insurance

- **Owner's Policy** / Protects the buyer against defects in title, liens, encumbrances, and other covered risks up to the purchase price. One-time premium at closing. / **Consequence**: Standard owner's policy excludes survey matters, unrecorded easements, and parties in possession; extended coverage (ALTA Owner's Policy) eliminates most standard exceptions for an additional **10-15%** premium.
- **Lender's Policy** / Required by virtually all mortgage lenders; protects the lender's security interest up to the loan amount. / **Consequence**: Lender's policy does NOT protect the buyer; buyer must separately obtain an owner's policy. Lender's policy coverage decreases as the loan is paid down.
- **Title Commitment** / Preliminary report issued before closing showing the current state of title, requirements for insurance, and exceptions. / **Consequence**: Buyer has a review period (typically **5-15 business days**) to object to title exceptions; failure to object within the period may waive the right to require cure.
- **Title Endorsements** / Additional coverages (zoning, access, contiguity, survey, environmental lien) added to the base policy. / **Consequence**: Key endorsements include ALTA 3.1 (zoning), ALTA 9 (restrictions), ALTA 17 (access); cost is **$50-$500** per endorsement but significantly expands coverage.
- **Premium Rates** / Title insurance premiums are regulated in most states; rates are either filed or promulgated. / **Consequence**: In Texas, rates are promulgated by the Texas Department of Insurance (no negotiation). In California and New York, rates are filed but competitive; premium can be negotiated.

### Surveys

- **ALTA/NSPS Survey** / The standard for commercial real estate transactions. Shows boundaries, improvements, easements, encroachments, setback lines, flood zones, and access. / **Consequence**: Required for removal of the standard survey exception from the title policy; cost ranges from **$3,000 to $25,000+** depending on property size and complexity.
- **Table A Items** / Optional additional survey items (parking counts, exterior dimensions of buildings, utility locations, wetlands). / **Consequence**: Table A items 1, 2, 3, 4, 6(a), 6(b), 8, 9, 10(a), 11(a), and 13 are commonly requested in commercial transactions; each adds to survey cost and timeline.
- **Survey Certification** / Survey must be certified to the buyer, lender, and title company to be relied upon. / **Consequence**: An uncertified survey has limited utility; re-certification of an existing survey is faster and cheaper than a new survey (**$500-$2,000** for re-certification vs. full cost for new survey).
- **Survey Accuracy** / Minimum accuracy standards: 1:10,000 for urban, 1:15,000 for suburban, 1:20,000 for rural. / **Consequence**: Survey below minimum accuracy may not be accepted by the title company; boundary disputes arising from inaccurate surveys may expose the surveyor to professional liability.

### Due Diligence Periods

- **Standard Period** / Commercial transactions: **30-60 days** for due diligence (inspection period). Residential: **10-17 days** typical. / **Consequence**: Due diligence period is the buyer's window to investigate and terminate without penalty (subject to contract terms); extension requests are common but not guaranteed.
- **Scope of Diligence** / Physical inspection, environmental assessment, title review, survey, zoning verification, lease audit, financial analysis, estoppel certificates. / **Consequence**: Failure to conduct thorough diligence during the period may waive objections; "as-is" clauses limit post-closing claims for conditions discoverable during diligence.
- **Estoppel Certificates** / Tenants confirm lease terms, rent amounts, security deposits, and defaults. Required within **10-15 days** of request (per most leases). / **Consequence**: Buyer relies on estoppels to verify rent roll; material discrepancies between estoppels and landlord's representations are grounds for price adjustment or termination.

### Representations and Warranties

- **Seller Representations** / Title, authority, no litigation, no violations, environmental condition, lease status, compliance with laws. / **Consequence**: Seller representations typically survive closing for **6-12 months** (commercial); buyer must bring claims within the survival period or they are time-barred.
- **Knowledge Qualifier** / Seller may limit representations to matters "within seller's actual knowledge" (sometimes "constructive knowledge"). / **Consequence**: "Actual knowledge" is the narrowest qualifier; "knowledge after reasonable inquiry" is broader and more protective for buyer. Define the individuals whose knowledge is imputed.
- **Material Adverse Change** / Seller represents that no material adverse change will occur between signing and closing. / **Consequence**: MAC clause gives buyer a closing condition; what constitutes "material" is heavily negotiated and fact-specific.

### Closing Conditions and Procedures

- **Closing Conditions** / Title in agreed condition, representations true at closing, no material adverse change, tenant estoppels received, lender approval. / **Consequence**: Failure of a closing condition gives the non-defaulting party the right to terminate; deposits may be refundable if termination is based on a failed condition (vs. buyer default).
- **Earnest Money Deposit** / Typically **1-3%** of purchase price for commercial transactions; **1-5%** for residential. / **Consequence**: Deposit typically goes "hard" (non-refundable) after the due diligence period expires; liquidated damages clauses may limit seller's remedy to retention of the deposit.
- **Closing Timeline** / Commercial: **30-90 days** from signing to closing. Residential: **30-45 days** typical. / **Consequence**: Delays beyond the closing date may constitute default; contracts should include a cure period (**5-10 business days**) and clear remedies for delay.

### Recording

- **Recording Requirements** / Deed must be recorded with the county recorder in the county where the property is located. / **Consequence**: Recording provides constructive notice to subsequent purchasers; failure to record may subordinate the buyer's interest to a subsequent bona fide purchaser.
- **Recording Sequence** / Deed records first, followed by the mortgage/deed of trust. / **Consequence**: Incorrect recording sequence can affect lien priority; simultaneous closings (like-kind exchanges) require careful coordination.
- **Race-Notice Jurisdictions** / Majority of states are race-notice: a subsequent bona fide purchaser who records first prevails over a prior unrecorded interest. / **Consequence**: Prompt recording after closing is critical; even a brief delay creates risk in race-notice jurisdictions.

### Transfer Taxes

- **State Transfer Taxes** / Vary widely by state: California ($1.10/$1,000), New York (0.4% base + 0.25% mansion tax for $1M+), Pennsylvania (2%), Delaware (4% combined state/county). / **Consequence**: Transfer taxes can be a significant closing cost; allocation between buyer and seller is negotiable and varies by local custom.
- **City/Local Transfer Taxes** / Additional local taxes in some municipalities: San Francisco (up to **6%** for $25M+), New York City (**1-2.625%**), Chicago (**$3.75/$500**). / **Consequence**: Combined state and local transfer taxes can exceed **8-9%** in high-tax jurisdictions; entity transfer structures may avoid transfer tax in some states (but anti-avoidance rules apply).
- **Exemptions** / Common exemptions: transfers between related entities, mergers, foreclosures, government conveyances. / **Consequence**: Exemption eligibility must be confirmed before closing; improper claim of exemption triggers back taxes, penalties, and interest.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Easements and Encumbrances** — Title exceptions identified during title review; survey reveals encroachments
- **Environmental Covenants** — Phase I/II as closing condition; environmental indemnities in PSA
- **Zoning and Land Use** — Zoning compliance verification during due diligence; permitted use representations
- **Corporate** — Entity formation for property holding; authority documentation for closing
- **Financial Services** — Loan underwriting, lender requirements, mortgage recording

## Zoning Land Use

# Zoning and Land Use

## Applicability

Applies when a real estate matter involves permitted uses, development approvals, zoning compliance, variances, conditional use permits, or environmental review under land use regulations. Relevant to acquisitions, development, and change-of-use scenarios.

## Key Requirements

### Use Classifications

- **Residential (R)** / Zoning districts from single-family (R-1) through high-density multifamily (R-5+). / **Consequence**: Commercial or industrial use in residential zones is prohibited absent a variance or rezoning; violation subjects owner to injunctive relief and fines.
- **Commercial (C)** / Retail, office, and service uses; sub-classifications (C-1 neighborhood, C-2 general, C-3 regional). / **Consequence**: Use outside the permitted category requires conditional use permit or rezoning; operating without approval is a continuing violation with daily penalties.
- **Industrial (I/M)** / Light industrial (I-1/M-1) and heavy industrial (I-2/M-2) districts. / **Consequence**: Environmental and nuisance regulations layer on top of zoning; buffer zone requirements may limit adjacent development.
- **Mixed-Use / Planned Unit Development (PUD)** / Flexible zoning allowing multiple use types; subject to specific development agreements. / **Consequence**: Developer bound by approved development plan; material deviation requires amendment and new public hearing.

### Variances

- **Area/Dimensional Variance** / Relief from setback, height, lot coverage, or parking requirements. Standard: **practical difficulty** (less stringent). / **Consequence**: Granted more readily than use variances; typically must show hardship is property-specific, not self-created.
- **Use Variance** / Permits a use not allowed in the zoning district. Standard: **unnecessary hardship** (most stringent). / **Consequence**: Requires showing the property cannot yield a reasonable return under any permitted use; rarely granted and highly fact-specific.
- **Processing Timeline** / Variance applications typically take **60-120 days** from filing to hearing. / **Consequence**: Acquisition timelines and due diligence periods must account for zoning approval delays; failure to secure variance may trigger termination rights.

### Conditional Use Permits (CUPs)

- **Standards** / Use is permitted in the zone but requires special conditions (traffic mitigation, hours of operation, screening). / **Consequence**: CUP conditions run with the land and bind successors; violation of conditions may result in revocation.
- **Public Hearing** / Required before CUP issuance; notice to adjacent property owners within **300-500 feet** (varies by jurisdiction). / **Consequence**: Opposition at hearing can delay or defeat the application; community engagement is critical.
- **Duration** / CUPs may be permanent or time-limited (commonly **3-5 year** terms with renewal). / **Consequence**: Time-limited CUPs create business continuity risk; renewal is not guaranteed.

### Nonconforming Use

- **Legal Nonconforming Use (Grandfathered)** / Use that was lawful when established but no longer conforms to current zoning. / **Consequence**: May continue but typically cannot be expanded, intensified, or resumed after **6-12 months** of abandonment (varies by jurisdiction).
- **Amortization** / Some jurisdictions allow amortization periods (**3-10 years**) to phase out nonconforming uses. / **Consequence**: After the amortization period, the use must cease or face enforcement action; constitutional takings challenges may apply.
- **Destruction** / If a nonconforming structure is damaged beyond **50-75%** of its value (varies), it cannot be rebuilt to nonconforming specifications. / **Consequence**: Must rebuild in compliance with current zoning, potentially losing the nonconforming use.

### Spot Zoning

- **Definition** / Rezoning a single parcel inconsistent with the surrounding area and comprehensive plan. / **Consequence**: Vulnerable to legal challenge as arbitrary and capricious; courts may invalidate the rezoning.
- **Defense** / Rezoning is defensible if consistent with the comprehensive plan and benefits the community. / **Consequence**: Strong comprehensive plan consistency is the best defense against spot zoning challenges.

### Entitlements

- **Development Agreement** / Contract between developer and municipality fixing development rights for a term (typically **10-20 years**). / **Consequence**: Protects developer from subsequent zoning changes; municipality may require community benefits (affordable housing, infrastructure).
- **Vested Rights** / Once substantial expenditures are made in reliance on an approved permit, rights may vest. / **Consequence**: Vesting thresholds vary significantly by state; some states have statutory vesting, others rely on common law estoppel.

### Environmental Review

- **CEQA (California)** / Projects with potential significant environmental impacts require an Environmental Impact Report (EIR). Categorical exemptions for minor projects. / **Consequence**: EIR process adds **12-24 months** to project timeline; inadequate EIR is grounds for project invalidation.
- **NEPA (Federal)** / Required for federal actions (permits, funding, land). Environmental Assessment (EA) or full Environmental Impact Statement (EIS). / **Consequence**: EIS process typically takes **18-36 months**; legal challenges under NEPA can delay projects for years.
- **Mitigated Negative Declaration** / Available when mitigation measures reduce all impacts to less-than-significant levels. / **Consequence**: Faster than full EIR (**4-8 months**) but vulnerable to challenge if mitigation is inadequate.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Environmental & ESG** — Environmental review requirements overlay zoning approvals; contamination may limit permitted uses
- **Construction** — Building permits require zoning compliance; construction cannot begin before zoning approvals
- **Corporate** — Entity structuring for development projects; development agreements may require specific entity types
- **Real Estate Transactions** — Zoning due diligence is critical in acquisitions; representations regarding permitted uses
