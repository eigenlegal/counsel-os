# Corporate and LLC Governance

## Overview
Corporate and LLC governance encompasses the rules, processes, and structures by which entities are directed and controlled, including the rights and obligations of boards, officers, members, managers, and equity holders. Governance requirements derive from statutory law, governing documents (charter, bylaws, operating agreement), and fiduciary duties.

## Key Requirements
- **Board of directors (corporations):** The board manages the business and affairs of the corporation. Directors owe fiduciary duties of care (informed decision-making) and loyalty (no self-dealing, corporate opportunities belong to the corporation). The business judgment rule protects informed, disinterested, good-faith decisions from judicial second-guessing.
- **Stockholder rights (corporations):** Stockholders elect directors, approve fundamental transactions (mergers, charter amendments, dissolution, sale of substantially all assets), and have inspection rights. Default voting requirements vary by state but typically require a majority of shares present at a meeting with a quorum.
- **Manager/member governance (LLCs):** LLCs may be member-managed (all members participate) or manager-managed (designated managers run day-to-day operations). Governance rules are primarily set by the operating agreement. Delaware allows significant flexibility in modifying default fiduciary duties.
- **Officer authority:** Officers act on behalf of the entity within the scope of their authority as delegated by the board or operating agreement. Apparent authority may bind the entity even when actual authority is lacking.
- **Written consents:** Most states allow board and stockholder/member actions by written consent in lieu of a meeting, subject to specific requirements (e.g., Delaware corporations require unanimous written consent for board action unless the charter provides otherwise; stockholder written consent requires sufficient votes).
- **Quorum requirements:** Board quorum typically requires a majority of directors. Stockholder quorum typically requires a majority of outstanding shares. Operating agreements specify LLC quorum rules.
- **Indemnification and advancement:** Corporations may (and typically do) indemnify directors and officers for expenses, judgments, and settlements in actions arising from their service, subject to statutory conditions. Delaware permits mandatory advancement of expenses, which is common in charter/bylaw provisions and D&O indemnification agreements.
- **Conflicts of interest:** Interested director transactions must comply with safe harbor procedures (disclosure + disinterested approval or ratification, or entire fairness). Controlling stockholder transactions require entire fairness review unless cleansed through independent committee approval and disinterested stockholder vote (MFW framework under Delaware law).
- **Minutes and records:** Entities should maintain records of board and stockholder/member actions, including meeting minutes and written consents, as evidence of proper authorization and compliance with fiduciary duties.

## Common Contract Issues
- Contracts executed without proper board or member authorization — verify that the signatory has authority and that required approvals have been obtained.
- Indemnification agreements with directors/officers that are narrower than statutory maximums or fail to include advancement provisions.
- Operating agreements with ambiguous governance provisions — unclear allocation of decision-making authority between members and managers, missing approval thresholds for major decisions.
- Investor agreements (stockholder agreements, voting agreements) that conflict with charter or bylaw provisions.
- Protective provisions giving investors veto rights over specified corporate actions without clear scope definitions.
- Missing or inadequate deadlock resolution mechanisms in 50/50 joint ventures or closely held companies.
- Proxy and voting agreements that fail to comply with statutory requirements or conflict with governing documents.
- Bylaws or operating agreements that do not address remote meetings, electronic voting, or emergency governance procedures.

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **Corporate (M&A):** Board and stockholder approval is required for fundamental transactions; fiduciary duties constrain the board's conduct during M&A processes (Revlon duties, Unocal analysis under Delaware law).
- **Securities:** Governance provisions in investor rights agreements, voting agreements, and stockholder agreements are negotiated in conjunction with equity issuances and must be consistent with securities law requirements.
- **Employment:** Officer employment agreements must be consistent with the board's authority to appoint and remove officers; executive compensation requires board (or compensation committee) approval.
- **Litigation:** Derivative litigation, books-and-records demands (Section 220), and appraisal actions arise from governance disputes. Proper governance procedures are often a defense in litigation.
- **Insurance:** D&O insurance coverage must align with indemnification and advancement provisions in governing documents and indemnification agreements.
