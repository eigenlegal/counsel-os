# Corporate and LLC Governance

## Applicability

Load when any of the following arise: board of directors actions or composition questions, stockholder or member voting requirements, quorum determinations, written consent actions, officer authority or appointments, annual or special meeting requirements, record-keeping obligations, D&O indemnification or advancement provisions, conflicts of interest and interested transactions, bylaws or operating agreement governance provisions, committee formation, or state-specific governance variations.

## Key Requirements

### Board of Directors — Composition and Authority

- **What**: The board of directors manages the business and affairs of the corporation. Directors owe fiduciary duties of care and loyalty. Board size, qualifications, and classification are set by the charter and bylaws.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Under DGCL Section 141(b), a corporation must have at least 1 director. Board size is typically fixed by the bylaws or by board resolution within a range authorized by the bylaws. Classified (staggered) boards divide directors into classes (typically 3) serving staggered multi-year terms (typically 3 years each). Annual election of all directors is the default absent classification.
- **Consequence**: Board actions taken without a properly constituted board (e.g., after director resignations reducing the board below the minimum required by bylaws) may be voidable. Classified boards provide continuity but limit stockholder ability to replace the full board in a single election cycle.

### Quorum Requirements

- **What**: The minimum number of directors or stockholders that must be present (in person or by proxy) to conduct valid business at a meeting.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Board quorum: default under DGCL Section 141(b) is a majority of the total number of directors. The charter or bylaws may set a different quorum but no less than one-third of the total. Stockholder quorum: default under DGCL Section 216 is a majority of shares entitled to vote, present in person or by proxy. The charter may set a different quorum, but not less than one-third of shares entitled to vote. LLC quorum: set by operating agreement (no statutory default in Delaware).
- **Consequence**: Actions taken without a quorum are void. Quorum is determined at the commencement of the meeting; a quorum once established is typically not broken by the departure of members (unless the bylaws provide otherwise).

### Voting Standards

- **What**: The vote required to approve an action once a quorum is established.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Board actions: default is a majority of directors present at a meeting at which a quorum is present (DGCL Section 141(b)). Stockholder actions: default under DGCL Section 216 is a majority of shares present and entitled to vote (for matters other than director elections). Director elections: plurality voting is the DGCL default, but many companies adopt majority voting bylaws (director must receive more "for" than "withhold" votes). Supermajority provisions (66.7%, 75%, or 80%) may be required by charter for specified actions (charter amendments, mergers, removal of classified directors).
- **Consequence**: Failure to meet the required voting threshold means the action is not approved. Supermajority requirements in the charter can only be amended by the same supermajority vote, creating entrenchment.

### Written Consent in Lieu of Meeting

- **What**: Most states allow board and stockholder actions by written consent without holding a meeting, subject to specific requirements.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Board written consent: under DGCL Section 141(f), requires unanimous written consent of all directors unless the certificate of incorporation provides otherwise. Stockholder written consent: under DGCL Section 228, requires the minimum number of votes that would be necessary to authorize the action at a meeting at which all shares entitled to vote were present. The certificate of incorporation may restrict or prohibit stockholder action by written consent. LLC: governed by operating agreement provisions.
- **Consequence**: Board written consents that are not unanimous (absent charter authorization) are invalid. Written consents must be signed and delivered to the corporation, and the effective date of the action is the date the last required signature is obtained (unless a later date is specified). A 60-day time limit applies to collecting stockholder written consents under DGCL Section 228(c).

### Officer Authority

- **What**: Officers act on behalf of the corporation within the scope of authority delegated by the board of directors, the bylaws, or (for LLCs) the operating agreement.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Under DGCL Section 142, a corporation must have the officers designated in the bylaws or appointed by the board. The same person may hold multiple offices. Officers serve at the pleasure of the board and may be removed with or without cause at any time (unless an employment agreement provides otherwise, though removal itself remains valid -- the officer may have a contract claim for damages).
- **Consequence**: Contracts executed by officers within their actual or apparent authority bind the corporation. Apparent authority may bind the corporation even when the officer lacked actual authority -- third parties are entitled to rely on the officer's apparent authority unless they had notice of the limitation. Officers owe the same fiduciary duties as directors.

### Annual and Special Meetings

- **What**: Corporations must hold annual stockholder meetings for the election of directors. Special meetings may be called for specific purposes.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Under DGCL Section 211(b), an annual meeting must be held each year for the election of directors on a date and at a time designated by the board. If no annual meeting is held for 13 months after the last annual meeting, any stockholder may petition the Court of Chancery to order one. Special meetings: may be called by the board or by persons authorized in the charter or bylaws. Record date for determining stockholders entitled to vote: not more than 60 days nor less than 10 days before the meeting (DGCL Section 213). Notice: 10-60 days before the meeting (DGCL Section 222).
- **Consequence**: Failure to hold annual meetings does not invalidate prior board actions but creates governance risk and may invite stockholder petitions. Delaware permits meetings by remote communication (DGCL Section 211(a)(2)) if the corporation implements measures to verify stockholder identity and allow participation.

### Record-Keeping Requirements

- **What**: Corporations and LLCs must maintain specified records as evidence of proper authorization and compliance with legal requirements.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Under DGCL Section 224, the corporation must keep books and records of account. Under DGCL Section 220, any stockholder may inspect corporate books and records for a proper purpose. Records that should be maintained: certificate of incorporation and all amendments, bylaws and all amendments, minutes of board and stockholder meetings, written consents, stock ledger, financial records, and material contracts. LLCs: DLLCA Section 18-305 grants members inspection rights.
- **Consequence**: Failure to maintain records weakens defenses against fiduciary duty claims (no evidence of informed decision-making) and veil-piercing claims (failure to observe formalities). Books-and-records demands under DGCL Section 220 are a powerful pre-litigation tool -- courts broadly enforce them and may award attorneys' fees for wrongful refusal.

### D&O Indemnification and Advancement

- **What**: Corporations may (and typically do) indemnify directors and officers for expenses, judgments, fines, and settlements incurred in their service to the corporation, and advance expenses before final resolution.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: DGCL Section 145(a)-(b): permissive indemnification for directors and officers in third-party and derivative actions, provided the person acted in good faith and in a manner reasonably believed to be in the corporation's best interest. DGCL Section 145(c): mandatory indemnification when a director or officer is successful on the merits or otherwise in the defense of any action. DGCL Section 145(e): permits advancement of expenses upon receipt of an undertaking to repay if indemnification is ultimately not warranted. Advancement rights in bylaws or indemnification agreements are typically enforceable as written (Homestore v. Tafeen).
- **Consequence**: Mandatory advancement provisions create an obligation to pay legal fees as incurred -- the undertaking to repay is typically unsecured. The combination of charter exculpation (DGCL Section 102(b)(7)), bylaw indemnification, indemnification agreements, and D&O insurance forms a comprehensive protection framework. Gaps between these layers are a common governance risk.

### Conflicts of Interest — Safe Harbor

- **What**: Interested director transactions must comply with safe harbor procedures to avoid being voidable solely because of the director's interest.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Under DGCL Section 144, an interested transaction is not void or voidable if: (a) the material facts of the interest are disclosed and the transaction is approved by a majority of disinterested directors (even if less than a quorum), (b) the material facts are disclosed and the transaction is approved by a stockholder vote, or (c) the transaction is fair to the corporation at the time it is authorized. For controlling stockholder transactions, the MFW framework (Kahn v. M&F Worldwide, 2014) provides business judgment review if the transaction is conditioned ab initio on both special committee approval and majority-of-minority stockholder vote.
- **Consequence**: Failure to follow safe harbor procedures subjects the transaction to entire fairness review, placing the burden on the interested party to prove both fair dealing and fair price.

### State-Specific Variations

- **What**: Governance rules vary by state of incorporation. Delaware (DGCL) is the baseline for this file, but key variations exist in other major jurisdictions.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: California: cumulative voting is mandatory for corporations with fewer than 100 stockholders (Cal. Corp. Code Section 708); broader inspection rights; court-ordered provisional directors. New York: no stockholder action by written consent without unanimity unless the certificate provides otherwise (NYBCL Section 615). Nevada: broader director protections (NRS 78.138 -- directors presumed to act in good faith; no damages liability absent breach of duty of loyalty or acts not in good faith); no entire fairness doctrine equivalent. MBCA states: may require a majority of votes cast (rather than majority of shares present) as the default voting standard.
- **Consequence**: Multi-state governance analysis is required when the state of incorporation differs from the state of principal operations. Internal affairs doctrine generally applies the law of the state of incorporation to governance disputes, but some states (notably California -- Cal. Corp. Code Section 2115) impose local governance requirements on foreign corporations with significant California connections.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Corporate (M&A)**: Board and stockholder approval is required for fundamental transactions. Fiduciary duties constrain board conduct during M&A processes (Revlon duties, Unocal analysis). Governance provisions determine required approvals and vote thresholds.
- **Corporate (Fiduciary Duties)**: Governance structures implement fiduciary duties. Board composition, committee independence, and approval processes are the mechanisms through which duties of care and loyalty are discharged.
- **Securities**: Governance provisions in investor rights agreements, voting agreements, and stockholder agreements are negotiated alongside equity issuances. Public company governance is additionally regulated by SEC rules, stock exchange listing standards, and Sarbanes-Oxley.
- **Employment**: Officer employment agreements must be consistent with board authority. Executive compensation requires board or compensation committee approval. Indemnification agreements are common for senior executives.
- **Litigation**: Derivative litigation, books-and-records demands (DGCL Section 220), and appraisal actions arise from governance disputes. Proper governance procedures are a primary defense.
- **Insurance**: D&O insurance coverage must align with indemnification and advancement provisions. Policy structure (Side A, B, C) should be coordinated with the entity's governance framework.
