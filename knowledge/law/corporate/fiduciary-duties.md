## Fiduciary Duties

# Fiduciary Duties

## Applicability

Load when any of the following arise: director or officer decision-making under scrutiny, self-dealing or interested transactions, corporate opportunity disputes, change-of-control or sale transactions, oversight and compliance failures (Caremark claims), controlling stockholder transactions, demand futility analysis in derivative litigation, or exculpation and indemnification provisions.

## Key Requirements

### Duty of Care

- **What**: Directors and officers must act on an informed basis, in good faith, and with the care that a reasonably prudent person would exercise in similar circumstances. Requires reviewing material information reasonably available before making a decision.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: The standard is process-oriented -- courts examine whether the board made a reasonable effort to become informed, not whether the decision was correct. Under Delaware law, a board that fails to inform itself at all (gross negligence) loses business judgment rule protection. DGCL Section 102(b)(7) permits charter provisions eliminating director monetary liability for duty of care breaches (but not loyalty, bad faith, or intentional misconduct).
- **Consequence**: Breach of duty of care absent exculpation can result in personal monetary liability for damages. In the M&A context, failure to conduct an adequate market check or obtain a fairness opinion may support a duty of care claim.

### Duty of Loyalty

- **What**: Directors, officers, and controlling stockholders must act in the best interests of the corporation and its stockholders, not in their own self-interest. Prohibits self-dealing, usurpation of corporate opportunities, and competition with the corporation.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: A transaction is "interested" when a director or controlling stockholder stands on both sides or derives a personal benefit not shared by the stockholders generally. Under DGCL Section 144, an interested transaction is not void or voidable solely because of the director's interest if it is (a) approved by disinterested directors after disclosure, (b) approved by stockholders after disclosure, or (c) fair to the corporation at the time authorized.
- **Consequence**: Breach of loyalty cannot be exculpated under DGCL Section 102(b)(7). Remedies include disgorgement of profits, rescission of the transaction, and personal liability for damages. Under the corporate opportunity doctrine, a fiduciary who takes a business opportunity belonging to the corporation must account for all profits.

### Business Judgment Rule

- **What**: A presumption that directors acted on an informed basis, in good faith, and in the honest belief that the action was in the corporation's best interest. Places the burden on the plaintiff to rebut the presumption.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: The rule applies when directors (1) made an informed decision (duty of care), (2) acted in good faith, (3) had no personal financial interest in the decision, and (4) reasonably believed the decision served the corporation's interests. If rebutted, the burden shifts to defendants to prove entire fairness.
- **Consequence**: When the business judgment rule applies, courts will not second-guess the substantive merits of the board's decision, even if the decision proves to be poor. When rebutted, the transaction is reviewed under the more demanding entire fairness standard.

### Entire Fairness Test

- **What**: The most demanding standard of judicial review, applied to interested transactions where the business judgment rule is rebutted or inapplicable.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Requires proof of both (1) fair dealing (process -- timing, initiation, structure, negotiation, disclosure) and (2) fair price (economic terms). In controlling stockholder squeeze-out mergers, the MFW framework (Kahn v. M&F Worldwide, 2014) shifts review back to business judgment if the transaction is conditioned ab initio on (a) approval by a fully empowered special committee of independent directors and (b) an informed, uncoerced vote of a majority of the minority stockholders.
- **Consequence**: If entire fairness is not established, the transaction may be rescinded or defendants held personally liable for damages measured by the difference between the price paid and the fair value.

### Revlon Duties

- **What**: When a board initiates an active bidding process, agrees to a change of control, or abandons the company's long-term strategy in favor of a breakup, the board's duty shifts to obtaining the best price reasonably available for stockholders.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Triggered in sale-of-control transactions. The board must conduct a reasonable auction or market check. Defensive measures and deal protections (termination fees, no-shop clauses, matching rights) must be reasonable and not preclusive of superior offers. Termination fees typically range from 2-4% of deal value.
- **Consequence**: Breach of Revlon duties results in enhanced judicial scrutiny of the sale process. Unreasonable deal protections may be enjoined. Directors may face personal liability if their conduct was not in good faith.

### Caremark Duties (Oversight Liability)

- **What**: Directors have a duty to implement and monitor a reasonable compliance and reporting system. Liability attaches for sustained, systematic failure to exercise oversight (Marchand v. Barnhill, 2019, clarifying the standard).
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Plaintiff must show either (a) the directors utterly failed to implement any reporting or information system (prong one), or (b) having implemented such a system, the directors consciously failed to monitor or oversee its operations, disabling themselves from being informed of risks or problems requiring their attention (prong two). The claim requires bad faith -- a conscious disregard of known duties.
- **Consequence**: Caremark claims survive exculpation under DGCL Section 102(b)(7) because they sound in bad faith (a loyalty breach). Liability can include damages for the corporation's losses resulting from the oversight failure.

### State Variations

- **What**: Fiduciary duty standards vary significantly by jurisdiction. Delaware is the dominant body of corporate fiduciary duty law, but other states may apply different standards.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Delaware LLCs may modify or eliminate fiduciary duties by contract under DLLCA Section 18-1101(c), though the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing cannot be eliminated. California applies broader fiduciary duties to majority shareholders in close corporations (Jones v. H.F. Ahmanson). Some states (e.g., Nevada, NRS 78.138) provide broader director protections than Delaware.
- **Consequence**: Choice of entity jurisdiction directly affects the scope of fiduciary duties, available defenses, and litigation risk. Forum selection clauses designating Delaware courts are enforceable for internal affairs (Salzberg v. Sciabacucchi, as modified by federal forum provisions).

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Corporate (Governance)**: Fiduciary duties define the standard of conduct for all governance decisions -- board actions, officer authority, and stockholder protections are evaluated through fiduciary duty analysis.
- **Corporate (M&A)**: Revlon, Unocal, and entire fairness standards govern board conduct in change-of-control transactions. Deal protections are evaluated against fiduciary duties.
- **Securities**: Fiduciary duty claims frequently arise alongside federal securities fraud claims (Section 10(b), Rule 10b-5) in public company transactions. Disclosure obligations overlap.
- **Litigation**: Derivative lawsuits, class actions, and books-and-records demands (DGCL Section 220) are the primary mechanisms for enforcing fiduciary duties.
- **Insurance**: D&O insurance responds to fiduciary duty claims. Policy coverage (Side A, B, C) and exclusions must align with the entity's indemnification and exculpation provisions.
