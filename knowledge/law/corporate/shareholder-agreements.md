## Shareholder Agreements

# Shareholder Agreements

## Applicability

Load when any of the following arise: voting agreements or arrangements, buy-sell agreements, right of first refusal (ROFR) or right of first offer (ROFO) provisions, tag-along or drag-along rights in non-investment contexts, stockholder or member deadlock resolution, transfer restrictions on equity, shotgun (buy-sell) provisions, cross-purchase or redemption arrangements, co-sale agreements, or closely held company governance disputes.

## Key Requirements

### Voting Agreements

- **What**: Contracts among stockholders or members committing to vote their shares in a specified manner on designated matters, most commonly board elections and fundamental transactions.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Under DGCL Section 218, voting agreements between stockholders are specifically enforceable. Voting agreements typically specify board composition (e.g., investor-designated directors, founder-designated directors, independent directors) and are tied to ownership thresholds. Standard venture-backed voting agreements (per NVCA model) grant board designation rights contingent on continued ownership of a specified percentage of shares.
- **Consequence**: A party that breaches a voting agreement may be subject to specific performance (court-ordered compliance). Irrevocable proxies coupled with an interest are the strongest enforcement mechanism. Voting agreements that conflict with charter or bylaw provisions may create enforcement complications -- the charter generally controls.

### Buy-Sell Agreements

- **What**: Contractual arrangements that establish the terms under which equity owners may or must sell their interests, and other owners or the entity may or must purchase those interests, upon specified triggering events.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Common triggers: death, disability, termination of employment, retirement, voluntary withdrawal, bankruptcy, divorce (to prevent transfer to ex-spouse). Two primary structures: cross-purchase (other owners buy the departing owner's interest directly) and redemption (the entity buys back the interest). Valuation methods: formula-based (book value, multiple of earnings/revenue), periodic agreed value, or independent appraisal at time of trigger.
- **Consequence**: Cross-purchase agreements result in a step-up in basis for the purchasing owners; redemptions do not provide a basis step-up to remaining owners (but may trigger Section 302 dividend treatment if not a complete termination). Life insurance is commonly used to fund buy-sell obligations on death. Failure to properly structure and fund buy-sell agreements creates liquidity risk and valuation disputes.

### Right of First Refusal (ROFR)

- **What**: A contractual right that requires a selling equity holder, upon receiving a bona fide third-party offer, to first offer the shares to specified rights holders (other stockholders, the company, or both) on the same terms before completing the third-party sale.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Standard process: (a) selling holder receives and accepts a third-party offer, (b) selling holder delivers transfer notice specifying the material terms, (c) ROFR holders have a specified exercise period (typically 15-30 days) to elect to purchase on the same terms, (d) if ROFR is not exercised, the selling holder may complete the third-party sale within a specified window (typically 60-90 days) on terms no more favorable to the buyer. ROFR may be held by the company (primary) and/or other stockholders (secondary).
- **Consequence**: ROFR provisions create a "chilling effect" on third-party offers because buyers may be unwilling to invest diligence resources knowing the deal may be matched. Standard exceptions: transfers to family members, trusts, or affiliates; transfers for estate planning purposes; and pledges to financial institutions.

### Right of First Offer (ROFO)

- **What**: A contractual right requiring a selling equity holder to first offer the shares to specified rights holders before soliciting or accepting third-party offers.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Standard process: (a) selling holder delivers an offer notice to ROFO holders, (b) ROFO holders have a specified period (typically 15-30 days) to make an offer, (c) if the selling holder rejects the ROFO offer (or no offer is made), the selling holder may sell to third parties, but typically only at a price at or above the ROFO offer (or a specified percentage thereof, e.g., 110%). ROFO is less restrictive than ROFR because the rights holder does not get to match a specific third-party offer.
- **Consequence**: ROFO provisions are less likely to chill third-party interest but provide weaker protection to rights holders. Market practice varies -- ROFR is more common in closely held companies and NVCA-standard agreements.

### Tag-Along (Co-Sale) Rights

- **What**: Rights allowing minority holders to participate in a sale of equity by a major holder, on the same terms and conditions, pro rata to their ownership.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Triggered when a specified holder (typically a founder or major stockholder) proposes to transfer shares to a third party. Tag-along holders may elect to include their shares pro rata, reducing the number of shares the selling holder can sell. Exercise period: typically 15-20 days from notice. Standard exceptions align with ROFR exceptions (family transfers, trusts, affiliates).
- **Consequence**: Tag-along rights protect minority holders from being left behind in a change-of-control or partial sale. If the third-party buyer is unwilling to purchase the additional shares, the selling holder must reduce their sale proportionally.

### Drag-Along Rights

- **What**: Rights allowing a specified majority to compel all equity holders to participate in a sale of the company on the same terms and conditions.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Typically requires approval of (a) a majority of the board, (b) holders of a majority (or supermajority) of outstanding common stock, and (c) holders of a majority (or specified percentage) of outstanding preferred stock. Dragged holders must vote in favor of the transaction, tender their shares, and execute all transaction documents. The price and terms must be the same for all holders (subject to existing liquidation preference waterfall).
- **Consequence**: Drag-along eliminates minority holdout problems. Key protections for dragged holders: same price per share (on an as-converted basis), no personal liability post-closing beyond escrow/indemnification obligations shared pro rata, and release of all guarantees. Fiduciary duties still apply to the exercise of drag-along rights.

### Deadlock Resolution

- **What**: Mechanisms for resolving governance impasses in entities with equal ownership splits (50/50) or where veto rights create the potential for deadlock.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Common mechanisms: (a) mandatory mediation followed by binding arbitration, (b) escalation to designated senior executives with a specified resolution period (typically 30-60 days), (c) buy-sell (shotgun) provisions, (d) appointment of a neutral tie-breaking director, (e) dissolution as a last resort. Shotgun provisions: one party names a price; the other party must either buy at that price or sell at that price.
- **Consequence**: Absence of deadlock provisions in 50/50 entities can lead to judicial dissolution proceedings, appointment of a custodian or receiver, and destruction of value. Courts may order dissolution of a deadlocked entity under DGCL Section 226 (appointment of custodian) or Section 273 (dissolution of joint venture corporation).

### Transfer Restrictions

- **What**: Contractual and charter-based limitations on the ability of equity holders to transfer their interests, ensuring that ownership remains among approved persons.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Common restrictions: (a) board approval required for any transfer, (b) ROFR/ROFO as described above, (c) prohibition on transfers to competitors, (d) compliance with securities laws (Rule 144 holding periods: 6 months for reporting companies, 12 months for non-reporting companies with volume and manner-of-sale limitations), (e) lock-up agreements (typically 180 days post-IPO). Stock certificates (or book-entry notations) must bear restrictive legends referencing applicable restrictions.
- **Consequence**: Transfers in violation of valid restrictions are voidable. Securities Act Section 5 violations for unregistered transfers can result in rescission rights and SEC enforcement. Transfer restrictions must be reasonable in scope and duration to be enforceable.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Securities**: Transfer restrictions must comply with federal and state securities laws. Rule 144 safe harbor governs resale of restricted securities. Stockholder agreements often contain representations about accredited investor status.
- **Corporate (Governance)**: Voting agreements directly affect board composition and corporate governance. Conflicts between stockholder agreements and charter/bylaw provisions must be resolved under state corporate law.
- **Corporate (Fiduciary Duties)**: Exercise of drag-along and other controlling stockholder rights is subject to fiduciary duty constraints. Entire fairness may apply to squeeze-out transactions.
- **Employment**: Buy-sell triggers frequently overlap with employment events (termination, retirement). Equity repurchase rights upon termination must comply with applicable employment law and Section 409A.
- **Litigation**: Stockholder agreement disputes are among the most common forms of closely held company litigation. Forum selection and arbitration clauses affect dispute resolution.
