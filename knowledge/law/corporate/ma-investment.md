# Mergers, Acquisitions, and Investment Transactions

## Overview
Mergers, acquisitions, and investment transactions encompass the legal frameworks governing changes of control, business combinations, equity investments, and corporate restructurings. These transactions require careful attention to deal structure, regulatory requirements, due diligence, definitive documentation, and post-closing integration.

## Key Requirements
- **Transaction structures:** Stock/equity purchase (buyer acquires target equity directly from holders), asset purchase (buyer acquires specified assets and assumes specified liabilities), statutory merger (target merges into buyer or a subsidiary), and tender offer (buyer makes an offer directly to target stockholders). Each structure has distinct legal, tax, regulatory, and liability implications.
- **Hart-Scott-Rodino Act (HSR):** Parties must file pre-merger notifications with the FTC and DOJ and observe a waiting period (typically 30 days, extendable) before closing transactions that meet the size-of-transaction and size-of-person thresholds. Thresholds are adjusted annually (2024: $111.4M transaction size threshold). Gun-jumping (pre-clearance coordination) is prohibited.
- **CFIUS review:** The Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States reviews transactions that could result in control of a US business by a foreign person for national security implications. Mandatory filings are required for certain transactions involving critical technology, critical infrastructure, or sensitive personal data (per FIRRMA).
- **Due diligence:** Comprehensive investigation of the target's legal, financial, operational, regulatory, and tax status. Covers corporate organization, contracts, litigation, IP, employment, environmental, real estate, insurance, data privacy, regulatory compliance, and financial condition.
- **Representations and warranties:** Statements of fact about the target's condition that allocate risk between buyer and seller. Standard reps cover organization, authority, capitalization, financial statements, absence of undisclosed liabilities, compliance with law, contracts, IP, employment, tax, environmental, and litigation. Reps may be qualified by disclosure schedules and materiality/MAE qualifiers.
- **Material adverse effect (MAE/MAC) clauses:** Define events or conditions that would give a party the right to terminate the agreement or refuse to close. Typical carve-outs include general economic conditions, industry-wide changes, changes in law, and acts of war/terrorism.
- **Indemnification:** Post-closing remedy for breaches of representations, warranties, and covenants. Key terms include survival periods, caps, baskets/deductibles, escrows, and representation and warranty insurance (RWI).
- **Venture capital and growth equity investments:** Typically structured as preferred stock purchases with negotiated protective provisions, liquidation preferences, anti-dilution protections, information rights, registration rights, board representation, and drag-along/tag-along rights. NVCA model documents are widely used as starting points.
- **Closing conditions:** Conditions precedent to closing typically include accuracy of representations, performance of covenants, regulatory approvals, absence of legal proceedings, third-party consents, and delivery of closing documents.
- **Post-closing integration:** Contractual provisions governing transition services, employee matters, earn-outs, non-competition covenants, and indemnification claims.

## Common Contract Issues
- Disclosure schedule preparation — incomplete or inaccurate disclosure schedules create post-closing indemnification exposure.
- MAE clause scope — overly broad or narrow definitions, missing carve-outs, and interaction with "bring-down" closing conditions.
- Indemnification cap, basket, and survival period negotiations — misalignment between deal economics and risk allocation.
- Anti-sandbagging provisions — whether a buyer's pre-closing knowledge of a breach affects its right to indemnification.
- Earn-out provisions — disputes over post-closing financial targets, accounting methodology, and the acquirer's obligation to operate the business in a manner that supports earn-out achievement.
- Interim operating covenants — overly restrictive or vague covenants governing the target's operations between signing and closing.
- Non-compete and non-solicitation scope in M&A — enforceability varies by jurisdiction, and FTC's proposed non-compete rule (if effective) may limit post-acquisition non-competes.
- Rollover equity and management incentive arrangements — securities law, tax, and employment law implications.
- Representation and warranty insurance (RWI) — coordination between RWI policy and indemnification provisions.

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **Securities:** Equity issuances in investment transactions must comply with federal and state securities laws, including registration requirements or exemptions. Public company M&A involves additional SEC disclosure and proxy requirements.
- **Antitrust:** HSR Act filings and antitrust analysis of competitive effects are required for transactions meeting applicable thresholds. International merger control filings may also be required.
- **Employment:** M&A transactions raise issues of employee transfer, benefit plan assumption, WARN Act compliance, change-of-control payments, and retention arrangements.
- **IP and Technology:** IP due diligence, IP representations and warranties, IP assignment and licensing provisions, and open-source compliance are critical in technology M&A.
- **Data Privacy:** Target's data privacy compliance is a significant due diligence issue; data processing activities may require consent for transfer in M&A transactions.
- **International Trade:** CFIUS review, export control considerations, and sanctions compliance are critical for cross-border M&A.
- **Tax:** Transaction structure drives tax consequences for all parties; Section 338 and 336 elections, Section 368 reorganizations, QSBS implications, and state/local tax considerations are integral to deal structuring.
