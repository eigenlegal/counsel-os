# Competition Law

## Overview
US antitrust law, primarily the Sherman Act and Clayton Act, prohibits agreements that unreasonably restrain trade, monopolization and attempted monopolization, and mergers that may substantially lessen competition. Enforcement is carried out by the DOJ Antitrust Division and the FTC, with private enforcement through treble damages actions. Analogous competition laws exist in virtually all major jurisdictions worldwide.

## Key Requirements
- **Sherman Act Section 1 — agreements in restraint of trade:**
  - **Per se illegal:** Horizontal price-fixing, bid rigging, market allocation among competitors, and group boycotts are conclusively presumed unreasonable regardless of actual competitive effects.
  - **Rule of reason:** Most other agreements (vertical restraints, competitor collaborations with procompetitive justifications) are analyzed under the rule of reason, which weighs procompetitive benefits against anticompetitive harms in the relevant market.
  - **Quick look:** Applied to agreements that appear anticompetitive on their face but do not fit neatly into per se categories; shifts the burden to the defendant to demonstrate procompetitive justification.

- **Sherman Act Section 2 — monopolization:**
  - Monopolization requires (1) possession of monopoly power in a relevant market and (2) willful acquisition or maintenance of that power through exclusionary conduct (as distinguished from growth through superior product, business acumen, or historic accident).
  - Attempted monopolization requires (1) specific intent to monopolize, (2) anticompetitive conduct, and (3) a dangerous probability of achieving monopoly power.
  - Relevant market definition (product market and geographic market) is critical to monopolization analysis.

- **Horizontal agreements and competitor collaborations:**
  - Joint ventures among competitors are analyzed under the rule of reason if they involve integration of resources to achieve procompetitive efficiencies. Ancillary restraints (non-competes, exclusivity) must be reasonably necessary to achieve the venture's objectives.
  - Information exchanges among competitors can facilitate collusion. Sharing competitively sensitive information (current/future pricing, customer-specific data, capacity plans) is high-risk. Aggregated, historical, anonymized data sharing through third parties is generally lower risk.
  - Standard-setting organization participation requires FRAND (fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory) licensing commitments for standard-essential patents.

- **Vertical restraints:**
  - Resale price maintenance (RPM): Minimum RPM is analyzed under the rule of reason after Leegin (2007). Maximum RPM and suggested pricing are generally lawful. Some states continue to apply per se analysis under state antitrust laws.
  - Exclusive dealing: Evaluated under rule of reason; anticompetitive if it forecloses a substantial share of the relevant market. Duration, market share, and justifications are key factors.
  - Tying: Conditioning the sale of one product (tying product) on purchase of another (tied product) may violate Section 1 if the seller has market power in the tying product market and a substantial amount of commerce is foreclosed in the tied product market.
  - MFN/price parity clauses: "Wide" MFNs (requiring sellers to offer best price across all platforms) face heightened scrutiny; "narrow" MFNs (requiring parity with seller's own direct channel) are generally lower risk but still subject to analysis.

- **Merger review:**
  - HSR Act requires pre-merger notification for transactions meeting size thresholds ($111.4M in 2024, adjusted annually).
  - DOJ/FTC Merger Guidelines (2023) evaluate competitive effects based on market concentration (HHI), elimination of competition between merging parties, trend toward consolidation, multisided platforms, and risk of coordinated effects.
  - Remedies: Divestitures (structural remedies) are preferred over behavioral remedies (conduct restrictions). Consent decrees impose ongoing compliance obligations.

- **Labor market antitrust:**
  - No-poach agreements between competing employers (agreements not to solicit or hire each other's employees) are treated as criminal per se violations by the DOJ.
  - Wage-fixing agreements between competitors are similarly criminal per se violations.
  - These are distinct from non-solicitation provisions within a legitimate business transaction (M&A, joint venture, franchise).

## Common Contract Issues
- Competitor collaboration agreements (joint ventures, co-marketing, data sharing) that do not include adequate antitrust guardrails (scope limitations, information firewalls, independent pricing decisions).
- Exclusivity provisions in distribution agreements that may foreclose a substantial share of the relevant market.
- MFN/price parity clauses in platform agreements, marketplace terms, and distribution agreements.
- Non-compete clauses between competitors that extend beyond the scope necessary for a legitimate transaction.
- Resale price maintenance provisions that set minimum resale prices (analyze under both federal and state antitrust law).
- Joint venture agreements that do not limit the venture's scope or duration, or that facilitate competitively sensitive information sharing between parent competitors.
- Standard-setting participation agreements that do not address FRAND licensing commitments for essential patents.
- Franchise agreements with territorial restrictions, tying arrangements, or no-poach provisions between franchisees.

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **Corporate (M&A):** HSR Act filing requirements and merger review are integral to M&A transactions above applicable thresholds. Antitrust risk assessment influences deal structure, timing, and regulatory strategy.
- **Employment (Non-Competes):** No-poach agreements between competitors are criminal antitrust violations; distinguished from employer-employee non-solicitation provisions.
- **IP and Technology:** Patent licensing restrictions, standard-essential patent FRAND commitments, and patent pool arrangements raise antitrust issues.
- **Consumer Protection:** FTC uses Section 5 authority for both consumer protection and competition enforcement.
- **International Trade:** Cross-border transactions may require merger control filings in multiple jurisdictions (EU, UK, China, Japan, etc.), each with different thresholds, timelines, and analytical frameworks.
- **Financial Services:** Bank merger review involves additional review by banking regulators (OCC, FDIC, Federal Reserve) in addition to DOJ antitrust review.
