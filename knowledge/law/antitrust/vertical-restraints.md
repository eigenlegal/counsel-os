## Vertical Restraints

# Vertical Restraints

## Applicability

Vertical restraints are restrictions imposed in agreements between parties at different levels of the distribution chain (manufacturer-distributor, franchisor-franchisee, platform-seller, licensor-licensee). These are analyzed primarily under Sherman Act Section 1 (15 U.S.C. 1) and Clayton Act Section 3 (15 U.S.C. 14). This file applies when ANY of the following are present:

- A contract sets or restricts resale prices (minimum, maximum, or advertised prices).
- An agreement requires exclusive dealing or exclusive purchasing obligations.
- A seller conditions the sale of one product on the purchase of another (tying or bundling).
- A contract imposes territorial, customer, or location restrictions on a distributor or reseller.
- A platform or marketplace imposes MFN/price parity clauses on sellers.
- A supplier or manufacturer refuses to deal with a distributor or terminates a distribution relationship.

Unlike horizontal restraints, most vertical restraints are analyzed under the rule of reason, which weighs procompetitive benefits against anticompetitive harms in the relevant market.

## Key Requirements

### Resale Price Maintenance (RPM)

- **Minimum RPM**
  - **What:** A manufacturer or supplier sets a minimum price at which distributors or retailers may resell the product.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Rule of reason since Leegin Creative Leather Products v. PSKS (2007), 551 U.S. 877 (overruling Dr. Miles per se rule).
  - **Consequence:** Evaluated under totality of circumstances: market power of the party imposing RPM, number of manufacturers using RPM (risk of facilitating horizontal dealer cartel), source of the restraint (manufacturer-initiated vs. dealer-pressure-driven), and procompetitive justifications (free-rider prevention, brand investment protection, new market entry). Anticompetitive if used to facilitate a dealer cartel or imposed by a dominant manufacturer to exclude discounters.

- **Maximum RPM and suggested pricing**
  - **What:** Setting a ceiling price or recommending a retail price (MSRP).
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Per se legal under State Oil v. Khan (1997), 522 U.S. 3.
  - **Consequence:** Maximum resale prices and manufacturer's suggested retail prices are lawful. However, a "maximum" that functions as a fixed price (i.e., effectively becomes the actual price through pressure or enforcement) may be challenged.

- **State law divergence**
  - **What:** Several states reject Leegin and maintain per se illegality for minimum RPM under state antitrust statutes.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** California (Cartwright Act), New York (Donnelly Act), Maryland (first state to legislatively reject Leegin, 2009), and other states may treat minimum RPM as per se illegal.
  - **Consequence:** Multistate distribution requires state-by-state analysis. A minimum RPM policy lawful under federal law may still violate state law. See `state-antitrust.md` for state-specific standards.

### Exclusive Dealing

- **What:** A buyer agrees to purchase all or substantially all of its requirements from a single supplier, or a supplier agrees to sell only through a designated distributor.

- **Standard:** Rule of reason. Anticompetitive if it forecloses a "substantial share" of the relevant market from competition.

- **Key factors:**
  - Percentage of market foreclosed (courts have found foreclosure of 30-40%+ problematic, though no bright line exists).
  - Duration of the exclusivity (longer duration = more anticompetitive; short-term exclusive dealing is more likely to be upheld).
  - Availability of alternative channels for excluded competitors.
  - Procompetitive justifications: investment protection, quality assurance, free-riding prevention, supply security.

- **Threshold/Timeline:** Clayton Act Section 3 applies to exclusive dealing in goods; Sherman Act Section 1 applies to both goods and services. De facto exclusivity (requirements contracts, loyalty rebates reaching 80-100% of a buyer's needs) is analyzed the same as express exclusivity.

- **Consequence:** Injunctive relief, treble damages under Clayton Act Section 4, FTC Section 5 enforcement. Exclusive dealing that substantially lessens competition may also support a monopolization claim under Sherman Act Section 2 when imposed by a dominant firm. See `monopolization.md`.

### Tying Arrangements

- **What:** Conditioning the sale of one product (tying product) on the purchase of another (tied product).

- **Standard:** Per se illegal under the traditional formulation when four elements are met:
  1. Two separate products or services exist.
  2. The seller has sufficient market power in the tying product market.
  3. The buyer is coerced into purchasing the tied product (no choice to buy tying product alone).
  4. A not insubstantial amount of commerce in the tied product market is foreclosed.

- **Threshold/Timeline:** Market power in the tying product is required but is not presumed from a patent or copyright after Illinois Tool Works v. Independent Ink (2006), 547 U.S. 28. Some circuits (including the Ninth Circuit in the Microsoft case) apply a modified rule of reason or quasi-per se approach.

- **Consequence:** Clayton Act Section 3 (goods), Sherman Act Section 1 (goods and services), FTC Act Section 5. Treble damages available under Clayton Act Section 4.

- **Bundling distinction:** Pure bundling (products available only as a package) faces greater scrutiny than mixed bundling (products available separately and as a discounted package). Discount attribution test: anticompetitive if the effective price of the competitive product, after attributing the full discount to that product, is below the seller's cost.

- **Technological tying:** Integrating features into a single product is generally analyzed more leniently than contractual tying. Courts distinguish genuine product improvements from pretextual integration designed to foreclose competitors.

### MFN / Price Parity Clauses

- **Wide MFN**
  - **What:** Seller must offer the platform its lowest price across all sales channels, including competing platforms.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Under active enforcement: DOJ sued Google over wide MFN clauses (2023). FTC and state AGs have challenged Amazon's wide MFN practices. EU vertical block exemption (Regulation 2022/720) excludes wide MFNs from the safe harbor.
  - **Consequence:** Wide MFNs can dampen price competition across platforms and facilitate tacit collusion. Likely subject to rule of reason challenge; may be condemned if the platform has significant market power.

- **Narrow MFN**
  - **What:** Seller must offer the platform a price no higher than what it charges on its own direct sales channel.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Generally lower risk but still subject to rule of reason analysis. Can inhibit entry of new, lower-cost platforms if widely adopted.
  - **Consequence:** EU distinguishes wide from narrow MFNs; narrow MFNs benefit from the vertical block exemption. US law does not draw an explicit statutory distinction but treats narrow MFNs more favorably.

### Territorial and Customer Restrictions

- **What:** Manufacturer-imposed restrictions on where or to whom a distributor may sell.

- **Standard:** Rule of reason since Continental T.V. v. GTE Sylvania (1977), 433 U.S. 36.

- **Threshold/Timeline:** Manufacturer-imposed territorial restrictions on distributors are generally lawful when they promote interbrand competition, even if they reduce intrabrand competition. Complete prohibition on sales outside territory (airtight territories) receives closer scrutiny. Location clauses (requiring operation from a specified location) are permissible.

- **Dual distribution:** When a manufacturer sells both directly and through distributors, the relationship has historically been analyzed as vertical (rule of reason). DOJ/FTC 2023 Merger Guidelines and recent case law suggest that dual distribution may warrant horizontal analysis when the manufacturer competes directly with its own distributors for the same customers.

### Refusal to Deal

- **General rule:** Firms generally have no duty to deal with competitors or distributors under Verizon Communications v. Trinko (2004), 540 U.S. 398. A firm may unilaterally choose its trading partners for any reason, absent a specific statutory obligation or a prior voluntary course of dealing.

- **Aspen Skiing exception:** A monopolist's termination of a prior profitable voluntary course of dealing, with no legitimate business justification, may constitute exclusionary conduct under Sherman Act Section 2 (Aspen Skiing Co. v. Aspen Highlands Skiing Corp., 472 U.S. 585 (1985)). Trinko characterized this as "at or near the outer boundary" of Section 2 liability. Narrowly applied.

- **Essential facilities doctrine:** Control of a facility essential to competition, with feasibility of providing access, may create a duty to deal. Doctrine is of uncertain vitality after Trinko. Never expressly adopted by the Supreme Court. See `monopolization.md` for detailed analysis.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection:** MFN clauses and tying arrangements in consumer-facing agreements may also violate FTC Act Section 5 unfair methods of competition and state UDAP statutes. See `consumer-protection/`.
- **IP and Technology:** Patent and copyright licensing restrictions (field-of-use, territorial, customer restrictions) are analyzed under antitrust principles but benefit from additional latitude under the DOJ/FTC IP Licensing Guidelines (2017). Tying of patented and unpatented products is subject to Illinois Tool Works. See `ip-and-technology/`.
- **Financial Services:** Exclusive dealing arrangements in payment processing, banking relationships, and insurance distribution may raise both antitrust and financial regulatory concerns. See `financial-services/`.
- **Monopolization:** Vertical restraints imposed by a firm with monopoly power may support a Section 2 monopolization claim. Exclusive dealing and tying are common theories. See `monopolization.md`.
- **State Antitrust:** Several states apply stricter standards to vertical restraints, particularly minimum RPM. See `state-antitrust.md`.
