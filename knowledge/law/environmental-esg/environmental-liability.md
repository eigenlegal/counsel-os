# Environmental Liability

## Applicability

Applies when a matter involves potential liability under federal or state environmental statutes, including contamination, hazardous waste management, air emissions, water discharges, or regulatory enforcement. Critical in transactions involving industrial properties, manufacturing operations, and any entity that generates, transports, or disposes of hazardous substances.

## Key Requirements

### CERCLA (Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act)

- **Strict Liability** / Liability under Section 107 does not require proof of negligence or intent; mere ownership, operation, or arrangement for disposal is sufficient. / **Consequence**: Current owners/operators face full cleanup liability regardless of fault; contractual indemnities from sellers do not shield against EPA enforcement (EPA can pursue any PRP directly).
- **Joint and Several Liability** / Any single PRP may be held liable for the entire cost of remediation, even if multiple parties contributed. / **Consequence**: The most solvent or accessible PRP often bears disproportionate costs; contribution actions under Section 113(f) allow PRPs to seek allocation from other responsible parties, but only AFTER being sued or entering a settlement with the government.
- **Retroactive Liability** / Applies to contamination events occurring before CERCLA's enactment in 1980. / **Consequence**: A company that lawfully disposed of waste in the 1960s may be fully liable for cleanup costs decades later; no statute of limitations for initial cost recovery actions by the government.
- **PRP Categories** / Four categories: (1) current owners/operators, (2) owners/operators at time of disposal, (3) arrangers (generators), (4) transporters who selected the site. / **Consequence**: "Arranger" liability extends to parties who arranged for treatment or disposal even if they did not physically transport the waste; Burlington Northern (2009) narrowed but did not eliminate arranger liability.
- **Contribution and Allocation** / Section 113(f) allows PRPs to seek contribution using equitable factors (Gore factors): volume, toxicity, cooperation, ability to pay. / **Consequence**: Allocation is highly fact-intensive; parties settling with EPA receive contribution protection (Section 113(f)(2)), incentivizing early settlement.
- **Cleanup Cost Magnitude** / Superfund site cleanups average **$12-30 million**; complex sites can exceed **$500 million**. / **Consequence**: Environmental liability can dwarf the value of the underlying transaction; environmental insurance and indemnity escrows are essential risk management tools.
- **PFAS as CERCLA Hazardous Substances** / EPA designated PFOA and PFOS as CERCLA hazardous substances by final rule (89 Fed. Reg. 39124), effective **July 8, 2024**, exposing PRPs to Superfund response-cost and contribution liability for those compounds. / **Consequence**: The designation is under challenge in the D.C. Circuit (*Chamber of Commerce v. EPA*, No. 24-1193); EPA announced on September 17, 2025 that it will continue to **defend** the rule, and oral argument was held January 20, 2026 with a decision pending as of this check. EPA's April 2024 PFAS CERCLA Enforcement Discretion and Settlement Policy focuses enforcement on significant contributors (PFAS manufacturers, federal facilities, industrial users) and signals discretion not to pursue "passive receivers" such as farmers, water utilities, POTWs, municipal landfills, airports, and fire departments — but it does NOT bar private parties from seeking §113 contribution from those entities. EPA also intends future rulemaking on the §102(a) designation framework. Treat PFOA/PFOS as designated unless and until the rule is vacated; verify the litigation outcome before advising on transactional PFAS allocation.

### RCRA (Resource Conservation and Recovery Act)

- **Cradle-to-Grave Tracking** / Generators, transporters, and treatment/storage/disposal facilities (TSDFs) must track hazardous waste from generation to disposal via manifest system. / **Consequence**: Generator liability does not end at disposal; improper disposal at a TSDF creates CERCLA liability for the generator as an "arranger."
- **Generator Categories** / Large Quantity Generators (LQGs: **1,000+ kg/month**), Small Quantity Generators (SQGs: **100-1,000 kg/month**), Very Small Quantity Generators (VSQGs: **<100 kg/month**). / **Consequence**: LQGs face the most stringent requirements — biennial reporting, personnel training, contingency plans, **90-day** maximum accumulation time. VSQGs have reduced requirements but are not exempt from CERCLA liability.
- **Corrective Action** / EPA can require facility-wide cleanup of all releases at RCRA-permitted facilities under Section 3008(h). / **Consequence**: Corrective action obligations can extend to contamination unrelated to the permitted operations; costs can rival Superfund cleanup costs.

### Clean Air Act (CAA)

- **Title V Operating Permits** / Major sources (emitting **100 tons/year** or more of any criteria pollutant, or **10 tons/year** of any HAP, or **25 tons/year** of combined HAPs) must obtain Title V permits. / **Consequence**: Operating without a Title V permit is a criminal offense; penalties up to **$100,000/day** per violation. Permit conditions become federally enforceable, allowing citizen suits.
- **New Source Review (NSR)** / New construction or major modifications at stationary sources in nonattainment areas require NSR permits with Lowest Achievable Emission Rate (LAER) technology. / **Consequence**: NSR can add **12-24 months** to project timelines; failure to obtain NSR permits before construction triggers enforcement, mandatory shutdown, and penalties.
- **GHG Reporting** / Facilities emitting **25,000+ metric tons CO2e/year** have reported under EPA's Greenhouse Gas Reporting Program (40 CFR Part 98). / **Consequence**: Reported data is publicly available; feeds into climate disclosure and enforcement. **Status (checked 2026-06-11): the GHGRP is in flux.** EPA proposed to rescind most of the program on September 16, 2025 (FR Doc. 2025-17923) — under the proposal, facilities in ~46 source categories would no longer report after reporting year 2024; the proposal remains pending and EPA extended the RY2025 reporting deadline to October 30, 2026. Relatedly, EPA finalized rescission of the 2009 GHG endangerment finding and the motor-vehicle GHG standards effective April 20, 2026 (FR Doc. 2026-03157). Confirm whether a facility still has a current Part 98 reporting obligation before advising.

### Clean Water Act (CWA)

- **NPDES Permits** / Any discharge of pollutants from a point source to waters of the United States requires a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System permit. / **Consequence**: Unpermitted discharges face substantial per-day civil penalties; both EPA and citizens can bring enforcement actions. The per-day maximums are adjusted for inflation annually — confirm the current figure in **40 CFR 19.4** (CWA judicial/Class II per-day amounts were in the ~$27,000-$28,000/day range for 2025, with separate, higher aggregate caps) rather than relying on a fixed dollar amount.
- **Stormwater Permits** / Construction activities disturbing **1+ acre** require a stormwater construction general permit; industrial facilities with specific SIC codes require multi-sector general permits. / **Consequence**: Failure to obtain stormwater permits is one of the most common CWA violations; penalties are substantial and citizen suit enforcement is active.
- **Wetlands (Section 404)** / Fill or dredge of wetlands requires a Section 404 permit from the Army Corps of Engineers. / **Consequence**: Unauthorized fill of wetlands triggers restoration obligations, per-day civil penalties (inflation-adjusted; see 40 CFR 19.4), and potential criminal prosecution for knowing violations. *Sackett v. EPA*, 598 U.S. 651 (2023) narrowed "waters of the United States" to wetlands with a continuous surface connection to a relatively permanent water; significant wetland protections remain, and agency WOTUS rulemaking has continued to shift — verify the current WOTUS definition for jurisdictional calls.

### Penalty Framework

- **Civil Penalties** / EPA penalties across environmental statutes range from **$25,000 to $100,000+ per day** per violation, adjusted annually for inflation. / **Consequence**: Multi-year violations can result in penalties in the tens of millions; penalty calculations consider gravity, economic benefit of noncompliance, history of violations, and ability to pay.
- **Criminal Penalties** / Knowing violations of RCRA, CAA, and CWA carry criminal penalties including imprisonment (up to **15 years** for knowing endangerment under RCRA). / **Consequence**: Individual officers and managers face personal criminal liability; "responsible corporate officer" doctrine does not require proof that the individual personally committed the violation.
- **Supplemental Environmental Projects (SEPs)** / Community-benefit projects historically used to offset a portion of federal civil penalties. / **Consequence**: **Status (checked 2026-06-11): federal SEPs are currently disfavored/prohibited.** DOJ rescinded the memoranda authorizing SEPs and third-party/community payments on February 5, 2025, on the view that such payments to non-victim third parties are improper; do not assume a federal SEP is available in EPA/DOJ settlements without checking current DOJ/EPA policy. State SEP programs (e.g., California, Texas) are unaffected and continue to operate.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Real Estate** — CERCLA liability in property transactions, Phase I/II requirements, environmental covenants
- **Corporate** — Officer/director liability for environmental violations, D&O coverage for environmental claims
- **Securities** — Environmental liabilities as material contingencies requiring disclosure in SEC filings
- **Insurance** — Pollution exclusions in CGL policies, pollution legal liability policies, cleanup cost cap insurance
- **Bankruptcy & Restructuring** — Environmental claims in bankruptcy (government cleanup costs as administrative priority claims)
