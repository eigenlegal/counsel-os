# ESG Reporting

## Applicability

Applies when a company prepares, reviews, or is subject to environmental, social, and governance (ESG) reporting obligations or voluntary frameworks. Covers both mandatory disclosure regimes and voluntary standards that may create binding representations through repetition, incorporation into contracts, or reliance by investors.

## Key Requirements

### GRI (Global Reporting Initiative)

- **GRI Universal Standards** / GRI 1 (Foundation), GRI 2 (General Disclosures), GRI 3 (Material Topics). Required for any organization claiming GRI-conformant reporting. / **Consequence**: Claiming "in accordance with GRI" without meeting all requirements is misleading; partial use must be clearly labeled as "with reference to GRI." Stakeholder groups and regulators increasingly treat GRI reports as binding commitments.
- **Impact Materiality** / GRI assesses materiality based on the organization's impact on economy, environment, and people — not financial materiality to the organization. / **Consequence**: GRI's impact materiality can require disclosure of issues that are not financially material under SEC standards; creates broader disclosure obligations than investor-focused frameworks.
- **Sector Standards** / GRI is developing sector-specific standards (oil & gas, agriculture, mining, etc.) that supplement the universal standards. / **Consequence**: Sector standards introduce additional mandatory disclosures for organizations in covered sectors reporting under GRI.

### SASB (Sustainability Accounting Standards Board)

- **Industry-Specific Metrics** / SASB provides disclosure topics and metrics for **77 industries** grouped into **11 sectors**, focused on financially material sustainability issues. / **Consequence**: SASB metrics are designed for investor decision-making; now consolidated under the IFRS Foundation (ISSB), SASB standards continue as industry-specific guidance feeding into IFRS S1/S2.
- **Financial Materiality** / SASB applies a financial materiality lens — only issues reasonably likely to affect a company's financial condition or operating performance. / **Consequence**: Narrower than GRI's impact materiality; a company reporting under both SASB and GRI may need to disclose on different topics for the same subject area.

### TCFD / ISSB

- **Four Pillars** / TCFD framework (now subsumed into ISSB): Governance, Strategy, Risk Management, Metrics and Targets. / **Consequence**: TCFD has been adopted as the basis for mandatory reporting in the UK, EU (CSRD), Japan, Singapore, and California (SB 261). TCFD recommendations formally transferred to ISSB as of **October 2023**.
- **Scenario Analysis** / TCFD recommends climate scenario analysis (at minimum, a 2 degrees C or below scenario) to assess resilience of strategy. / **Consequence**: Scenario analysis is now mandatory under CSRD (ESRS E1) and California SB 261; SEC rules require qualitative discussion of scenario analysis if used internally.
- **ISSB Adoption Timeline** / IFRS S1 and S2 effective for periods beginning **January 1, 2024**; jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction adoption varies. / **Consequence**: Companies operating in multiple jurisdictions may face overlapping ISSB, CSRD, and SEC requirements with different scopes and timelines; convergence is incomplete.

### CSRD Double Materiality

- **Assessment Process** / Companies must conduct a double materiality assessment identifying topics that are material from both financial (outside-in) and impact (inside-out) perspectives. / **Consequence**: The double materiality assessment must be documented and forms the basis for determining which ESRS topics require disclosure; auditors will review the assessment methodology.
- **Mandatory Standards** / ESRS includes **12 standards** covering environment (5), social (4), and governance (1), plus cross-cutting standards (2). All are mandatory subject to materiality assessment. / **Consequence**: Even topics assessed as not material must include an explanation of why they were excluded; the "rebuttable presumption" for climate (ESRS E1) means climate is always material unless the company can prove otherwise.
- **Value Chain Scope** / CSRD requires reporting on the entire value chain, not just the reporting entity's own operations. / **Consequence**: Companies must collect data from suppliers, customers, and other value chain partners; **3-year transitional relief** for value chain data using estimates and sector averages.

### Mandatory vs. Voluntary Reporting

- **Mandatory Regimes** / SEC (U.S. public companies), CSRD (EU large companies + listed SMEs), California SB 253/261, UK Sustainability Disclosure Requirements. / **Consequence**: Mandatory disclosure carries regulatory enforcement, auditor attestation requirements, and securities liability for misstatements.
- **Voluntary Commitments** / Voluntary ESG reports, CDP disclosures, Science-Based Targets initiative (SBTi) commitments, RE100 pledges. / **Consequence**: Voluntary commitments can become legally binding through incorporation into contracts, reasonable investor reliance, or FTC/SEC enforcement if claims are materially misleading. **Once published, withdrawal creates its own reputational and legal risk.**
- **UN Global Compact / PRI** / Voluntary frameworks requiring annual communication on progress; PRI signatories must meet minimum reporting requirements. / **Consequence**: Failure to meet minimum requirements results in delisting (PRI has delisted **over 400 signatories** since 2020); delisting signals governance failure to institutional investors.

### Assurance Requirements

- **Limited vs. Reasonable Assurance** / Limited assurance (negative assurance — "nothing has come to our attention") vs. reasonable assurance (positive assurance — "in our opinion, the information is materially correct"). / **Consequence**: CSRD requires limited assurance initially, moving to reasonable assurance by **2028**. SEC climate rules require limited assurance progressing to reasonable assurance for LAFs. California SB 253 follows a similar phase-in.
- **Assurance Provider Qualifications** / CSRD: statutory auditors or accredited independent assurance services providers. SEC: registered public accounting firms or qualified attestation providers. / **Consequence**: Assurance market capacity is currently constrained; companies should engage assurance providers early to secure capacity and establish baseline processes.

### Greenwashing Enforcement

- **SEC Enforcement** / SEC has brought enforcement actions against investment advisers and companies for misleading ESG claims (e.g., DWS/BaFin investigation, Goldman ESG fund settlement). / **Consequence**: SEC penalties for ESG misrepresentation range from **$1.5 million to $19 million** in recent settlements; ESG task force within the Division of Enforcement is active.
- **EU Green Claims Directive** / Proposed directive requiring substantiation of environmental claims with recognized scientific evidence and lifecycle assessments before making public claims. / **Consequence**: Once adopted, blanket claims like "eco-friendly" or "sustainable" will require specific, verifiable substantiation; penalties determined by member states.
- **FTC Enforcement** / FTC pursues deceptive environmental marketing claims under Section 5 of the FTC Act, guided by the Green Guides. / **Consequence**: FTC enforcement includes consent orders, civil penalties for violation of consent orders (up to **$50,120 per violation**), and corrective advertising requirements.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Securities** — ESG disclosures in SEC filings, materiality determinations, safe harbor applicability
- **Consumer Protection** — Greenwashing claims in marketing, FTC Green Guides compliance
- **Data Privacy** — ESG data collection from employees and supply chain partners
- **Corporate** — Board ESG oversight, fiduciary duties, stakeholder engagement
- **Climate Disclosure** — ESG reporting frameworks feed into mandatory climate disclosure regimes
