## Climate Disclosure

# Climate Disclosure

## Applicability

Applies when a company is subject to or may become subject to mandatory climate-related disclosure requirements. Relevant to public companies, large private companies, and any entity in the value chain of a reporting company. Triggered by SEC filings, EU operations, California revenue thresholds, or voluntary climate commitments that may become binding representations.

## Key Requirements

### SEC Climate Disclosure Rules

- **Scope 1 and Scope 2 Emissions** / Large accelerated filers must disclose Scope 1 (direct) and Scope 2 (purchased energy) greenhouse gas emissions, subject to materiality. / **Consequence**: Emissions data must be audited by an independent attestation provider; phased timeline begins with limited assurance, moving to reasonable assurance. Material misstatement triggers securities fraud liability under Section 10(b) and Rule 10b-5.
- **Scope 3 Emissions** / Disclosure of Scope 3 (value chain) emissions if material or if the company has set a Scope 3 target. Safe harbor for Scope 3 data applies. / **Consequence**: Scope 3 disclosure is the most challenging — requires supply chain data that may be unreliable; safe harbor protects against private litigation but not SEC enforcement for knowingly false statements.
- **Material Climate Risks** / Disclosure of material climate-related risks (physical and transition), their actual and potential impacts on strategy, business model, and financial statements. / **Consequence**: Must quantify financial impact where reasonably estimable; qualitative disclosure required at minimum. Failure to disclose material climate risks may constitute omission under Rule 10b-5.
- **Governance and Risk Management** / Board oversight of climate-related risks, management's role, and integration into enterprise risk management. / **Consequence**: Creates accountability trail; directors may face derivative claims if governance disclosures do not match actual oversight practices.

### EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)

- **Applicability Thresholds** / Large EU companies and listed SMEs. Non-EU companies with **EUR 150 million** net turnover in the EU and at least one EU subsidiary or branch. / **Consequence**: First reporting cycle for large companies: fiscal years starting **January 1, 2024**; non-EU companies: fiscal years starting **January 1, 2028**. Non-compliance penalties set by member states, potentially up to **5% of annual turnover**.
- **Double Materiality** / Must report on both financial materiality (outside-in: how sustainability affects the company) and impact materiality (inside-out: how the company affects environment and society). / **Consequence**: Broader than SEC's financial materiality standard; requires assessment of the company's impact on climate regardless of financial relevance to the company.
- **European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS)** / Mandatory detailed standards covering climate change (ESRS E1), pollution, water, biodiversity, and circular economy. / **Consequence**: ESRS E1 requires transition plan disclosure, Scope 1/2/3 emissions, and Paris-alignment analysis; far more prescriptive than SEC requirements.

### California Climate Disclosure Laws

- **SB 253 (Climate Corporate Data Accountability Act)** / Companies doing business in California with total annual revenue exceeding **$1 billion** must report Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions annually. / **Consequence**: Scope 1 and 2 reporting begins **2026** (for 2025 data); Scope 3 begins **2027**. Third-party assurance required: limited assurance by **2026**, reasonable assurance by **2030**. Penalties up to **$500,000** per reporting year for non-filing.
- **SB 261 (Climate-Related Financial Risk Act)** / Companies doing business in California with total annual revenue exceeding **$500 million** must prepare and publish biennial climate-related financial risk reports in accordance with TCFD framework. / **Consequence**: First report due **January 1, 2026**. Penalties up to **$50,000** per reporting year for non-compliance. Reports must be publicly available.
- **"Doing Business in California"** / Threshold broadly interpreted — includes any company with California revenue, employees, or property. / **Consequence**: Captures many out-of-state and international companies; the revenue threshold is based on worldwide revenue, not California-specific revenue.

### ISSB Standards (International Sustainability Standards Board)

- **IFRS S1 (General Sustainability Disclosures)** / Requires disclosure of material sustainability-related financial information, including governance, strategy, risk management, and metrics/targets. / **Consequence**: Effective for annual reporting periods beginning **January 1, 2024** (adoption varies by jurisdiction). Jurisdictions adopting ISSB include the UK, Canada, Australia, Japan, Singapore, and others.
- **IFRS S2 (Climate-Related Disclosures)** / Climate-specific standard requiring Scope 1/2/3 emissions, transition plans, climate-related targets, and use of carbon credits. / **Consequence**: Scope 3 relief in first year of application; must disclose the approach used for measuring emissions (GHG Protocol or equivalent). Builds on and supersedes TCFD recommendations.

### Penalties and Enforcement

- **SEC Enforcement** / Securities fraud claims for material misstatements or omissions in climate disclosures. / **Consequence**: Civil penalties up to **$2.1 million** per violation for entities; officer/director liability for certifications; private class action exposure.
- **State AG Enforcement** / State attorneys general (particularly New York, California) have pursued greenwashing and climate disclosure cases under state consumer protection and securities laws. / **Consequence**: Penalties vary by state; New York Martin Act does not require scienter, lowering the enforcement threshold.
- **EU Enforcement** / CSRD non-compliance enforced by member state competent authorities. / **Consequence**: Sanctions include financial penalties, public statements, and potential delisting; auditor liability for assurance opinions on sustainability reports.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Securities** — Climate disclosures are part of SEC filings; materiality analysis under securities law applies
- **Corporate** — Board governance and oversight requirements; fiduciary duty to manage climate risk
- **Consumer Protection** — Climate claims in marketing must be consistent with formal disclosures
- **Data Privacy** — Supply chain data collection for Scope 3 may implicate data sharing agreements
- **Environmental Liability** — Physical climate risks overlap with environmental compliance obligations
