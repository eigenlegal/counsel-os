## Sustainability Representations

# Sustainability Representations

## Applicability

Applies when a company makes environmental or sustainability claims in marketing, advertising, product labeling, contracts, or public communications. Covers FTC Green Guides, EU Green Claims Directive, contractual sustainability warranties, and the legal risk of unsubstantiated environmental claims (greenwashing).

## Key Requirements

### FTC Green Guides

- **General Environmental Claims** / Unqualified claims like "eco-friendly," "green," or "environmentally safe" convey that the product has no negative environmental impact. / **Consequence**: Such broad claims are almost impossible to substantiate and are presumptively deceptive under the Green Guides. FTC enforcement results in consent orders requiring cessation, corrective advertising, and civil penalties up to **$50,120 per violation** of a consent order.
- **Carbon Neutral / Net Zero Claims** / Claims of carbon neutrality must be substantiated with reliable offset credits and transparent methodology. / **Consequence**: FTC is revising the Green Guides (expected **2025-2026**) to specifically address carbon offset and net-zero claims. Current guidance requires that offsets be real, additional, permanent, and verified. Unsubstantiated carbon neutral claims have triggered FTC investigations and state AG enforcement.
- **Recyclable Claims** / A product may only be labeled "recyclable" if it can be collected, separated, and recovered through an established recycling program available to a **substantial majority** (at least **60%**) of consumers or communities where the product is sold. / **Consequence**: If recycling access is limited, the claim must be qualified (e.g., "recyclable in communities where facilities exist"); unqualified claims when access is below 60% are deceptive.
- **Compostable Claims** / Product must completely break down into usable compost in a safe and timely manner in an appropriate composting facility or home compost. / **Consequence**: If the product requires industrial composting, the claim must be qualified; consumers assume "compostable" means home-compostable. Misleading compostable claims face the same enforcement as other deceptive environmental claims.
- **Made with Renewable Materials / Renewable Energy** / Claims must specify the percentage and type of renewable material or energy. / **Consequence**: "Made with renewable energy" when only **10%** of energy is renewable is deceptive without qualification; must disclose the actual percentage.
- **Degradable Claims** / Product must completely decompose within **1 year** after customary disposal. / **Consequence**: Landfill conditions generally prevent degradation; "degradable" claims for products typically disposed in landfills are deceptive.

### EU Green Claims Directive (Proposed)

- **Substantiation Requirement** / Before making any environmental claim, companies must conduct a science-based assessment using recognized methodologies (e.g., PEF, OEF). / **Consequence**: Claims must be verified by an accredited third-party verifier before publication; unsubstantiated claims are prohibited. Penalties set by member states, expected to include fines of at least **4% of annual turnover**.
- **Comparative Claims** / Environmental comparisons must use equivalent methodologies, cover the same lifecycle stages, and use up-to-date data. / **Consequence**: Selective comparisons (e.g., comparing only one environmental attribute while ignoring others where the product performs worse) are prohibited.
- **Offset-Based Claims** / Claims like "climate neutral" or "carbon compensated" based solely on carbon offsets are effectively prohibited. / **Consequence**: Companies cannot use offsets to claim environmental neutrality; offsets must be supplementary to actual emissions reductions, and this must be clearly communicated.
- **Sustainability Labels** / Only labels based on official certification schemes or established by public authorities will be permitted; new private label schemes require government approval. / **Consequence**: Proliferation of private eco-labels will be curtailed; existing labels must meet transparency and third-party verification requirements.

### Substantiation Requirements

- **Competent and Reliable Scientific Evidence** / FTC standard: tests, analyses, research, or studies conducted and evaluated by qualified professionals using accepted methods. / **Consequence**: Anecdotal evidence, internal assessments without methodology, and outdated studies do not constitute adequate substantiation; the burden of proof is on the claim-maker.
- **Lifecycle Assessment (LCA)** / Comprehensive environmental assessment from raw material extraction through disposal. ISO 14040/14044 standards. / **Consequence**: Full LCA is the gold standard for substantiation but costs **$50,000-$200,000** and takes **6-12 months**; streamlined LCA may be acceptable for specific claims.
- **Pre-Claim Substantiation** / Substantiation must exist BEFORE the claim is made, not developed after an enforcement inquiry. / **Consequence**: Retroactive substantiation is a red flag for enforcers; companies should maintain a substantiation file for every environmental claim.

### Contract Clauses — Sustainability Representations

- **Supplier Sustainability Warranties** / Contractual representations that products meet specified environmental standards (carbon footprint, recycled content, chemical restrictions). / **Consequence**: Breach of sustainability warranty creates contractual liability; damages may include cost of recall, replacement, regulatory penalties, and reputational harm. Warranty survival periods of **2-3 years** are typical.
- **Supply Chain Compliance** / Representations that the supply chain complies with specified environmental and social standards (e.g., no deforestation, fair labor). / **Consequence**: EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) will require companies to conduct and disclose supply chain due diligence; contractual flow-down provisions are essential.
- **Environmental Indemnity for Claims** / Indemnification from supplier if sustainability representations prove false and buyer faces regulatory action or third-party claims. / **Consequence**: Indemnity should cover defense costs, penalties, corrective advertising, and consequential damages (product recall, lost sales); caps should be sized to the exposure.
- **Audit and Verification Rights** / Buyer right to audit supplier's sustainability claims and supporting documentation, typically with **30 days** notice. / **Consequence**: Without audit rights, sustainability representations are unverifiable; audit rights should include access to sub-suppliers for supply chain claims.

### Enforcement Trends

- **FTC Actions** / FTC has increased greenwashing enforcement, targeting both large corporations and smaller companies making unsubstantiated claims. / **Consequence**: Recent actions include cases involving plastic recyclability claims, "bamboo" textile labeling (actually rayon), and biodegradable claims for products disposed in landfills.
- **State AG Actions** / State attorneys general, particularly in New York, California, and Massachusetts, actively pursue greenwashing under state consumer protection statutes. / **Consequence**: State actions can proceed in parallel with FTC enforcement; state penalties can be substantial (New York: up to **$5,000 per violation** under GBL Section 350).
- **Class Action Litigation** / Private plaintiffs increasingly file class actions alleging deceptive environmental marketing under state consumer protection statutes and common law fraud. / **Consequence**: Class action settlements for greenwashing claims have ranged from **$5 million to $50 million+**; even unsuccessful claims impose significant defense costs.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection** — Greenwashing enforcement under FTC Act and state UDAP statutes
- **ESG Reporting** — Sustainability claims in reports must be consistent with marketing claims
- **Climate Disclosure** — Carbon neutral claims must align with formal emissions disclosures
- **IP and Technology** — Sustainability certifications and eco-label trademarks
- **International Trade** — EU Green Claims Directive and Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) affect imported goods
