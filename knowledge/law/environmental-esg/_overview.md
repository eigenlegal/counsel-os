## Key Constraints

These are non-overridable legal requirements from this area. They cannot be modified by practice/ or matters/ overrides. Several headline obligations in this area are in active flux (the SEC climate rule, the EU CSRD/CSDDD Omnibus changes, EPA deregulatory rulemakings) — verify the current status in the relevant sub-file before relying on a date or threshold.

- CERCLA imposes strict, joint-and-several, and retroactive liability on potentially responsible parties; private contractual indemnities reallocate cost between parties but cannot extinguish a PRP's direct liability to the government (42 U.S.C. § 9607).
- PFOA and PFOS are designated CERCLA hazardous substances (effective July 8, 2024), subjecting PRPs to Superfund response-cost and contribution liability; the designation is under D.C. Circuit challenge (No. 24-1193), so confirm its current validity before relying on it.
- To preserve the CERCLA innocent-purchaser, contiguous-property-owner, and bona fide prospective purchaser defenses, All Appropriate Inquiry (40 CFR Part 312, satisfied by a current ASTM E1527-21 Phase I) must be completed BEFORE acquisition; a post-closing Phase I does not qualify.
- Discharging pollutants from a point source to waters of the United States without an NPDES permit, and discharging regulated air pollutants without required Clean Air Act permits, are prohibited regardless of contractual arrangements; criminal liability can attach to responsible corporate officers.
- Environmental marketing claims must be truthful, non-misleading, and substantiated by competent and reliable evidence at the time the claim is made; the FTC Green Guides (16 CFR Part 260) and Section 5 of the FTC Act prohibit deceptive green claims, and substantiation cannot be manufactured retroactively.
- Mandatory sustainability/climate disclosure obligations arise from specific live regimes (e.g., California SB 253; EU CSRD as amended by the Omnibus process; ISSB-adopting jurisdictions), not from the 2024 SEC climate rule, which never took effect and is proposed for rescission (Release 33-11421, May 29, 2026); general securities anti-fraud and materiality principles still govern any climate statements an issuer chooses or is required to make.
- Confirmed contamination exceeding applicable regulatory screening levels generally triggers mandatory reporting to the relevant agency within statutory timeframes; failure to report known contamination is independently actionable and may be criminal.
