## Key Constraints

These are non-overridable legal requirements from this area. They cannot be modified by practice/ or matters/ overrides.

- Horizontal price-fixing, bid rigging, and market allocation agreements between competitors are per se illegal under Sherman Act Section 1 and are federal crimes subject to imprisonment and substantial fines.
- No-poach and wage-fixing agreements between competing employers are treated as criminal antitrust violations by the DOJ Antitrust Division.
- HSR Act pre-merger notification is required for transactions meeting applicable thresholds; closing before expiration of the waiting period is a violation subject to penalties of up to $51,744 per day.
- Antitrust criminal investigations carry substantial individual liability, including imprisonment (up to 10 years per Sherman Act violation) and individual fines (up to $1M per violation).
- Antitrust leniency programs incentivize self-reporting of cartel conduct; the first participant to report and cooperate may receive immunity from criminal prosecution (DOJ Leniency Program).
- Treble damages are available to private plaintiffs in antitrust civil actions under Clayton Act Section 4, creating substantial financial exposure for antitrust violations.

## Horizontal Restraints

# Horizontal Restraints

## Applicability

Sherman Act Section 1 (15 U.S.C. 1) prohibits contracts, combinations, and conspiracies in restraint of trade. Horizontal restraints involve agreements between competitors operating at the same level of the supply chain. These provisions apply when ANY of the following are true:

- Two or more competitors enter an agreement, understanding, or coordinated practice affecting price, output, market allocation, or bidding.
- Competing employers agree on employee compensation terms, hiring practices, or no-poach commitments.
- Competitors participate in information exchanges involving competitively sensitive data (current or future pricing, capacity, customer-specific terms).
- Competitors form joint ventures, trade associations, or standard-setting organizations that may facilitate coordination.

An "agreement" under Section 1 does not require a written contract. Circumstantial evidence of parallel conduct plus "plus factors" (e.g., actions against individual self-interest, meetings or communications, market structure conducive to collusion) can establish an agreement.

## Key Requirements

### Per Se Illegal Agreements

Certain horizontal agreements are conclusively presumed unreasonable. No procompetitive justification, market power analysis, or competitive effects evidence is permitted as a defense.

- **Price-fixing**
  - **What:** Any agreement among competitors to raise, lower, fix, peg, or stabilize prices or any component of price (discounts, credit terms, surcharges).
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Criminal felony. No minimum threshold of commerce or market share required.
  - **Consequence:** Up to $100M corporate fine, $1M individual fine, and 10 years imprisonment per violation (15 U.S.C. 1, as amended by the Antitrust Criminal Penalty Enhancement and Reform Act). Treble damages in private civil actions under Clayton Act Section 4 (15 U.S.C. 15). Alternative fine of 2x gain or 2x loss if greater than the statutory maximum.

- **Bid rigging**
  - **What:** Agreement to predetermine the winning bid, suppress bids, rotate winning bids, or submit complementary (cover) bids to create the appearance of competition.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Criminal felony. Commonly prosecuted in government procurement, construction, and defense contracting.
  - **Consequence:** Same criminal penalties as price-fixing. DOJ prosecutes as a federal crime. Debarment from government contracts is a common collateral consequence.

- **Market allocation**
  - **What:** Agreements dividing customers, territories, or product lines among competitors.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Criminal felony. Includes customer allocation, territorial allocation, and product market division.
  - **Consequence:** Same criminal penalties as price-fixing. Applies even when the allocated markets are not currently served by all conspirators.

- **Group boycotts (concerted refusals to deal)**
  - **What:** Competitors agree to exclude a rival, supplier, or customer, or to coerce compliance from a third party.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Per se illegal when undertaken by competitors with market power or to enforce anticompetitive restraints. Some boycotts analyzed under rule of reason if involving standard-setting or self-regulation with legitimate objectives.
  - **Consequence:** Treble damages. Injunctive relief. Criminal prosecution less common than for price-fixing or bid rigging but available.

### Labor Market Per Se Violations

- **No-poach agreements**
  - **What:** Competing employers agree not to solicit or hire each other's employees, outside a legitimate business collaboration.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** DOJ treats as criminal per se violations since October 2016 (DOJ/FTC Antitrust Guidance for Human Resource Professionals). First criminal indictments brought in 2020-2021.
  - **Consequence:** Same penalties as price-fixing. Applies to agreements between any competing employers, including franchise systems.

- **Wage-fixing**
  - **What:** Competing employers agree to fix, depress, or stabilize compensation levels.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Criminal per se violation. Applies to salary, benefits, bonuses, and other terms of compensation.
  - **Consequence:** Same penalties as horizontal price-fixing.

- **Carve-out:** Non-solicitation provisions that are ancillary to a legitimate business transaction (M&A, joint venture, franchise, outsourcing) are analyzed under the rule of reason, not per se. The restraint must be reasonably necessary to achieve the transaction's procompetitive objectives and limited in scope and duration.

### Rule of Reason Agreements

Agreements among competitors that involve meaningful integration of economic activity and plausible procompetitive justifications are analyzed under the rule of reason or the "quick look" framework.

- **Joint ventures**
  - **What:** Competitors combine resources, capital, or operations to pursue a specific business objective.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Must involve meaningful integration (not merely a label). Ancillary restraints (non-competes, exclusivity) must be reasonably necessary to achieve the venture's procompetitive objectives.
  - **Consequence:** Naked restraints disguised as joint venture provisions are still per se illegal. DOJ/FTC Competitor Collaboration Guidelines (2000) provide the analytical framework.

- **Information exchanges**
  - **What:** Sharing competitively sensitive data among competitors.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** High risk: current or future pricing, customer-specific data, capacity plans, bid intentions. Lower risk: aggregated, historical (3+ months old), anonymized data shared through an independent third party.
  - **Consequence:** Information exchange that facilitates price-fixing or market allocation may be treated as evidence of a per se violation, not merely a rule of reason case.

- **Standard-setting organizations**
  - **What:** Collaborative development of technical standards among competitors.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Must include safeguards: open participation, balanced representation, FRAND (fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory) licensing commitments for standard-essential patents.
  - **Consequence:** Risk of "hold-up" when participants fail to disclose essential patents during the standard-setting process. See `ip-and-technology/` for FRAND analysis.

- **Quick look analysis**
  - **What:** Intermediate standard for agreements that appear facially anticompetitive but involve some integration.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Burden shifts to the defendant to demonstrate procompetitive justifications. If justifications are offered, full rule of reason applies.
  - **Consequence:** Applied in cases such as NCAA v. Board of Regents (1984) and California Dental Association (1999).

### Statutes of Limitations

- **Private civil actions:** 4 years from accrual of the cause of action (Clayton Act Section 4B, 15 U.S.C. 15b). Tolled during any government antitrust proceeding involving the same conduct. Fraudulent concealment may extend the period.
- **Criminal prosecutions:** 5 years from the last act in furtherance of the conspiracy (18 U.S.C. 3282). Continuing conspiracy doctrine means the SOL runs from the last overt act, not the date of the original agreement.

### Leniency and Immunity

- **DOJ Leniency Program**
  - **What:** First corporation to self-report cartel conduct and fully cooperate receives immunity from criminal prosecution.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Available only to the first applicant. Must not have been the leader or originator of the conspiracy. Must provide full, continuing, and complete cooperation. Individuals within the leniency applicant also receive immunity.
  - **Consequence:** Leniency applicant is still exposed to single (not treble) civil damages under ACPERA (Antitrust Criminal Penalty Enhancement and Reform Act of 2004, reauthorized 2020). Subsequent cooperators may receive reduced fines and sentencing recommendations.

### Penalties Summary

| Category | Criminal Fine (Corp.) | Criminal Fine (Indiv.) | Imprisonment | Civil Damages |
|----------|----------------------|----------------------|--------------|---------------|
| Per se violation | Up to $100M | Up to $1M | Up to 10 years | Treble (3x actual) |
| Alternative fine | 2x gain or 2x loss (if greater than statutory max) | Same | Same | Plus attorneys' fees |

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment:** No-poach and wage-fixing between competitors are criminal antitrust violations, distinct from employer-employee non-compete agreements (see `employment/` for FTC non-compete rule and state law restrictions). Franchise no-poach provisions are under active scrutiny.
- **IP and Technology:** Standard-setting FRAND commitments and patent pool arrangements must comply with horizontal restraint principles. See `ip-and-technology/` for patent licensing analysis.
- **Consumer Protection:** FTC enforces Section 5 of the FTC Act (unfair methods of competition) as a complement to Sherman Act Section 1. FTC Act has a broader reach and does not require proof of an "agreement."
- **International Trade:** Cartels frequently span multiple jurisdictions. EU Article 101 TFEU prohibits equivalent horizontal agreements. Cross-border leniency applications may be necessary (but leniency in one jurisdiction does not guarantee leniency in another).
- **Corporate (M&A):** Pre-closing coordination between merging parties can constitute an illegal horizontal agreement (gun-jumping). See `merger-review.md` for HSR compliance.
- **State Antitrust:** State AGs enforce horizontal restraint prohibitions under both federal and state law. Parens patriae actions allow recovery on behalf of state consumers. See `state-antitrust.md`.

## Merger Review

# Merger Review

## Applicability

The Hart-Scott-Rodino Antitrust Improvements Act of 1976 (HSR Act, 15 U.S.C. 18a) and Clayton Act Section 7 (15 U.S.C. 18) govern pre-merger notification and the substantive review of mergers and acquisitions. This file applies when ANY of the following are present:

- A proposed acquisition of voting securities, assets, or non-corporate interests meets HSR Act size thresholds.
- A merger, acquisition, joint venture formation, or other business combination may substantially lessen competition in any relevant market.
- A transaction involves parties that are competitors, vertically related, or active in adjacent markets.
- A transaction may trigger merger control filings in non-US jurisdictions (EU, UK, China, Japan, etc.).

## Key Requirements

### HSR Act Pre-Merger Notification

- **Size-of-transaction threshold**
  - **What:** Acquisitions of voting securities, assets, or non-corporate interests exceeding the applicable threshold require pre-merger notification.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** $119.5M (2024, adjusted annually by GNP deflator; FTC publishes revised thresholds each February). Transactions below the threshold are exempt from HSR filing but remain subject to Clayton Act Section 7 substantive review.
  - **Consequence:** Both the acquiring and acquired persons must file an HSR Form with the DOJ and FTC.

- **Size-of-persons test**
  - **What:** For transactions valued between $119.5M and $478M (2024), the parties must meet a size-of-persons threshold.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Filing required only if one party has total assets or annual net sales of $23.9M+ and the other has $239M+ (or vice versa). If the transaction value exceeds $478M, the size-of-persons test does not apply and filing is required regardless of the parties' size. All thresholds are adjusted annually.
  - **Consequence:** The size-of-persons test provides a safe harbor for small transactions between small parties.

- **Filing fees (2024)**
  - $30,000 for transactions valued $119.5M-$173.3M
  - $105,000 for transactions valued $173.3M-$536.5M
  - $260,000 for transactions valued $536.5M-$1.098B
  - $415,000 for transactions valued $1.098B-$2.195B
  - $830,000 for transactions valued $2.195B-$5.488B
  - $2,335,000 for transactions valued above $5.488B

- **Non-filing penalties**
  - **What:** Failure to file a required HSR notification or closing before expiration of the waiting period.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Civil penalties of up to $51,744 per day of violation (2024, adjusted annually).
  - **Consequence:** The DOJ or FTC may also seek injunctive relief to unwind a consummated transaction. The acquiring person, the acquired person, and responsible individuals may all be subject to penalties.

### Waiting Period

- **Initial waiting period**
  - **What:** Mandatory waiting period before the transaction may close.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** 30 calendar days from the filing date (15 days for cash tender offers and bankruptcy acquisitions). Early termination may be granted by the reviewing agency.
  - **Consequence:** The transaction may not close before expiration or early termination of the waiting period.

- **Second Request**
  - **What:** Request for Additional Information and Documentary Material issued by the reviewing agency.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Must be issued before the initial waiting period expires. Extends the waiting period until 30 days after the parties have substantially complied with the request.
  - **Consequence:** Second Request investigations typically take 6-12 months and involve production of millions of documents, extensive data analysis, customer and competitor interviews, and economic expert analysis. Compliance costs routinely exceed $10M-$50M for large transactions.

- **Timing agreements**
  - **What:** Voluntary agreements extending the investigation period beyond the statutory waiting period.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Provide predictability and may avoid litigation over the adequacy of Second Request compliance.

### DOJ vs. FTC Allocation

Both the DOJ Antitrust Division and FTC Bureau of Competition have authority to review mergers. They coordinate through a "clearance" process to assign each transaction to one agency based on industry expertise:

- **DOJ typically reviews:** Defense, telecommunications, financial markets, beer/spirits, airlines, media.
- **FTC typically reviews:** Healthcare, pharmaceuticals, retail, technology, energy, consumer products.
- Clearance disputes can delay investigations. The allocation is not binding on future transactions.

### Substantive Standard (Clayton Act Section 7)

- **What:** A merger may not be consummated if its effect "may be substantially to lessen competition, or to tend to create a monopoly" in any line of commerce in any section of the country (15 U.S.C. 18).

- **Threshold/Timeline:** The "may be" language establishes an incipiency standard lower than Sherman Act Section 2. The government need not prove that the merger will lessen competition, only that it may do so.

- **DOJ/FTC 2023 Merger Guidelines:** The agencies evaluate mergers under a structured framework:
  1. Mergers should not significantly increase concentration in highly concentrated markets.
  2. Mergers should not eliminate substantial competition between merging firms.
  3. Mergers should not increase the risk of coordination.
  4. Mergers should not eliminate a potential entrant in a concentrated market.
  5. Mergers should not entrench or extend a dominant position.

### Market Concentration (HHI)

The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) measures market concentration by summing the squares of each firm's market share percentage.

- **Unconcentrated markets**
  - **Threshold:** HHI below 1,500.
  - **Consequence:** Mergers are unlikely to raise competitive concerns and typically require no further analysis.

- **Moderately concentrated markets**
  - **Threshold:** HHI between 1,500 and 2,500.
  - **Consequence:** Mergers that increase HHI by more than 100 points warrant scrutiny. The 2023 Guidelines presume that mergers resulting in HHI above 1,800 with a change of more than 100 are likely to enhance market power.

- **Highly concentrated markets**
  - **Threshold:** HHI above 2,500.
  - **Consequence:** Mergers that increase HHI by more than 200 points are presumed likely to enhance market power. This presumption is rebuttable but shifts the burden to the merging parties to demonstrate that the merger is unlikely to substantially lessen competition.

### Remedies

- **Divestiture (structural remedy)**
  - **What:** Sale of overlapping business units, assets, or product lines to a competitively viable buyer.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Preferred remedy for both DOJ and FTC. Must include all assets necessary for the buyer to compete effectively (personnel, IP, customer contracts, physical assets).
  - **Consequence:** "Fix-it-first" (divesting before consummation) and "up-front buyer" (identifying the divestiture buyer before the consent decree) are increasingly required. Divestiture to a competitor of the merging parties may raise its own competitive concerns.

- **Consent decree (behavioral remedy)**
  - **What:** Court order or FTC administrative order imposing conduct restrictions (firewalls, non-discrimination, mandatory licensing, supply commitments).
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Consent decrees typically have a term of 10-20 years.
  - **Consequence:** Agencies view behavioral remedies as less effective because they require ongoing monitoring and may not fully restore competition. Used when structural remedies are not feasible.

- **Litigation to block**
  - **What:** The agency seeks a preliminary injunction in federal court to block the merger.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** FTC may proceed in its own administrative tribunal (Part III) or in federal court. DOJ must proceed in federal court.
  - **Consequence:** Standard for a preliminary injunction: whether the merger is "reasonably likely" to substantially lessen competition.

### Gun-Jumping

- **What:** Premature transfer of beneficial ownership, operational control, or integration of business activities before expiration of the HSR waiting period.

- **Prohibited conduct:** Premature transfer of operational control, joint pricing or marketing before closing, inappropriate exchange of competitively sensitive information, directing the target's day-to-day competitive decisions. The parties must continue to operate as independent competitors until closing.

- **Consequence:** Civil penalties of up to $51,744 per day (same as HSR non-filing penalties). DOJ and FTC have brought gun-jumping enforcement actions resulting in penalties exceeding $5M. Flakeboard (2014) and Duke Energy/Progress Energy (2014) are notable enforcement examples.

### Exemptions and Special Rules

- **Filing exemptions:** Acquisitions of 10% or less held solely for investment; acquisitions of goods in the ordinary course of business; certain intra-person transactions; real property acquisitions (generally exempt). Foreign-to-foreign transactions may be exempt if neither party has US nexus meeting applicable thresholds.

- **Failed firm defense:** A merger with a failing firm may be permitted if: (1) the firm is unable to meet financial obligations, (2) it cannot reorganize under Chapter 11, (3) good-faith efforts to find an alternative acquirer have failed, and (4) absent the acquisition, assets would exit the market. The standard is stringent; a firm that is merely "struggling" does not qualify.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Corporate (M&A):** HSR compliance must be integrated into deal timelines and closing conditions. Merger agreements should include antitrust-specific provisions: HSR filing obligations, regulatory approval conditions, cooperation covenants, and reverse break fees for regulatory failure. See `corporate/`.
- **Horizontal Restraints:** Pre-closing information exchanges between competitors must be carefully managed to avoid gun-jumping and horizontal restraint violations. See `horizontal-restraints.md`.
- **Monopolization:** Mergers creating or enhancing monopoly power are challenged under Clayton Act Section 7. Post-merger monopolization claims may arise under Sherman Act Section 2. See `monopolization.md`.
- **Financial Services:** Bank mergers require additional review by banking regulators (OCC, FDIC, Federal Reserve Board) under the Bank Merger Act and Bank Holding Company Act. See `financial-services/`.
- **International Trade:** Cross-border transactions may require merger control filings in multiple jurisdictions (EU Merger Regulation 139/2004, UK CMA, China SAMR, Japan JFTC), each with different thresholds, timelines, and analytical frameworks. See `international-trade/`.

## Monopolization

# Monopolization

## Applicability

Sherman Act Section 2 (15 U.S.C. 2) prohibits monopolization, attempted monopolization, and conspiracy to monopolize any part of interstate or foreign trade or commerce. This file applies when ANY of the following are present:

- A firm holds a dominant market position (typically 70%+ market share in a properly defined relevant market) and engages in conduct that may exclude competitors.
- A firm with significant market share engages in predatory pricing, exclusive dealing, tying, or refusal to deal that may foreclose competition.
- A transaction or business practice is alleged to create, maintain, or enhance monopoly power through exclusionary (as opposed to competitive) means.
- A firm with a substantial market share takes actions with specific intent to achieve monopoly power (attempted monopolization).

Monopoly power itself is not illegal. Section 2 prohibits the willful acquisition or maintenance of monopoly power through exclusionary conduct, as distinguished from growth through a superior product, business acumen, or historic accident (United States v. Grinnell Corp., 384 U.S. 563 (1966)).

## Key Requirements

### Monopolization Elements

Two elements must be proven:

- **Monopoly power**
  - **What:** Possession of monopoly power in a relevant market, defined as the power to control prices or exclude competition.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Market share is the primary indicator. Courts have generally found monopoly power at 70%+ market share (Alcoa, 1945: 90% "is enough to constitute a monopoly; it is doubtful whether sixty or sixty-four percent would be enough; and certainly thirty-three percent is not"). Some courts have found monopoly power at 65%+ with significant barriers to entry. Market share alone is not sufficient; barriers to entry, market dynamics, and the durability of the position must also be evaluated.
  - **Consequence:** Market definition (product market and geographic market) is often the critical and dispositive issue. A narrow market definition increases the defendant's apparent share; a broad definition decreases it.

- **Exclusionary conduct**
  - **What:** Willful acquisition or maintenance of monopoly power through anticompetitive means.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Conduct is exclusionary if it tends to impair the opportunities of rivals without providing an offsetting procompetitive benefit, or if the anticompetitive harm outweighs any procompetitive justification. The conduct must go beyond competition on the merits.
  - **Consequence:** Courts apply various tests: the "no economic sense" test (conduct unprofitable but for eliminating competition), the "equally efficient competitor" test (conduct excludes only less efficient firms), and the balancing test (weighing procompetitive and anticompetitive effects).

### Predatory Pricing

- **What:** A monopolist or would-be monopolist prices below cost to drive competitors from the market, then raises prices to supracompetitive levels to recoup losses.

- **Standard:** Brooke Group Ltd. v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco (1993), 509 U.S. 209 established a two-part test:

  1. **Below-cost pricing:** The defendant priced below an appropriate measure of cost.
     - Average variable cost (AVC) is the most common measure.
     - Some courts use average avoidable cost (AAC) or long-run average incremental cost (LRAIC).
     - Pricing above cost is virtually never predatory, even if it eliminates competitors.

  2. **Recoupment:** The defendant had a dangerous probability of recouping its investment in below-cost pricing through later monopoly profits.
     - Factors: market structure, barriers to entry, likelihood of supracompetitive pricing post-predation, duration required for recoupment.
     - Without recoupment, below-cost pricing benefits consumers and does not support an antitrust claim.

- **Consequence:** Treble damages under Clayton Act Section 4. Injunctive relief. Criminal prosecution under Section 2 is theoretically possible but extremely rare (DOJ has not brought a criminal Section 2 case in decades). Predatory pricing claims are difficult to prove and rarely succeed after Brooke Group.

### Exclusive Dealing as Monopolization

- **What:** A monopolist or near-monopolist locks up a substantial share of a critical input, distribution channel, or customer base through exclusive contracts.

- **Threshold/Timeline:** Exclusive dealing imposed by a dominant firm faces heightened scrutiny under Section 2 compared to the same practice by a firm with a smaller market share. Microsoft (D.C. Cir. 2001) found exclusive dealing with OEMs and ISPs anticompetitive when it maintained monopoly power in the PC operating system market.

- **Key factors:** Foreclosure of a "substantial share" of the relevant market; duration and breadth of exclusivity; availability of alternatives for excluded rivals; whether the exclusive dealing forecloses competitors from reaching minimum viable scale.

- **Consequence:** Structural and behavioral remedies. Treble damages in private actions. The Microsoft consent decree (2001) imposed behavioral remedies including mandatory API disclosure and non-discrimination requirements.

### Essential Facilities Doctrine

- **What:** A monopolist's control of a facility essential to competition may create an obligation to provide access on reasonable terms.

- **Elements (MCI v. AT&T, 7th Cir. 1983):**
  1. Control of an essential facility by a monopolist.
  2. Inability of a competitor to practically or reasonably duplicate the facility.
  3. Denial of use to a competitor.
  4. Feasibility of providing access.

- **Current status:** Uncertain vitality after Verizon Communications v. Trinko (2004), 540 U.S. 398. The Supreme Court declined to recognize or repudiate the doctrine, stating it had "never recognized such a doctrine." Lower courts continue to apply it in limited circumstances. The doctrine is more likely to apply to regulated industries or physical infrastructure than to technology products or digital platforms.

### Attempted Monopolization

Three elements must be proven:

- **Specific intent**
  - **What:** The defendant must have had a specific intent to monopolize the relevant market.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Intent may be inferred from conduct, internal documents, business plans, or the nature of the anticompetitive act. This is a higher intent standard than for monopolization (which requires only general intent to engage in the exclusionary conduct).

- **Anticompetitive conduct**
  - **What:** The defendant must have engaged in predatory or exclusionary conduct (same types as monopolization: predatory pricing, exclusive dealing, tying, refusal to deal, raising rivals' costs).

- **Dangerous probability of success**
  - **What:** There must be a dangerous probability that the defendant will actually achieve monopoly power.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Evaluated based on defendant's market share (50%+ has been found sufficient in some cases when combined with significant barriers to entry), market structure, barriers to entry, and the nature of the anticompetitive conduct. No bright-line market share threshold exists.

### Conspiracy to Monopolize

- **What:** Agreement between two or more persons to monopolize a relevant market, plus an overt act in furtherance.
- **Threshold/Timeline:** Requires proof of a conspiracy (agreement), specific intent to monopolize, and an overt act. Unlike monopolization, does not require proof of monopoly power or dangerous probability of success. Less commonly prosecuted.
- **Consequence:** Same penalties as monopolization: up to $100M corporate fine, $1M individual fine, 10 years imprisonment for criminal violations. Treble damages for civil violations.

### Remedies in Monopolization Cases

- **Structural remedies (divestiture/breakup):** The most significant remedy. Applied in Standard Oil (1911) and AT&T (1984). Proposed but not ultimately imposed in Microsoft. DOJ and FTC increasingly favor structural remedies over behavioral remedies as more effective at restoring competition.

- **Behavioral remedies:** Injunctions requiring or prohibiting specific conduct. Examples include consent decrees, prohibition of exclusive dealing, mandatory licensing, interoperability requirements. Concerns about ongoing monitoring and enforcement effectiveness. Consent decrees typically have a term of 10-20 years.

- **Damages:** Treble damages in private actions under Clayton Act Section 4 (15 U.S.C. 15). Measured as three times the actual damages caused by the anticompetitive conduct, plus reasonable attorneys' fees. Damages may include lost profits, overcharges, and other quantifiable competitive harm.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Vertical Restraints:** Exclusive dealing and tying imposed by a monopolist are analyzed under Section 2 as well as Section 1. The Section 2 analysis considers the defendant's market power more centrally. See `vertical-restraints.md`.
- **Horizontal Restraints:** Conspiracy to monopolize overlaps with Section 1 horizontal agreements. A horizontal agreement among competitors may support both a Section 1 and a Section 2 conspiracy claim. See `horizontal-restraints.md`.
- **IP and Technology:** Monopoly power derived from patents or copyrights is lawful (IP confers a legal right to exclude). However, leveraging IP-based market power through anticompetitive licensing, standard-essential patent hold-up, or sham litigation may constitute monopolization. See `ip-and-technology/`.
- **Merger Review:** Mergers that create or enhance monopoly power are challenged under Clayton Act Section 7, not Sherman Act Section 2. However, post-merger conduct may give rise to Section 2 claims. See `merger-review.md`.
- **Consumer Protection:** Monopolistic conduct that harms consumers may also implicate FTC Section 5 unfair methods of competition. FTC has independent authority to challenge monopolistic conduct. See `consumer-protection/`.

## State Antitrust

# State Antitrust

## Applicability

All 50 US states, the District of Columbia, and US territories have antitrust statutes that operate alongside and supplement federal antitrust law. State antitrust enforcement applies when ANY of the following are present:

- Anticompetitive conduct affects consumers, businesses, or markets within a particular state.
- A state attorney general initiates an investigation or enforcement action on behalf of state residents.
- State antitrust law provides broader protections or different standards than federal law (e.g., per se treatment of minimum RPM in states that reject Leegin).
- A merger or acquisition may substantially lessen competition in a state or regional market, even if it does not trigger federal enforcement.
- A private plaintiff brings a state antitrust claim, including indirect purchaser claims unavailable under federal law.

State antitrust law is not preempted by federal antitrust law. Both regimes apply simultaneously, and the stricter standard governs for compliance purposes.

## Key Requirements

### State Attorney General Enforcement

- **Parens patriae authority**
  - **What:** State AGs may sue in federal court on behalf of natural persons residing in the state for violations of the Sherman Act under Clayton Act Section 4C (15 U.S.C. 15c).
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** AG must demonstrate that the violation affected state residents. No requirement for individual proof of injury by each consumer.
  - **Consequence:** Treble damages on behalf of all state residents harmed by the violation. Damages are distributed to injured persons or applied to the benefit of the state. Parens patriae is a powerful tool for challenging price-fixing cartels that harm state consumers.

- **State statute enforcement**
  - **What:** State AGs enforce their own state antitrust statutes, which may provide additional or broader remedies than federal law.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Enforcement tools include civil investigative demands (CIDs), injunctive actions, civil penalties, and criminal prosecution (in states where antitrust violations are criminal offenses).
  - **Consequence:** Many state statutes authorize the AG to seek restitution, disgorgement, and civil penalties in addition to or instead of damages. State enforcement is independent of federal DOJ/FTC decisions.

- **Multi-state investigations**
  - **What:** State AGs frequently coordinate multistate investigations and enforcement actions through the National Association of Attorneys General (NAAG).
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Multistate antitrust actions have targeted technology platforms (Google, 2020), pharmaceutical companies (generic drug price-fixing), and financial institutions.
  - **Consequence:** Multistate investigations can proceed independently of or in parallel with federal DOJ/FTC investigations. State AG coalitions can bring significant enforcement pressure even when federal agencies decline to act.

### Key State Antitrust Statutes

- **California Cartwright Act (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code 16700-16770)**
  - **What:** California's primary antitrust statute, broader than the Sherman Act in several respects.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Covers trusts formed to "prevent competition" (a lower threshold than the federal "restraint of trade" standard). Maintains per se treatment of minimum RPM (California has not adopted Leegin). California also enforces the Unfair Competition Law (Bus. & Prof. Code 17200) for injunctive and restitutionary relief.
  - **Consequence:** Criminal penalties: up to $250,000 fine and 3 years imprisonment for individuals. Treble damages for private plaintiffs. AG may seek injunctive relief and civil penalties.

- **New York Donnelly Act (N.Y. Gen. Bus. Law 340-347)**
  - **What:** New York's primary antitrust statute, modeled on the Sherman Act but interpreted independently by New York courts.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Per se treatment of minimum RPM (rejecting Leegin). Broadly interpreted to cover conduct "within this state."
  - **Consequence:** Class E felony for individuals (up to 4 years imprisonment). Civil penalties up to $1M per violation. NY AG has been active in antitrust enforcement against technology platforms and pharmaceutical companies.

- **Texas Free Enterprise and Antitrust Act (Tex. Bus. & Com. Code 15.01-15.52)**
  - **What:** Tracks federal antitrust law but provides additional state remedies.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** AG enforcement and private right of action.
  - **Consequence:** Civil penalties up to $1M per violation. AG may seek injunctive relief and restitution. Private plaintiffs may recover treble damages and attorneys' fees.

- **Illinois Antitrust Act (740 ILCS 10/)**
  - **What:** Provides for both criminal and civil enforcement of antitrust violations.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Class 4 felony for individuals. AG enforcement with civil penalties.
  - **Consequence:** Private treble damages. Illinois applies the indirect purchaser doctrine, allowing downstream purchasers to sue.

### Indirect Purchaser Standing (Illinois Brick)

- **Federal rule (Illinois Brick Co. v. Illinois, 1977)**
  - **What:** Under federal antitrust law, only direct purchasers have standing to sue for treble damages (431 U.S. 720).
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Rationale: avoiding duplicative recovery and the complexity of tracing overcharges through the distribution chain.
  - **Consequence:** Indirect purchasers (e.g., consumers who bought from a retailer that bought from a price-fixing manufacturer) cannot sue for damages under federal law. They may seek injunctive relief.

- **State law rejection ("Illinois Brick repealers")**
  - **What:** At least 35 states and the District of Columbia have enacted statutes allowing indirect purchasers to sue under state antitrust law.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** States rejecting Illinois Brick include California (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code 16750), New York, Illinois, Florida, Michigan, Minnesota, Wisconsin, and many others.
  - **Consequence:** Critically important for consumer class actions: harmed end consumers can bring state law claims even when they lack standing under federal law. Antitrust defendants face potential liability to both direct purchasers (federal law) and indirect purchasers (state law) for the same overcharge, creating parallel class actions in federal and state courts.

### State Merger Review

- **State AG merger challenges**
  - **What:** State AGs have authority to challenge mergers under both federal and state antitrust law, independent of federal agency decisions.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** States may challenge mergers that the DOJ/FTC decline to challenge, particularly when competitive effects are concentrated in state or regional markets.
  - **Consequence:** State AG challenges have blocked or modified hospital mergers, grocery mergers, and telecommunications mergers with localized competitive impact. In T-Mobile/Sprint (2020), a coalition of state AGs challenged the merger even after the DOJ approved it with conditions.

- **State-specific geographic markets**
  - **What:** State AGs may define relevant geographic markets more narrowly than federal agencies.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** A merger that does not substantially lessen competition nationally may substantially lessen competition in a particular state or region.
  - **Consequence:** State AGs have been particularly active in healthcare, agriculture, and retail sectors where competitive effects are geographically concentrated.

- **Coordination with federal agencies**
  - **What:** State AGs may join DOJ or FTC merger challenges as co-plaintiffs, or file separate independent actions.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** States have independent enforcement authority that is not preempted by federal agency decisions.
  - **Consequence:** Federal agency clearance of a merger does not bind state AGs, who may pursue their own challenges.

### State Criminal Antitrust Enforcement

- **What:** Many state antitrust statutes include criminal penalties for hardcore cartel conduct (price-fixing, bid rigging, market allocation).

- **Threshold/Timeline:** State criminal prosecution is most likely for conduct with a direct nexus to the state: bid rigging on state/local government contracts, price-fixing affecting state consumers, and antitrust violations by in-state businesses.

- **State criminal penalties (selected):**
  - California: up to $250,000 fine, 3 years imprisonment.
  - New York: Class E felony, up to 4 years imprisonment.
  - Illinois: Class 4 felony, up to 3 years imprisonment.
  - Texas: civil penalties up to $1M per violation (criminal prosecution rare).

- **Consequence:** State criminal enforcement is less common than federal but provides an additional layer of deterrence. State and federal criminal prosecutions may proceed in parallel for the same conduct (dual sovereignty).

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Horizontal Restraints:** State AGs are important enforcers of per se rules against price-fixing, bid rigging, and market allocation. Parens patriae actions supplement DOJ criminal enforcement and private treble damage actions. See `horizontal-restraints.md`.
- **Vertical Restraints:** State antitrust statutes may impose stricter standards on vertical restraints, particularly minimum RPM. Multistate distribution programs must account for state-by-state variation. See `vertical-restraints.md`.
- **Consumer Protection:** State antitrust enforcement frequently overlaps with state consumer protection (UDAP) enforcement. State AGs often bring parallel antitrust and consumer protection claims arising from the same conduct. See `consumer-protection/`.
- **Merger Review:** State AGs have independent authority to challenge mergers and may define relevant markets differently than federal agencies. See `merger-review.md`.
- **Employment:** State AGs have been active in challenging no-poach agreements, particularly franchise no-poach provisions (e.g., Washington AG actions against fast-food franchise no-poach). See `employment/`.

## Vertical Restraints

# Vertical Restraints

## Applicability

Vertical restraints are restrictions imposed in agreements between parties at different levels of the distribution chain (manufacturer-distributor, franchisor-franchisee, platform-seller, licensor-licensee). These are analyzed primarily under Sherman Act Section 1 (15 U.S.C. 1) and Clayton Act Section 3 (15 U.S.C. 14). This file applies when ANY of the following are present:

- A contract sets or restricts resale prices (minimum, maximum, or advertised prices).
- An agreement requires exclusive dealing or exclusive purchasing obligations.
- A seller conditions the sale of one product on the purchase of another (tying or bundling).
- A contract imposes territorial, customer, or location restrictions on a distributor or reseller.
- A platform or marketplace imposes MFN/price parity clauses on sellers.
- A supplier or manufacturer refuses to deal with a distributor or terminates a distribution relationship.

Unlike horizontal restraints, most vertical restraints are analyzed under the rule of reason, which weighs procompetitive benefits against anticompetitive harms in the relevant market.

## Key Requirements

### Resale Price Maintenance (RPM)

- **Minimum RPM**
  - **What:** A manufacturer or supplier sets a minimum price at which distributors or retailers may resell the product.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Rule of reason since Leegin Creative Leather Products v. PSKS (2007), 551 U.S. 877 (overruling Dr. Miles per se rule).
  - **Consequence:** Evaluated under totality of circumstances: market power of the party imposing RPM, number of manufacturers using RPM (risk of facilitating horizontal dealer cartel), source of the restraint (manufacturer-initiated vs. dealer-pressure-driven), and procompetitive justifications (free-rider prevention, brand investment protection, new market entry). Anticompetitive if used to facilitate a dealer cartel or imposed by a dominant manufacturer to exclude discounters.

- **Maximum RPM and suggested pricing**
  - **What:** Setting a ceiling price or recommending a retail price (MSRP).
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Per se legal under State Oil v. Khan (1997), 522 U.S. 3.
  - **Consequence:** Maximum resale prices and manufacturer's suggested retail prices are lawful. However, a "maximum" that functions as a fixed price (i.e., effectively becomes the actual price through pressure or enforcement) may be challenged.

- **State law divergence**
  - **What:** Several states reject Leegin and maintain per se illegality for minimum RPM under state antitrust statutes.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** California (Cartwright Act), New York (Donnelly Act), Maryland (first state to legislatively reject Leegin, 2009), and other states may treat minimum RPM as per se illegal.
  - **Consequence:** Multistate distribution requires state-by-state analysis. A minimum RPM policy lawful under federal law may still violate state law. See `state-antitrust.md` for state-specific standards.

### Exclusive Dealing

- **What:** A buyer agrees to purchase all or substantially all of its requirements from a single supplier, or a supplier agrees to sell only through a designated distributor.

- **Standard:** Rule of reason. Anticompetitive if it forecloses a "substantial share" of the relevant market from competition.

- **Key factors:**
  - Percentage of market foreclosed (courts have found foreclosure of 30-40%+ problematic, though no bright line exists).
  - Duration of the exclusivity (longer duration = more anticompetitive; short-term exclusive dealing is more likely to be upheld).
  - Availability of alternative channels for excluded competitors.
  - Procompetitive justifications: investment protection, quality assurance, free-riding prevention, supply security.

- **Threshold/Timeline:** Clayton Act Section 3 applies to exclusive dealing in goods; Sherman Act Section 1 applies to both goods and services. De facto exclusivity (requirements contracts, loyalty rebates reaching 80-100% of a buyer's needs) is analyzed the same as express exclusivity.

- **Consequence:** Injunctive relief, treble damages under Clayton Act Section 4, FTC Section 5 enforcement. Exclusive dealing that substantially lessens competition may also support a monopolization claim under Sherman Act Section 2 when imposed by a dominant firm. See `monopolization.md`.

### Tying Arrangements

- **What:** Conditioning the sale of one product (tying product) on the purchase of another (tied product).

- **Standard:** Per se illegal under the traditional formulation when four elements are met:
  1. Two separate products or services exist.
  2. The seller has sufficient market power in the tying product market.
  3. The buyer is coerced into purchasing the tied product (no choice to buy tying product alone).
  4. A not insubstantial amount of commerce in the tied product market is foreclosed.

- **Threshold/Timeline:** Market power in the tying product is required but is not presumed from a patent or copyright after Illinois Tool Works v. Independent Ink (2006), 547 U.S. 28. Some circuits (including the Ninth Circuit in the Microsoft case) apply a modified rule of reason or quasi-per se approach.

- **Consequence:** Clayton Act Section 3 (goods), Sherman Act Section 1 (goods and services), FTC Act Section 5. Treble damages available under Clayton Act Section 4.

- **Bundling distinction:** Pure bundling (products available only as a package) faces greater scrutiny than mixed bundling (products available separately and as a discounted package). Discount attribution test: anticompetitive if the effective price of the competitive product, after attributing the full discount to that product, is below the seller's cost.

- **Technological tying:** Integrating features into a single product is generally analyzed more leniently than contractual tying. Courts distinguish genuine product improvements from pretextual integration designed to foreclose competitors.

### MFN / Price Parity Clauses

- **Wide MFN**
  - **What:** Seller must offer the platform its lowest price across all sales channels, including competing platforms.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Under active enforcement: DOJ sued Google over wide MFN clauses (2023). FTC and state AGs have challenged Amazon's wide MFN practices. EU vertical block exemption (Regulation 2022/720) excludes wide MFNs from the safe harbor.
  - **Consequence:** Wide MFNs can dampen price competition across platforms and facilitate tacit collusion. Likely subject to rule of reason challenge; may be condemned if the platform has significant market power.

- **Narrow MFN**
  - **What:** Seller must offer the platform a price no higher than what it charges on its own direct sales channel.
  - **Threshold/Timeline:** Generally lower risk but still subject to rule of reason analysis. Can inhibit entry of new, lower-cost platforms if widely adopted.
  - **Consequence:** EU distinguishes wide from narrow MFNs; narrow MFNs benefit from the vertical block exemption. US law does not draw an explicit statutory distinction but treats narrow MFNs more favorably.

### Territorial and Customer Restrictions

- **What:** Manufacturer-imposed restrictions on where or to whom a distributor may sell.

- **Standard:** Rule of reason since Continental T.V. v. GTE Sylvania (1977), 433 U.S. 36.

- **Threshold/Timeline:** Manufacturer-imposed territorial restrictions on distributors are generally lawful when they promote interbrand competition, even if they reduce intrabrand competition. Complete prohibition on sales outside territory (airtight territories) receives closer scrutiny. Location clauses (requiring operation from a specified location) are permissible.

- **Dual distribution:** When a manufacturer sells both directly and through distributors, the relationship has historically been analyzed as vertical (rule of reason). DOJ/FTC 2023 Merger Guidelines and recent case law suggest that dual distribution may warrant horizontal analysis when the manufacturer competes directly with its own distributors for the same customers.

### Refusal to Deal

- **General rule:** Firms generally have no duty to deal with competitors or distributors under Verizon Communications v. Trinko (2004), 540 U.S. 398. A firm may unilaterally choose its trading partners for any reason, absent a specific statutory obligation or a prior voluntary course of dealing.

- **Aspen Skiing exception:** A monopolist's termination of a prior profitable voluntary course of dealing, with no legitimate business justification, may constitute exclusionary conduct under Sherman Act Section 2 (Aspen Skiing Co. v. Aspen Highlands Skiing Corp., 472 U.S. 585 (1985)). Trinko characterized this as "at or near the outer boundary" of Section 2 liability. Narrowly applied.

- **Essential facilities doctrine:** Control of a facility essential to competition, with feasibility of providing access, may create a duty to deal. Doctrine is of uncertain vitality after Trinko. Never expressly adopted by the Supreme Court. See `monopolization.md` for detailed analysis.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection:** MFN clauses and tying arrangements in consumer-facing agreements may also violate FTC Act Section 5 unfair methods of competition and state UDAP statutes. See `consumer-protection/`.
- **IP and Technology:** Patent and copyright licensing restrictions (field-of-use, territorial, customer restrictions) are analyzed under antitrust principles but benefit from additional latitude under the DOJ/FTC IP Licensing Guidelines (2017). Tying of patented and unpatented products is subject to Illinois Tool Works. See `ip-and-technology/`.
- **Financial Services:** Exclusive dealing arrangements in payment processing, banking relationships, and insurance distribution may raise both antitrust and financial regulatory concerns. See `financial-services/`.
- **Monopolization:** Vertical restraints imposed by a firm with monopoly power may support a Section 2 monopolization claim. Exclusive dealing and tying are common theories. See `monopolization.md`.
- **State Antitrust:** Several states apply stricter standards to vertical restraints, particularly minimum RPM. See `state-antitrust.md`.
