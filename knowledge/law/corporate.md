## Key Constraints

These are non-overridable legal requirements from this area. They cannot be modified by practice/ or matters/ overrides.

- Fiduciary duties of directors, officers, managers, and controlling stockholders/members cannot be fully eliminated by contract (though Delaware LLCs permit modification to the extent allowed by DLLCA Section 18-1101(c); the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing cannot be eliminated).
- Corporate formalities (e.g., board/stockholder approvals) required by statute for fundamental transactions (mergers, dissolution, sale of all/substantially all assets) must be followed regardless of contractual provisions.
- HSR Act pre-merger notification filing requirements apply when transaction size and party size thresholds are met ($119.5M size-of-transaction threshold for 2024); closing before HSR clearance is illegal with penalties up to $51,744 per day.
- CFIUS review and national security considerations cannot be waived by contract when applicable; mandatory declarations are required for certain TID US business transactions.
- Tax classification elections (entity classification, S-corp election, QSBS) have statutory deadlines and requirements that override contractual flexibility (S-corp: 75-day filing window; check-the-box: 60-month re-election bar).
- Annual franchise tax and reporting obligations must be maintained to preserve entity good standing and liability protection; failure results in administrative dissolution or voiding.
- Asset distribution in dissolution must follow the mandatory priority order (creditors before equity holders); directors authorizing distributions in violation face personal liability.
- The 3-year statutory survival period for dissolved Delaware corporations (DGCL Section 278) cannot be shortened by contract and governs post-dissolution litigation rights.

## Dissolution Wind Down

# Dissolution and Wind-Down

## Applicability

Load when any of the following arise: voluntary dissolution by board and stockholder/member action, involuntary or judicial dissolution, administrative dissolution or revocation, winding up of entity affairs, creditor notification and claims bar processes, asset distribution in dissolution, revival or reinstatement of dissolved entities, or assignment for benefit of creditors (ABC).

## Key Requirements

### Voluntary Dissolution (Corporations)

- **What**: A corporation may be dissolved voluntarily by action of the board of directors and stockholders, following the statutory procedures of the state of incorporation.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Under DGCL Section 275: (a) the board must adopt a resolution recommending dissolution and submit it to stockholders, (b) stockholders must approve by a majority of outstanding shares entitled to vote (unless the certificate requires a higher threshold). Alternatively, dissolution can be effected by written consent of all stockholders (DGCL Section 275(c)) without board action. A certificate of dissolution must be filed with the Secretary of State.
- **Consequence**: Dissolution does not terminate the entity immediately. Under DGCL Section 278, a dissolved corporation continues to exist for 3 years after dissolution (or longer if the Court of Chancery so directs) solely for the purpose of winding up, including prosecuting and defending lawsuits, settling debts, and distributing assets. Acts outside the scope of winding up are ultra vires.

### Voluntary Dissolution (LLCs)

- **What**: An LLC may be dissolved by the events specified in its operating agreement, by consent of members, or by judicial decree.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Under DLLCA Section 18-801, dissolution occurs upon: (a) the occurrence of events specified in the operating agreement, (b) written consent of members as specified in the operating agreement (or, if silent, consent of all members), (c) entry of a judicial decree of dissolution, or (d) administrative dissolution by the Secretary of State. Certificate of cancellation must be filed with the Secretary of State upon completion of winding up.
- **Consequence**: The operating agreement controls the dissolution process for LLCs to a much greater extent than the statute controls for corporations. Absent operating agreement provisions, default statutory rules apply, which may not align with the members' intent.

### Winding Up Process

- **What**: After dissolution, the entity must wind up its affairs -- completing existing business, collecting debts, selling assets, paying creditors, and distributing remaining assets to equity holders.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Delaware corporations may provide for notice to creditors under DGCL Section 280 (optional safe harbor) or Section 281 (alternative procedure). Section 280 procedure: publish notice of dissolution in a newspaper and mail notice to known creditors, specifying a claims deadline of at least 60 days from the mailing date. Creditors who do not submit claims within the deadline are barred. Section 281(b) allows the corporation to pay or provide for claims without the formal notice process, but with less certainty.
- **Consequence**: Directors who authorize distributions to stockholders without adequately providing for creditor claims face personal liability up to the amount distributed. The 3-year survival period under DGCL Section 278 limits but does not eliminate post-dissolution litigation risk.

### Creditor Notification and Claims Bar

- **What**: Statutory procedures that allow a dissolving entity to establish a deadline for creditor claims, after which unfiled claims are barred.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: DGCL Section 280 (elective safe harbor): (a) mail written notice to known creditors with at least 60 days to present claims, (b) publish notice in a newspaper for unknown creditors with at least 60 days to present claims, (c) offer security to creditors whose claims are contingent, conditional, or unmatured. For LLCs, DLLCA Section 18-804 provides a similar framework. Many states outside Delaware follow comparable procedures under the MBCA (Sections 14.06-14.07), which provides for a 5-year survival period.
- **Consequence**: Proper compliance with the claims bar procedure protects directors and members from personal liability for post-dissolution claims. Failure to follow the procedure leaves the entity (and potentially its directors or members) exposed to claims for the full statutory survival period.

### Asset Distribution Order

- **What**: Upon winding up, assets must be distributed in a mandatory priority order established by statute and the entity's governing documents.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Standard distribution priority for corporations: (1) secured creditors (to the extent of their security), (2) priority claims (employee wages, taxes), (3) unsecured creditors, (4) preferred stockholders (per liquidation preference terms in the charter), (5) common stockholders (pro rata). For LLCs, the operating agreement controls distribution order, subject to DLLCA Section 18-804: (1) creditors (including members who are creditors), (2) members for prior distributions owed, (3) members for capital contributions, (4) members per operating agreement (or pro rata if silent).
- **Consequence**: Distributions to equity holders before all creditors are satisfied expose directors, officers, and managers to personal liability. Fraudulent conveyance laws (state UVTA and federal bankruptcy law) provide additional protections for creditors, with look-back periods of 2-6 years depending on the jurisdiction and theory.

### Administrative Dissolution and Revival

- **What**: A state may administratively dissolve or revoke an entity's authority for failure to comply with statutory requirements, most commonly failure to pay franchise taxes or file annual reports.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Delaware: failure to pay franchise tax results in voiding of the entity's charter. Reinstatement requires payment of all back taxes, penalties, and interest, plus a reinstatement fee. The reinstatement fee is $375 for corporations. Back taxes accrue from the date of voiding. California: suspension for failure to file returns or pay minimum franchise tax ($800/year). Revival requires filing all delinquent returns and paying all taxes, penalties, and interest.
- **Consequence**: A voided or suspended entity generally cannot maintain lawsuits, enter into contracts, or conduct business. In Delaware, reinstatement under DGCL Section 312 is retroactive -- the entity is treated as if it had never been voided, validating acts taken during the voided period. However, rights of third parties that vested during the voided period may be protected.

### Involuntary (Judicial) Dissolution

- **What**: A court may order dissolution of an entity upon petition by stockholders, members, creditors, or the state, based on statutory grounds.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Common grounds: (a) deadlock among directors or members that cannot be resolved and is causing irreparable harm (DGCL Section 226 -- appointment of custodian; Section 273 -- dissolution of joint venture corporation), (b) fraud, mismanagement, or abuse of authority by those in control, (c) impracticability of continuing the business. Under the MBCA (Section 14.30), a stockholder may petition when directors are deadlocked, acts of the directors are illegal/oppressive/fraudulent, or corporate assets are being misapplied.
- **Consequence**: Judicial dissolution is a drastic remedy that courts grant reluctantly. Courts often prefer appointing a custodian or provisional director (DGCL Section 226) before ordering dissolution. In closely held corporations, courts may order a buyout of the petitioning stockholder as an alternative to dissolution.

### Assignment for Benefit of Creditors (ABC)

- **What**: A state-law alternative to federal bankruptcy in which the entity transfers all assets to an assignee (trustee) who liquidates them and distributes proceeds to creditors. Common for startups and technology companies as a faster, cheaper alternative to Chapter 7 bankruptcy.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Available under state law (e.g., California CCP Sections 493.010-493.060, Delaware common law). No court filing required (in most states). The assignee takes possession of all assets, provides notice to creditors, and distributes proceeds. Typical timeline: 6-18 months. Board approval required; stockholder approval may not be required if characterized as winding up rather than a sale of substantially all assets.
- **Consequence**: Unlike federal bankruptcy, ABCs do not provide an automatic stay against creditor actions. ABCs do not discharge debts of the entity or its guarantors. Secured creditors retain their lien rights. ABCs are not available to entities with significant federal tax liabilities (IRS can force the matter into federal bankruptcy).

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment**: WARN Act obligations (60-day notice for plant closings or mass layoffs of 100+ employees) apply to entity wind-downs. Final wage payment timing requirements vary by state (California: immediately upon termination). COBRA continuation coverage must be offered.
- **Data Privacy**: Disposition of personal data during wind-down must comply with applicable privacy laws (GDPR Article 17 right to erasure, CCPA consumer rights). Data must be properly destroyed or transferred to a successor in compliance with privacy policies.
- **Securities**: Dissolution may trigger registration or exemption requirements for distributions of non-cash assets. Investor rights agreements may contain consent requirements for dissolution (protective provisions).
- **Litigation**: Dissolved entities retain the right to prosecute and defend lawsuits during the statutory survival period. Claims not filed within the claims bar period may be extinguished.
- **Tax**: Dissolution triggers final tax return obligations (federal and state). Asset distributions may result in gain recognition at both the entity and owner level. Tax clearance certificates may be required before final dissolution in some states.

## Entity Formation

# Entity Formation

## Applicability

Load when any of the following arise: new entity formation, entity conversion or domestication, choice-of-entity analysis, LLC vs. corporation comparison, tax classification elections (check-the-box, S-corp), series LLC structures, professional entity requirements, foreign qualification, or multi-entity structuring.

## Key Requirements

### Entity Type Comparison

- **C Corporation**: Formed under state corporation statutes (DGCL, MBCA). Taxed at entity level (21% federal corporate rate as of 2024) with potential double taxation on distributions. Unlimited number and types of stockholders. Preferred for venture capital and institutional investment. Required formalities: certificate of incorporation, bylaws, organizational resolutions, stock issuance, annual meetings, annual reports, franchise tax.
- **S Corporation**: Tax election (IRS Form 2553) on an eligible corporation. Maximum 100 shareholders, one class of stock (voting differences permitted), only eligible shareholders (individuals, certain trusts, estates -- no partnerships, corporations, or non-resident aliens). Must file Form 2553 within 75 days of formation or by March 15 for calendar-year election. Pass-through taxation eliminates double taxation. Inadvertent termination occurs if eligibility requirements are violated.
- **LLC**: Formed under state LLC acts. Default tax treatment as partnership (multi-member) or disregarded entity (single-member). May elect C-corp or S-corp taxation via IRS Form 8832 (check-the-box) or Form 2553. Maximum governance flexibility through operating agreement. Fewer mandatory formalities. Delaware LLCs permit broad modification of fiduciary duties.
- **Limited Partnership (LP)**: One or more general partners (unlimited liability, management authority) and limited partners (limited liability, no management authority). Common for PE/VC fund structures. Tax-transparent. Governed by partnership agreement and state LP acts (DRULPA in Delaware).
- **General Partnership**: Formed by association of two or more persons in business for profit. No filing required in most states. All partners have unlimited joint and several liability. Governed by RUPA and partnership agreement.

### Formation Requirements by State

- **Delaware**: Certificate of Incorporation (corporations) or Certificate of Formation (LLCs) filed with Division of Corporations. Filing fee: $89 (corp) or $90 (LLC). Annual franchise tax required -- corporations: minimum $400, calculated by authorized shares or assumed par value capital method. LLCs: flat $300 annual tax. No requirement to conduct business in Delaware.
- **California**: Articles of Incorporation (corporations) or Articles of Organization (LLCs) filed with Secretary of State. Filing fee: $100 (corp) or $70 (LLC). Annual minimum franchise tax: $800 for all entities (waived first year for some). Statement of Information required annually (corps) or biennially (LLCs). California imposes entity-level tax on LLCs with gross receipts exceeding $250,000.
- **New York**: Certificate of Incorporation (corporations) or Articles of Organization (LLCs) filed with Department of State. Filing fee: $125 (corp) or $200 (LLC). LLCs must publish formation notice in two newspapers designated by the county clerk within 120 days of formation -- estimated cost $500-$2,000+. Biennial statement filing required.

### Tax Classification (Check-the-Box)

- **What**: IRS default classification rules under Treas. Reg. Section 301.7701-3 determine how an entity is taxed absent an election.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Default classification applies automatically. A domestic entity with two or more members defaults to partnership; single-member defaults to disregarded entity. Election to change classification via Form 8832 is effective on the date specified (up to 75 days retroactive or 12 months prospective). Once changed, cannot re-elect for 60 months.
- **Consequence**: Incorrect or untimely classification election can result in unexpected entity-level taxation, loss of pass-through treatment, or recognition of gain on deemed conversion transactions.

### S-Corp Election Requirements

- **What**: Eligible corporations elect S-corp status by filing IRS Form 2553 with consent of all shareholders.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Must be filed within 2 months and 15 days of the beginning of the tax year for which the election is to be effective. Late election relief available under Rev. Proc. 2013-30 if requirements met.
- **Consequence**: Termination triggers a 5-year waiting period before re-election. Passive investment income exceeding 25% of gross receipts for 3 consecutive years terminates S status if the corporation has accumulated E&P from C-corp years.

### Series LLCs

- **What**: Certain states (Delaware, Illinois, Nevada, Texas, others) permit formation of series LLCs where each series can hold separate assets, incur separate liabilities, and have separate members/managers.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Delaware: authorized by DLLCA Section 18-215. Must be established in the operating agreement with proper notice in the certificate of formation.
- **Consequence**: Liability segregation between series is recognized in the forming state but may not be recognized in other jurisdictions. Federal tax treatment remains uncertain -- IRS proposed regulations (REG-119921-09) treat each series as a separate entity for tax purposes.

### Professional Entities

- **What**: Professionals (attorneys, physicians, accountants, architects, engineers) must form professional corporations (PC), professional LLCs (PLLC), or professional limited liability partnerships (PLLP) in most states.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: State-specific requirements; typically require all owners/members to hold applicable professional licenses. May require approval from the relevant licensing board before formation.
- **Consequence**: Formation as a standard entity without professional designation may be void or voidable. Professional liability remains personal regardless of entity form -- the entity shield does not protect against malpractice claims.

### Foreign Qualification

- **What**: Entities must register (foreign qualify) in each state where they "transact business" beyond their state of formation.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Definition of "transacting business" varies by state. Generally triggered by maintaining an office, employees, or substantial business activity in the state. Filing fees range from $50-$750+ depending on the state.
- **Consequence**: Failure to qualify can result in inability to maintain lawsuits in that state, fines, penalties, and liability for back taxes. Does not affect the validity of contracts entered into in the state (in most jurisdictions).

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Securities**: Issuance of equity interests (stock, membership interests, partnership interests) implicates federal and state securities laws regardless of entity type. Exemption availability may depend on entity structure.
- **Employment**: Entity type affects employment tax obligations, worker classification, and benefit plan eligibility. Multi-entity structures require analysis of joint employer risk.
- **IP and Technology**: IP ownership must be properly assigned to the correct entity at formation. Founder IP assignment agreements should be executed contemporaneously with formation.
- **Data Privacy**: Entity structure affects data controller/processor relationships in multi-entity organizations.
- **Tax**: Entity type determines available tax treatment, deduction eligibility (QSBS under Section 1202 requires C-corp status), and transaction structuring options.

## Fiduciary Duties

# Fiduciary Duties

## Applicability

Load when any of the following arise: director or officer decision-making under scrutiny, self-dealing or interested transactions, corporate opportunity disputes, change-of-control or sale transactions, oversight and compliance failures (Caremark claims), controlling stockholder transactions, demand futility analysis in derivative litigation, or exculpation and indemnification provisions.

## Key Requirements

### Duty of Care

- **What**: Directors and officers must act on an informed basis, in good faith, and with the care that a reasonably prudent person would exercise in similar circumstances. Requires reviewing material information reasonably available before making a decision.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: The standard is process-oriented -- courts examine whether the board made a reasonable effort to become informed, not whether the decision was correct. Under Delaware law, a board that fails to inform itself at all (gross negligence) loses business judgment rule protection. DGCL Section 102(b)(7) permits charter provisions eliminating director monetary liability for duty of care breaches (but not loyalty, bad faith, or intentional misconduct).
- **Consequence**: Breach of duty of care absent exculpation can result in personal monetary liability for damages. In the M&A context, failure to conduct an adequate market check or obtain a fairness opinion may support a duty of care claim.

### Duty of Loyalty

- **What**: Directors, officers, and controlling stockholders must act in the best interests of the corporation and its stockholders, not in their own self-interest. Prohibits self-dealing, usurpation of corporate opportunities, and competition with the corporation.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: A transaction is "interested" when a director or controlling stockholder stands on both sides or derives a personal benefit not shared by the stockholders generally. Under DGCL Section 144, an interested transaction is not void or voidable solely because of the director's interest if it is (a) approved by disinterested directors after disclosure, (b) approved by stockholders after disclosure, or (c) fair to the corporation at the time authorized.
- **Consequence**: Breach of loyalty cannot be exculpated under DGCL Section 102(b)(7). Remedies include disgorgement of profits, rescission of the transaction, and personal liability for damages. Under the corporate opportunity doctrine, a fiduciary who takes a business opportunity belonging to the corporation must account for all profits.

### Business Judgment Rule

- **What**: A presumption that directors acted on an informed basis, in good faith, and in the honest belief that the action was in the corporation's best interest. Places the burden on the plaintiff to rebut the presumption.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: The rule applies when directors (1) made an informed decision (duty of care), (2) acted in good faith, (3) had no personal financial interest in the decision, and (4) reasonably believed the decision served the corporation's interests. If rebutted, the burden shifts to defendants to prove entire fairness.
- **Consequence**: When the business judgment rule applies, courts will not second-guess the substantive merits of the board's decision, even if the decision proves to be poor. When rebutted, the transaction is reviewed under the more demanding entire fairness standard.

### Entire Fairness Test

- **What**: The most demanding standard of judicial review, applied to interested transactions where the business judgment rule is rebutted or inapplicable.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Requires proof of both (1) fair dealing (process -- timing, initiation, structure, negotiation, disclosure) and (2) fair price (economic terms). In controlling stockholder squeeze-out mergers, the MFW framework (Kahn v. M&F Worldwide, 2014) shifts review back to business judgment if the transaction is conditioned ab initio on (a) approval by a fully empowered special committee of independent directors and (b) an informed, uncoerced vote of a majority of the minority stockholders.
- **Consequence**: If entire fairness is not established, the transaction may be rescinded or defendants held personally liable for damages measured by the difference between the price paid and the fair value.

### Revlon Duties

- **What**: When a board initiates an active bidding process, agrees to a change of control, or abandons the company's long-term strategy in favor of a breakup, the board's duty shifts to obtaining the best price reasonably available for stockholders.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Triggered in sale-of-control transactions. The board must conduct a reasonable auction or market check. Defensive measures and deal protections (termination fees, no-shop clauses, matching rights) must be reasonable and not preclusive of superior offers. Termination fees typically range from 2-4% of deal value.
- **Consequence**: Breach of Revlon duties results in enhanced judicial scrutiny of the sale process. Unreasonable deal protections may be enjoined. Directors may face personal liability if their conduct was not in good faith.

### Caremark Duties (Oversight Liability)

- **What**: Directors have a duty to implement and monitor a reasonable compliance and reporting system. Liability attaches for sustained, systematic failure to exercise oversight (Marchand v. Barnhill, 2019, clarifying the standard).
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Plaintiff must show either (a) the directors utterly failed to implement any reporting or information system (prong one), or (b) having implemented such a system, the directors consciously failed to monitor or oversee its operations, disabling themselves from being informed of risks or problems requiring their attention (prong two). The claim requires bad faith -- a conscious disregard of known duties.
- **Consequence**: Caremark claims survive exculpation under DGCL Section 102(b)(7) because they sound in bad faith (a loyalty breach). Liability can include damages for the corporation's losses resulting from the oversight failure.

### State Variations

- **What**: Fiduciary duty standards vary significantly by jurisdiction. Delaware is the dominant body of corporate fiduciary duty law, but other states may apply different standards.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Delaware LLCs may modify or eliminate fiduciary duties by contract under DLLCA Section 18-1101(c), though the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing cannot be eliminated. California applies broader fiduciary duties to majority shareholders in close corporations (Jones v. H.F. Ahmanson). Some states (e.g., Nevada, NRS 78.138) provide broader director protections than Delaware.
- **Consequence**: Choice of entity jurisdiction directly affects the scope of fiduciary duties, available defenses, and litigation risk. Forum selection clauses designating Delaware courts are enforceable for internal affairs (Salzberg v. Sciabacucchi, as modified by federal forum provisions).

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Corporate (Governance)**: Fiduciary duties define the standard of conduct for all governance decisions -- board actions, officer authority, and stockholder protections are evaluated through fiduciary duty analysis.
- **Corporate (M&A)**: Revlon, Unocal, and entire fairness standards govern board conduct in change-of-control transactions. Deal protections are evaluated against fiduciary duties.
- **Securities**: Fiduciary duty claims frequently arise alongside federal securities fraud claims (Section 10(b), Rule 10b-5) in public company transactions. Disclosure obligations overlap.
- **Litigation**: Derivative lawsuits, class actions, and books-and-records demands (DGCL Section 220) are the primary mechanisms for enforcing fiduciary duties.
- **Insurance**: D&O insurance responds to fiduciary duty claims. Policy coverage (Side A, B, C) and exclusions must align with the entity's indemnification and exculpation provisions.

## Governance

# Corporate and LLC Governance

## Applicability

Load when any of the following arise: board of directors actions or composition questions, stockholder or member voting requirements, quorum determinations, written consent actions, officer authority or appointments, annual or special meeting requirements, record-keeping obligations, D&O indemnification or advancement provisions, conflicts of interest and interested transactions, bylaws or operating agreement governance provisions, committee formation, or state-specific governance variations.

## Key Requirements

### Board of Directors — Composition and Authority

- **What**: The board of directors manages the business and affairs of the corporation. Directors owe fiduciary duties of care and loyalty. Board size, qualifications, and classification are set by the charter and bylaws.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Under DGCL Section 141(b), a corporation must have at least 1 director. Board size is typically fixed by the bylaws or by board resolution within a range authorized by the bylaws. Classified (staggered) boards divide directors into classes (typically 3) serving staggered multi-year terms (typically 3 years each). Annual election of all directors is the default absent classification.
- **Consequence**: Board actions taken without a properly constituted board (e.g., after director resignations reducing the board below the minimum required by bylaws) may be voidable. Classified boards provide continuity but limit stockholder ability to replace the full board in a single election cycle.

### Quorum Requirements

- **What**: The minimum number of directors or stockholders that must be present (in person or by proxy) to conduct valid business at a meeting.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Board quorum: default under DGCL Section 141(b) is a majority of the total number of directors. The charter or bylaws may set a different quorum but no less than one-third of the total. Stockholder quorum: default under DGCL Section 216 is a majority of shares entitled to vote, present in person or by proxy. The charter may set a different quorum, but not less than one-third of shares entitled to vote. LLC quorum: set by operating agreement (no statutory default in Delaware).
- **Consequence**: Actions taken without a quorum are void. Quorum is determined at the commencement of the meeting; a quorum once established is typically not broken by the departure of members (unless the bylaws provide otherwise).

### Voting Standards

- **What**: The vote required to approve an action once a quorum is established.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Board actions: default is a majority of directors present at a meeting at which a quorum is present (DGCL Section 141(b)). Stockholder actions: default under DGCL Section 216 is a majority of shares present and entitled to vote (for matters other than director elections). Director elections: plurality voting is the DGCL default, but many companies adopt majority voting bylaws (director must receive more "for" than "withhold" votes). Supermajority provisions (66.7%, 75%, or 80%) may be required by charter for specified actions (charter amendments, mergers, removal of classified directors).
- **Consequence**: Failure to meet the required voting threshold means the action is not approved. Supermajority requirements in the charter can only be amended by the same supermajority vote, creating entrenchment.

### Written Consent in Lieu of Meeting

- **What**: Most states allow board and stockholder actions by written consent without holding a meeting, subject to specific requirements.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Board written consent: under DGCL Section 141(f), requires unanimous written consent of all directors unless the certificate of incorporation provides otherwise. Stockholder written consent: under DGCL Section 228, requires the minimum number of votes that would be necessary to authorize the action at a meeting at which all shares entitled to vote were present. The certificate of incorporation may restrict or prohibit stockholder action by written consent. LLC: governed by operating agreement provisions.
- **Consequence**: Board written consents that are not unanimous (absent charter authorization) are invalid. Written consents must be signed and delivered to the corporation, and the effective date of the action is the date the last required signature is obtained (unless a later date is specified). A 60-day time limit applies to collecting stockholder written consents under DGCL Section 228(c).

### Officer Authority

- **What**: Officers act on behalf of the corporation within the scope of authority delegated by the board of directors, the bylaws, or (for LLCs) the operating agreement.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Under DGCL Section 142, a corporation must have the officers designated in the bylaws or appointed by the board. The same person may hold multiple offices. Officers serve at the pleasure of the board and may be removed with or without cause at any time (unless an employment agreement provides otherwise, though removal itself remains valid -- the officer may have a contract claim for damages).
- **Consequence**: Contracts executed by officers within their actual or apparent authority bind the corporation. Apparent authority may bind the corporation even when the officer lacked actual authority -- third parties are entitled to rely on the officer's apparent authority unless they had notice of the limitation. Officers owe the same fiduciary duties as directors.

### Annual and Special Meetings

- **What**: Corporations must hold annual stockholder meetings for the election of directors. Special meetings may be called for specific purposes.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Under DGCL Section 211(b), an annual meeting must be held each year for the election of directors on a date and at a time designated by the board. If no annual meeting is held for 13 months after the last annual meeting, any stockholder may petition the Court of Chancery to order one. Special meetings: may be called by the board or by persons authorized in the charter or bylaws. Record date for determining stockholders entitled to vote: not more than 60 days nor less than 10 days before the meeting (DGCL Section 213). Notice: 10-60 days before the meeting (DGCL Section 222).
- **Consequence**: Failure to hold annual meetings does not invalidate prior board actions but creates governance risk and may invite stockholder petitions. Delaware permits meetings by remote communication (DGCL Section 211(a)(2)) if the corporation implements measures to verify stockholder identity and allow participation.

### Record-Keeping Requirements

- **What**: Corporations and LLCs must maintain specified records as evidence of proper authorization and compliance with legal requirements.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Under DGCL Section 224, the corporation must keep books and records of account. Under DGCL Section 220, any stockholder may inspect corporate books and records for a proper purpose. Records that should be maintained: certificate of incorporation and all amendments, bylaws and all amendments, minutes of board and stockholder meetings, written consents, stock ledger, financial records, and material contracts. LLCs: DLLCA Section 18-305 grants members inspection rights.
- **Consequence**: Failure to maintain records weakens defenses against fiduciary duty claims (no evidence of informed decision-making) and veil-piercing claims (failure to observe formalities). Books-and-records demands under DGCL Section 220 are a powerful pre-litigation tool -- courts broadly enforce them and may award attorneys' fees for wrongful refusal.

### D&O Indemnification and Advancement

- **What**: Corporations may (and typically do) indemnify directors and officers for expenses, judgments, fines, and settlements incurred in their service to the corporation, and advance expenses before final resolution.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: DGCL Section 145(a)-(b): permissive indemnification for directors and officers in third-party and derivative actions, provided the person acted in good faith and in a manner reasonably believed to be in the corporation's best interest. DGCL Section 145(c): mandatory indemnification when a director or officer is successful on the merits or otherwise in the defense of any action. DGCL Section 145(e): permits advancement of expenses upon receipt of an undertaking to repay if indemnification is ultimately not warranted. Advancement rights in bylaws or indemnification agreements are typically enforceable as written (Homestore v. Tafeen).
- **Consequence**: Mandatory advancement provisions create an obligation to pay legal fees as incurred -- the undertaking to repay is typically unsecured. The combination of charter exculpation (DGCL Section 102(b)(7)), bylaw indemnification, indemnification agreements, and D&O insurance forms a comprehensive protection framework. Gaps between these layers are a common governance risk.

### Conflicts of Interest — Safe Harbor

- **What**: Interested director transactions must comply with safe harbor procedures to avoid being voidable solely because of the director's interest.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Under DGCL Section 144, an interested transaction is not void or voidable if: (a) the material facts of the interest are disclosed and the transaction is approved by a majority of disinterested directors (even if less than a quorum), (b) the material facts are disclosed and the transaction is approved by a stockholder vote, or (c) the transaction is fair to the corporation at the time it is authorized. For controlling stockholder transactions, the MFW framework (Kahn v. M&F Worldwide, 2014) provides business judgment review if the transaction is conditioned ab initio on both special committee approval and majority-of-minority stockholder vote.
- **Consequence**: Failure to follow safe harbor procedures subjects the transaction to entire fairness review, placing the burden on the interested party to prove both fair dealing and fair price.

### State-Specific Variations

- **What**: Governance rules vary by state of incorporation. Delaware (DGCL) is the baseline for this file, but key variations exist in other major jurisdictions.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: California: cumulative voting is mandatory for corporations with fewer than 100 stockholders (Cal. Corp. Code Section 708); broader inspection rights; court-ordered provisional directors. New York: no stockholder action by written consent without unanimity unless the certificate provides otherwise (NYBCL Section 615). Nevada: broader director protections (NRS 78.138 -- directors presumed to act in good faith; no damages liability absent breach of duty of loyalty or acts not in good faith); no entire fairness doctrine equivalent. MBCA states: may require a majority of votes cast (rather than majority of shares present) as the default voting standard.
- **Consequence**: Multi-state governance analysis is required when the state of incorporation differs from the state of principal operations. Internal affairs doctrine generally applies the law of the state of incorporation to governance disputes, but some states (notably California -- Cal. Corp. Code Section 2115) impose local governance requirements on foreign corporations with significant California connections.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Corporate (M&A)**: Board and stockholder approval is required for fundamental transactions. Fiduciary duties constrain board conduct during M&A processes (Revlon duties, Unocal analysis). Governance provisions determine required approvals and vote thresholds.
- **Corporate (Fiduciary Duties)**: Governance structures implement fiduciary duties. Board composition, committee independence, and approval processes are the mechanisms through which duties of care and loyalty are discharged.
- **Securities**: Governance provisions in investor rights agreements, voting agreements, and stockholder agreements are negotiated alongside equity issuances. Public company governance is additionally regulated by SEC rules, stock exchange listing standards, and Sarbanes-Oxley.
- **Employment**: Officer employment agreements must be consistent with board authority. Executive compensation requires board or compensation committee approval. Indemnification agreements are common for senior executives.
- **Litigation**: Derivative litigation, books-and-records demands (DGCL Section 220), and appraisal actions arise from governance disputes. Proper governance procedures are a primary defense.
- **Insurance**: D&O insurance coverage must align with indemnification and advancement provisions. Policy structure (Side A, B, C) should be coordinated with the entity's governance framework.

## Investment Transactions

# Investment Transactions

## Applicability

Load when any of the following arise: SAFE agreements, convertible notes, preferred stock financings, venture capital or angel investments, Series Seed or Series A+ rounds, preemptive rights or anti-dilution provisions, liquidation preference analysis, protective provisions negotiation, information rights, pro rata rights, or investor rights agreements.

## Key Requirements

### SAFE Agreements (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)

- **What**: A SAFE is an investment instrument that converts into equity upon a triggering event (typically a priced equity round, dissolution, or change of control). Post-money SAFEs (Y Combinator standard since 2018) calculate ownership based on a post-money valuation cap, giving investors a deterministic ownership percentage at the time of investment.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Valuation cap sets the maximum price at which the SAFE converts. Discount rate (typically 15-25%) applies when conversion price at the triggering round exceeds the cap. Post-money SAFEs include the SAFE itself and all other converting securities in the post-money capitalization but exclude the new money in the priced round. No maturity date, no interest, no repayment obligation (unlike convertible notes).
- **Consequence**: SAFEs are not debt instruments and do not appear on the balance sheet as liabilities in most accounting treatments. However, multiple SAFEs with different caps can create complex capitalization scenarios. Post-money SAFEs cause dilution to founders (not to SAFE holders relative to each other) when additional SAFEs are issued at the same cap.

### Convertible Notes

- **What**: A debt instrument that converts into equity upon specified triggering events. Combines debt features (interest, maturity) with equity conversion mechanics.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Interest rate: typically 2-8% (simple interest, not compounded). Maturity: 12-24 months. Conversion triggers: qualified financing (minimum raise threshold, typically $1M+), change of control, maturity. Valuation cap and discount (typically 15-25%) function similarly to SAFEs. At maturity, if no conversion event has occurred, the note is technically due and payable -- though parties typically extend or convert.
- **Consequence**: Convertible notes are debt -- they create creditor rights, appear as liabilities, and have priority over equity in dissolution. Failure to repay at maturity is an event of default. State usury laws may apply to the interest rate. Securities law compliance is required for issuance (typically Rule 506(b) or 506(c) under Regulation D).

### Preferred Stock -- Liquidation Preference

- **What**: The right of preferred stockholders to receive a specified return on their investment before common stockholders receive any distribution in a liquidation event (including deemed liquidation events such as M&A transactions and asset sales).
- **Threshold/Timeline**: 1x non-participating preferred is the current market standard for venture financings. This entitles the holder to the greater of (a) 1x their original investment amount or (b) their pro rata share of proceeds as if converted to common stock. Participating preferred (less common, more investor-favorable) receives 1x preference plus pro rata share of remaining proceeds. Participation may be capped (e.g., 3x return cap).
- **Consequence**: In down-round or modest-exit scenarios, liquidation preferences can eliminate or substantially reduce common stockholder (founder/employee) returns. Stacked preferences across multiple rounds compound this effect. Seniority among preferred series (standard: last-in, first-out / pari passu within a series) affects distribution waterfall.

### Anti-Dilution Protections

- **What**: Provisions that adjust the conversion price of preferred stock if the company issues equity at a price below the preferred stock's original conversion price (a "down round").
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Broad-based weighted average is the current market standard. It adjusts the conversion price using a formula that accounts for the number of new shares issued and the price reduction, weighted across all outstanding shares (on an as-converted basis). Narrow-based weighted average uses a smaller denominator (only outstanding preferred), resulting in greater adjustment. Full ratchet (rare, highly investor-favorable) adjusts the conversion price to the new lower price regardless of the number of shares issued.
- **Consequence**: Anti-dilution adjustments increase the number of common shares into which preferred stock converts, diluting common stockholders and other series without anti-dilution protection. Pay-to-play provisions (requiring investors to participate in down rounds to maintain anti-dilution protections) are a counterbalance mechanism.

### Protective Provisions

- **What**: Contractual veto rights granted to preferred stockholders (typically voting as a separate class) over specified corporate actions, regardless of the preferred holders' ownership percentage.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Standard protective provisions (per NVCA model documents) cover: (a) amendment of charter or bylaws adversely affecting preferred rights, (b) authorization or issuance of senior or pari passu securities, (c) redemption or repurchase of equity (other than repurchases at cost from departing employees), (d) declaration of dividends, (e) change in board size, (f) incurrence of debt above a threshold, (g) liquidation, dissolution, or deemed liquidation events. Thresholds for investor consent typically require a majority of the outstanding preferred (or a specific series).
- **Consequence**: Protective provisions give minority investors effective veto power over fundamental corporate decisions. Overly broad protective provisions can create governance deadlocks and impede the company's operational flexibility.

### Information Rights

- **What**: Contractual rights of investors (typically major investors above a specified ownership threshold) to receive periodic financial and operational information from the company.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Standard information rights (per NVCA model) include: annual audited financial statements (within 120 days of fiscal year end), quarterly unaudited financial statements (within 45 days of quarter end), annual budget and business plan (within 30 days of fiscal year start), and monthly financial statements for larger investors. "Major investor" threshold is typically defined as holders of a specified number of shares (e.g., 500,000 shares) or a percentage of preferred stock.
- **Consequence**: Information rights typically terminate upon an IPO (when public reporting obligations commence). Failure to provide required information can be a breach of the investor rights agreement but is rarely the basis for material legal claims absent other issues.

### Preemptive Rights (Pro Rata Rights)

- **What**: The right of existing investors to participate in future equity issuances to maintain their ownership percentage.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Typically granted to major investors. The right to purchase a pro rata share of new securities (based on fully diluted ownership) in subsequent rounds. Standard exceptions: employee equity incentive issuances, strategic partner issuances, equipment financing, and acquisitions. Exercise period typically 10-20 days from notice.
- **Consequence**: Preemptive rights can complicate future financings by requiring notice to and participation by existing investors. "Super pro rata" rights (right to invest more than pro rata) are increasingly common in competitive rounds. Rights typically terminate upon IPO.

### Drag-Along and Tag-Along Rights

- **What**: Drag-along rights allow a specified majority to compel all stockholders to participate in a sale. Tag-along rights allow minority holders to participate in a sale on the same terms as the selling majority.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Drag-along typically requires approval of (a) the board, (b) a majority of preferred stock, and (c) a majority of common stock. Tag-along is triggered when a specified percentage of shares (often 50%+) is being sold to a third party. Tag-along holders have the right to include their shares pro rata.
- **Consequence**: Drag-along prevents minority holdout problems in M&A exits. Tag-along protects minority holders from being left behind in a change-of-control transaction at a less favorable price or without liquidity.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Securities**: All investment instruments (SAFEs, convertible notes, preferred stock) are securities. Issuance must comply with federal registration requirements or exemptions (Regulation D Rule 506(b)/506(c), Regulation A, Regulation CF) and state blue sky laws. Accredited investor verification required for Rule 506(c).
- **Corporate (Governance)**: Protective provisions, board composition rights, and voting agreements affect corporate governance. Charter amendments for new preferred series require board and stockholder approval.
- **Corporate (Fiduciary Duties)**: Board must exercise fiduciary duties in approving investment terms, particularly when existing investors lead or participate in rounds (potential conflicts).
- **Employment**: Equity incentive plans, option pools (typically 10-20% of fully diluted shares), and Section 409A valuation requirements for common stock options interact with investment round pricing.
- **Data Privacy**: Investor due diligence may involve disclosure of personal data, requiring compliance with applicable privacy laws.

## Mergers Acquisitions

# Mergers and Acquisitions

## Applicability

Load when any of the following arise: M&A transactions (mergers, stock purchases, asset purchases, tender offers), letters of intent or term sheets for acquisitions, due diligence processes, representation and warranty negotiation, indemnification structuring, MAE/MAC clause drafting or analysis, HSR or CFIUS filings, earnout disputes, post-closing integration, or corporate restructurings.

## Key Requirements

### Transaction Structures

- **Forward Merger**: Target merges directly into buyer (or a buyer subsidiary). Target ceases to exist. Requires target board and stockholder approval (typically majority of outstanding shares). Buyer stockholder approval may be required if buyer issues more than 20% of its shares as consideration (stock exchange rules).
- **Reverse Merger**: Buyer subsidiary merges into target; target survives. Used to preserve target's contracts, licenses, and permits that might not be assignable. Same approval requirements as forward merger.
- **Reverse Triangular Merger**: Buyer forms a merger subsidiary that merges into target, with target surviving as a wholly owned subsidiary of buyer. Most common M&A structure because it (a) preserves target's legal existence and contracts and (b) insulates buyer from target's undisclosed liabilities at the parent level.
- **Asset Purchase**: Buyer acquires specified assets and assumes specified liabilities. No automatic assumption of undisclosed liabilities (subject to successor liability doctrines). Requires target board approval; stockholder approval required only if "all or substantially all" assets are being sold (varies by state -- Delaware courts apply qualitative and quantitative tests).
- **Stock/Equity Purchase**: Buyer acquires target equity directly from holders. Target continues as a going concern with all assets and liabilities. No target board or stockholder vote required (individual stockholder decision), but buyer inherits all liabilities including unknown ones.
- **Tender Offer + Back-End Merger**: Buyer makes a direct offer to target stockholders. If buyer acquires 90%+ of shares, it can effect a short-form merger without a stockholder vote (DGCL Section 253). Faster than a one-step merger for public company acquisitions.

### HSR Act (Pre-Merger Notification)

- **What**: The Hart-Scott-Rodino Antitrust Improvements Act requires parties to file pre-merger notifications with the FTC and DOJ for transactions exceeding applicable thresholds.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: 2024 thresholds: size-of-transaction test of $119.5M (adjusted annually). If the transaction exceeds $478M, the size-of-person test does not apply. Waiting period: 30 days from filing (15 days for cash tender offers). Second request extends the waiting period until substantial compliance. Filing fee: tiered from $30,000 to $2,250,000 based on transaction value.
- **Consequence**: Closing before HSR clearance (gun-jumping) is a per-day violation carrying fines up to $51,744 per day (2024 amount, adjusted annually for inflation). Criminal penalties are also possible for willful violations.

### CFIUS Review

- **What**: The Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States reviews transactions that could result in foreign control of, or foreign access to, a US business for national security implications under the Defense Production Act as amended by FIRRMA (2018).
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Mandatory declarations required for transactions involving critical technology where a US regulatory authorization would be required (effective since 2020), certain TID US businesses (technology, infrastructure, data), and transactions where a foreign government has a substantial interest. Voluntary filings are accepted for other covered transactions. Declaration review: 45 days. Full notice review: up to 90 days (extendable by 15 days, plus investigation period).
- **Consequence**: CFIUS can impose conditions on transactions, require mitigation agreements, or recommend that the President block or unwind a completed transaction. Presidential authority to block is not subject to judicial review.

### Representations and Warranties

- **What**: Statements of fact about the target's condition that allocate risk between buyer and seller. Standard representations cover organization, authority, capitalization, financial statements, undisclosed liabilities, compliance with law, material contracts, IP, employment, tax, environmental, and litigation.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Survival periods: general representations typically survive 12-18 months post-closing. Fundamental representations (organization, authority, capitalization, tax, brokers) typically survive 3-6 years or indefinitely. Special representations (environmental, tax, employee benefits) may have extended survival tied to applicable statutes of limitations.
- **Consequence**: Breach of a representation that survives closing is the basis for indemnification claims. Representations qualified by disclosure schedules shift known issues out of the representation framework.

### Indemnification Framework

- **What**: Post-closing contractual remedy for losses arising from breaches of representations, warranties, and covenants.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Caps: typically 10-20% of deal value for general representations; fundamental representations often uncapped or capped at full purchase price. Baskets: deductible (losses below threshold are not recoverable) or tipping (once threshold is exceeded, all losses from dollar one are recoverable), typically 0.5-1.5% of deal value. Escrow: typically 5-15% of deal value held for 12-18 months.
- **Consequence**: Representation and warranty insurance (RWI) is now standard in middle-market M&A (deals above approximately $20M). RWI shifts indemnification risk to an insurer, with retention (deductible) typically 1-3% of enterprise value, dropping to 0.5-1% after 12 months.

### Material Adverse Effect (MAE/MAC)

- **What**: Contractual provision defining events or conditions that would give a party the right to terminate the agreement or refuse to close. Functions as a risk allocation mechanism for adverse developments between signing and closing.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Delaware courts (Akorn v. Fresenius, 2018) have held that an MAE requires a durationally significant adverse impact on the target's earnings -- generally interpreted as a decline materially affecting the business for a period of years, not months. Typical carve-outs: general economic conditions, industry-wide changes, changes in law or GAAP, acts of war/terrorism, pandemics, and effects of the transaction announcement itself.
- **Consequence**: An MAE finding permits the buyer to refuse to close without liability. However, MAE clauses are rarely successfully invoked -- the burden is high and courts heavily scrutinize the claim.

### Closing Conditions

- **What**: Conditions precedent that must be satisfied (or waived) before each party is obligated to close the transaction.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Standard conditions include: accuracy of representations (qualified by MAE or specified materiality thresholds at closing -- "bring-down" condition), performance of pre-closing covenants in all material respects, absence of legal proceedings or governmental orders prohibiting closing, receipt of required regulatory approvals (HSR, CFIUS, industry-specific), third-party consents, and delivery of specified closing documents.
- **Consequence**: Failure to satisfy a closing condition entitles the other party to refuse to close. If a party fails to close when conditions are satisfied, the other party may seek specific performance (if available) or terminate and claim a reverse termination fee (typically 3-6% of deal value in public company transactions).

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Antitrust**: HSR filings and substantive antitrust analysis of competitive effects. International merger control filings may be required in multi-jurisdictional deals.
- **Securities**: Public company M&A involves SEC disclosure (Schedule 14A proxy, Schedule TO tender offer), proxy requirements, and Regulation M-A compliance. Equity consideration in private deals implicates securities exemptions.
- **Employment**: WARN Act compliance (60-day notice for plant closings/mass layoffs affecting 100+ employees), change-of-control payments, benefit plan assumption, and employee retention.
- **IP and Technology**: IP due diligence, representations and warranties, assignment and licensing provisions, open-source compliance, and technology transfer restrictions.
- **Data Privacy**: GDPR and CCPA due diligence, consent requirements for data transfers, and post-closing data integration obligations.
- **International Trade**: CFIUS, export control considerations (EAR, ITAR), and sanctions compliance (OFAC) for cross-border transactions.
- **Corporate (Fiduciary Duties)**: Board fiduciary duties (Revlon, Unocal, entire fairness) constrain board conduct throughout the M&A process.

## Shareholder Agreements

# Shareholder Agreements

## Applicability

Load when any of the following arise: voting agreements or arrangements, buy-sell agreements, right of first refusal (ROFR) or right of first offer (ROFO) provisions, tag-along or drag-along rights in non-investment contexts, stockholder or member deadlock resolution, transfer restrictions on equity, shotgun (buy-sell) provisions, cross-purchase or redemption arrangements, co-sale agreements, or closely held company governance disputes.

## Key Requirements

### Voting Agreements

- **What**: Contracts among stockholders or members committing to vote their shares in a specified manner on designated matters, most commonly board elections and fundamental transactions.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Under DGCL Section 218, voting agreements between stockholders are specifically enforceable. Voting agreements typically specify board composition (e.g., investor-designated directors, founder-designated directors, independent directors) and are tied to ownership thresholds. Standard venture-backed voting agreements (per NVCA model) grant board designation rights contingent on continued ownership of a specified percentage of shares.
- **Consequence**: A party that breaches a voting agreement may be subject to specific performance (court-ordered compliance). Irrevocable proxies coupled with an interest are the strongest enforcement mechanism. Voting agreements that conflict with charter or bylaw provisions may create enforcement complications -- the charter generally controls.

### Buy-Sell Agreements

- **What**: Contractual arrangements that establish the terms under which equity owners may or must sell their interests, and other owners or the entity may or must purchase those interests, upon specified triggering events.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Common triggers: death, disability, termination of employment, retirement, voluntary withdrawal, bankruptcy, divorce (to prevent transfer to ex-spouse). Two primary structures: cross-purchase (other owners buy the departing owner's interest directly) and redemption (the entity buys back the interest). Valuation methods: formula-based (book value, multiple of earnings/revenue), periodic agreed value, or independent appraisal at time of trigger.
- **Consequence**: Cross-purchase agreements result in a step-up in basis for the purchasing owners; redemptions do not provide a basis step-up to remaining owners (but may trigger Section 302 dividend treatment if not a complete termination). Life insurance is commonly used to fund buy-sell obligations on death. Failure to properly structure and fund buy-sell agreements creates liquidity risk and valuation disputes.

### Right of First Refusal (ROFR)

- **What**: A contractual right that requires a selling equity holder, upon receiving a bona fide third-party offer, to first offer the shares to specified rights holders (other stockholders, the company, or both) on the same terms before completing the third-party sale.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Standard process: (a) selling holder receives and accepts a third-party offer, (b) selling holder delivers transfer notice specifying the material terms, (c) ROFR holders have a specified exercise period (typically 15-30 days) to elect to purchase on the same terms, (d) if ROFR is not exercised, the selling holder may complete the third-party sale within a specified window (typically 60-90 days) on terms no more favorable to the buyer. ROFR may be held by the company (primary) and/or other stockholders (secondary).
- **Consequence**: ROFR provisions create a "chilling effect" on third-party offers because buyers may be unwilling to invest diligence resources knowing the deal may be matched. Standard exceptions: transfers to family members, trusts, or affiliates; transfers for estate planning purposes; and pledges to financial institutions.

### Right of First Offer (ROFO)

- **What**: A contractual right requiring a selling equity holder to first offer the shares to specified rights holders before soliciting or accepting third-party offers.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Standard process: (a) selling holder delivers an offer notice to ROFO holders, (b) ROFO holders have a specified period (typically 15-30 days) to make an offer, (c) if the selling holder rejects the ROFO offer (or no offer is made), the selling holder may sell to third parties, but typically only at a price at or above the ROFO offer (or a specified percentage thereof, e.g., 110%). ROFO is less restrictive than ROFR because the rights holder does not get to match a specific third-party offer.
- **Consequence**: ROFO provisions are less likely to chill third-party interest but provide weaker protection to rights holders. Market practice varies -- ROFR is more common in closely held companies and NVCA-standard agreements.

### Tag-Along (Co-Sale) Rights

- **What**: Rights allowing minority holders to participate in a sale of equity by a major holder, on the same terms and conditions, pro rata to their ownership.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Triggered when a specified holder (typically a founder or major stockholder) proposes to transfer shares to a third party. Tag-along holders may elect to include their shares pro rata, reducing the number of shares the selling holder can sell. Exercise period: typically 15-20 days from notice. Standard exceptions align with ROFR exceptions (family transfers, trusts, affiliates).
- **Consequence**: Tag-along rights protect minority holders from being left behind in a change-of-control or partial sale. If the third-party buyer is unwilling to purchase the additional shares, the selling holder must reduce their sale proportionally.

### Drag-Along Rights

- **What**: Rights allowing a specified majority to compel all equity holders to participate in a sale of the company on the same terms and conditions.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Typically requires approval of (a) a majority of the board, (b) holders of a majority (or supermajority) of outstanding common stock, and (c) holders of a majority (or specified percentage) of outstanding preferred stock. Dragged holders must vote in favor of the transaction, tender their shares, and execute all transaction documents. The price and terms must be the same for all holders (subject to existing liquidation preference waterfall).
- **Consequence**: Drag-along eliminates minority holdout problems. Key protections for dragged holders: same price per share (on an as-converted basis), no personal liability post-closing beyond escrow/indemnification obligations shared pro rata, and release of all guarantees. Fiduciary duties still apply to the exercise of drag-along rights.

### Deadlock Resolution

- **What**: Mechanisms for resolving governance impasses in entities with equal ownership splits (50/50) or where veto rights create the potential for deadlock.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Common mechanisms: (a) mandatory mediation followed by binding arbitration, (b) escalation to designated senior executives with a specified resolution period (typically 30-60 days), (c) buy-sell (shotgun) provisions, (d) appointment of a neutral tie-breaking director, (e) dissolution as a last resort. Shotgun provisions: one party names a price; the other party must either buy at that price or sell at that price.
- **Consequence**: Absence of deadlock provisions in 50/50 entities can lead to judicial dissolution proceedings, appointment of a custodian or receiver, and destruction of value. Courts may order dissolution of a deadlocked entity under DGCL Section 226 (appointment of custodian) or Section 273 (dissolution of joint venture corporation).

### Transfer Restrictions

- **What**: Contractual and charter-based limitations on the ability of equity holders to transfer their interests, ensuring that ownership remains among approved persons.
- **Threshold/Timeline**: Common restrictions: (a) board approval required for any transfer, (b) ROFR/ROFO as described above, (c) prohibition on transfers to competitors, (d) compliance with securities laws (Rule 144 holding periods: 6 months for reporting companies, 12 months for non-reporting companies with volume and manner-of-sale limitations), (e) lock-up agreements (typically 180 days post-IPO). Stock certificates (or book-entry notations) must bear restrictive legends referencing applicable restrictions.
- **Consequence**: Transfers in violation of valid restrictions are voidable. Securities Act Section 5 violations for unregistered transfers can result in rescission rights and SEC enforcement. Transfer restrictions must be reasonable in scope and duration to be enforceable.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Securities**: Transfer restrictions must comply with federal and state securities laws. Rule 144 safe harbor governs resale of restricted securities. Stockholder agreements often contain representations about accredited investor status.
- **Corporate (Governance)**: Voting agreements directly affect board composition and corporate governance. Conflicts between stockholder agreements and charter/bylaw provisions must be resolved under state corporate law.
- **Corporate (Fiduciary Duties)**: Exercise of drag-along and other controlling stockholder rights is subject to fiduciary duty constraints. Entire fairness may apply to squeeze-out transactions.
- **Employment**: Buy-sell triggers frequently overlap with employment events (termination, retirement). Equity repurchase rights upon termination must comply with applicable employment law and Section 409A.
- **Litigation**: Stockholder agreement disputes are among the most common forms of closely held company litigation. Forum selection and arbitration clauses affect dispute resolution.
