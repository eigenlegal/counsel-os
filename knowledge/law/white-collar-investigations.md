## Corporate Compliance Programs

# Corporate Compliance Programs

## Applicability

Applies to organizations designing, implementing, or evaluating corporate compliance programs. Centers on the DOJ Evaluation of Corporate Compliance Programs (June 2020 + updates), which provides the definitive framework prosecutors use to assess program effectiveness. Relevant to sentencing mitigation, enforcement negotiations, and proactive compliance.

## Key Requirements

### DOJ Evaluation Framework — Three Fundamental Questions

#### Question 1: Is the Compliance Program Well-Designed?

- **Risk Assessment** / Company must conduct periodic, comprehensive risk assessment tailored to its specific business, industry, geography, and regulatory environment / **Threshold**: Assessment must be updated to reflect current risks (not a stale document); must cover all compliance risk areas / **Consequence**: No risk assessment = program is per se deficient; generic risk assessment not tailored to company = inadequate
- **Policies and Procedures** / Written policies that address identified risks; must be accessible, understandable, and operationalized (not just "paper" compliance) / **Consequence**: Policies that exist but are not followed = worse than no policies (shows awareness without action)
- **Training and Communication** / Risk-based training program: frequency (at minimum annual; more frequent for high-risk roles), format (interactive preferred over passive), tracking (completion rates, comprehension testing), tailored content by function and risk level / **Consequence**: 100% completion target; training must be in languages employees understand; failure to train = individual and corporate liability exposure
- **Confidential Reporting and Investigation** / Anonymous reporting mechanism (hotline, web portal); no retaliation; prompt and thorough investigation of all reports; tracking and trend analysis / **Consequence**: Underutilized hotline raises concern about awareness or trust; failure to investigate reports = evidence of program failure
- **Third-Party Management** / Risk-based due diligence on third parties (agents, distributors, joint venture partners, vendors); ongoing monitoring; contractual compliance provisions / **Consequence**: Third-party misconduct attributed to company if due diligence inadequate; high-risk jurisdictions and relationships require enhanced diligence
- **Mergers and Acquisitions** / Pre-acquisition due diligence for compliance risks; post-acquisition integration of acquired entity into compliance program / **Timeline**: Compliance integration should begin immediately post-closing; full integration within reasonable time (6-12 months typical) / **Consequence**: Acquiring company inherits liability for pre-acquisition misconduct if it knew or should have known; DOJ expects compliance diligence in M&A

#### Question 2: Is the Program Adequately Resourced and Empowered?

- **CCO Authority and Stature** / Chief Compliance Officer (or equivalent) must have: (1) sufficient seniority and authority; (2) direct access to the board or audit committee; (3) autonomy from management (especially from business functions that create compliance risk) / **Consequence**: CCO who reports only to General Counsel without board access = structural deficiency; CCO must be empowered to escalate without fear of retaliation
- **Budget and Resources** / Compliance function must have adequate budget, staffing, and technology to fulfill its mandate / **Threshold**: No specific dollar amount; DOJ assesses proportionality to company size, risk, and complexity / **Consequence**: Underfunded compliance department = evidence that company does not prioritize compliance; budget cuts following misconduct = aggravating factor
- **Board and Senior Management Engagement** / Board (or audit committee) must exercise meaningful oversight of compliance program; regular reporting from CCO; board should ask probing questions, not rubber-stamp / **Consequence**: Passive board oversight = governance failure; DOJ evaluates quality of board engagement, not just frequency of reports
- **Data and Technology** / Compliance function should have access to relevant data and analytics to detect and prevent misconduct / **Consequence**: Company that has data capabilities but does not apply them to compliance = missed opportunity that DOJ will note

#### Question 3: Does the Program Work in Practice?

- **Testing and Monitoring** / Compliance program must be tested through audits, control testing, and continuous monitoring (not just self-assessment) / **Consequence**: Untested program = no evidence it works; DOJ wants to see proactive testing, not just reactive response to problems
- **Investigation Quality** / Internal investigations must be prompt, thorough, independent, and properly scoped; root cause analysis required / **Consequence**: Superficial or outcome-oriented investigations = program failure; must demonstrate genuine truth-seeking
- **Remediation** / Misconduct must result in meaningful remediation: disciplinary action (consistently applied), control enhancements, root cause correction / **Consequence**: Failure to discipline senior employees = evidence of selective enforcement; remediation must address systemic issues, not just individual conduct
- **Continuous Improvement** / Program must evolve based on lessons learned from investigations, audits, regulatory developments, and industry trends / **Consequence**: Static program = stale program; DOJ evaluates whether program has improved over time
- **Incentive Structure** / Compensation and promotion criteria should incorporate compliance performance; DOJ 2023 update specifically evaluates: (1) compliance incorporated in compensation decisions, (2) clawback provisions for misconduct, (3) prohibition on bonuses/raises during investigation of misconduct / **Consequence**: Compensating employees despite compliance failures = undermining program; financial incentives must align with compliance expectations

### Culture of Compliance

- **Tone at the Top** / Senior leadership must visibly and consistently support compliance program; personal conduct must model expected behavior / **Consequence**: Leadership that tolerates "gray areas" or prioritizes revenue over compliance = culture problem; DOJ evaluates whether leadership walked the talk
- **Tone at the Middle** / Middle management must translate senior leadership's message into day-to-day operational reality / **Consequence**: Gap between executive statements and middle management behavior = ineffective program; training and accountability for middle managers is critical
- **Speak-Up Culture** / Employees must feel safe raising concerns without fear of retaliation; measured through surveys, hotline utilization, exit interview data / **Consequence**: Fear of retaliation = chilling effect; DOJ evaluates whether employees actually use reporting mechanisms

### USSG Chapter 8 — Compliance as Sentencing Factor

- **Effective Program** / Organization with effective compliance program at time of offense may receive reduced culpability score (3-point reduction) / **Threshold**: Program must have been in place and functioning (not adopted after the fact); high-level personnel must not have participated in or condoned the offense / **Consequence**: Culpability score reduction translates to significantly lower fine range (multiplier can drop from 2.0 to 0.4)
- **Seven Minimum Requirements** / (1) Compliance standards and procedures; (2) specific high-level oversight; (3) due care in delegating authority; (4) effective communication and training; (5) monitoring, auditing, and reporting; (6) consistent enforcement and discipline; (7) response and remediation after detection / **Consequence**: Must meet all seven; failure on any = program not "effective" for sentencing purposes
- **Post-Offense Conduct** / Even if program was not effective at time of offense, post-offense adoption of effective program and cooperation = additional sentencing credit / **Consequence**: Remediation after detection is valuable but less powerful than pre-existing effective program

## Doj Cooperation

# DOJ Cooperation and Corporate Resolution

## Applicability

Applies to organizations subject to DOJ investigation, considering voluntary self-disclosure, or negotiating corporate resolution. Covers the Monaco Memo, DOJ Corporate Enforcement Policy, cooperation credit factors, deferred prosecution agreements (DPAs), non-prosecution agreements (NPAs), and independent compliance monitors.

## Key Requirements

### Monaco Memo and Individual Accountability (2022)

- **Core Principle** / DOJ prioritizes individual accountability; companies seeking cooperation credit must provide all non-privileged information about individuals involved in or responsible for misconduct / **Consequence**: Partial disclosure of individual involvement = no cooperation credit; "all or nothing" approach
- **Timely Disclosure** / Information about individuals must be provided at the earliest opportunity, including during initial self-disclosure / **Consequence**: Delayed identification of responsible individuals reduces cooperation credit; "drip-drip" disclosure viewed negatively
- **Prior Misconduct** / DOJ considers all prior misconduct by the company (criminal, civil, regulatory, foreign) in evaluating resolution / **Consequence**: History of repeat misconduct = aggravating factor; prior DPA/NPA does not protect from prosecution for new conduct
- **Compliance Monitor** / DOJ will consider need for independent compliance monitor in every corporate resolution; will not agree to forgo monitor without satisfying specific criteria / **Consequence**: Monitor imposition is default; company bears burden of demonstrating adequate compliance program

### DOJ Corporate Enforcement Policy (Updated 2023)

- **Voluntary Self-Disclosure (VSD)** / Presumption of declination if company: (1) voluntarily self-discloses misconduct to DOJ, (2) fully cooperates with investigation, (3) timely and appropriately remediates / **Consequence**: Strongest benefit available; presumption of declination means DOJ will not prosecute absent aggravating factors
- **Aggravating Factors Overcoming Presumption** / (1) Involvement of executive management; (2) significant profit from misconduct; (3) pervasiveness within organization; (4) criminal recidivism / **Consequence**: Even with VSD, aggravating factors may result in guilty plea, DPA, or NPA rather than declination
- **Cooperation Credit (Without VSD)** / Companies that cooperate but do not voluntarily self-disclose may still receive reduced penalties: up to 50% reduction from applicable sentencing guidelines range / **Consequence**: Cooperation without VSD = penalties but reduced; VSD = potential declination
- **Cooperation Factors** / (1) Timeliness and voluntariness of disclosure; (2) quality of cooperation (proactive disclosure, not just responsive); (3) preservation and production of evidence; (4) identification of individuals; (5) making witnesses available; (6) no interference with individual investigations / **Consequence**: DOJ evaluates cooperation quality, not just occurrence; passive cooperation = minimal credit

### Corporate Resolution Types

- **Declination** / DOJ declines to prosecute; may include disgorgement of profits / **Consequence**: Best outcome; no criminal charge; no public admission; company avoids collateral consequences of conviction
- **Non-Prosecution Agreement (NPA)** / Written agreement not to prosecute in exchange for cooperation, remediation, and compliance commitments / **Timeline**: Typically 2-3 year term / **Consequence**: No criminal charge filed; terms include cooperation, compliance enhancements, potential penalty; breach = prosecution on original conduct
- **Deferred Prosecution Agreement (DPA)** / Criminal information or indictment filed but prosecution deferred; charges dismissed if company complies with agreement terms / **Timeline**: Typically 2-3 year term (up to 5 years in rare cases) / **Consequence**: Public filing creates reputational impact; terms more stringent than NPA; breach = immediate prosecution on filed charges
- **Guilty Plea** / Company pleads guilty to criminal charges / **Consequence**: Most severe resolution; triggers collateral consequences (debarment, loss of licenses, securities implications); sentencing under USSG Chapter 8
- **Resolution Trends** / Increasing use of DPAs and NPAs; guilty pleas reserved for repeat offenders and most egregious conduct; financial penalties have increased substantially / **Consequence**: Average corporate criminal penalty has grown significantly; must model financial exposure early

### Independent Compliance Monitors

- **Appointment** / DOJ may require appointment of independent compliance monitor as condition of DPA/NPA / **Timeline**: Typically 2-3 year monitorship / **Consequence**: Monitor reports to DOJ; costs borne by company ($2M-$20M+ annually); monitor has broad access to personnel, documents, and operations
- **Selection Process** / DOJ selects from candidates proposed by the company; monitor must be independent with no conflicts / **Consequence**: Company should propose qualified candidates proactively; DOJ has final approval
- **Monitor Scope** / Assess and test effectiveness of compliance program; make recommendations for improvement; report to DOJ on implementation progress / **Consequence**: Monitor recommendations are effectively mandatory; failure to implement = potential breach of resolution agreement
- **Criteria to Avoid Monitor** / Company must demonstrate: (1) compliance program has been tested and proven effective; (2) root causes of misconduct have been addressed; (3) sufficient time has passed to assess remediation; (4) adequate resources devoted to compliance / **Consequence**: Avoiding monitor requires strong evidence of existing compliance; most resolutions include monitor

### Sentencing Guidelines (USSG Chapter 8)

- **Base Fine** / Calculated from offense level and pecuniary gain or loss / **Consequence**: Starting point for penalty calculation; adjusted by culpability score
- **Culpability Score** / Ranges from 0.05 to 4.0 multiplier; factors: (1) organizational size and involvement of high-level personnel (+); (2) prior history (+); (3) violation of court order (+); (4) obstruction (+); (5) effective compliance program (-); (6) self-reporting, cooperation, acceptance of responsibility (-) / **Consequence**: Effective compliance program can reduce multiplier by up to 3 points; self-reporting + cooperation can reduce by up to 5 points
- **Effective Compliance Program** / Must meet seven minimum requirements: (1) standards and procedures; (2) organizational leadership and culture; (3) reasonable efforts to exclude bad actors; (4) training and communication; (5) monitoring, auditing, and reporting; (6) incentives and discipline; (7) response to violations and remediation / **Consequence**: Program meeting these elements = potential sentencing reduction; but not a defense to criminal liability

## Fcpa Enforcement

# FCPA Enforcement

## Applicability

Applies to organizations and individuals subject to the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act, which prohibits bribing foreign government officials and requires accurate books and records for issuers. Covers dual DOJ/SEC enforcement tracks, resolution types, declination factors, disgorgement, and coordination with international enforcement authorities.

## Key Requirements

### FCPA Structure — Dual Track

- **Anti-Bribery Provisions (DOJ)** / Prohibit corruptly offering, paying, promising, or authorizing payment of anything of value to a foreign official to obtain or retain business / **Jurisdiction**: (1) Domestic concerns (US persons and entities); (2) issuers (companies listed on US exchanges); (3) any person acting within US territory / **Consequence**: Criminal penalties: individuals up to $250K and 5 years imprisonment; entities up to $2M per violation; alternative fines of 2x gain/loss
- **Books and Records / Internal Controls (SEC)** / Issuers must: (1) make and keep accurate books, records, and accounts (Section 13(b)(2)(A)); (2) maintain adequate internal accounting controls (Section 13(b)(2)(B)) / **Consequence**: Civil and criminal liability; no materiality threshold for books and records violations; strict liability for failure to maintain controls
- **DOJ Criminal Enforcement** / DOJ Criminal Division, Fraud Section prosecutes anti-bribery violations and willful books-and-records violations / **Consequence**: Criminal conviction; imprisonment; criminal fines; debarment from government contracting
- **SEC Civil Enforcement** / SEC Division of Enforcement pursues civil anti-bribery violations by issuers and books-and-records/internal-controls violations / **Consequence**: Civil penalties; disgorgement of profits; injunctive relief; officer/director bars

### Resolution Types

- **Guilty Plea** / Company or individual pleads guilty to FCPA charges / **Consequence**: Most severe; conviction triggers collateral consequences; reserved for most egregious cases or repeat offenders
- **Deferred Prosecution Agreement (DPA)** / Charges filed but deferred; dismissed upon compliance with terms / **Timeline**: Typically 3 years / **Consequence**: Public filing; financial penalties; compliance and cooperation obligations; monitor may be imposed
- **Non-Prosecution Agreement (NPA)** / Agreement not to prosecute; less formal than DPA / **Timeline**: Typically 3 years / **Consequence**: No charges filed; less public stigma than DPA; still involves financial penalties and compliance commitments
- **Declination** / DOJ declines to prosecute / **Consequence**: Best outcome; DOJ Corporate Enforcement Policy creates presumption of declination for voluntary self-disclosure + full cooperation + timely remediation; may require disgorgement
- **SEC Administrative Orders** / Cease-and-desist orders for books-and-records and internal-controls violations / **Consequence**: Civil penalties; disgorgement; public order; may require compliance undertakings

### Declination Factors

- **Voluntary Self-Disclosure** / Company identified and disclosed misconduct before government discovery / **Consequence**: Single most important factor; creates presumption of declination under Corporate Enforcement Policy
- **Full Cooperation** / Timely preservation and production of evidence; identification of individuals; making witnesses available; no selective disclosure / **Consequence**: Cooperation must be genuine, proactive, and complete; partial cooperation = reduced credit
- **Timely Remediation** / Root cause analysis; disciplinary action against responsible individuals; compliance program enhancements; termination of corrupt relationships / **Consequence**: Must demonstrate concrete remedial steps, not just promises; remediation should address systemic issues
- **Disgorgement** / Even in declinations, DOJ may require disgorgement of profits attributable to misconduct / **Consequence**: Declination is not cost-free; company must disgorge ill-gotten gains

### Disgorgement and Penalties

- **Disgorgement** / Return of profits obtained through corrupt conduct; calculated as net profit (revenue minus direct costs) / **Threshold**: SEC disgorgement limited by Kokesh v. SEC (5-year statute of limitations) and Liu v. SEC (cannot exceed net profits) / **Consequence**: Disgorgement can be substantial; multi-million or billion-dollar amounts in major cases
- **Criminal Fines** / Statutory: up to $2M per violation for entities; up to $250K per violation for individuals; alternative: 2x pecuniary gain or loss / **Consequence**: DOJ typically seeks penalties above statutory maximum using alternative fine provision
- **Civil Penalties** / SEC civil penalties tiered: Tier 1 ($10K-$100K), Tier 2 ($50K-$500K for fraud/deceit), Tier 3 ($100K-$1M for fraud + substantial losses) per violation / **Consequence**: Multiple violations compound rapidly
- **Credit for Parallel Payments** / DOJ and SEC coordinate to avoid duplicative penalties; credit given for payments to other authorities (foreign and domestic) / **Consequence**: Must negotiate credit across enforcement authorities; total resolution may involve 3-5+ agencies

### International Enforcement Coordination

- **Multi-Jurisdictional Investigations** / Increasing coordination between DOJ/SEC and foreign authorities (UK SFO, French PNF, Brazilian MPF, Dutch OM, Swiss OAG) / **Consequence**: Single corrupt scheme may trigger enforcement in 3-5+ jurisdictions; must coordinate defense strategy across jurisdictions
- **UK Bribery Act** / Broader than FCPA: covers commercial bribery, no facilitating payments exception, strict liability for corporate failure to prevent bribery / **Consequence**: UK nexus (even minimal) can create parallel liability; "adequate procedures" defense requires robust compliance program
- **Global Settlement Coordination** / Authorities coordinate total penalty amount and allocate credit for payments across jurisdictions / **Consequence**: Total global resolution may exceed $1B for major cases (e.g., Airbus: $3.9B; Ericsson: $1.06B)

### Individual Prosecution

- **Emphasis on Individuals** / DOJ prioritizes individual accountability; company cooperation credit requires identification of all individuals involved / **Consequence**: Corporate resolution does not protect individuals; DOJ may prosecute individuals even while company receives declination
- **Travel Act and Money Laundering** / DOJ may use Travel Act (18 U.S.C. 1952), money laundering (18 U.S.C. 1956-1957), and wire fraud (18 U.S.C. 1343) as additional charges / **Consequence**: Additional charges expand sentencing exposure; money laundering carries up to 20 years imprisonment
- **Statute of Limitations** / 5 years for FCPA anti-bribery; 5 years for books and records; conspiracy and money laundering may extend exposure / **Consequence**: Statute may be tolled by tolling agreements during cooperation; DOJ frequently obtains tolling agreements as condition of cooperation

### FCPA Compliance Program Elements

- **Tone at the Top** / Senior management commitment to anti-corruption compliance / **Consequence**: DOJ evaluates leadership's genuine commitment, not just statements
- **Risk Assessment** / Country risk, industry risk, third-party risk, transaction risk / **Consequence**: Must be specific and updated; generic assessment is insufficient
- **Third-Party Due Diligence** / Risk-based diligence on agents, consultants, JV partners, distributors / **Threshold**: Enhanced diligence for high-risk third parties (government connections, high-corruption jurisdictions, unusual payment terms) / **Consequence**: Third-party misconduct is the primary source of FCPA liability; inadequate diligence = corporate liability
- **Financial Controls** / Review and approval processes for: gifts and hospitality, travel expenses, charitable donations, political contributions, facilitation payments / **Consequence**: Corrupt payments often disguised in these categories; controls must detect anomalies

## Internal Investigations

# Internal Investigations

## Applicability

Applies to organizations conducting internal investigations in response to whistleblower complaints, regulatory inquiries, compliance concerns, or suspected misconduct. Covers investigation scope and mandate, attorney-client privilege preservation, document holds, Upjohn warnings, and reporting obligations.

## Key Requirements

### Investigation Scope and Mandate

- **Written Mandate** / Investigation should be authorized by board, audit committee, or appropriate governing body with defined scope, reporting lines, and authority / **Consequence**: Unclear mandate = scope creep, privilege disputes, and potential waiver; board resolution creates contemporaneous record of legal purpose
- **Independence** / Investigator (internal or external counsel) must be independent from subjects of the investigation / **Consequence**: Lack of independence undermines credibility with regulators; DOJ evaluates whether investigation was thorough and independent
- **Scope Decisions** / Define what is being investigated, time period, geographic scope, and what is out of scope / **Consequence**: Too narrow = missed issues; too broad = cost overruns and disruption; document scope decisions and rationale

### Upjohn Warnings (Corporate Miranda)

- **Requirement** / When interviewing employees as part of an internal investigation, counsel must provide Upjohn warnings before substantive discussion / **Consequence**: Failure to warn = employee may reasonably believe counsel represents them; privilege may be jeopardized; ethical violations for counsel
- **Warning Elements** / Must convey: (1) The lawyer represents the company, not the individual employee; (2) The purpose of the interview is to gather facts for the company's legal counsel; (3) The communication is protected by attorney-client privilege, but the privilege belongs to the company, not the employee; (4) The company may choose to waive the privilege and disclose the conversation to third parties, including the government / **Consequence**: Failure on any element may undermine privilege claim or create individual representation issues
- **Documentation** / Warning should be memorialized (written acknowledgment preferred; at minimum, contemporaneous notes by interviewer) / **Consequence**: Dispute over whether warning was given = factual question that undermines privilege protection
- **Represented Employees** / If employee has personal counsel, coordinate with their counsel before interview; Model Rule 4.2 (no-contact rule) may apply / **Consequence**: Interview without counsel awareness = potential ethics violation; evidence may be suppressed

### Document Preservation and Holds

- **Litigation Hold** / Must implement hold when litigation or government investigation is reasonably anticipated / **Timeline**: Immediately upon triggering event (complaint, subpoena, demand letter, credible allegation) / **Consequence**: Failure to preserve = spoliation sanctions including adverse inference, monetary penalties, case dismissal in extreme cases
- **Hold Scope** / Must be reasonably comprehensive: all relevant custodians, data sources (email, files, Slack/Teams, cloud, backups, personal devices if used for business) / **Consequence**: Over-preservation increases cost; under-preservation = spoliation risk; document decisions
- **Hold Notice** / Written notice to custodians identifying obligation to preserve, types of materials, and prohibition on deletion / **Consequence**: Oral-only hold = difficult to enforce and prove; written notice with acknowledgment is standard
- **Ongoing Obligation** / Must monitor compliance with hold; send reminders; update as scope evolves / **Consequence**: Initial hold without follow-up = constructive spoliation if employees delete relevant materials

### Investigation Reports

- **Oral vs. Written Reports** / Significant strategic decision: oral reports to board/committee reduce waiver risk; written reports create discoverable documents if privilege is waived / **Consequence**: Written reports provide complete record but are high-risk if government seeks production; oral reports with board minutes noting conclusions (not details) are common practice
- **Privilege Protection** / Reports prepared by counsel at direction of client for purpose of providing legal advice are privileged; dual-purpose reports (legal + business) create risk / **Consequence**: If report would have been prepared in substantially similar form for business purposes (regardless of litigation), privilege may not attach (see Capital One test)
- **Government Production** / Selective waiver doctrine (sharing privileged materials with government without broader waiver) is not recognized in most circuits / **Consequence**: Producing investigation report to DOJ/SEC likely waives privilege as to all third parties; consider providing oral proffer or factual summaries instead

### Reporting Obligations

- **Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs)** / Financial institutions must file SARs for transactions involving $5,000+ where institution knows or suspects fraud, money laundering, or BSA violation / **Timeline**: 30 days from detection (60 days if no suspect identified) / **Consequence**: Failure to file = BSA penalties ($500K+ per violation); SAR filing is confidential — disclosure of SAR filing is prohibited
- **Securities Violations** / Public companies must assess whether investigation findings require securities disclosure (Form 8-K, 10-Q, 10-K) / **Consequence**: Failure to disclose material findings = securities fraud liability; but premature disclosure may harm investigation
- **Regulatory Self-Reporting** / Some regulations require self-reporting of violations (e.g., FCPA, healthcare fraud, government contracts) / **Consequence**: Voluntary self-disclosure may provide cooperation credit; failure to report when required = additional violations and enhanced penalties
- **Professional Obligations** / Counsel may have reporting obligations under Model Rules (e.g., Rule 1.13 — reporting up within organization; SOX 307 — SEC attorney conduct rules) / **Consequence**: In-house counsel must "report up" to CLO/board if officers fail to address material violations; potential "noisy withdrawal" obligation

### Employee Issues During Investigations

- **No Retaliation** / Cannot terminate, demote, or take adverse action against employees for participating in investigation or reporting concerns / **Consequence**: Retaliation claims under SOX, Dodd-Frank, state whistleblower laws; see `whistleblower.md`
- **Suspension/Leave** / May place subjects on administrative leave during investigation; should be paid leave to avoid constructive discharge claims / **Consequence**: Unpaid suspension before conclusion of investigation = potential wrongful termination claim
- **Privacy Considerations** / Employee monitoring and data access during investigations must comply with applicable privacy laws (ECPA, state privacy, GDPR if international) / **Consequence**: Improperly obtained evidence may be inadmissible; potential independent liability for privacy violations

## Whistleblower

# Whistleblower Protections and Programs

## Applicability

Applies to organizations subject to federal and state whistleblower protection statutes, including public companies, government contractors, healthcare providers, and financial institutions. Covers Sarbanes-Oxley Section 806, Dodd-Frank SEC Whistleblower Program, False Claims Act qui tam provisions, and state whistleblower laws.

## Key Requirements

### Sarbanes-Oxley Section 806

- **Covered Employees** / Employees of publicly traded companies and their subsidiaries, contractors, subcontractors, and agents / **Consequence**: Broad coverage; includes employees of private subsidiaries of public companies
- **Protected Activity** / Reporting fraud against shareholders, securities violations, wire/mail/bank fraud, or any federal offense relating to fraud against shareholders; filing, testifying, or participating in SEC proceedings / **Consequence**: Protection extends to internal reporting (supervisor, compliance, audit committee) and external reporting (federal agencies, Congress, supervisors)
- **Filing Requirement** / Complaint must be filed with OSHA within 180 days of the adverse action / **Timeline**: 180 calendar days — strict statute of limitations / **Consequence**: Late filing = claim dismissed; no tolling for continuing violations in most circuits
- **OSHA Process** / OSHA investigates; preliminary order within 60 days; either party may request ALJ hearing within 30 days of OSHA findings / **Consequence**: OSHA can order preliminary reinstatement while case is adjudicated
- **Remedies** / Reinstatement, back pay with interest, compensatory damages (including attorney fees, litigation costs, expert witness fees), special damages / **Consequence**: No punitive damages under SOX 806; but compensatory damages can include emotional distress
- **Criminal Prohibition** / SOX Section 1107: criminal penalties for retaliation against any person who provides truthful information to law enforcement about a federal offense / **Consequence**: Fines and/or up to 10 years imprisonment; applies to all entities, not just public companies

### Dodd-Frank SEC Whistleblower Program

- **Eligibility** / Any individual who voluntarily provides original information to the SEC that leads to successful enforcement action with sanctions exceeding $1 million / **Consequence**: Only individuals (not companies) eligible; information must be "original" (derived from independent knowledge or analysis)
- **Award Amount** / 10-30% of monetary sanctions collected in SEC enforcement action exceeding $1 million / **Threshold**: $1M minimum sanctions threshold; SEC has discretion on percentage within 10-30% range based on significance of information, cooperation, programmatic interest / **Consequence**: Awards have exceeded $100M for single whistleblowers; program has awarded over $1 billion total through 2024
- **Anti-Retaliation** / Prohibits retaliation by employers against individuals who: (1) provide information to SEC, (2) initiate or assist in SEC investigation, (3) make disclosures required by SOX, securities laws, or other laws under SEC jurisdiction / **Consequence**: Private right of action in federal court (no OSHA filing required); 2x back pay, reinstatement, attorney fees; 6-year statute of limitations (3 years from when facts known, but no more than 6 years from violation)
- **Internal Reporting Credit** / SEC considers whether whistleblower reported internally first (120-day safe harbor: report internally, then to SEC within 120 days and receive credit as of internal reporting date) / **Consequence**: Internal reporting encouraged but not required; organizations benefit from effective internal reporting channels
- **Confidentiality** / SEC prohibited from disclosing whistleblower identity except as required by court or in connection with public proceeding / **Consequence**: Whistleblower identity protected; but practical identification may occur through nature of information disclosed

### False Claims Act (FCA) — Qui Tam Provisions

- **Scope** / Covers fraud against the federal government: false claims for payment, false statements, reverse false claims (concealing obligation to pay government), conspiracy / **Consequence**: Applies to government contractors, healthcare providers (Medicare/Medicaid), grant recipients, and any entity submitting claims to the government
- **Qui Tam Action** / Private individual (relator) files lawsuit on behalf of the government under seal; DOJ has 60 days (extendable) to investigate and decide whether to intervene / **Consequence**: Complaint remains under seal during government investigation; relator must serve government but not defendant during seal period
- **Relator's Share — Government Intervenes** / 15-25% of recovery if the government intervenes and proceeds with the action / **Consequence**: Government typically intervenes in cases with strong evidence; intervention significantly increases recovery likelihood
- **Relator's Share — Government Declines** / 25-30% of recovery if the government declines to intervene and relator proceeds independently / **Consequence**: Relator bears litigation costs and risk; lower success rate without government support
- **Damages and Penalties** / Treble damages (3x government's loss) plus civil penalties of $13,946-$27,894 per false claim (adjusted annually for inflation) / **Consequence**: Penalties compound rapidly with volume of false claims; single government contract with repeated false billing = massive exposure
- **Public Disclosure Bar** / Qui tam action barred if substantially based on publicly disclosed information unless relator is an "original source" (direct and independent knowledge, voluntarily disclosed to government before public disclosure) / **Consequence**: Prior government investigation, media reports, or audit findings may bar qui tam claim
- **Retaliation Protection** / FCA Section 3730(h): protection for employees who investigate, initiate, testify, or assist in FCA action / **Consequence**: 2x back pay, reinstatement, special damages, attorney fees; 3-year statute of limitations

### State Whistleblower Laws

- **Scope** / Most states have whistleblower protection statutes covering public and/or private sector employees / **Consequence**: May provide broader protection than federal statutes; different filing requirements, statutes of limitations, and remedies
- **Key State Programs** / California (Labor Code 1102.5 — broad protection, punitive damages available), New York (Labor Law 740 — 2 years SOL, reinstatement + back pay + front pay + attorney fees + $10K civil penalty), Illinois (Whistleblower Act — 740 ILCS 174), New Jersey (Conscientious Employee Protection Act — broad "reasonable belief" standard) / **Consequence**: Must analyze state law in each relevant jurisdiction; state protections often run parallel to federal
- **State False Claims Acts** / 30+ states and DC have enacted state false claims acts modeled on federal FCA, covering state Medicaid funds / **Consequence**: Parallel state and federal qui tam actions possible; relator may recover under both

### Organizational Best Practices

- **Internal Reporting Channels** / Establish anonymous hotline/reporting system; communicate availability regularly; ensure reports reach appropriate level (compliance, audit committee) / **Consequence**: Effective internal channels may: (1) resolve issues before government involvement, (2) demonstrate compliance culture, (3) qualify for cooperation credit
- **Non-Retaliation Policy** / Explicit written policy prohibiting retaliation; train managers on compliance; investigate and address any suspected retaliation immediately / **Consequence**: Policy alone is insufficient — must be enforced; single retaliation incident undermines entire compliance program credibility
- **Investigation Protocol** / All whistleblower reports investigated promptly and documented; findings reported to appropriate governance body / **Consequence**: Failure to investigate = evidence of indifference; regulators and courts view unfavorably
