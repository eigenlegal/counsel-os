## Warnings Labels

# Warnings and Labels

## Applicability

Load this file when a matter involves product warning adequacy, labeling requirements, failure-to-warn claims, post-sale duty to warn, Proposition 65 compliance, OSHA hazard communication, product-specific labeling regulations (food, cosmetics, chemicals, drugs), or bilingual/multilingual labeling requirements. Also load when reviewing warning language in product documentation, packaging, or user manuals.

## Key Requirements

### Warning Adequacy (Tort Law)

- **Content adequacy**
  - **Standard:** Warning must identify the specific hazard, the nature and severity of potential harm, and instructions for safe use or avoidance.
  - **Consequence:** A warning that merely states "Danger" or "Use with caution" without specifying the nature and severity of the risk is legally inadequate. Vague or generic warnings are treated as equivalent to no warning under Restatement Third, Section 2(c). Must address foreseeable uses and reasonably foreseeable misuses. Must communicate: what the hazard is, how it can cause injury, what to do (or not do) to avoid injury, and what to do if exposure occurs. Failure to include any of these elements may render the warning inadequate even if a warning label exists.

- **Prominence and conspicuousness**
  - **Standard:** Warning must be positioned where a user will encounter it before exposure to the hazard, in a size, color, and format reasonably calculated to attract attention.
  - **Consequence:** A warning buried in a multi-page manual, printed in small type, or on discarded interior packaging may be inadequate regardless of content. ANSI Z535 series provides voluntary consensus standards for safety signs, labels, and product safety information. Signal word hierarchy: DANGER (red — will result in death or serious injury), WARNING (orange — could result in death or serious injury), CAUTION (yellow — could result in minor or moderate injury). Safety alert symbol (triangle with exclamation point) precedes the signal word. Courts increasingly reference ANSI Z535 as evidence of the standard of care.

- **Comprehensibility**
  - **Standard:** Warning must be understandable to the reasonably foreseeable user population, accounting for literacy, language, age, cognitive ability, and expertise.
  - **Consequence:** Consumer warnings must use plain language (6th-8th grade reading level recommended), pictograms/symbols, and avoid jargon. Technical warnings for professionals may use industry terminology. Where the foreseeable user population includes non-English speakers, bilingual or symbol-based warnings may be required. ISO 3864 and ANSI Z535.3 provide standardized safety symbols.

- **Over-warning dilution**
  - **Standard:** Excessive warnings about trivial, obvious, or scientifically unsupported risks can dilute the effectiveness of warnings about serious risks.
  - **Consequence:** Courts recognize that "warning fatigue" from over-warning can itself render a scheme inadequate. Cotton v. Buckeye Gas Products Co. (D.C. Cir. 1988). The goal is proportionate, risk-calibrated warnings that draw appropriate attention to the most significant risks.

- **State-of-the-art defense for warnings**
  - **Standard:** The risk was not known or reasonably knowable given the state of scientific and technical knowledge at the time of distribution.
  - **Consequence:** In recognizing jurisdictions, a manufacturer is not liable for failure to warn of a risk undiscoverable at time of sale. Once the risk becomes known, the post-sale duty to warn is triggered. Does not apply where the manufacturer had internal data suggesting the risk but failed to investigate.

### Post-Sale Duty to Warn

- **Restatement Third Section 10**
  - **Threshold:** Manufacturer learns or should learn of a product risk after sale, and a reasonable person in the seller's position would provide a warning.
  - **Consequence:** Manufacturer must take reasonable steps to warn current users. Four factors: (1) severity of the risk, (2) likelihood of harm, (3) feasibility of identifying and reaching affected users (product registration, distribution records, connected product channels), (4) burden of providing the warning relative to the risk. Does not always require a full recall — may be satisfied by targeted notification, updated manuals, safety bulletins, retrofit labels, or software updates for connected products. Interacts with CPSC Section 15(b) reporting (see recalls.md).

- **Continuing duty for known users**
  - **Threshold:** Manufacturer maintains a customer registry, warranty database, or reasonable means of contacting purchasers (including connected product channels).
  - **Consequence:** Where a direct communication channel exists (product registration, subscription, IoT connectivity), the post-sale duty is heightened. Failure to use an available channel for safety-critical information strengthens a failure-to-warn claim. Connected product capabilities (push notifications, firmware updates) may raise the standard of care.

### Learned Intermediary Doctrine

- **Prescription drugs and medical devices**
  - **Standard:** For Rx products, the manufacturer's duty to warn runs to the prescribing physician, not the end patient.
  - **Consequence:** Manufacturer satisfies its duty by providing the physician with adequate information about risks, side effects, contraindications, and interactions through the package insert, physician labeling, Dear Healthcare Provider letters, and medical literature. The physician evaluates and communicates relevant risks. Manufacturer must update warnings when new safety information emerges (FDA-required labeling supplements).

- **Exceptions to learned intermediary**
  - **Threshold:** Direct-to-consumer (DTC) advertising, mass immunization programs, contraceptive devices (some jurisdictions), oral contraceptives (FDA-required patient inserts).
  - **Consequence:** Where the manufacturer communicates directly with patients via DTC advertising, some courts hold the manufacturer assumes a direct duty to warn consumers (Perez v. Wyeth Laboratories, NJ 1999). Not universally adopted. For mass immunizations without individualized physician consultation, the manufacturer has a direct duty. FDA-mandated patient medication guides supplement the doctrine.

### Bilingual and Multilingual Requirements

- **Federal requirements**
  - **Threshold:** Specific product categories and workplace contexts with federal bilingual mandates.
  - **Consequence:** Pesticide labels must include Spanish-language safety information where used by a workforce with significant Spanish-speaking workers (EPA under FIFRA; 40 C.F.R. Part 156). OSHA requires hazard communication in a language workers understand — may require translated materials or oral training. No general federal mandate for bilingual consumer labeling, but failure to provide multilingual warnings where the foreseeable user population includes non-English speakers may support a failure-to-warn tort claim.

- **State and local requirements**
  - **Threshold:** Certain states and municipalities require bilingual labeling for specific products.
  - **Consequence:** Requirements vary by jurisdiction and product type. Failure to comply creates per se regulatory violation exposure and strengthens tort claims. Best practice: include symbol-based warnings (ISO 3864 pictograms) and bilingual text for products with foreseeable non-English-speaking users.

### California Proposition 65

- **Safe Drinking Water and Toxic Enforcement Act (Prop 65)**
  - **Threshold:** Product contains or exposes individuals to any chemical on the Prop 65 list (900+ chemicals including lead, cadmium, BPA, acrylamide, formaldehyde, certain phthalates) at levels exceeding safe harbor thresholds — NSRL (no significant risk level) for carcinogens, MADL (maximum allowable dose level) for reproductive toxicants.
  - **Consequence:** Requires a "clear and reasonable" warning before exposure. Since August 2018 amendments (27 CCR Section 25603 et seq.), warning must include: the name of at least one listed chemical, the specific health endpoint (cancer and/or reproductive harm), the triangular warning symbol, and URL (www.P65Warnings.ca.gov). Civil penalties up to $2,500 per day per violation. Enforced primarily through private bounty actions — plaintiff's attorneys receive 25% of penalties. Applies to any product sold in California regardless of where manufactured. No de minimis exception — either below safe harbor or must warn.

- **Prop 65 safe harbor warnings**
  - **Threshold:** Use of specific warning language prescribed in 27 CCR Section 25603 et seq., tailored by product type.
  - **Consequence:** Safe harbor warnings provide a rebuttable presumption of adequacy. Short-form and long-form options available. Must be prominently displayed on product, packaging, shelf tag, or at point of sale. Online sellers must provide warning before purchase — cannot be buried in post-purchase documentation. Catalog sellers must provide in a manner the purchaser is likely to see before completing the purchase.

### OSHA Hazard Communication Standard (HCS)

- **Safety Data Sheets (SDS)**
  - **Threshold:** All chemical manufacturers and importers must prepare a 16-section SDS for hazardous chemicals; employers must maintain accessible SDS for all workplace hazardous chemicals.
  - **Consequence:** GHS-aligned 16-section format: (1) identification, (2) hazard identification, (3) composition, (4) first-aid, (5) fire-fighting, (6) accidental release, (7) handling/storage, (8) exposure controls/PPE, (9) physical/chemical properties, (10) stability/reactivity, (11) toxicological, (12) ecological, (13) disposal, (14) transport, (15) regulatory, (16) other. Updated SDS required within 3 months of new significant information. Penalties: serious violation up to $16,131; willful up to $161,323.

- **Container labels (HCS)**
  - **Threshold:** All containers of hazardous chemicals shipped or in the workplace must bear HCS-compliant labels.
  - **Consequence:** Shipped containers must include: product identifier, signal word, hazard statements, precautionary statements, GHS pictograms (flame, skull and crossbones, exclamation mark, corrosion, health hazard, etc.), and supplier identification. Workplace containers may use alternative labeling (NFPA 704, HMIS) if equivalent and employees are trained. Labels must be legible, prominent, and in English.

### Product-Specific Labeling Requirements

- **Food labeling**
  - **Threshold:** All packaged food products sold in the U.S.
  - **Consequence:** Nutrition Facts panel (21 C.F.R. Part 101), ingredient list in descending order of predominance, allergen declaration under FALCPA — Top 9 allergens (milk, eggs, fish, shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat, soybeans, sesame under FASTER Act 2021) in plain English. Misbranding is a prohibited act under FDCA. FDA Warning Letters and recall authority. Country of origin labeling (COOL) for certain products.

- **Cosmetics labeling**
  - **Threshold:** All cosmetic products sold in the U.S.
  - **Consequence:** INCI ingredient names in descending order, net quantity, distributor/manufacturer identity, warnings for specific ingredients (coal tar dyes, sunscreen drug facts). MoCRA (2022) adds: mandatory serious adverse event reporting (15 business days), facility registration, product listing with ingredient disclosure to FDA, FDA mandatory recall authority, fragrance allergen disclosure (phased implementation).

- **Chemical labeling (TSCA / state laws)**
  - **Threshold:** Chemical substances manufactured, imported, or processed in the U.S.
  - **Consequence:** EPA may require labeling under TSCA Section 6 risk management rules. SNURs under TSCA Section 5(a)(2) may impose notification and labeling obligations. State laws add requirements: CA Cleaning Product Right to Know Act (SB 258) requires ingredient disclosure; WA Children's Safe Products Act requires reporting of chemicals of high concern.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Product Liability (product-liability.md):** Failure to warn is one of three product liability theories under Restatement Third. Warning adequacy analysis here directly supports the failure-to-warn analysis in product-liability.md. State-of-the-art and learned intermediary are defenses to failure-to-warn claims.
- **Recalls (recalls.md):** Post-sale duty to warn intersects with recall reporting — the same risk may trigger both. Recall notices are post-sale warnings and must meet adequacy standards.
- **Consumer Product Safety (consumer-product-safety.md):** CPSIA tracking labels facilitate post-sale warning delivery. FHSA-required warning labels (small parts, choking hazard) are mandatory product-specific warnings.
- **Consumer Protection:** False or misleading labeling triggers FTC and state AG enforcement. Advertising claims contradicting product warnings create inconsistency liability.
- **Data Privacy:** Prop 65 warnings on websites must be delivered before purchase. Connected product post-sale warnings raise questions about customer data use.
- **Employment:** OSHA HCS protects workers handling hazardous chemicals. Employer training obligations complement product warnings.
