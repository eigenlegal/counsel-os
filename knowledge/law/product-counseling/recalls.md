# Product Recalls

## Applicability

Load this file when a matter involves a product safety incident, potential or actual product recall, CPSC reporting obligations, FDA recall classification, NHTSA vehicle safety defect reporting, recall cost allocation in supply chain agreements, or post-recall litigation risk assessment. Also load when drafting recall cooperation provisions in manufacturing, distribution, or supplier agreements.

## Key Requirements

### Voluntary vs. Mandatory Recalls

- **Voluntary recall (CPSC)**
  - **Threshold:** Company identifies or is notified of a substantial product hazard and initiates corrective action cooperatively with CPSC.
  - **Consequence:** Company works with CPSC to develop a corrective action plan (CAP) — refund, repair, or replacement at no cost to consumers. CPSC publishes recall notice on cpsc.gov with product photos, hazard description, incident count, and remedy instructions. Approximately 99% of CPSC recalls are voluntary. Company bears all costs. Cooperation typically results in more favorable enforcement terms and reduced civil penalty exposure. CPSC may require periodic progress reports on recall effectiveness and remedy completion rates.

- **Mandatory recall (CPSC)**
  - **Threshold:** CPSC determines through administrative proceedings that a product presents a substantial product hazard and the company refuses voluntary corrective action.
  - **Consequence:** CPSC issues an order under Section 15(c)-(d) of CPSA compelling recall, repair, replacement, or refund. Requires formal adjudication with hearing before an administrative law judge. Additional civil penalties for failure to comply. Mandatory recalls are rare (fewer than 1% of all CPSC recalls) but signal maximum regulatory confrontation. The mandatory process is slow — typically 12-18 months — during which the defective product may remain on the market, increasing liability exposure.

- **FDA Class I recall**
  - **Threshold:** Reasonable probability that use of or exposure to the product will cause serious adverse health consequences or death.
  - **Consequence:** Most urgent classification. Triggers public health notification, press releases, and depth-of-distribution checks. Company must account for all product in distribution and provide FDA with a recall strategy including scope, depth (wholesale, retail, consumer level), and public notification plan. FDA may request a health hazard evaluation. Examples: contaminated food with pathogen, mislabeled drugs with wrong active ingredient, medical devices with life-threatening malfunction. FDA has authority to order mandatory recall for food (FSMA) and devices (21 U.S.C. 360h).

- **FDA Class II recall**
  - **Threshold:** Use of or exposure may cause temporary or medically reversible adverse health consequences, or probability of serious consequences is remote.
  - **Consequence:** Requires product retrieval or correction. Company must conduct effectiveness checks to verify recall reaches sub-recipients — FDA specifies frequency (typically 2-3 checks). Most common FDA recall class by volume. Company must provide FDA with status reports at specified intervals.

- **FDA Class III recall**
  - **Threshold:** Use of or exposure is not likely to cause adverse health consequences.
  - **Consequence:** Typically involves minor labeling issues or technical violations. May not require public notification or effectiveness checks, but product must still be corrected or removed from distribution.

- **NHTSA vehicle recall**
  - **Threshold:** Motor vehicle or equipment contains a safety-related defect or does not comply with a Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standard (FMVSS).
  - **Consequence:** Manufacturer must notify NHTSA within 5 business days of determining a defect exists, then notify owners/purchasers/dealers by first-class mail within a reasonable time (60 days for vehicles, 70 days for tires). Must provide a free remedy. 49 U.S.C. Section 30118. NHTSA can initiate investigation even without manufacturer report. Recall campaigns must achieve adequate completion rates — NHTSA monitors and may require additional outreach.

### Reporting Obligations

- **CPSC Section 15(b) reporting**
  - **Timeline:** Within 24 hours of obtaining information reasonably supporting that a product: (1) fails to comply with a consumer product safety rule, (2) contains a defect creating a substantial product hazard, or (3) creates an unreasonable risk of serious injury or death.
  - **Consequence:** Failure to report is a federal offense — civil penalties up to $120,000 per violation, $17.15 million cap. Knowing failure can result in criminal prosecution ($50,000 fine, 5 years imprisonment). Applies to manufacturers, importers, distributors, and retailers. The 24-hour clock starts when any employee obtains reportable information — not just senior management. Late reporting is one of the most common bases for CPSC civil penalties.

- **CPSC Section 37 reporting (lawsuit patterns)**
  - **Timeline:** Within 30 days of becoming aware that 3+ civil actions filed in federal or state court within 24 months allege death or grievous bodily injury from a particular product model.
  - **Consequence:** Must report to CPSC even if no recall initiated and even if claims are believed meritless. Triggers CPSC investigation into potential substantial product hazard. Same civil and criminal penalties as Section 15(b) for failure to report.

- **FDA MedWatch / MDR reporting**
  - **Timeline:** 15 calendar days for serious adverse events involving medical devices (21 C.F.R. Part 803); 5 calendar days for events requiring remedial action to prevent unreasonable risk.
  - **Consequence:** Failure to report is a prohibited act under FDCA. FDA may issue warning letter, pursue injunction, or bring criminal prosecution. Importers and user facilities (hospitals, nursing homes) also have independent reporting obligations. Reported through FDA FAERS (drugs) and MAUDE database (devices).

- **NHTSA Early Warning Reporting (EWR)**
  - **Timeline:** Quarterly reports for manufacturers meeting production thresholds (500+ vehicles/year); defect and noncompliance reports within 5 business days of determination.
  - **Consequence:** Failure subject to civil penalties up to $26,315 per violation, $131.6 million maximum. TREAD Act requires quarterly reporting of: property damage claims, injuries, deaths, warranty claims for specified components, consumer complaints, and field reports. Must also report foreign safety recalls within 5 business days.

### Recall Process Steps

- **Investigation phase:**
  Identify scope — affected models, lot numbers, date codes, serial number ranges, production facilities, and time periods. Conduct root cause analysis. Compile injury/incident reports, consumer complaints, warranty returns, and field data. Investigation records are discoverable in subsequent litigation. Engage outside counsel early for privilege protections — but purely factual investigation records (testing results, engineering analyses) may not be privileged.

- **Determination phase:**
  Assess whether the defect constitutes a substantial product hazard (CPSC), safety defect (NHTSA), or Class I/II/III hazard (FDA). Factors: frequency of failure, severity of potential injury, foreseeability of misuse, affected population size, and existence of a feasible remedy. The determination decision should involve legal counsel, as it triggers reporting obligations and has significant litigation implications.

- **Notification phase:**
  For CPSC — submit a Full Report via saferproducts.gov. For FDA — submit recall report to appropriate district office. For NHTSA — file Part 573 Defect and Noncompliance Report. Direct consumer notification required: first-class mail (NHTSA), email, phone, social media (CPSC encourages multi-channel). Public notice through agency databases and press release. Recall notice must clearly describe: the product, the hazard, incident count, and the remedy.

- **Remedy phase:**
  Provide consumers a remedy at no cost — refund, repair, or replacement. Establish consumer contact channel (phone, website, email). Track completion rates — CPSC and NHTSA monitor effectiveness and may require additional outreach waves. CPSC may require effectiveness assessments at 3, 6, and 12 months. Retain records of all contacts, fulfillment, and returned/destroyed products. Disposal must prevent re-entry into commerce.

### Cost Allocation and Insurance

- **Manufacturer responsibility:**
  Recall costs — investigation, root cause analysis, notification, product retrieval, remedy, disposal/destruction, regulatory compliance, public relations, and customer service — are borne by the manufacturer as a regulatory obligation. Cannot be shifted to consumers.

- **Supply chain cost allocation:**
  Contractual indemnification provisions govern cost allocation between commercial parties. Absent express provisions, the party whose component or process caused the defect typically bears cost under common law indemnification. Key provisions: recall cooperation obligations, root cause investigation costs, indemnification for recall losses, insurance requirements, and duty to preserve evidence.

- **Insurance coverage:**
  Standard CGL policies exclude recall costs under the "sistership" exclusion (CGL exclusion (n)). Product recall insurance (product contamination/withdrawal insurance) is a separate line covering: notification costs, product retrieval, disposal/destruction, business interruption, third-party liability, replacement product costs, and crisis management expenses. Premiums vary by product type, distribution volume, and claims history.

### Liability Implications of Recalls

- **Recall as evidence of defect:**
  A recall announcement may be used by plaintiffs as evidence of a known and acknowledged defect. Some courts admit recall evidence; others exclude it as a subsequent remedial measure under FRE 407 (but FRE 407 does not apply to government-mandated actions). Careful drafting of recall language is essential.

- **Post-recall injuries:**
  Injuries occurring after a recall — particularly where the consumer was not effectively reached — may support enhanced damages including punitive damages. The adequacy of the recall effort (notification scope, remedy availability, completion rate) directly impacts litigation exposure.

- **Failure to recall:**
  A decision not to recall after learning of a defect may itself be evidence of conscious disregard for safety, supporting punitive damages. Document the analysis and rationale for any decision not to recall.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Product Liability (product-liability.md):** A recall does not shield against tort liability — it may mitigate damages but constitutes evidence of a known defect. Post-recall injuries may support punitive damages. Recall cooperation provisions should be coordinated with indemnification clauses.
- **Consumer Product Safety (consumer-product-safety.md):** Section 15(b) overlaps with the substantial product hazard analysis. CPSIA tracking labels facilitate recall effectiveness.
- **Warnings and Labels (warnings-labels.md):** Post-sale duty to warn (Restatement Third Section 10) may be triggered by the same information. Recall notices must meet warning adequacy standards.
- **Consumer Protection:** Recall communications must be accurate. Deceptive or inadequate notices may trigger FTC or state AG enforcement. State AGs may independently investigate and coordinate with CPSC.
- **Insurance:** Recall insurance coverage, CGL exclusions, and additional insured requirements are critical. Review policy triggers and limits before a recall event.
- **Data Privacy:** Recall notifications involving registered users raise data privacy considerations — generally permitted as a safety exception, but communications should be limited to recall content.
