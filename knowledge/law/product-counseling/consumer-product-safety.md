# Consumer Product Safety

## Applicability

Load this file when a matter involves consumer product manufacturing, importing, or distribution subject to CPSC jurisdiction; children's product requirements; product testing and certification; lead or phthalate limits; tracking label requirements; product import compliance; or potential substantial product hazard determinations. Also load when reviewing supplier quality agreements, import contracts, or product compliance representations.

## Key Requirements

### Consumer Product Safety Act (CPSA)

- **Substantial product hazard**
  - **Standard:** A product defect which (because of the pattern of defect, the number distributed, severity of risk, or otherwise) creates a substantial risk of injury to the public, OR a product failing to comply with an applicable consumer product safety rule that creates a risk of injury.
  - **Consequence:** Triggers mandatory reporting under Section 15(b) within 24 hours (see recalls.md). CPSC may seek corrective action including recall, repair, replacement, or refund. Companies refusing voluntary corrective action face mandatory recall through administrative adjudication and civil penalties. CPSC evaluates by considering: pattern of defect, number of products distributed, severity of risk, and likelihood of injury.

- **CPSC jurisdiction**
  - **Standard:** Any article, or component part thereof, produced or distributed for sale to, or for the personal use, consumption, or enjoyment of a consumer in or around a household, residence, school, recreation, or otherwise (15 U.S.C. 2052(a)(5)).
  - **Consequence:** Broad jurisdiction covers most consumer goods including household products, sporting goods, electronics, children's products, and furniture. Statutory exclusions: tobacco, motor vehicles (NHTSA), pesticides (EPA), aircraft (FAA), boats (Coast Guard), drugs/medical devices/cosmetics/food (FDA). Dual-use products (consumer and commercial) are covered to the extent they reach consumers.

- **Banned hazardous products**
  - **Threshold:** Products presenting an unreasonable risk of injury that cannot be made safe through a safety standard.
  - **Consequence:** CPSC may ban outright under Section 8 of CPSA. Current bans include: lawn darts with elongated tips, unstable refuse bins, fireworks with certain chemical mixtures, products containing certain asbestos forms, and small high-powered magnets marketed to children (effective 2023). Manufacturing, importing, distributing, or selling a banned product is a prohibited act subject to civil and criminal penalties.

- **Consumer product safety rules**
  - **Threshold:** CPSC issues mandatory rules under Section 7 for product categories where voluntary standards are inadequate.
  - **Consequence:** Rules prescribe performance, composition, design, construction, packaging, or labeling requirements. Non-compliant products are prohibited from manufacture, import, and sale. Current mandatory rules cover: baby cribs (16 C.F.R. 1219-1220), mattress flammability (16 C.F.R. 1632-1633), clothing textiles (16 C.F.R. 1610), children's sleepwear (16 C.F.R. 1615-1616), bicycle helmets (16 C.F.R. 1203), and many others. CPSC frequently incorporates voluntary standards (ASTM, UL) by reference as mandatory.

### Consumer Product Safety Improvement Act (CPSIA 2008)

- **Lead content limits — children's products**
  - **Threshold:** Total lead content must not exceed 100 ppm (0.01%) in any accessible part of a children's product (designed or intended primarily for children 12 and under).
  - **Consequence:** Violation renders the product a banned hazardous substance. Third-party testing required by a CPSC-accepted laboratory before certification and importation. Inaccessible component parts (per 16 C.F.R. 1500.87) are exempt from the 100 ppm limit but not from accessibility testing. Functional exceptions available (16 C.F.R. Part 1307) where lead is necessary and no practical alternative exists. Lead in surface coatings (paint) limited to 90 ppm under FHSA — applies to all consumer products, not just children's.

- **Phthalate limits — children's products**
  - **Threshold:** Children's toys and child care articles must not contain more than 0.1% (1,000 ppm) of these permanently banned phthalates: DEHP, DBP, BBP, DINP, DIBP, DPENP, DHEXP, and DCHP.
  - **Consequence:** Permanent ban. Third-party testing required. Products exceeding limits are banned hazardous products subject to seizure, recall, and penalties. "Child care article" means a product designed to facilitate sleep, feeding, sucking, or teething by children 3 and under. The CPSC Chronic Hazard Advisory Panel (CHAP) periodically reviews restrictions and may recommend additional bans.

- **Third-party testing requirement**
  - **Threshold:** All children's products must be tested by a CPSC-accepted third-party conformity assessment body for compliance with all applicable children's product safety rules before certification and importation.
  - **Consequence:** CPSC maintains a public list of accepted laboratories. Testing must cover all applicable rules: lead, phthalates, flammability, small parts (16 C.F.R. 1501), sharp edges, ASTM F963, and all product-specific rules. Periodic testing required — at least annually or upon material change in design, manufacturing process, or component sourcing. Component part testing permitted if the component is not altered during assembly. Costs borne by manufacturer or importer.

- **Children's Product Certificate (CPC)**
  - **Threshold:** Required for all children's products (children 12 and under) manufactured or imported for sale in the U.S.
  - **Consequence:** Must accompany each product or shipment and be furnished to retailers and distributors. Required contents: product identification, each applicable safety rule, CPSC-accepted laboratory (name, address, phone), date and place of manufacture, date and place of testing, certifier contact information. Issued by the domestic manufacturer or importer — not by the testing laboratory. Must be in English. Available to CPSC, CBP, and state AGs on request. Failure to provide a valid CPC may result in port detention and civil penalties.

- **General Certificate of Conformity (GCC)**
  - **Threshold:** Required for all non-children's consumer products subject to a CPSC rule, ban, standard, or regulation.
  - **Consequence:** Manufacturer or importer certifies based on testing or a reasonable testing program. Does not require third-party testing — self-certification acceptable. Required contents: product identification, applicable CPSC rules, testing entity, date/place of manufacture and testing. Available to CPSC and CBP on request.

- **Tracking labels**
  - **Threshold:** All children's products manufactured on or after August 14, 2009 must bear a permanent, distinguishing tracking label on the product and packaging (to extent practicable).
  - **Consequence:** Must identify: manufacturer or private labeler, production location (factory), production date (at least month/year), and cohort information (batch, lot, run number). Purpose: facilitate recalls by enabling precise identification of affected production cohorts. Labels must be permanent (survive normal use), legible, and visible. Failure to include tracking labels is a CPSIA violation subject to civil penalties.

### Import Requirements

- **Port of entry compliance**
  - **Threshold:** All imported consumer products must comply with applicable CPSC regulations; children's products must be accompanied by a CPC.
  - **Consequence:** CPSC works with CBP to screen imports. CPSC's Risk Assessment Methodology (RAM) system targets high-risk shipments based on product type, country of origin, manufacturer, importer history, and prior violations. Non-compliant products may be detained, refused entry, seized, or destroyed. Importers bear the same obligations as domestic manufacturers.

- **Filing of certificates**
  - **Threshold:** Electronic filing of CPC or GCC with CBP may be required upon importation under CPSC's eFiling program.
  - **Consequence:** Filed electronically through CBP's Automated Commercial Environment (ACE). Must be in English. Products without proper certification may be denied entry. The eFiling program is expanding to require electronic submission for an increasing range of product categories.

- **Importer obligations**
  - **Threshold:** Importer of record is the responsible party for CPSC compliance of imported products.
  - **Consequence:** Treated as "manufacturer" for compliance — including Section 15(b) reporting, certification, and recall responsibility. If the foreign manufacturer is unreachable, the importer bears full liability. Importers should maintain contracts covering: compliance with U.S. safety rules, testing requirements, indemnification, right to audit facilities, and recall cooperation.

### Penalties and Enforcement

- **Civil penalties**
  - **Threshold:** Any person who knowingly violates the CPSA or a rule, regulation, standard, or ban issued under it.
  - **Consequence:** Up to $120,000 per individual violation; $17.15 million maximum for a related series (inflation-adjusted). Each product unit and each day may constitute a separate violation. CPSC considers: nature, severity, and number of violations; ability to pay; effect on continued business; violation history; degree of culpability; and whether prompt corrective action was taken.

- **Criminal penalties**
  - **Threshold:** Knowing and willful violation of CPSA after CPSC notice, or knowing and willful violation of Section 15(b) reporting.
  - **Consequence:** Individuals: up to $50,000 fine, 5 years imprisonment, or both. Second offense doubles penalties ($100,000, 10 years). Corporate entities subject to Sentencing Guidelines criminal fines. DOJ prosecutes on CPSC referral. Requires both knowing (awareness of conduct) and willful (intentional disregard of law).

- **Section 17 enforcement (seizure and injunction)**
  - **Threshold:** Product is imminently hazardous or violates a ban or standard.
  - **Consequence:** CPSC may file suit seeking seizure and/or injunction against further manufacture, distribution, or sale. Seized products may be condemned and destroyed at owner's expense. Preliminary injunctions available on emergency basis. CPSC may also seek disgorgement and consumer redress.

### ASTM F963 (Toy Safety Standard)

- **Mandatory toy safety standard**
  - **Threshold:** All toys intended for children 14 and under must comply with ASTM F963.
  - **Consequence:** Incorporated by reference as a mandatory CPSC standard under CPSIA Section 106. Covers: mechanical/physical requirements (small parts, sharp edges, projectiles, strings/cords, stability), flammability, chemical limits (heavy metals in surface coatings — Sb, As, Ba, Cd, Cr, Pb, Hg, Se; migration limits), electrical and thermal requirements, battery-operated products, ride-on toys, and labeling. Third-party testing required. Periodically updated — manufacturers must comply with the CPSC-incorporated version.

- **Age determination guidelines**
  - **Threshold:** Manufacturers must accurately determine and label the intended age group for toys.
  - **Consequence:** CPSC Age Determination Guidelines provide criteria based on developmental stages and play patterns. Mis-aging (marketing small-parts toys to under-3) may violate mandatory safety rules, trigger enforcement, and create product liability exposure. Age grading determines which CPSIA requirements apply — products for children 12 and under trigger full CPSIA obligations; toys for under-3 trigger small parts regulations (16 C.F.R. 1501).

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Product Liability (product-liability.md):** CPSC safety standard violations constitute evidence of defect in tort litigation — may establish per se or rebuttable presumption depending on jurisdiction. Compliance is evidence of due care but not a complete defense. Testing and certification records are discoverable.
- **Recalls (recalls.md):** Section 15(b) reporting is the primary intersection. Tracking labels facilitate recall identification. Recall cost allocation in supply chain agreements must account for CPSC compliance costs.
- **Warnings and Labels (warnings-labels.md):** CPSC-mandated warnings (small parts, choking hazard under FHSA) are a subset of the broader adequacy analysis. CPSIA tracking labels serve a different purpose (recall facilitation) than safety warnings.
- **Consumer Protection:** CPSC enforcement complements FTC and state AG enforcement. State AGs have independent authority to enforce CPSA in federal court.
- **International Trade:** Foreign certifications (EU CE, EN 71) do not satisfy CPSC requirements. CPSC-accepted labs may be located abroad but must meet accreditation requirements. EU and U.S. requirements differ significantly.
- **Data Privacy:** Connected toys collecting personal information must comply with COPPA in addition to product safety requirements. Smart toys with internet connectivity present cybersecurity risks.
