## Discrimination Harassment

# Discrimination and Harassment

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting employment policies, hiring
practices, termination decisions, accommodation requests, workplace conduct policies,
or any matter involving complaints of discrimination, harassment, or retaliation.
It governs federal anti-discrimination statutes (Title VII, ADA, ADEA, EPA, PDA,
GINA, PWFA) and key state variations.

## Key Requirements

### Coverage Thresholds by Statute

| Statute | Min. Employees | Protected Characteristic |
|---------|---------------|--------------------------|
| **Title VII** (42 USC 2000e) | 15+ | Race, color, religion, sex (incl. sexual orientation & gender identity per Bostock v. Clayton County, 2020), national origin |
| **ADA** (42 USC 12101) | 15+ | Disability (physical/mental impairment substantially limiting a major life activity) |
| **ADEA** (29 USC 621) | 20+ | Age (40 and older only) |
| **Equal Pay Act** (29 USC 206(d)) | Virtually all employers | Sex-based wage disparities (no minimum employee count; covered under FLSA) |
| **Pregnancy Discrimination Act** (42 USC 2000e(k)) | 15+ (amends Title VII) | Pregnancy, childbirth, related medical conditions |
| **PWFA** (Pregnant Workers Fairness Act, 2023) | 15+ | Requires reasonable accommodation for known limitations related to pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions |
| **GINA** (42 USC 2000ff) | 15+ | Genetic information including family medical history |
| **Section 1981** (42 USC 1981) | No minimum | Race and ethnicity in contracts (no administrative exhaustion required; 4-year SOL) |

- **State laws often go further**: California FEHA covers employers with 5+ employees
  for discrimination and 1+ for harassment. New York State HRL covers 4+ employees.
  New York City HRL covers 4+ employees and is interpreted under the most liberal
  standard. Many states protect additional classes: sexual orientation, gender
  identity, marital status, political affiliation, criminal history, military status,
  and reproductive health decisions.
- **Consequence**: An employer below federal thresholds may still be fully covered
  under state or local laws with broader protections and lower thresholds.

### Theories of Discrimination

**Disparate Treatment (Intentional Discrimination)**

- **What**: An employer intentionally treats an employee less favorably because of
  a protected characteristic. Requires proof of discriminatory intent.
- **Threshold**: Under McDonnell Douglas burden-shifting (1973): (1) employee
  establishes prima facie case (member of protected class, qualified, adverse action,
  circumstances suggesting discrimination), (2) employer articulates legitimate
  nondiscriminatory reason, (3) employee shows that stated reason is pretextual.
  Direct evidence of intent bypasses this framework.
- **Consequence**: Full Title VII remedies: back pay, front pay, compensatory damages
  (emotional distress), punitive damages, injunctive relief, and attorneys' fees.
  Compensatory + punitive damages capped by employer size:
  - 15-100 employees: **$50,000**
  - 101-200 employees: **$100,000**
  - 201-500 employees: **$200,000**
  - 500+ employees: **$300,000**
  Section 1981 race claims and state law claims are not subject to these federal caps.

**Disparate Impact (Unintentional but Disproportionate Effect)**

- **What**: A facially neutral employment practice (testing, hiring criteria, degree
  requirement, algorithm) that disproportionately excludes a protected group.
  No intent to discriminate is required.
- **Threshold**: Plaintiff demonstrates statistically significant adverse impact
  (commonly using the 4/5ths rule as a screening tool). Employer defends by showing
  the practice is job-related and consistent with business necessity. Plaintiff may
  rebut by identifying a less discriminatory alternative.
- **Consequence**: Same remedies as disparate treatment except punitive damages are
  generally unavailable for pure disparate impact claims. Applies to hiring criteria,
  standardized tests, promotion requirements, criminal background policies, and
  algorithmic hiring tools.

### Harassment and Hostile Work Environment

- **What**: Unwelcome conduct based on a protected characteristic that is sufficiently
  severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of employment.
- **Standard**: Both subjective (the employee found it hostile) and objective
  (a reasonable person in the same position would find it hostile). Factors: frequency,
  severity, whether physically threatening or humiliating, whether it unreasonably
  interferes with work performance. A single incident can suffice if severe enough
  (e.g., physical assault, use of a racial epithet by a supervisor).
- **Supervisor harassment**: Employer is vicariously liable for supervisor harassment
  resulting in tangible employment action (termination, demotion, undesirable transfer).
  For harassment without tangible action, employer may assert the Faragher/Ellerth
  affirmative defense: (1) employer exercised reasonable care to prevent and promptly
  correct, and (2) employee unreasonably failed to use available corrective
  opportunities (complaint procedure).
- **Co-worker harassment**: Employer is liable only if it knew or should have known
  about the harassment and failed to take prompt, effective remedial action.
- **Consequence**: Full Title VII remedies. Absence of a complaint procedure and
  anti-harassment policy effectively eliminates the Faragher/Ellerth defense.
  California FEHA imposes strict liability for supervisor harassment with no
  affirmative defense available.

### ADA Reasonable Accommodation

- **What**: Employers must provide reasonable accommodations to qualified individuals
  with disabilities unless doing so would cause undue hardship on the operation of
  the business.
- **Interactive process**: Mandatory once the employer is aware (or should be aware)
  of the need for accommodation. The employer must engage in a good-faith, timely
  dialogue to identify effective accommodations. Neither party may obstruct or
  delay the process.
- **Examples**: Modified work schedule, telecommuting, reassignment to a vacant
  position, equipment modification, leave of absence beyond FMLA entitlement,
  removal of marginal job functions, modified break schedules.
- **Undue hardship**: Considers the accommodation's net cost, the employer's overall
  financial resources, the number of employees, the nature of the operations, and
  the impact on the ability to conduct business. Small employers face a lower bar
  for demonstrating undue hardship.
- **Consequence**: Failure to engage in the interactive process is itself an ADA
  violation, even if no reasonable accommodation ultimately exists. Remedies: back
  pay, compensatory and punitive damages (within Title VII caps), reinstatement,
  attorneys' fees.

### EEOC Administrative Process

- **Filing deadlines**: **180 days** from the discriminatory act in states without
  a state fair employment agency. **300 days** in states with such an agency (the
  majority). ADEA follows the same 180/300-day framework. Equal Pay Act: no EEOC
  filing required; 2-year SOL (3 years if willful).
- **Process**: Charge filed with EEOC. Investigation and possible mediation.
  Determination of reasonable cause or dismissal. Right-to-sue letter issued.
  Employee has **90 days** from receipt of right-to-sue letter to file suit.
- **Consequence**: Failure to timely file an EEOC charge bars the federal lawsuit
  (subject to limited equitable tolling). State-law claims have separate filing
  deadlines (e.g., California DFEH/CRD: 3 years; New York: 3 years state, 1 year
  city). EEOC charge requirement does not apply to Section 1981 race claims or
  Equal Pay Act claims.

### Retaliation

- **What**: Employers may not retaliate against employees who oppose unlawful
  practices, file charges, participate in investigations, or testify in proceedings.
- **Protected activity**: Formal EEOC charges, internal complaints (even if informal),
  refusing to participate in discriminatory conduct, cooperating with investigations,
  serving as a witness. The employee's belief of unlawfulness need only be reasonable,
  not correct.
- **Adverse action standard**: Any action that would dissuade a reasonable worker
  from engaging in protected activity (Burlington Northern v. White, 2006). Broader
  than the adverse action standard for discrimination claims — includes schedule
  changes, increased scrutiny, negative references, and exclusion from meetings.
- **Consequence**: Retaliation is the most frequently filed EEOC charge category
  (over 55% of all charges in recent years). Full remedies available. Retaliation
  claims frequently survive even when the underlying discrimination claim does not.

### Pay Equity

- **What**: Equal Pay Act prohibits sex-based wage disparities for substantially
  equal work. Title VII also covers compensation discrimination.
- **Threshold**: Jobs need not be identical, but must involve substantially equal
  skill, effort, and responsibility under similar working conditions. Defenses:
  seniority system, merit system, quantity/quality of production system, or any
  factor other than sex (narrowly construed in most jurisdictions).
- **Consequence**: EPA: 2-year SOL (3 years willful), liquidated damages (double
  back pay), attorneys' fees, no EEOC charge required. States have enacted broader
  laws covering additional protected characteristics: California (all protected
  classes), Massachusetts (gender), Colorado (all protected classes), New York
  (all protected classes). Several states ban salary history inquiries to prevent
  perpetuation of pay gaps (California, New York, Massachusetts, Colorado, and
  approximately 20 other jurisdictions).

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (At-Will):** At-will status does not authorize discriminatory
  termination. Every termination of a protected-class member must be evaluated.
- **Employment (Severance):** Releases must satisfy OWBPA for ADEA claims and
  knowing/voluntary standards for Title VII and ADA claims. Defective releases
  are unenforceable as to the specific statute.
- **Employment (Wage-and-Hour):** EPA claims frequently accompany FLSA wage claims.
  Pay audits should address both compliance frameworks.
- **Employment (Immigration):** National origin discrimination in hiring and
  visa-related employment decisions must be managed under both Title VII and INA 274B.
- **Data Privacy:** ADA medical information must be in separate confidential files.
  GINA prohibits requesting genetic information. Background check processes must
  comply with FCRA and state ban-the-box laws.
- **Litigation:** Burden-shifting frameworks (McDonnell Douglas), statistical
  evidence, and class certification under Rule 23 for pattern-or-practice claims.
