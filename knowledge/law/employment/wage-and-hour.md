# Wage and Hour

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting compensation provisions, employment
agreements, independent contractor agreements, or any matter involving exempt/non-exempt
classification, overtime calculations, minimum wage compliance, meal and rest breaks,
tip credits, or wage payment practices. It governs federal requirements under the
Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) and key state variations.

## Key Requirements

### FLSA Exempt vs. Non-Exempt Classification

- **What**: Employees must be classified as exempt or non-exempt from FLSA overtime
  and minimum wage requirements. Exempt employees are not entitled to overtime pay;
  non-exempt employees must receive overtime for all hours worked over 40 per week.
- **Salary threshold**: $35,568/year ($684/week). The DOL's 2024 final rule raised
  this to $43,888 (July 2024) and would have reached $58,656 (January 2025), but the
  Eastern District of Texas vacated the rule nationwide in November 2024. DOL enforces
  the 2019 levels and in May 2026 issued a technical amendment restoring the 2019
  regulatory text to 29 CFR part 541; a new rulemaking could change the figure, so
  confirm the effective threshold at the time of classification.
- **Salary basis test**: Exempt employees must be paid on a salary basis — a
  predetermined, fixed amount not subject to reduction based on quality or quantity
  of work performed. Impermissible deductions from salary (other than safe harbor
  exceptions) can destroy the exemption for the entire class of employees in that
  job classification.
- **Duties tests** (salary alone is never sufficient):
  - **Executive**: Primary duty is management of the enterprise or a recognized
    department/subdivision. Regularly directs the work of 2+ other employees. Has
    authority to hire or fire, or significant influence on those decisions.
  - **Administrative**: Primary duty is office or non-manual work directly related to
    management or general business operations of the employer or its customers.
    Exercises discretion and independent judgment on matters of significance.
  - **Professional (Learned)**: Primary duty requires advanced knowledge in a field
    of science or learning customarily acquired through prolonged specialized study.
  - **Professional (Creative)**: Primary duty requires invention, imagination,
    originality, or talent in a recognized field of artistic or creative endeavor.
  - **Computer Employee**: Systems analyst, programmer, or software engineer. Must
    be paid at least $684/week salary OR $27.63/hour. Duties must involve systems
    analysis, design, testing, or programming.
  - **Outside Sales**: Primary duty is making sales or obtaining orders/contracts
    away from the employer's place of business. No salary threshold.
  - **Highly Compensated Employee (HCE)**: Total annual compensation of $107,432+.
    Must customarily and regularly perform at least one exempt duty of an executive,
    administrative, or professional employee. The 2024 rule's higher HCE figures were
    vacated along with the rest of that rule; $107,432 is the operative number.
- **Consequence**: Misclassification results in liability for unpaid overtime for
  up to 2 years (3 years if willful), plus liquidated damages equal to unpaid
  overtime (effectively doubling recovery), plus attorneys' fees. FLSA collective
  actions (opt-in) and state class actions (opt-out) create enterprise-wide risk.

### Overtime Requirements

- **Federal (FLSA)**: Non-exempt employees must receive at least 1.5x their regular
  rate for all hours worked over 40 in a single workweek. The workweek is a fixed,
  recurring 168-hour period. Employers may not average hours across multiple weeks
  (except in healthcare under the 8/80 exception, 29 USC 207(j)).
- **California daily overtime**: 1.5x after 8 hours in a single workday. 2x after
  12 hours in a workday. 1.5x for the first 8 hours on the 7th consecutive day
  worked in a workweek. 2x after 8 hours on the 7th consecutive day.
- **Other state variations**: Colorado requires overtime after 12 hours per day or
  40 hours per week. Alaska requires daily overtime after 8 hours. Nevada requires
  overtime after 8 hours per day at 1.5x for employees earning less than 1.5x the
  minimum wage.
- **Regular rate calculation**: Includes base hourly rate plus non-discretionary
  bonuses, commissions, shift differentials, and piece rates. Excludes: truly
  discretionary bonuses, gifts, holiday premium pay, employer contributions to
  bona fide benefit plans, and paid time off.
- **Consequence**: Overtime violations carry FLSA liquidated damages (equal to unpaid
  overtime, effectively doubling liability), plus mandatory attorneys' fees. California
  Labor Code 510 and 1194 provide additional penalties and fee-shifting.

### Minimum Wage

- **Federal**: $7.25/hour (unchanged since July 24, 2009).
- **State minimums (selected, 2026; most are indexed and change each January 1 —
  Oregon adjusts July 1)**: California $16.90, Washington $17.13, New York $16.00
  ($17.00 in NYC, Long Island, and Westchester), Massachusetts $15.00,
  Colorado $15.16, Oregon $15.05 standard / $16.30 Portland metro (rising to
  $15.55 / $16.80 on July 1, 2026), Connecticut $16.94. Over 30 states and numerous
  cities exceed the federal minimum; confirm current rates (including local
  ordinances and industry-specific rates such as California fast food and
  healthcare) before advising.
- **Tipped employees**: Federal tipped minimum wage is $2.13/hour, provided tips
  bring total compensation to at least $7.25/hour (tip credit of up to $5.12/hour).
  Seven states prohibit tip credits entirely (employer must pay full minimum wage
  before tips): California, Oregon, Washington, Alaska, Montana, Minnesota, Nevada.
- **Youth/training wage**: FLSA allows $4.25/hour for workers under 20 during their
  first 90 consecutive calendar days of employment. Rarely used in practice.
- **Consequence**: Minimum wage violations trigger back pay, FLSA liquidated damages
  (double back pay), attorneys' fees, and potential state penalties. California
  waiting time penalties of up to 30 days' wages for final pay violations
  (Labor Code 203) can multiply exposure significantly.

### Meal and Rest Breaks

- **Federal (FLSA)**: No meal or rest break requirement. However, short breaks
  (5-20 minutes) that are provided must be compensated as hours worked. Bona fide
  meal periods (typically 30+ minutes) may be unpaid if the employee is completely
  relieved of all duties.
- **California (most stringent)**:
  - 30-minute unpaid meal period before end of 5th hour of work.
  - Second 30-minute meal before end of 10th hour.
  - 10-minute paid rest break per 4 hours (or major fraction thereof).
  - **Penalty**: 1 additional hour of pay at the employee's regular rate for each
    workday a meal or rest break is missed. For 100 employees missing one meal break
    daily over a year, exposure exceeds $2.6M in premium pay alone.
- **New York**: 30-minute meal for shifts of 6+ hours spanning 11am-2pm. Additional
  20-minute meal for shifts before 11am through after 7pm. Additional 45-minute
  meal for shifts of 6+ hours between 1pm-6am in factories.
- **Washington**: 30-minute meal for 5+ hour shifts. 10-minute rest per 4 hours.
  Meal must be no later than 5 hours into the shift.
- **Illinois**: 20-minute meal break for 7.5+ hour shifts, no later than 5 hours
  after the start of the shift.
- **Colorado**: 30-minute uninterrupted meal for 5+ hour shifts. 10-minute paid
  rest per 4 hours.
- **Consequence**: Missed-break penalties accumulate rapidly, especially in California
  where they are assessed per employee per day. Class action exposure for systematic
  break violations is among the highest-value employment litigation categories.

### Wage Payment and Final Pay

- **What**: States regulate the timing, manner, and frequency of wage payments, and
  impose strict deadlines for final pay upon termination.
- **California**: Final pay due immediately upon involuntary termination. Due within
  72 hours if the employee quits without advance notice, or immediately if 72+ hours
  of notice was given. Waiting time penalty: average daily wages for each day late,
  up to a maximum of 30 days (Labor Code 201-203).
- **New York**: Final pay by the next regular payday. Wage payment frequency: weekly
  for manual workers, semimonthly for clerical and other workers.
- **Illinois**: Final pay due by the next regular payday.
- **Massachusetts**: Final pay due on the day of involuntary termination. Treble
  damages for late payment (Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 149, 150).
- **Consequence**: Missed final-pay deadlines trigger per-day penalties that can
  quickly exceed the underlying wages owed. California and Massachusetts have the
  most severe penalty structures.

### Recordkeeping

- **What**: FLSA requires employers to maintain payroll records for non-exempt
  employees for at least 3 years (general records) and 2 years (supplemental records
  including time cards and work schedules).
- **Required records**: Employee name, address, birth date (if under 19), sex,
  occupation, time/day workweek begins, hours worked per day and per week, basis
  of pay and regular rate, total daily or weekly straight-time earnings, total
  overtime earnings, all additions to or deductions from wages, total wages paid
  each pay period, date of payment and period covered.
- **Consequence**: Failure to maintain records shifts the burden of proof to the
  employer in wage disputes. Employees may estimate their hours worked using the
  "just and reasonable inference" standard (Anderson v. Mt. Clemens Pottery, 1946),
  which the employer must then disprove with records.

### Statute of Limitations and Damages

- **FLSA**: 2-year SOL for non-willful violations; 3 years for willful. Liquidated
  damages (equal to back pay, effectively doubling recovery) are presumed unless
  employer demonstrates good faith and reasonable grounds. Attorneys' fees mandatory
  for prevailing employees.
- **California**: 3-year SOL for statutory wage claims (Labor Code); 4 years under
  UCL (Bus. & Prof. Code 17200).
- **PAGA (post-reform)**: The default civil penalty remains $100/employee per pay
  period, but the June 2024 reform (AB 2288/SB 92) rewrote exposure for PAGA notices
  filed on or after June 19, 2024: the plaintiff must have personally experienced
  each violation alleged; the $200 subsequent-violation penalty applies only after a
  prior agency/court finding or for malicious, fraudulent, or oppressive conduct;
  penalties are capped at 15% of the maximum if the employer took "all reasonable
  steps" toward compliance before receiving the notice (30% if within 60 days after);
  reduced penalties apply to isolated, short-duration violations and to derivative
  and wage-statement claims; cure mechanisms were expanded (an LWDA process for
  employers under 100 employees, court early-evaluation conferences for larger ones);
  and aggrieved employees now receive 35% of penalties (65% to LWDA). Pre-reform
  penalty math substantially overstates exposure in post-June 2024 cases; pre-reform
  rules still govern earlier-noticed actions.
- **Collective / class actions**: FLSA permits collective actions (opt-in); state
  laws often permit Rule 23 class actions (opt-out). Aggregate exposure across
  hundreds or thousands of affected employees can reach tens of millions.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (Contractor Classification):** Reclassified workers are entitled to
  retroactive minimum wage, overtime, and break protections for the full period
  of misclassification.
- **Employment (At-Will):** Termination in retaliation for filing wage complaints
  violates FLSA 15(a)(3) anti-retaliation provisions and state whistleblower laws.
- **Employment (Employee Benefits):** Non-exempt employees must be compensated for
  mandatory training, meetings, and benefit-enrollment activities.
- **Employment (Discrimination-Harassment):** Equal Pay Act claims often accompany
  FLSA claims. Pay equity audits should address both frameworks simultaneously.
- **Data Privacy:** Biometric time-tracking systems may trigger BIPA (Illinois) or
  state biometric privacy laws requiring notice, consent, and data retention policies.
