## Employee Benefits

# Employee Benefits

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting employment agreements with benefits
provisions, employee benefit plan documents, summary plan descriptions, severance
plans, or any matter involving ERISA compliance, retirement plans, health insurance
obligations, COBRA continuation coverage, ACA employer mandate requirements, or
HIPAA portability and nondiscrimination rules.

## Key Requirements

### ERISA Framework

- **What**: The Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA, 29 USC 1001 et seq.)
  governs employer-sponsored retirement plans (defined benefit, defined contribution,
  401(k), profit-sharing) and welfare benefit plans (health, disability, life
  insurance, severance).
- **Preemption**: ERISA preempts virtually all state laws that "relate to" an employee
  benefit plan. This means state-law contract, tort, and statutory claims for benefit
  denials are displaced, channeling all claims to federal court under ERISA Section 502.
- **Exemptions**: Government plans, church plans (unless they elect coverage), plans
  maintained solely to comply with workers' compensation, unemployment, or disability
  insurance laws, and plans maintained outside the United States for nonresident aliens.
- **Fiduciary duties**: Any person who exercises discretionary authority over plan
  management, administration, or asset disposition is a fiduciary. Fiduciaries must:
  (1) act solely in the interest of participants and beneficiaries, (2) act with the
  care, skill, prudence, and diligence of a prudent person, (3) diversify plan
  investments, and (4) act in accordance with plan documents (to the extent consistent
  with ERISA).
- **Consequence**: Personal liability for plan losses caused by fiduciary breach.
  DOL enforcement actions, participant lawsuits under ERISA 502(a)(2), and equitable
  relief under 502(a)(3). Fiduciary liability cannot be disclaimed, indemnified,
  or insured away by the plan sponsor (though fiduciary liability insurance is
  permissible if the insurer has subrogation rights).

### 401(k) and Retirement Plans

- **Annual contribution limits (2024)**:
  - Employee elective deferral: **$23,000** ($30,500 for age 50+)
  - Employer + employee combined (415 limit): **$69,000** ($76,500 for age 50+)
  - Highly compensated employee (HCE) threshold: earnings above **$155,000**
- **Nondiscrimination testing**: HCEs cannot defer disproportionately more than
  non-HCEs. Actual Deferral Percentage (ADP) test for employee contributions.
  Actual Contribution Percentage (ACP) test for employer matching. Testing is
  required annually unless the plan qualifies for safe harbor status.
- **Safe harbor provisions** (avoids nondiscrimination testing):
  - **Basic match**: 100% of first 3% of compensation + 50% of next 2%
  - **Enhanced match**: At least as generous as basic match (e.g., 100% of first 4%)
  - **Non-elective contribution**: 3% of compensation to all eligible employees
    regardless of whether they contribute
  - Annual safe harbor notice must be provided 30-90 days before plan year start.
    Safe harbor contributions must be **100% immediately vested**.
- **SECURE Act 2.0 (December 2022)**:
  - Mandatory automatic enrollment for new 401(k) and 403(b) plans established
    after December 29, 2022, at 3-10% deferral with annual 1% auto-escalation
    up to 10-15%.
  - Exempt: employers with 10 or fewer employees, businesses less than 3 years old,
    church plans, and government plans.
  - New Roth catch-up requirement: employees with wages exceeding $145,000 must
    make catch-up contributions on a Roth (after-tax) basis only (effective 2026).
- **Consequence**: Nondiscrimination test failure requires corrective distributions
  to HCEs (taxable) or qualified nonelective contributions (QNECs) to non-HCEs.
  Plan disqualification results in loss of tax-deferred status for all participants.
  DOL and IRS audit risk increases with plan asset size.

### ACA Employer Mandate (Employer Shared Responsibility)

- **What**: Applicable large employers (ALEs) must offer minimum essential coverage
  (MEC) that is affordable and provides minimum value (MV) to full-time employees
  (30+ hours/week) and their dependents (children under 26).
- **ALE threshold**: **50+ full-time equivalent employees** in the prior calendar year.
  FTEs are calculated by: (total monthly hours of all part-time employees) / 120,
  added to the count of full-time employees.
- **Penalties (2024)**:
  - **4980H(a)** ("sledgehammer"): If employer fails to offer MEC to at least
    **95% of full-time employees** and any employee receives a premium tax credit:
    **$2,970/year** per full-time employee (minus first 30). For an employer with
    200 full-time employees: up to $504,900 per year.
  - **4980H(b)** ("tack hammer"): If employer offers coverage but it is unaffordable
    (employee cost exceeds **8.39% of household income** in 2024) or does not provide
    minimum value (**60% actuarial value**): **$4,460/year** per employee who
    actually receives a premium tax credit.
- **Reporting**: Forms 1094-C and 1095-C filed annually with the IRS and furnished
  to employees. Late filing penalty: **$310/return** (2024), maximum
  **$3,783,500/year**.
- **Consequence**: ACA penalties are not deductible as business expenses. No penalty
  for failing to cover part-time employees (under 30 hours/week).

### COBRA Continuation Coverage

- **What**: The Consolidated Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act (29 USC 1161 et seq.)
  requires employers with **20+ employees** to offer continuation of group health
  plan coverage to qualified beneficiaries after qualifying events.
- **Qualifying events and continuation periods**:
  - Voluntary or involuntary termination (not gross misconduct) or reduction in
    hours: **18 months**
  - Employee entitled to Medicare: **18 months** for spouse/dependents
  - Divorce or legal separation: **36 months** for spouse/dependents
  - Death of covered employee: **36 months** for dependents
  - Dependent child losing eligibility: **36 months**
  - Social Security disability determination within first 60 days: extends to
    **29 months**
- **Election period**: Qualified beneficiary has **60 days** from the later of the
  qualifying event or the date the COBRA election notice is provided.
- **Premium**: Employer may charge up to **102%** of the full premium cost. For
  disability extension months 19-29: up to **150%**.
- **Notice requirements**: Employer must notify the plan administrator within
  **30 days** of a qualifying event (for employer-triggered events). Plan administrator
  must provide the election notice within **14 days** of receiving notice.
- **Consequence**: Failure to provide timely COBRA notice: excise tax of **$100/day**
  per affected individual (IRC 4980B, up to $200/day if more than one qualified
  beneficiary in the family), statutory penalty of up to **$110/day** per participant
  under ERISA 502(c), and liability for medical expenses incurred during the gap.
- **State mini-COBRA**: Many states extend COBRA-like rights to smaller employers
  and/or provide longer periods: California Cal-COBRA (2-19 employees, 36 months),
  New York (36 months), Connecticut (36 months for employers with 2+), Illinois
  (12 months for under 20 employees), Massachusetts (36 months for under 20).

### HIPAA Portability and Nondiscrimination

- **What**: HIPAA's group health plan provisions prohibit discrimination in enrollment
  eligibility or premiums based on health factors, and ensure portability when
  employees change jobs.
- **Health factors**: Health status, medical condition, claims experience, receipt of
  health care, medical history, genetic information, evidence of insurability,
  disability. Plans may not use these factors to deny enrollment or charge higher
  premiums to individuals.
- **Portability**: Group health plans that impose waiting periods must credit prior
  creditable coverage (reducing or eliminating the waiting period).
- **Consequence**: Violations of HIPAA nondiscrimination: excise tax of **$100/day**
  per affected individual under IRC 4980D, up to **$500,000/year** for failures
  that are not due to reasonable cause.

### FMLA and Benefits Continuation

- **What**: During FMLA leave, employers must maintain group health insurance on the
  same terms as if the employee continued working.
- **Threshold**: Employers with **50+ employees within 75 miles**. Eligible employees:
  12 months of service and 1,250 hours worked in prior 12 months. Up to 12 weeks
  unpaid leave per year (26 weeks for military caregiver leave).
- **Consequence**: Failure to maintain health benefits during FMLA leave violates the
  FMLA and may create independent ERISA claims. The employee must continue paying
  their share of premiums. If the employee does not return from leave (for reasons
  other than a serious health condition or other circumstances beyond control),
  the employer may recover its share of premiums paid during leave.

### Vesting Requirements

- **What**: ERISA mandates minimum vesting schedules for employer contributions to
  qualified retirement plans.
- **Schedules**: (1) **3-year cliff vesting** (0% until 3 years, then 100%), or
  (2) **6-year graded vesting** (20% at year 2, increasing 20%/year to 100% at
  year 6). Safe harbor contributions and employee elective deferrals must be
  **100% immediately vested**. Top-heavy plans must use 3-year cliff or 6-year
  graded (2-year/20% increments).
- **Consequence**: Plans that fail to meet minimum vesting schedules risk
  disqualification. Accelerated vesting is required upon plan termination or
  partial termination. A reduction in force affecting 20%+ of plan participants
  may constitute a partial termination triggering full vesting.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (Severance):** ERISA may govern severance plans if they constitute
  employee welfare benefit plans. COBRA is triggered by all termination events.
- **Employment (Discrimination-Harassment):** ADA requires reasonable accommodation
  in benefits. GINA restricts genetic information use in benefits eligibility.
- **Employment (Wage-and-Hour):** ACA full-time (30 hrs/week) differs from FLSA
  overtime (40 hrs/week). Time in mandatory benefits enrollment may be compensable.
- **Employment (Contractor Classification):** Reclassified workers may claim
  retroactive benefits eligibility. ACA FTE calculations must include them.
- **Data Privacy:** HIPAA privacy and security rules govern group health plan
  administration. Plan sponsors need administrative, technical, and physical safeguards.
- **Corporate (M&A):** Benefits plan succession, COBRA transition, and ACA reporting
  are critical M&A deal items. 401(k) plan mergers require DOL/IRS compliance.
