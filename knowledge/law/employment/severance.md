# Severance and Separation Agreements

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting separation agreements, release-of-claims
provisions, severance plans, reduction-in-force packages, WARN Act compliance, or any
matter involving employee termination with consideration in exchange for a release.
It governs OWBPA requirements for ADEA releases, WARN Act notice obligations, and
tax treatment of severance payments.

## Key Requirements

### OWBPA Release Requirements (Age 40+ Employees)

- **What**: The Older Workers Benefit Protection Act (OWBPA, 29 USC 626(f)) imposes
  mandatory requirements for a "knowing and voluntary" release of Age Discrimination
  in Employment Act (ADEA) claims. A release that fails any single OWBPA requirement
  is void as to ADEA claims — no ratification or cure is possible.
- **Individual termination requirements** (ALL must be satisfied):
  1. Written in a manner calculated to be understood by the individual employee
     (or the average individual eligible to participate)
  2. Specifically refers to rights or claims arising under the ADEA
  3. Does not waive rights or claims that may arise after the date of execution
  4. Supported by consideration beyond anything the employee is already entitled to
  5. Contains written advice to consult with an attorney before signing
  6. **21-day consideration period** before the employee signs
  7. **7-day revocation period** after signing (agreement is not effective until
     this period expires without revocation)
- **Group termination / exit incentive requirements** (all individual requirements
  above, plus):
  - **45-day consideration period** (replaces 21-day period)
  - Written disclosure identifying the **decisional unit** (the group from which
    selections were made), the **eligibility factors** and **time limits** for
    the program, and the **job titles and ages** of all individuals who were
    eligible or selected for the program and those who were not (the "disclosure
    schedule")
- **Consequence**: A release that fails any OWBPA requirement is void and
  unenforceable as to ADEA claims. Under Oubre v. Entergy Operations, 522 U.S. 422
  (1998), the employee may retain the severance payment and still bring an ADEA
  claim. The employer has no "tender back" or "ratification" defense. This makes
  OWBPA compliance a strict, non-negotiable requirement.

### General Release Requirements (Non-ADEA Claims)

- **What**: For claims other than ADEA (Title VII, ADA, state discrimination, breach
  of contract, tort claims), a valid release requires knowing and voluntary consent,
  adequate consideration, and compliance with applicable state law.
- **Totality-of-circumstances factors**: Clarity and specificity of the agreement,
  the employee's education and experience, the time given to consider, whether the
  employee was advised to consult an attorney, adequacy of consideration, whether
  the employee had meaningful input in negotiating terms.
- **Non-waivable claims**: Certain claims cannot be released regardless of the
  agreement: workers' compensation rights, unemployment insurance benefits, FLSA
  claims (in most circuits, without DOL or court approval), prospective discrimination
  claims, rights under NLRA Section 7, and OSHA whistleblower rights.
- **Consequence**: Overly broad or ambiguous releases may be challenged as
  unconscionable or void as against public policy. Courts closely scrutinize
  releases obtained in coercive circumstances (e.g., employee given no time to
  review, told they must sign immediately or lose the offer).

### WARN Act (Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification)

- **What**: Federal WARN (29 USC 2101 et seq.) requires covered employers to provide
  **60 calendar days** advance written notice before plant closings or mass layoffs.
- **Covered employers**: **100+ employees** (excluding part-time workers who average
  fewer than 20 hours/week). Part-time workers are counted only for determining
  triggering thresholds, not for coverage.
- **Triggering events**:
  - **Plant closing**: Shutdown of a single site or operating unit resulting in
    employment loss for **50+ employees** during any 30-day period
  - **Mass layoff**: Employment loss at a single site for **500+ employees**, OR
    **50-499 employees** constituting **33%+ of the active workforce**, during any
    30-day period
  - **90-day aggregation**: Separate layoffs within a 90-day period are aggregated
    and treated as a single event unless the employer demonstrates each was caused
    by a separate and distinct action or cause
- **Notice must be provided to**: (1) affected workers or their union representative,
  (2) the state rapid response dislocated worker unit, and (3) the chief elected
  official of the local government unit.
- **Limited exceptions** (employer bears the burden of proof):
  - **Faltering company**: Actively seeking capital, and giving notice would
    jeopardize the capital-raising effort (plant closings only, not mass layoffs)
  - **Unforeseeable business circumstances**: The closing/layoff is caused by
    business circumstances not reasonably foreseeable at the time notice would
    have been required
  - **Natural disaster**: Flood, earthquake, drought, storm, tidal wave, etc.
  Even under exceptions, the employer must provide as much notice as practicable
  and explain why the full 60 days was not given.
- **Consequence**: Employers who violate WARN owe **back pay and benefits for each
  day of the violation, up to 60 days**. Civil penalty of up to **$500/day**
  payable to the local government (curable by paying employees within 3 weeks of
  the closing/layoff). Attorneys' fees to prevailing plaintiffs. WARN claims are
  frequently brought as class actions.

### State Mini-WARN Acts

- **California (Cal-WARN)**: **75+ employees**; **60-day notice**; covers relocations
  of 100+ miles. Penalties: 60 days of back pay and benefits per employee.
- **New York**: **50+ employees** (at plant or within state); **90-day notice**.
  Penalties: 60 days of back pay plus $500/day to local government.
- **New Jersey**: **100+ employees**; **90-day notice**. Unique: as amended effective
  April 2023, covered mass layoffs require mandatory severance of **1 week of pay per
  full year of service** for every affected employee regardless of notice, plus
  **4 additional weeks** if the required notice is not given.
- **Illinois**: **75+ full-time employees**; **60-day notice**. Applies to mass
  layoffs of 250+ employees or plant closings of 75+.
- **Massachusetts**: **50+ employees**; **90-day notice**. Director of Labor and
  Workforce Development may extend to 1 year for large layoffs.
- **Consequence**: State WARN remedies are cumulative with federal WARN. An employer
  may owe damages under both federal and state law for the same layoff event.

### Tax Treatment of Severance

- **What**: Severance payments are ordinary income subject to federal income tax
  withholding, FICA (Social Security and Medicare), and FUTA.
- **Key authority**: United States v. Quality Stores, Inc., 572 U.S. 141 (2014) —
  the Supreme Court held that severance payments to involuntarily separated employees
  are "wages" for FICA purposes, regardless of payment method (lump sum or
  installments). This resolved a circuit split.
- **Section 409A**: Severance may constitute "deferred compensation" subject to
  IRC 409A timing rules. Payments must comply with one of:
  - **Short-term deferral exception**: Paid by March 15 of the year following the
    year the right to payment is no longer subject to a substantial risk of forfeiture
  - **Two-year separation pay exception**: Total payments do not exceed the lesser
    of (a) 2x the employee's annual compensation, or (b) 2x the 401(a)(17) limit
    ($360,000 in 2026, indexed annually, meaning the exception covers up to
    **$720,000**)
  - **Specified employee delay**: Payments to "specified employees" of publicly
    traded companies triggered by separation from service must be delayed **6 months**
    following separation (or structured to avoid this delay under a permissible
    exception)
- **Consequence**: Section 409A violations trigger **20% additional tax** plus
  premium interest on the employee. Employer withholding obligations attach.
  Structuring severance within the safe harbors is critical.

### Restrictive Covenants in Severance Agreements

- **What**: Separation agreements frequently include or modify non-competition,
  non-solicitation, non-disparagement, confidentiality, and cooperation provisions.
- **NLRA Section 7 limits**: The NLRB's McLaren Macomb decision (2023) held that
  broad non-disparagement and confidentiality provisions in severance agreements
  may violate NLRA Section 7 by chilling employees' rights to engage in concerted
  activity. This applies to non-supervisory employees covered by the NLRA.
- **Status**: McLaren Macomb remains Board precedent, but its enforcement posture has
  softened — the General Counsel memo aggressively interpreting it (GC 23-05) was
  rescinded in February 2025, and the Board majority seated after the late-2025
  quorum restoration may revisit the decision. Treat carve-outs as the safe default
  and check current Board law before relying on aggressive provisions.
- **Consequence**: Severance agreements for non-supervisory employees should include
  carve-outs preserving Section 7 rights (right to discuss wages, working conditions,
  and terms of employment with co-workers, unions, government agencies, and in legal
  proceedings). Overly broad provisions may result in unfair labor practice charges
  and may void the restrictive provisions.

### Cooperation and Non-Disparagement Clauses

- **What**: Cooperation clauses require the departing employee to assist with
  litigation, investigations, and transition. Non-disparagement clauses restrict
  negative statements about the employer.
- **Threshold**: Cooperation clauses should specify reasonable scope (e.g., during
  business hours, with reimbursement of expenses). Non-disparagement must be mutual
  (employer and employee) and must carve out testimony under oath, communications
  with government agencies, and Section 7 protected activity.
- **Consequence**: One-sided non-disparagement (binding only the employee) may be
  unenforceable under McLaren Macomb for non-supervisory employees. SEC Rule 21F-17
  prohibits provisions that impede SEC whistleblower communications.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (At-Will):** Separation agreements formalize the end of at-will
  relationships. Constructive discharge may trigger WARN analysis.
- **Employment (Discrimination-Harassment):** Releases must satisfy OWBPA for ADEA
  claims. Discriminatory selection for layoff may accompany WARN claims.
- **Employment (Non-Competes):** Enhanced severance serves as consideration for
  restrictive covenants in jurisdictions requiring it.
- **Employment (Employee Benefits):** COBRA notice must accompany termination.
  Severance plans may be ERISA-governed. 401(k) distribution rules apply.
- **Corporate (M&A):** Change-of-control severance (golden parachutes) subject to
  IRC 280G: 20% excise tax on "excess parachute payments" exceeding 3x base amount.
- **Litigation:** Release adequacy is frequently litigated. WARN claims are often
  class actions with aggregate exposure in the millions.
