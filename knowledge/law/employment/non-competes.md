# Non-Competition and Post-Employment Restrictive Covenants

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting employment agreements, separation
agreements, equity award agreements, or any document containing non-competition,
non-solicitation, no-hire, or forfeiture-for-competition provisions. It also applies
when evaluating enforceability of existing restrictive covenants, choice-of-law
issues, or compliance with rapidly evolving state and federal restrictions.

## Key Requirements

### Types of Restrictive Covenants

**Non-Competition Agreements**
- **What**: Restrict the employee from working for a competitor or starting a
  competing business for a specified period after termination.
- **Threshold**: Must be reasonable in scope (duration, geography, activity), supported
  by adequate consideration, and necessary to protect a legitimate business interest
  (trade secrets, confidential information, customer relationships, specialized training).
- **Consequence**: Unenforceable covenants may be voided entirely (red pencil states)
  or reformed by the court (blue pencil/reformation states). Some states impose
  penalties on employers that attempt to enforce void non-competes.

**Non-Solicitation Agreements**
- **What**: Restrict solicitation of the employer's customers, clients, or employees.
- **Threshold**: Customer non-solicitation must typically be limited to customers with
  whom the employee had a material relationship during a defined look-back period
  (typically 12-24 months). Employee non-solicitation restricts recruiting former
  colleagues for a competing endeavor.
- **Consequence**: Generally more enforceable than non-competes because narrower. Courts
  in some jurisdictions treat overly broad non-solicitation as de facto non-competes.

**No-Hire / No-Raid Agreements**
- **What**: Restrict hiring or recruiting the employer's current employees.
- **Threshold**: Narrower than non-solicitation — applies to active recruitment, not to
  colleagues who independently seek out the departing employee.
- **Consequence**: Generally enforceable. Must be distinguished from employer-to-employer
  no-poach agreements, which are per se antitrust violations subject to DOJ
  criminal prosecution.

**Forfeiture-for-Competition Provisions**
- **What**: Employee forfeits unvested equity, deferred compensation, or other benefits
  upon engaging in competition.
- **Threshold**: Delaware (under the "employee choice doctrine") treats these as
  permissible conditions on at-will compensation, not restrictive covenants. California,
  Illinois, and Colorado treat them as non-competes subject to the same enforceability
  requirements and restrictions.
- **Consequence**: Jurisdictional analysis is essential. A forfeiture provision valid
  in Delaware may be entirely unenforceable in California.

### FTC Non-Compete Rule

- **What**: The FTC finalized a rule in April 2024 banning most non-competes nationwide.
- **Scope**: Existing non-competes for "senior executives" (earning >$151,164 annually
  AND in policy-making positions) would remain enforceable. No new non-competes
  could be entered for any worker. Does not apply to non-solicitation, NDA, or
  sale-of-business non-competes.
- **Status**: The Northern District of Texas (Ryan v. FTC, August 2024) issued a
  nationwide order setting aside the rule. The Eastern District of Pennsylvania
  (ATS Tree Services) reached the opposite conclusion. As of late 2024, the rule
  is NOT in effect. The matter is on appeal.
- **Consequence**: Counsel must monitor the appellate litigation continuously. If
  ultimately upheld, employers would need to provide notice to current employees
  that existing non-competes (for non-senior-executives) are no longer enforceable.

### Blue Pencil / Reformation Doctrine

- **Blue pencil states** (Texas, Georgia, Wisconsin): Courts may strike overbroad
  portions and enforce the remainder, but only by deleting — not rewriting — language.
- **Reformation states** (Massachusetts, New York, Ohio, Tennessee, Michigan): Courts
  may rewrite the covenant to make it reasonable and then enforce the modified version.
  Employers benefit from reformation but may face a "chilling effect" concern — overly
  broad drafting deters employees even if ultimately reformed.
- **Red pencil states** (Virginia, Nebraska, historically): Courts void the entire
  restriction if any portion is overbroad. No partial enforcement is permitted.
- **Consequence**: In red pencil states, an overbroad non-compete is worse than having
  no restriction. Drafting must be narrowly tailored.

### Choice-of-Law Issues

- **What**: Employers frequently select favorable governing law. Courts evaluate
  whether the chosen law has a substantial relationship to the parties and whether
  application would violate a fundamental policy of the employee's home state.
- **California rule**: California courts will not enforce non-competes against
  California-based employees under any governing law. Bus. & Prof. Code 16600 is a
  fundamental public policy that overrides contractual choice-of-law provisions.
  SB 699 (2024) codifies this and creates a private right of action. Application
  Group v. Hunter Group (1998); Edwards v. Arthur Andersen (2008).
- **Other restrictive states**: Colorado, Washington, and Oregon courts generally
  apply their own restrictive non-compete laws to in-state employees regardless
  of the contractual choice of law.
- **Consequence**: Choice-of-law clauses selecting Delaware, Texas, or other
  non-compete-friendly states will not save a non-compete when the employee resides
  and works in a restrictive state. The employee's work location typically controls.

### Consideration Requirements

- **At hire**: In most states, the offer of employment is sufficient consideration
  for a non-compete signed at the time of hiring.
- **After hire**: Many states require independent consideration beyond continued
  employment. Examples: promotion, raise, bonus, equity grant, access to confidential
  information. Illinois requires 2 years of continued employment as adequate
  consideration unless other independent consideration is provided. Texas requires
  the employer to provide confidential information, specialized training, or
  other enforceable obligations.
- **Consequence**: A non-compete signed mid-employment without new, independent
  consideration is unenforceable in many jurisdictions.

### Notice Requirements

- **Massachusetts**: Must be provided at earlier of formal offer or 10 business
  days before employment start (or at change in terms with garden leave).
- **Oregon**: Must be signed at least 2 weeks before employment start or upon
  bona fide advancement.
- **Washington**: Must disclose terms in writing at or before the time of hire,
  or with independent consideration upon a bona fide change in conditions.
- **Colorado**: Must be provided to the prospective worker before the worker
  accepts the offer of employment.
- **Consequence**: Failure to provide required notice renders the non-compete void
  in these states regardless of all other factors.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (At-Will):** Termination without cause voids non-competes in
  Massachusetts, Washington, and Oregon.
- **Employment (Contractor Classification):** Restrictive covenants for contractors
  may indicate misclassification and face weaker enforcement.
- **Employment (Severance):** Enhanced severance can serve as consideration for new
  or expanded non-competes in separation agreements.
- **Employment (Wage-and-Hour):** Illinois requires minimum $75K compensation;
  Washington $116,593; Colorado $123,750.
- **IP and Technology:** Trade secret protection (DTSA, UTSA) may provide alternative
  protection without non-compete enforceability issues.
- **Corporate (M&A):** Sale-of-business non-competes are broadly enforceable, typically
  allowing 3-5 year restrictions. FTC rule expressly exempts them.
- **Antitrust:** Employer-to-employer no-poach agreements are per se antitrust
  violations subject to DOJ criminal prosecution.
- **Litigation:** Disputes require expedited TRO/preliminary injunction proceedings.
  Forum and choice-of-law analysis are critical threshold issues.
