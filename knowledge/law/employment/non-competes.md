# Non-Competition and Post-Employment Restrictive Covenants

## Overview
Post-employment restrictive covenants — including non-competition, non-solicitation, no-hire, and non-disclosure agreements — restrict a former employee's activities after the employment relationship ends. Enforceability varies dramatically by jurisdiction and is subject to rapidly evolving legal developments, including the FTC's proposed federal ban on non-competes.

## Key Requirements
- **Non-competition agreements:** Restrict the employee from working for a competitor or starting a competing business for a specified period after termination. Must generally be reasonable in scope (duration, geography, activity restricted), supported by adequate consideration, and necessary to protect a legitimate business interest (trade secrets, confidential information, customer relationships, specialized training).
- **Non-solicitation agreements:** Restrict the employee from soliciting the employer's customers, clients, or employees. Generally more enforceable than non-competes because they are narrower in scope. Customer non-solicitation typically must be limited to customers with whom the employee had a relationship.
- **No-hire/no-raid agreements:** Restrict the employee from hiring or recruiting the employer's employees. Narrower than non-solicitation and generally more enforceable.
- **Confidentiality/NDA provisions:** Restrict disclosure and use of confidential information and trade secrets post-employment. Generally enforceable without the same limitations as non-competes, provided the definition of confidential information is reasonable and does not encompass general skills and knowledge.
- **State enforceability spectrum:**
  - **California:** Non-competes are void and unenforceable (Bus. & Prof. Code 16600), with narrow exceptions for sale-of-business contexts. AB 1076 and SB 699 (effective 2024) further prohibit employers from entering into or attempting to enforce non-competes against California employees, even if entered in another state.
  - **Oklahoma, North Dakota:** Non-competes generally unenforceable (similar to California).
  - **Colorado:** Non-competes enforceable only for highly compensated employees (above threshold adjusted annually) and for protection of trade secrets. Notice and disclosure requirements.
  - **Illinois:** Enforceable only for employees earning above a minimum threshold ($75K for non-competes, $45K for non-solicitation as of 2022). Requires adequate consideration (2 years of employment or other consideration).
  - **Massachusetts:** Enforceable with garden leave or other mutually agreed consideration. 12-month maximum duration. Not enforceable against non-exempt employees, undergraduate or graduate students in internships, or employees terminated without cause.
  - **Washington:** Enforceable only above compensation thresholds (adjusted annually). 18-month maximum duration. Void against employees laid off without cause unless garden leave is provided.
  - **Most other states:** Enforce non-competes if reasonable in duration (typically 1-2 years), geographic scope, and activity restricted, supported by adequate consideration and protecting a legitimate business interest.
- **Blue pencil / reformation:** Some states allow courts to modify (blue pencil) overbroad restrictive covenants to make them enforceable. Other states void the entire restriction if any part is overbroad. A few states follow a strict "red pencil" approach.
- **FTC Non-Compete Rule:** The FTC finalized a rule in 2024 banning most non-competes nationwide. Existing non-competes for senior executives (earning >$151,164 and in policy-making positions) remain enforceable, but no new non-competes may be entered. The rule's enforceability has been challenged in federal court; counsel must monitor litigation status.
- **Garden leave:** Employer continues to pay the employee during the restricted period while the employee remains employed but relieved of duties. Provides stronger enforceability in states requiring consideration.

## Common Contract Issues
- Non-competes with unreasonable scope (too broad geographically, too long in duration, or restricting activities beyond actual competitive threat).
- Lack of adequate consideration — in many states, continued employment alone is not adequate consideration for a non-compete signed after the start of employment; additional consideration (promotion, bonus, equity grant) may be required.
- Choice-of-law provisions attempting to apply a non-compete-friendly state's law to employees in restrictive states (e.g., applying Delaware law to California employees) — courts frequently refuse to enforce this.
- Failure to include mandatory notice provisions required by state law (e.g., Massachusetts, Oregon, Washington require advance notice before non-compete takes effect).
- Overly broad definitions of "competitive business" that restrict the employee from working in any capacity at a large company with a small competing division.
- Non-solicitation provisions that effectively function as non-competes (e.g., restricting solicitation of all customers rather than only those with whom the employee had contact).
- Forfeiture-for-competition provisions (forfeit unvested equity upon competition) that may be treated as non-competes in some jurisdictions.
- Failure to tailor restrictions to each employee's role, seniority, and access to confidential information.

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **Employment (At-Will):** Termination without cause may undermine non-compete enforcement in some jurisdictions (Massachusetts voiding non-competes for employees terminated without cause).
- **Employment (Contractor Classification):** Restrictive covenants in independent contractor agreements may be analyzed differently than employee non-competes; overly broad restrictions may indicate misclassification.
- **IP and Technology:** Non-competes often accompany invention assignment and trade secret protection agreements. The intersection of trade secret protection and non-competes determines what residual knowledge an employee can use at a new employer.
- **Corporate (M&A):** Non-competes in the sale-of-business context are generally more enforceable than employment non-competes. M&A transactions typically include seller non-competes as part of purchase consideration. The FTC rule exempts sale-of-business non-competes.
- **Antitrust:** No-poach and wage-fixing agreements between competitors (as opposed to employer-employee non-solicitation) are subject to criminal antitrust enforcement by the DOJ.
- **Litigation:** Non-compete disputes frequently result in expedited TRO/preliminary injunction proceedings requiring rapid response and forum analysis.
