# Immigration and Work Authorization

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting employment agreements for foreign
nationals, sponsoring work visas, conducting I-9 compliance audits, implementing
E-Verify, or any matter involving hiring authorization verification, visa-dependent
employment terms, or immigration-related compliance obligations.

## Key Requirements

### I-9 Employment Eligibility Verification

- **What**: The Immigration Reform and Control Act (IRCA, 8 USC 1324a) requires ALL
  employers to verify the identity and employment authorization of every person hired
  in the United States, regardless of citizenship or national origin, using Form I-9.
- **Timeline**:
  - Section 1 (employee portion): Must be completed by the employee **on or before
    the first day of employment for pay**.
  - Section 2 (employer portion): Must be completed by the employer **within 3
    business days** of the employee's first day of work. The employer must physically
    examine original documents (or use an authorized alternative procedure for
    remote hires under DHS authorization).
- **Acceptable documents**: Employee presents either one List A document (establishing
  both identity and work authorization, e.g., US passport, permanent resident card,
  EAD) OR a combination of one List B document (identity, e.g., driver's license)
  plus one List C document (work authorization, e.g., unrestricted Social Security
  card).
- **Anti-discrimination rule**: Employers must accept any document that appears
  reasonably genuine and relates to the employee. Employers must NOT request specific
  documents, reject facially valid documents, require additional documents beyond
  what is listed, or treat employees differently based on citizenship or national
  origin in the I-9 process (INA Section 274B, 8 USC 1324b).
- **Retention**: I-9 forms must be retained for the longer of **3 years after the
  date of hire** or **1 year after the date employment ends**.
- **Reverification**: Required when an employee's work authorization document expires.
  Must be completed on or before the expiration date. Do NOT reverify US citizens
  or permanent residents, or employees who presented List B identity documents that
  expired.

### I-9 Penalties (Adjusted Annually; 2025 Levels Carried Into 2026)

| Violation | Penalty Range |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Paperwork violations** | **$288 to $2,861** per I-9 form (ICE grades within the range by violation percentage and history) |
| **Knowingly hiring unauthorized worker (first)** | **$716 to $5,724** per worker |
| **Knowingly hiring (second offense)** | **$5,724 to $14,308** per worker |
| **Knowingly hiring (third+ offense)** | **$8,586 to $28,619** per worker |
| **Document fraud (first offense)** | **$590 to $4,730** per document (higher ranges for subsequent offenses) |

- **Indexing note**: These amounts adjust annually for inflation under the Federal
  Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act (8 CFR 274a.10). The 2025 levels carried
  into 2026 because the fall 2025 government shutdown prevented publication of the
  CPI data agencies use to adjust; check the current DHS/ICE schedule before quoting
  figures.
- **Consequence**: ICE Notices of Inspection (I-9 audits) have increased significantly.
  Penalties are assessed per violation (per form), so a company with 500 employees
  and systemic errors across all forms faces exposure approaching **$1.4 million**
  for paperwork violations alone. Criminal penalties apply for pattern or practice
  of knowingly hiring unauthorized workers: up to **$3,000 per worker** and **6 months
  imprisonment** per count.

### E-Verify

- **What**: Electronic system operated by DHS and SSA that compares I-9 information
  against government records to confirm employment eligibility.
- **Mandatory use requirements**:
  - **Federal contractors**: Executive Order 12989 (as amended by E.O. 13465)
    requires E-Verify for federal contracts above **$150,000** with a performance
    period exceeding **120 days** (FAR 52.222-54).
  - **State mandates**: States requiring E-Verify for all or most employers include
    Alabama, Arizona, Georgia, Mississippi, North Carolina, South Carolina,
    Tennessee, and Utah. Requirements vary by employer size and sector.
  - **Voluntary**: All other employers may voluntarily enroll.
- **Timeline**: E-Verify query must be initiated no later than **3 business days**
  after the employee's start date (concurrent with I-9 Section 2 completion).
- **Tentative Nonconfirmation (TNC)**: Employer must NOT terminate or take adverse
  action based on a TNC. Employee has **8 federal workdays** to begin the
  resolution process. Employer must continue employment during resolution.
- **Consequence**: Failure to follow E-Verify procedures can result in loss of federal
  contracts, referral for I-9 violations, and state-specific penalties. Arizona
  mandates license revocation for employers that knowingly employ unauthorized workers
  after E-Verify check (Legal Arizona Workers Act, upheld in Chamber of Commerce v.
  Whiting, 563 U.S. 582 (2011)).

### Common Work Visa Categories

**H-1B (Specialty Occupation)**
- **What**: Permits employment in a "specialty occupation" requiring a bachelor's
  degree or equivalent in a specific field directly related to the position.
- **Wage**: Employer must pay the **higher of the actual wage paid to similarly
  situated workers or the prevailing wage** for the occupation in the area. The
  statutory floor for H-1B dependent employers seeking exemption from displacement
  attestations is **$60,000/year**.
- **Annual cap**: **65,000** regular cap plus **20,000** for beneficiaries with a
  US master's degree or higher (total **85,000**). Cap-exempt employers: universities,
  nonprofit research organizations, government research organizations.
- **Duration**: Initial period up to **3 years**; extendable to a maximum of
  **6 years**. Extensions beyond 6 years available under AC21 Section 106 if a
  PERM labor certification or I-140 petition has been pending or approved for
  **365+ days**.
- **Labor Condition Application (LCA)**: Employer must file LCA with DOL attesting
  to: prevailing wage payment, no adverse effect on working conditions, no
  strike/lockout, and notice to workers (posted at worksite for 10 days or provided
  to bargaining representative).
- **Consequence**: LCA violations: up to **$1,000 per violation** for non-willful
  failures. Willful violations or misrepresentation: up to **$35,000 per violation**
  and **debarment from H-1B program for 2+ years** (statutory base amounts — DOL
  adjusts these annually for inflation; see 20 CFR 655.810). H-1B dependent employers
  (15%+ of workforce are H-1B) face additional displacement and recruitment
  attestation obligations.
- **$100,000 proclamation payment**: A September 2025 presidential proclamation
  conditions certain new H-1B petitions for beneficiaries outside the United States
  on a **$100,000 payment**, with effect from September 21, 2025 (stated 12-month
  duration). USCIS guidance exempts petitions for amendments, extensions, and
  changes of status granted for workers already in the US. Litigation challenging
  the proclamation is pending — verify current status and exemptions before any
  H-1B filing strategy decision.

**L-1 (Intracompany Transferee)**
- **L-1A** (managers/executives): Up to **7 years**. Requires 1 continuous year of
  employment with qualifying related entity abroad within the preceding 3 years.
  No annual cap. May qualify for EB-1C green card without PERM labor certification.
- **L-1B** (specialized knowledge): Up to **5 years**. Same 1-year foreign
  employment requirement. High denial rates due to USCIS restrictive interpretation
  of "specialized knowledge."
- **No prevailing wage requirement** (unlike H-1B).

**O-1 (Extraordinary Ability/Achievement)**
- **What**: For individuals with extraordinary ability in sciences, arts, education,
  business, or athletics (O-1A), or extraordinary achievement in the motion picture
  or television industry (O-1B).
- **Threshold**: Must demonstrate sustained national or international acclaim by
  meeting at least **3 of 8 evidentiary criteria** (major awards, published material,
  scholarly articles, judging, original contributions, high salary, etc.) or
  providing comparable evidence.
- **Duration**: Up to **3 years** initially, with **1-year extensions** without limit.
  No annual cap. No prevailing wage requirement. Requires peer group advisory opinion.

**E-2 (Treaty Investor)**
- **What**: For nationals of treaty countries investing a "substantial" amount of
  capital in a US enterprise.
- **Investment**: No fixed statutory minimum. Must be substantial relative to the
  total enterprise cost. Typical minimum: **$100,000-$200,000** for service
  businesses; higher for capital-intensive enterprises. Investor must have
  operational control and the investment must be at risk.
- **Duration**: Up to **5 years** initially, with unlimited **2-year or 5-year
  extensions** as long as the enterprise is operational.
- **Limitation**: Does not lead directly to permanent residence. Available only to
  nationals of countries with an E-2 treaty with the United States.

**TN (USMCA/NAFTA Professionals)**
- **What**: For Canadian and Mexican citizens in specified professional occupations
  listed in the USMCA agreement (formerly NAFTA).
- **Duration**: Up to **3 years**, renewable indefinitely. Canadian citizens may
  apply directly at the port of entry (no petition required).
- **Limitation**: Limited to the USMCA profession list. Extended stays may raise
  immigrant intent concerns.

### PERM Labor Certification (Green Card Process)

- **What**: Most employment-based green card categories (EB-2, EB-3) require the
  employer to first obtain a PERM labor certification from DOL, demonstrating no
  qualified, willing, and able US workers are available for the position.
- **Recruitment period**: Mandatory recruitment must occur within **30-180 days**
  before PERM filing. Professional positions require at least 2 print advertisements,
  30-day job order, and 3 additional recruitment steps from a prescribed list.
- **Processing timeline**: Prevailing wage determination: **6-12 months**. PERM
  processing: **6-12+ months**. Audit rates are significant (approximately 30-40%
  of filings).
- **Cost allocation**: The employer must bear ALL costs of the PERM process. The
  employer may NOT require the employee to reimburse PERM-related costs, including
  attorney fees, recruitment expenses, and filing fees.
- **Consequence**: Filing errors, misrepresentations, or failure to document
  recruitment may result in denial, supervised recruitment (DOL monitors the process),
  or debarment from the PERM program. Fraudulent filings can result in criminal
  prosecution.

### Immigration-Related Discrimination

- **What**: INA Section 274B (8 USC 1324b) prohibits citizenship status discrimination
  and national origin discrimination in hiring, firing, and recruitment. It also
  prohibits unfair documentary practices and retaliation.
- **Coverage**: **4+ employees** for citizenship discrimination. **4-14 employees**
  for national origin (employers with 15+ employees are covered by Title VII instead).
  Enforced by the DOJ Immigrant and Employee Rights Section (IER).
- **Filing deadline**: Charge must be filed within **180 days** of the discriminatory
  act.
- **Common violations**: Requiring specific documents during I-9 (e.g., demanding a
  green card when a driver's license and Social Security card are presented),
  refusing to hire work-authorized non-citizens, imposing citizenship requirements
  not mandated by law or regulation, and reverifying documents that do not require
  reverification (e.g., US passport).
- **Consequence**: Civil penalties are assessed per individual discriminated against,
  with ranges that escalate for repeat offenses and adjust annually for inflation —
  quote current amounts from 28 CFR 85.5 rather than a static figure. Back pay,
  front pay, attorneys' fees, and injunctive relief also available.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (At-Will):** Employment authorization is a lawful condition of
  employment. Reverification on document expiration is required, but over-verification
  is discriminatory.
- **Employment (Contractor Classification):** I-9 is required only for employees.
  However, companies using staffing agencies must ensure the agency completes I-9
  for assigned workers.
- **Employment (Discrimination-Harassment):** I-9 document abuse violates INA 274B
  and may violate Title VII. National origin discrimination must be carefully
  managed in visa sponsorship decisions.
- **Employment (Wage-and-Hour):** H-1B LCA wage requirements may exceed minimum wage.
  Employers must pay H-1B workers the full required wage even during non-productive
  time ("benching" is prohibited).
- **Corporate (M&A):** Corporate restructuring may require amended or new H-1B
  petitions. Successor-in-interest rules determine whether employees need new
  visa petitions after an acquisition.
- **Data Privacy:** I-9 and visa documents contain sensitive PII requiring secure
  storage, limited access, and proper destruction after retention period.
