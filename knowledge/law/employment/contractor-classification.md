# Independent Contractor Classification

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting independent contractor agreements,
consulting agreements, freelance arrangements, staffing agency agreements, or any
matter involving worker classification disputes. It governs the distinction between
employees and independent contractors for purposes of tax, benefits, labor
protections, and liability.

## Key Requirements

### Three Primary Classification Tests

**1. ABC Test (California AB5, Massachusetts, New Jersey, Illinois, and others)**

- **What**: Presumes the worker is an employee unless the hiring entity proves ALL
  three prongs:
  - (A) The worker is free from control and direction in performing the work, both
    under the contract and in fact.
  - (B) The work performed is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business.
  - (C) The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade,
    occupation, or business of the same nature.
- **Threshold**: Prong B is the most restrictive and most frequently failed — a
  software company hiring a freelance developer will likely fail Prong B because
  software development is within its usual course of business. All three prongs must
  be satisfied simultaneously. The burden is on the hiring entity.
- **Consequence**: Failure on any single prong means the worker is an employee.
  California AB5 (Labor Code 2775, effective January 1, 2020) applies this test for
  wage orders, the Labor Code, unemployment insurance, and workers' compensation.
  California PAGA penalties reach up to $200 per pay period per worker, subject to
  the June 2024 PAGA reform's standing rules, caps, and cure provisions (see
  wage-and-hour). Massachusetts
  imposes treble damages for misclassification (Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 149, 148B).
  New Jersey penalties include $250 per misclassified worker for first violation.
- **Exemptions**: California AB5 includes professional exemptions for certain
  occupations (lawyers, doctors, accountants, real estate agents, direct sales) that
  revert to the Borello multi-factor test. California Proposition 22 (2020) exempts
  app-based transportation and delivery platform workers from AB5, creating a third
  category with minimum earnings guarantees (120% of local minimum wage plus
  $0.30/mile for engaged driving time).

**2. Economic Reality Test (DOL / FLSA)**

- **What**: Six-factor totality-of-circumstances analysis determining whether the
  worker is economically dependent on the employer (employee) or in business for
  themselves (independent contractor).
- **Factors**: (1) Opportunity for profit or loss depending on managerial skill,
  (2) investments by the worker and the employer, (3) degree of permanence of the
  work relationship, (4) nature and degree of employer control, (5) extent the work
  is an integral part of the employer's business, (6) worker's skill and initiative.
  No single factor is determinative; the totality controls.
- **Threshold**: The DOL 2024 final rule (effective March 11, 2024) restored this
  multi-factor totality-of-circumstances test, replacing the 2021 rule that gave
  disproportionate weight to control and opportunity for profit/loss. Economic
  dependence, not control alone, is the touchstone.
- **Status (in flux)**: The 2024 rule remains on the books and applies in private
  litigation, but DOL announced in Field Assistance Bulletin 2025-1 (May 2025) that
  investigators will not apply it in enforcement, reverting to the longstanding
  Fact Sheet #13 framework. In February 2026 DOL proposed rescinding the 2024 rule
  in favor of a 2021-style test centered on control and opportunity for profit or
  loss. Verify the operative test before advising.
- **Consequence**: Misclassified workers are entitled to FLSA minimum wage ($7.25/hour
  federal; higher in most states), overtime (1.5x for hours over 40/week), and
  recordkeeping protections. Liquidated damages (double back pay) plus attorneys'
  fees. Statute of limitations: 2 years (3 years if willful).

**3. Common Law (Right-to-Control) Test (IRS / Federal Tax)**

- **What**: Examines three categories of evidence — behavioral control, financial
  control, and type of relationship — to determine whether the hiring entity has
  the right to control how the worker performs their services.
- **Behavioral control**: Does the company dictate when, where, and how work is done?
  Does it provide training? Does it set the sequence of work?
- **Financial control**: Does the worker have unreimbursed business expenses? Does the
  worker invest in their own equipment? Can the worker realize a profit or suffer
  a loss? Does the worker market services to others? How is the worker paid
  (by project vs. by hour/salary)?
- **Type of relationship**: Is there a written contract? Are benefits provided? Is the
  relationship permanent or project-based? Is the work a key aspect of the business?
- **Threshold**: No single factor is determinative. The IRS applies the totality of
  circumstances. Form SS-8 may be filed by either party for an IRS determination.
- **Consequence**: Misclassification triggers liability for the employer's share of
  FICA (7.65%), plus back withholding. Under IRC 3509, reduced penalties apply if
  Form 1099 was filed: 20% of employee's FICA share plus 1.5% of wages for income
  tax. Without 1099 filing, full 100% liability under IRC 3509(b).

### Penalties for Misclassification

- **Federal tax**: Employer liable for employer's FICA share (7.65%), plus employee
  FICA share (reduced rates under IRC 3509 if 1099 filed), plus income tax
  withholding. Total exposure can reach 30%+ of total compensation paid.
- **Willful misclassification**: Per-return information-return penalties for each
  Form W-2 that should have been filed (IRC 6721; indexed annually). Full 100% of
  un-withheld taxes. Criminal penalties under
  IRC 7202: up to $10,000 fine and 5 years imprisonment per occurrence for willful
  failure to collect or pay employment taxes.
- **State penalties**: California AB5 — penalties of $5,000-$25,000 per violation
  under Labor Code 226.8 for willful misclassification. Massachusetts — treble
  damages. New York — up to $50,000 for knowing misclassification. New Jersey —
  $250 per worker first offense, additional penalties for repeat violations.
- **Back benefits**: Misclassified workers may claim retroactive entitlement to health
  insurance, retirement plans, paid leave, workers' compensation, and unemployment
  insurance for the entire period of misclassification.
- **Joint liability**: Staffing agencies and client companies may face joint employer
  liability. Multiple states impose joint and several liability for misclassification
  by staffing intermediaries. For NLRA purposes, the NLRB's February 2026 rule
  formally withdrew the vacated 2023 joint-employer standard and reinstated the 2020
  rule, which requires substantial direct and immediate control over essential terms
  of employment.

### Section 530 Safe Harbor

- **What**: Provides relief from federal employment tax liability if the employer had
  a "reasonable basis" for treating workers as independent contractors and met
  reporting and consistency requirements.
- **Three requirements (all must be met)**:
  1. **Reasonable basis**: Judicial precedent, published IRS ruling, past IRS audit
     that did not reclassify similar workers, or long-standing recognized practice
     in the industry.
  2. **Substantive consistency**: Treated all workers in substantially similar positions
     the same way (all as contractors or all as employees).
  3. **Reporting consistency**: Filed all required Form 1099-NEC for the workers.
- **Consequence**: If all three requirements are met, the employer is not liable for
  federal employment taxes for that class of workers, even if later determined to be
  employees. However, Section 530 does NOT protect against state-law claims, FLSA
  claims, benefits claims, or discrimination claims.

### IRS Voluntary Classification Settlement Program (VCSP)

- **What**: Allows employers to voluntarily reclassify workers as employees with
  substantially reduced tax liability for past misclassification.
- **Eligibility**: Must not be under IRS employment tax audit. Must have consistently
  treated workers as contractors. Must have filed all required 1099 forms. Apply
  using Form 8952.
- **Consequence**: Pay only 10% of the employment tax liability for the most recent
  year, with no penalties, no interest, and no audits of prior years for that
  worker class. Employer must treat workers as employees prospectively.

### Gig Economy and Platform Workers

- **What**: Platform-based workers (rideshare, delivery, freelance marketplaces)
  are subject to intensive classification scrutiny across all three tests.
- **California Proposition 22**: Exempts app-based transportation and delivery workers
  from AB5, creating a hybrid category with minimum earnings guarantees (120% of
  local minimum wage for engaged time), healthcare stipends, and accident insurance.
  The California Supreme Court upheld Proposition 22 in 2024 (Castellanos v. State).
- **Federal status**: No comprehensive federal legislation addressing gig worker
  classification. The DOL 2024 rule applies the economic reality test to platform
  workers without a platform-specific carve-out.
- **Consequence**: A single platform's business model may result in different worker
  classifications across different states and under different legal regimes.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (At-Will):** Reclassified workers gain at-will employment protections
  and wrongful termination rights retroactively.
- **Employment (Non-Competes):** Non-compete enforcement for contractors may be
  weaker; overly broad restrictions may indicate misclassification.
- **Employment (Wage-and-Hour):** Reclassified workers are entitled to FLSA and state
  overtime, minimum wage, and break requirements retroactively.
- **Employment (Discrimination-Harassment):** Reclassified workers gain Title VII, ADA,
  and ADEA protections. Contractor headcount may affect coverage thresholds.
- **IP and Technology:** Copyright work-for-hire differs for employees (automatic for
  scope-of-employment works) and contractors (requires written agreement and qualifying
  work category under 17 USC 101).
- **Corporate:** Contractor headcount affects regulatory thresholds (50+ FTEs for ACA,
  100+ for EEO-1, WARN Act).
- **Data Privacy:** Classification affects GDPR data processing roles and access
  control requirements.
