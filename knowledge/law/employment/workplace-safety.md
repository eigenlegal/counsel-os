## Workplace Safety

# Workplace Safety

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting workplace safety policies, employment
agreements with safety provisions, vendor/contractor safety requirements, or any
matter involving OSHA compliance, workplace injuries, recordkeeping obligations,
reporting requirements, or whistleblower retaliation claims related to safety concerns.

## Key Requirements

### OSHA General Duty Clause

- **What**: Section 5(a)(1) of the Occupational Safety and Health Act (29 USC 654(a)(1))
  requires each employer to furnish a place of employment free from recognized hazards
  that are causing or are likely to cause death or serious physical harm to employees.
- **Coverage**: Virtually all private-sector employers with **1+ employees**. Does not
  cover self-employed individuals, family farm workers employing only immediate family,
  or workplaces regulated exclusively by other federal agencies (mining under MSHA,
  nuclear under NRC, transportation under DOT/FAA/FRA).
- **State and local government employees**: Covered only in states with OSHA-approved
  state plans (28 states and territories operate approved plans).
- **"Recognized hazard" standard**: The hazard must be known to the employer or
  recognized by the employer's industry. Evidence includes: industry consensus
  standards (ANSI, NFPA), employer's own safety manuals and policies, prior incidents
  or near-misses at the same workplace, employee complaints, expert testimony, and
  common knowledge among safety professionals.
- **Consequence**: Citations under the General Duty Clause carry the same penalty
  structure as specific-standard violations. Compliance with specific OSHA standards
  is not a defense if additional recognized hazards exist beyond those standards.

### OSHA Penalties (2024 Adjusted Amounts)

| Violation Type | Penalty Range |
|---------------|---------------|
| **Serious** (substantial probability of death/serious harm; employer knew or should have known) | Up to **$16,131** per violation |
| **Other-than-serious** (related to safety/health but unlikely to cause death/serious harm) | Up to **$16,131** per violation |
| **Willful** (intentional disregard or plain indifference to the law) | **$11,524** minimum to **$161,323** maximum per violation |
| **Repeat** (substantially similar violation within prior 5 years) | Up to **$161,323** per violation |
| **Failure to abate** (not corrected by abatement date) | Up to **$16,131 per day** beyond the abatement date |
| **Posting requirement** (failure to post OSHA poster or citations) | Up to **$16,131** |

- **Penalty adjustment**: OSHA adjusts penalty amounts annually for inflation under
  the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act Improvements Act of 2015.
  The amounts above are the 2024 adjusted figures.
- **Penalty factors**: OSHA considers gravity of the violation, employer size (fewer
  employees may reduce penalties), good faith efforts, and history of violations.
- **Criminal penalties**: Willful violation causing death of an employee: up to
  **$250,000** (individual) or **$500,000** (organization) and up to **6 months
  imprisonment** (first offense). Doubled for repeat offenses. Making false
  statements in OSHA records: up to **$10,000** and **6 months imprisonment**
  (18 USC 1001). Advance notice of an OSHA inspection to the employer: up to
  **$1,000** and **6 months imprisonment** (29 USC 666(f)).

### Recordkeeping Requirements (OSHA 300 Log)

- **What**: Employers must maintain records of work-related injuries and illnesses
  on OSHA Forms 300 (Log of Work-Related Injuries and Illnesses), 300A (Summary),
  and 301 (Injury and Illness Incident Report).
- **Coverage threshold**: Required for employers with **10+ employees** at any time
  during the prior calendar year. Certain low-hazard industries (e.g., retail, finance,
  insurance, real estate) are partially exempt from routine recordkeeping but must
  still report fatalities and severe injuries.
- **Electronic submission**: Establishments with **250+ employees** in industries
  covered by the recordkeeping regulation must electronically submit Form 300A data
  to OSHA annually. Establishments with **20-249 employees** in designated
  high-hazard industries must also submit Form 300A data electronically.
- **Recordable criteria**: An injury or illness is recordable if it is work-related
  and results in: (1) death, (2) days away from work, (3) restricted work activity
  or job transfer, (4) medical treatment beyond first aid, (5) loss of consciousness,
  or (6) a significant injury/illness diagnosed by a physician or licensed healthcare
  professional (e.g., cancer, chronic irreversible disease, fractured bone, punctured
  eardrum).
- **Retention**: Records must be maintained for **5 years** following the end of the
  calendar year they cover. Employees and their representatives have the right to
  access the 300 Log and 301 forms.
- **Annual posting**: Form 300A (Annual Summary) must be posted in a conspicuous
  location from **February 1 through April 30** of each year.
- **Consequence**: Failure to maintain or post: up to **$16,131** per violation.
  Intentional falsification of records: criminal prosecution under 18 USC 1001.
  OSHA has increasingly used recordkeeping violations as a basis for programmed
  inspections and willful citations.

### Reporting Requirements

- **Fatality**: Must be reported to OSHA within **8 hours** of the employer learning
  of the death. Applies to any work-related fatality, including those occurring within
  30 days of the incident.
- **In-patient hospitalization**: Must be reported within **24 hours** of the
  employer's knowledge. Applies to hospitalization for treatment, not just observation.
- **Amputation**: Must be reported within **24 hours**. Includes amputations,
  including fingertip amputations with or without bone loss.
- **Loss of an eye**: Must be reported within **24 hours**.
- **How to report**: Telephone to the nearest OSHA Area Office, the OSHA 24-hour
  hotline (1-800-321-OSHA / 1-800-321-6742), or online at www.osha.gov. The report
  must include business name, names of affected employees, location, time, brief
  description, and contact person.
- **Consequence**: Failure to report: up to **$161,323** if classified as willful,
  or **$16,131** for other-than-serious violations. Late reporting is one of the
  most common bases for OSHA citations.

### OSHA Inspections

- **Priorities** (in order): (1) imminent danger situations, (2) fatality/catastrophe
  investigations, (3) employee complaints and referrals, (4) programmed/planned
  inspections targeting high-hazard industries.
- **Employer rights**: Employer may require an inspection warrant (Marshall v.
  Barlow's, Inc., 436 U.S. 307 (1978)). However, requesting a warrant may escalate
  the inspection scope and does not prevent OSHA from obtaining the warrant.
  Employer has the right to participate in the opening conference, accompany the
  compliance officer during the walk-around, and attend the closing conference.
- **Employee rights**: Employees may file confidential complaints triggering an
  inspection. An authorized employee representative has the right to participate
  in the walk-around inspection. Employers may not retaliate against employees who
  file complaints, participate in inspections, or exercise any right under the Act.
- **Contesting citations**: Employer must file a Notice of Contest within **15
  working days** of receiving the citation and proposed penalty. Failure to timely
  contest makes the citation final and unappealable. Contested cases are heard by
  the Occupational Safety and Health Review Commission (OSHRC).
- **Consequence**: An uncontested citation becomes a final order of OSHRC.
  Subsequent violations of the same or substantially similar standard within 5 years
  may be classified as "repeat" violations with penalties up to $161,323.

### Whistleblower Protection — Section 11(c)

- **What**: Section 11(c) of the OSH Act (29 USC 660(c)) prohibits any person from
  discharging or discriminating against an employee because the employee exercised
  rights under the Act.
- **Filing deadline**: **30 days** from the adverse retaliatory action. This is one
  of the shortest filing periods in all of employment law.
- **Protected activities**: Filing an OSHA complaint or requesting an inspection.
  Reporting a workplace injury or illness. Refusing to perform work that the employee
  reasonably believes poses an imminent danger of death or serious injury (and there
  is no reasonable alternative and insufficient time to address through regular
  channels). Participating in an OSHA inspection. Exercising any right under the Act,
  including requesting access to OSHA records and exposure/medical records.
- **Enforcement**: OSHA investigates and, if it finds merit, files suit on the
  employee's behalf in federal district court. There is no private right of action
  under Section 11(c) — the employee cannot sue independently; the claim must go
  through OSHA.
- **Remedies**: Reinstatement to former position, back pay with interest,
  compensatory damages, and reasonable attorneys' fees.
- **Consequence**: The 30-day window is strictly enforced. Employees who miss it
  lose their Section 11(c) claim, though state whistleblower laws (with longer
  filing periods) may provide alternative remedies.

### Frequently Cited OSHA Standards

- **Hazard Communication (29 CFR 1910.1200)**: Most frequently cited. Requires
  Safety Data Sheets (SDS), container labeling, and employee training for
  hazardous chemicals.
- **Fall Protection (29 CFR 1926.501)**: Construction: protection at **6+ feet**.
  General industry (29 CFR 1910.28): protection at **4+ feet**.
- **Scaffolding (29 CFR 1926.451)**: Requirements for scaffold design, erection,
  and use in construction.
- **Respiratory Protection (29 CFR 1910.134)**: Medical evaluation, fit testing,
  training required before employees use respirators.
- **Lockout/Tagout (29 CFR 1910.147)**: Procedures for controlling hazardous energy
  during equipment servicing and maintenance.
- **Ladders (29 CFR 1926.1053)**: Requirements for ladder use, design, and condition.

### State OSHA Plans

- **What**: **22 states and territories** operate OSHA-approved state plans covering
  both private-sector and state/local government employees. An additional **6 states**
  cover only state and local government employees.
- **Requirement**: State plans must be "at least as effective as" federal OSHA.
  Many states impose stricter requirements. California Cal/OSHA has additional
  standards for heat illness prevention (outdoor and indoor), injury and illness
  prevention programs (IIPP, mandatory for all employers), and aerosol transmissible
  diseases.
- **Federal OSHA monitoring**: Federal OSHA retains authority to monitor state plans,
  conduct federal enforcement if the state fails to act, and revoke state plan
  approval.
- **Consequence**: In state-plan states, the state agency (not federal OSHA) conducts
  inspections and enforces standards. Employers operating in multiple states must
  comply with whichever standard (federal or state) is more protective.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (At-Will):** Terminating an employee for reporting safety concerns
  violates Section 11(c) and state whistleblower statutes regardless of at-will.
- **Employment (Contractor Classification):** Multi-employer worksite doctrine creates
  obligations for host employers, controlling employers, and exposing employers
  regarding contractor worker safety.
- **Employment (Wage-and-Hour):** Time spent on mandatory safety training is
  compensable under FLSA. Unpaid safety training may create wage-hour violations.
- **Employment (Discrimination-Harassment):** Safety-related retaliation may overlap
  with Title VII or state anti-retaliation protections.
- **Data Privacy:** OSHA recordkeeping and electronic reporting involve employee
  medical information subject to confidentiality requirements.
- **Litigation:** OSHA citations are not per se evidence of negligence in tort
  litigation but may be admitted as evidence of the standard of care. Workers'
  compensation generally provides the exclusive remedy, with exceptions for
  intentional employer misconduct in some states.
