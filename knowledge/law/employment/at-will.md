## At Will

# At-Will Employment

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting employment agreements, offer letters,
termination provisions, employee handbooks, separation agreements, or any matter
involving wrongful discharge claims. It governs the default termination framework
in all US states except Montana.

## Key Requirements

### Default Rule

- **What**: At-will employment means either party may terminate the relationship at any
  time, for any lawful reason or no reason, with or without notice.
- **Threshold**: Applies in 49 of 50 US states plus the District of Columbia.
  Montana is the sole exception.
- **Consequence**: Employers that fail to establish clear at-will status risk
  implied-contract claims that convert the relationship to termination-for-cause-only.

### Three Exception Categories

**1. Implied Contract Exception (recognized in ~38 states)**

- **What**: Employer statements, policies, or conduct can create an implied promise of
  continued employment or termination-only-for-cause, overriding at-will status.
- **Threshold**: Any written or oral representation suggesting job security, guaranteed
  duration, or progressive discipline before termination can trigger this exception.
  Employee handbooks are the most common source of implied contract claims.
- **Consequence**: Employee may sue for breach of implied contract. Damages typically
  include lost wages, lost benefits, and potentially front pay. Juries frequently
  award substantial damages where the employee relied on specific handbook promises.
- **Key states**: California (Foley v. Interactive Data Corp., 1988), Michigan
  (Toussaint v. Blue Cross, 1980), Illinois, New Jersey, and Arizona broadly
  recognize implied contract claims. New York recognizes this exception only for
  express limitations on at-will status, not general handbook provisions.
- **Risk factors**: Handbook progressive discipline policies, orientation materials
  promising "permanent" employment, supervisor emails referencing job security,
  practices of always following progressive discipline before termination.

**2. Public Policy Exception (recognized in ~44 states)**

- **What**: Termination that violates a clear mandate of public policy is actionable
  as wrongful discharge in tort, regardless of at-will status.
- **Threshold**: Employee must show termination was motivated by one of four categories:
  (a) refusal to commit an illegal act (e.g., perjury, fraud, safety violations),
  (b) exercise of a statutory right (e.g., filing workers' comp claim, voting),
  (c) performance of a public obligation (e.g., jury duty, military service), or
  (d) reporting illegal employer conduct (whistleblowing).
- **Consequence**: Tort damages including compensatory damages, emotional distress,
  and potentially punitive damages. Broader remedy than contract claims and not
  limited to lost wages. Statutes of limitation for tort claims vary by state
  (typically 1-3 years).
- **California breadth**: California recognizes all four categories broadly under
  Tameny v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (1980). Public policy sources include statutes,
  constitutional provisions, administrative regulations, and judicial decisions.
  This is one of the broadest public policy exceptions in the nation.
- **States not recognizing**: Georgia, Louisiana, Rhode Island, and Maine do not
  recognize a general public policy exception. Florida and New York recognize it only
  in narrow statutory contexts, relying instead on specific whistleblower statutes.

**3. Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing (recognized in ~11 states)**

- **What**: An implied covenant prevents termination made in bad faith, typically
  to deprive the employee of compensation already substantially earned.
- **Threshold**: Requires showing the employer terminated the employee specifically to
  avoid paying commissions, bonuses, pensions, or other accrued benefits that were
  substantially earned. This is the narrowest of the three exceptions.
- **Consequence**: Contract damages in most states. Some jurisdictions (Montana, Alaska)
  allow tort damages including punitive damages for bad-faith discharge.
- **Key states**: California (narrow — Fortune v. National Cash Register Co. principle,
  limited to preventing employers from terminating to deny already-earned compensation),
  Massachusetts (Fortune v. National Cash Register Co., 1977), Alaska (Mitford v.
  de Lasala, 1983), Arizona, Delaware, Idaho, Montana, Nevada, Utah, Wyoming.

### Montana Wrongful Discharge from Employment Act (WDEA)

- **What**: Montana is the only state requiring "good cause" for termination after a
  probationary period. Good cause means reasonable job-related grounds for dismissal
  based on failure to satisfactorily perform, disruption of operations, or other
  legitimate business reason.
- **Probationary period**: Defaults to 6 months unless extended by written agreement.
  Some positions may have up to 12 months. During probation, at-will rules apply.
- **Consequence**: Wrongful discharge damages capped at 4 years of lost wages and
  benefits. Punitive damages available only if employer acted with actual fraud or
  malice. Claim must be filed within 1 year of discharge or within 1 year of
  exhausting internal remedies. Jury trial is available.
- **Practical impact**: Montana employers must document cause before terminating
  post-probationary employees. No other state has enacted a comparable statute,
  though similar proposals have been introduced in multiple state legislatures.

### Handbook as Contract Risk

- **What**: Employee handbooks containing progressive discipline policies, termination
  procedures, or benefit promises can create binding contractual obligations.
- **Threshold**: Handbooks without conspicuous, prominently placed disclaimers are
  at risk in the ~38 states recognizing implied contract claims. Disclaimers must be
  consistent throughout — a disclaimer on page 1 can be undermined by specific
  promises on page 30.
- **Consequence**: Courts in California (Guz v. Bechtel National, 2000), Michigan
  (Toussaint, 1980), and other states have held handbook provisions create enforceable
  implied contracts. Once an implied contract is found, the employer must show cause
  for termination.
- **Best practice requirements**:
  1. Prominent at-will disclaimer on the acknowledgment signature page
  2. Separate at-will disclaimer within the body of the handbook
  3. Statement that the handbook does not create a contract of employment
  4. Reservation of the right to modify, revoke, or supplement any policy at any time
  5. Progressive discipline policies explicitly reserving the right to skip steps
  6. Avoidance of terms like "permanent employee," "job security," or "guaranteed"
  7. Annual re-acknowledgment by all employees

### Constructive Discharge

- **What**: If an employer makes working conditions so intolerable that a reasonable
  person would feel compelled to resign, the resignation is treated as an involuntary
  termination.
- **Threshold**: Conditions must be objectively intolerable under a "reasonable person"
  standard. Mere dissatisfaction, disagreement with management, or a single unpleasant
  event is generally insufficient. Factors: severity and pervasiveness of conditions,
  duration, whether employer was notified and failed to correct.
- **Consequence**: Triggers all claims available for involuntary termination, including
  wrongful discharge, discrimination, retaliation, and may activate severance
  provisions tied to involuntary termination. Also relevant for WARN Act analysis
  if part of a mass departure.

### Documentation Requirements

- **What**: While at-will employers are not legally required to document reasons for
  termination, the absence of documentation creates significant litigation risk.
- **Threshold**: An undocumented termination of an employee in a protected class, or
  who recently engaged in protected activity, creates a strong inference of pretext.
- **Consequence**: Undocumented terminations are substantially more difficult to defend.
  Contemporaneous documentation of performance issues, warnings, and the legitimate
  business reason for termination is the single most effective defense to wrongful
  termination claims.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (Non-Competes):** At-will termination without cause may undermine
  non-compete enforcement in Massachusetts, Washington, and Oregon.
- **Employment (Contractor Classification):** Reclassified workers gain at-will
  protections and wrongful termination rights retroactively.
- **Employment (Severance):** Separation agreements must comply with OWBPA for
  employees aged 40+. Constructive discharge may trigger WARN Act analysis.
- **Employment (Discrimination-Harassment):** At-will status does not authorize
  discriminatory termination. Every firing must be evaluated against anti-discrimination
  statutes regardless of at-will status.
- **Corporate (Governance):** Officer and director termination must follow corporate
  governance requirements (board approval, charter/bylaws) in addition to employment law.
- **Litigation:** Wrongful termination claims are the most common employment litigation.
  Proper documentation of at-will status and legitimate reasons is essential for defense.
