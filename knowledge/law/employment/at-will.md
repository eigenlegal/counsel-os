# At-Will Employment

## Overview
At-will employment is the default employment relationship in all US states except Montana, meaning either the employer or the employee may terminate the relationship at any time, for any lawful reason (or no reason), with or without notice. However, numerous statutory, contractual, and common-law exceptions significantly limit the at-will doctrine in practice.

## Key Requirements
- **Default rule:** Unless modified by contract, statute, or recognized common-law exception, employment is at-will. Employers should include clear at-will language in offer letters, employment agreements, and employee handbooks.
- **Statutory exceptions:** Federal and state anti-discrimination laws (Title VII, ADA, ADEA, state equivalents) prohibit termination based on protected characteristics. Whistleblower protection statutes prohibit retaliation for reporting illegal activity. FMLA, USERRA, and similar laws prohibit termination for exercising protected leave rights.
- **Contractual exceptions:** Express employment contracts specifying a term or requiring "cause" for termination override at-will status. Implied contracts may arise from handbook provisions, oral representations, or course of dealing. Severance agreements and separation agreements modify termination terms.
- **Public policy exception:** Most states recognize wrongful discharge claims when termination violates a clear public policy (e.g., termination for refusing to commit an illegal act, for exercising a statutory right, or for performing a public obligation like jury duty). Not recognized in a minority of states.
- **Implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing:** A minority of states (notably California in limited circumstances, Massachusetts) recognize a covenant of good faith limiting at-will termination, though the scope varies significantly.
- **Montana Wrongful Discharge from Employment Act (WDEA):** Montana is the only state that requires "good cause" for termination after a probationary period. Good cause includes reasonable job-related grounds for dismissal.
- **Offer letters and employment agreements:** Must clearly state at-will status if intended. Any language suggesting job security, guaranteed employment duration, or termination-only-for-cause can create an implied contract modifying at-will status.
- **Handbook disclaimers:** Employee handbooks should contain conspicuous disclaimers stating that the handbook does not create a contract and does not modify at-will status. Progressive discipline policies should explicitly reserve the right to skip steps and terminate at will.

## Common Contract Issues
- Offer letters or employment agreements with ambiguous at-will language or language that could be read to imply job security.
- Handbooks that contain progressive discipline policies without adequate at-will disclaimers, potentially creating implied contracts.
- Verbal representations during hiring that contradict written at-will provisions (oral promises of job security or guaranteed employment).
- Severance provisions that are triggered by termination "without cause" but fail to define "cause" clearly, leading to disputes.
- Change-of-control provisions that provide enhanced severance or accelerated vesting upon termination, requiring clear definitions of triggering events.
- Release and waiver provisions in separation agreements that fail to meet ADEA/OWBPA requirements for employees aged 40+.
- Garden leave provisions that extend the employment relationship beyond the practical termination date, affecting compensation, benefits, and restrictive covenant timing.
- Failure to document performance issues before terminating an at-will employee, creating inference of pretextual (discriminatory or retaliatory) termination.

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **Employment (Non-Competes):** At-will termination may affect enforceability of post-employment restrictive covenants in some jurisdictions — termination without cause may undermine enforcement of non-competes.
- **Employment (Contractor Classification):** Misclassified workers who are re-characterized as employees will be subject to at-will rules and entitled to employment protections and benefits.
- **Corporate (Governance):** Officer and director termination must follow corporate governance requirements (board approval) in addition to employment law considerations.
- **Litigation:** Wrongful termination claims are among the most common employment litigation matters; proper documentation of at-will status and termination reasons is essential for defense.
- **Securities:** Executive employment agreements with public companies must comply with SEC disclosure requirements for named executive officers.
