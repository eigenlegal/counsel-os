# State Consumer Protection Laws

## Applicability

This sub-file applies whenever a consumer-facing business operates in specific states, state AG enforcement is a concern, state-specific consumer rights are at issue, treble damages or fee-shifting exposure exists, multi-state consumer class action risk is present, or choice-of-law and forum selection clauses in consumer contracts need evaluation. Also load when assessing whether a contract provision may be unenforceable under state unconscionability doctrines.

## Key Requirements

### California — Unfair Competition Law (UCL, Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200)

- **What**: Prohibits any "unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent" business act or practice and any "unfair, deceptive, untrue, or misleading" advertising. The UCL has an extremely broad "unlawful" prong — any violation of any law can serve as the predicate for a UCL claim.
- **Threshold**: Post-Proposition 64, private standing requires injury in fact and "lost money or property" as a result of the unfair competition (Kwikset Corp. v. Superior Court (2011)). Representative claims must proceed as class actions, though only the named plaintiff must establish standing and — for misrepresentation-based claims — actual reliance (In re Tobacco II Cases (2009)). No intent to deceive is required. The "unfair" prong under Cel-Tech (when competitors sue) requires the practice threaten an incipient antitrust violation or violate the spirit of an antitrust law; for consumers, courts apply a balancing test.
- **Consequence**: Private remedies are equitable only — restitution and injunctive relief; no damages and no nonrestitutionary disgorgement of profits (Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003)). No attorney's fees under the UCL itself, though prevailing plaintiffs often seek fees under the private attorney general statute (Code Civ. Proc. § 1021.5). Civil penalties up to $2,500 per violation in enforcement actions by the AG and local prosecutors (§ 17206). A 4-year statute of limitations applies.

### California — Consumer Legal Remedies Act (CLRA, Civ. Code § 1750)

- **What**: Prohibits 29 enumerated deceptive practices in consumer transactions for goods or services (Civ. Code § 1770(a)(1)-(29)), including misrepresenting the source, sponsorship, approval, characteristics, uses, benefits, or quantities of goods or services — and, since July 1, 2024, advertising a price that does not include all mandatory fees or charges (the SB 478 junk-fee prohibition, § 1770(a)(29)).
- **Threshold**: Applies to transactions intended to result primarily in the sale or lease of goods or services to a consumer. Requires a 30-day demand letter before filing suit for damages (Civ. Code § 1782). Injunctive relief can be sought without the demand letter.
- **Consequence**: Actual damages (the total award in a class action may not be less than $1,000, § 1780(a)(1)), punitive damages, restitution, injunctive relief, and mandatory attorney's fees to a prevailing plaintiff. A senior citizen or disabled person may recover up to an additional $5,000 (§ 1780(b)). Any waiver of CLRA rights is void and unenforceable.

### California — False Advertising Law (FAL, Bus. & Prof. Code § 17500)

- **What**: Prohibits untrue or misleading advertising, including any advertising that is "untrue or misleading, and which is known, or which by the exercise of reasonable care should be known, to be untrue or misleading."
- **Threshold**: Applies to any form of advertising, including online, print, broadcast, and social media. The "reasonable care" standard means negligence is sufficient — no intent required.
- **Consequence**: Private plaintiffs (who must satisfy Proposition 64 standing) may obtain injunctive relief and restitution; no private right to damages. Civil penalties up to $2,500 per violation in actions by the AG or local prosecutors (§ 17536).

### New York — General Business Law §§ 349 and 350

- **What**: GBL § 349 prohibits deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any business, trade, or commerce in New York. GBL § 350 specifically addresses false advertising. Both provide private rights of action. The FAIR Business Practices Act (S8416, signed December 19, 2025, effective February 17, 2026) expands § 349 to also prohibit "unfair" and "abusive" acts or practices — but those new prongs are enforceable only by the AG; the private right of action remains limited to deceptive conduct.
- **Threshold**: A private § 349 claim requires (1) consumer-oriented conduct, (2) that is materially misleading, and (3) injury. Reliance is not an element of either § 349 or § 350 (Koch v. Acker, Merrall & Condit Co. (2012)), though causation must still be shown — the standard is whether the act was "likely to mislead a reasonable consumer." The FAIR Act abrogates the consumer-oriented limitation for AG actions, which can now redress harm to businesses and nonprofits as well.
- **Consequence**: Actual damages or $50 statutory damages (whichever is greater) under § 349(h); treble damages up to $1,000 for willful or knowing violations. For false advertising, § 350-e allows actual damages or $500 (whichever is greater), with treble damages up to $10,000 for willful or knowing violations. Attorney's fees available. AG actions carry civil penalties of $5,000 per violation (§ 350-d). 3-year statute of limitations.

### Massachusetts — Chapter 93A

- **What**: Prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices" in trade or commerce. Uniquely applies to both consumer and business-to-business transactions (§ 11 covers B2B claims). The AG has rulemaking power under § 2(c) — the basis for the 2025 junk-fee/negative-option regulations (940 CMR 38.00).
- **Threshold**: A 30-day demand letter is required before a consumer suit under § 9 — but not for § 11 B2B claims, counterclaims or cross-claims, or where the defendant does not maintain a place of business or keep assets in Massachusetts. A reasonable written tender of settlement in response to the demand can cap recovery at the amount tendered. No reliance requirement — the standard is whether the practice is within "at least the penumbra of some common-law, statutory, or other established concept of unfairness."
- **Consequence**: Recovery is actual damages or $25, whichever is greater. Multiple damages are mandatory for willful or knowing violations or a bad-faith refusal to settle after demand — not less than double and up to treble actual damages (§ 9(3)). Attorney's fees and costs awarded to prevailing plaintiffs. The AG may seek injunctions and civil penalties up to $5,000 per violation (§ 4). 4-year statute of limitations.

### Illinois — Consumer Fraud and Deceptive Business Practices Act (ICFA, 815 ILCS 505)

- **What**: Prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices, including the use of any deception, fraud, false pretense, false promise, or misrepresentation in the conduct of trade or commerce.
- **Threshold**: Private plaintiffs must show (1) a deceptive or unfair act or practice, (2) intent that the plaintiff rely on the deception, (3) the deception occurred in the course of trade or commerce, and (4) actual damages. The AG has broader standing and does not need to prove individual reliance.
- **Consequence**: Actual damages, punitive damages, attorney's fees and costs, and injunctive relief; 3-year statute of limitations. In AG actions: civil penalties up to $50,000, or up to $50,000 per violation where the practice was committed with intent to defraud, plus an additional penalty up to $10,000 per violation committed against a person 65 or older (815 ILCS 505/7).

### Texas — Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA, Bus. & Com. Code Ch. 17)

- **What**: Prohibits false, misleading, or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of trade or commerce. Enumerates a "laundry list" of 34 specific prohibited practices (§ 17.46(b)). Also provides a cause of action for breach of warranty and unconscionable actions.
- **Threshold**: "Consumer" is defined as anyone who seeks or acquires goods or services by purchase or lease. Business entities can be consumers, but a business consumer with assets of $25 million or more (or owned or controlled by an entity with assets of $25 million or more) is excluded (§ 17.45(4)). Exemptions also cover claims arising from written contracts over $100,000 where the consumer was represented by independent counsel, and transactions over $500,000 — in each case excluding the consumer's residence (§ 17.49(f)-(g)). A 60-day pre-suit notice is required (§ 17.505).
- **Consequence**: Economic damages, plus mental anguish damages and up to treble economic damages for "knowing" violations; up to treble economic and mental anguish damages for "intentional" violations. Attorney's fees to prevailing consumers. AG actions carry civil penalties up to $10,000 per violation, plus up to $250,000 more where the practice was calculated to deprive a consumer 65 or older of money or property (§ 17.47(c)). 2-year statute of limitations.

### Florida — Deceptive and Unfair Trade Practices Act (FDUTPA, Ch. 501)

- **What**: Prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices in trade or commerce. The statute is construed liberally in favor of consumers.
- **Threshold**: No individual reliance required. The standard is whether the practice was likely to deceive a consumer acting reasonably under the circumstances. No demand letter required before filing suit.
- **Consequence**: Actual damages, injunctive relief, and attorney's fees for prevailing party. No treble or punitive damages under FDUTPA itself (but other Florida statutes may provide them). Civil penalties up to $10,000 per willful violation in enforcement actions (§ 501.2075), increased to $15,000 per violation where the victim is a senior citizen (60+), a person with a disability, or a military servicemember or a servicemember's spouse or dependent child (§ 501.2077). 4-year statute of limitations.

### Washington — Consumer Protection Act (CPA, RCW 19.86)

- **What**: Prohibits "unfair methods of competition and unfair or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any trade or commerce" (RCW 19.86.020). Both consumers and businesses may sue.
- **Threshold**: A private claim requires the five Hangman Ridge elements: (1) an unfair or deceptive act or practice, (2) occurring in trade or commerce, (3) affecting the public interest, (4) injury to the plaintiff's business or property, and (5) causation. Many Washington statutes (including the My Health My Data Act) declare violations to be per se CPA violations, satisfying the first elements.
- **Consequence**: Actual damages, attorney's fees and costs, and discretionary treble damages capped at $25,000 (RCW 19.86.090). AG enforcement includes injunctions, restitution, and civil penalties up to $7,500 per violation of RCW 19.86.020 (RCW 19.86.140); the AG must periodically evaluate and report on inflation adjustments to the penalty maximums. 4-year statute of limitations (RCW 19.86.120).

### State Junk-Fee (Drip-Pricing) Laws

- **What**: A 2024-26 wave of state pricing-transparency laws requires advertised prices to include all mandatory fees. California: SB 478, the "Honest Pricing Law," codified at Civ. Code § 1770(a)(29) (effective July 1, 2024), as modified by SB 1524's carve-out for restaurants, bars, and food service, which may itemize service charges if clearly and conspicuously displayed. Minnesota: Minn. Stat. § 325F.074 (effective January 1, 2025). Massachusetts: AG regulations under Chapter 93A, 940 CMR 38.00 (enforceable September 2, 2025), which also impose trial-offer and subscription cancellation duties. Colorado: HB25-1090, amending the Colorado Consumer Protection Act (effective January 1, 2026), reaching goods, services, and residential leases.
- **Threshold**: Common core: a displayed or advertised price must be the total price including mandatory fees and surcharges; government-imposed taxes and reasonable, actually incurred shipping charges may generally be excluded; the nature, purpose, and amount of fees must be disclosed before payment. Variable mandatory fees generally require clear disclosure of how the total is calculated.
- **Consequence**: Each law plugs into its state's general UDAP machinery — CLRA remedies (class actions, fee-shifting) in California; AG enforcement under the Minnesota deceptive-practices framework; full 93A remedies (AG $5,000-per-violation penalties and § 9 private claims with double/treble exposure) in Massachusetts; deceptive-practice remedies under the CCPA in Colorado. These state laws sweep more broadly than the FTC's Fees Rule (16 C.F.R. Part 464), which covers only live-event tickets and short-term lodging — see `ftc-udap.md`.

### State Auto-Renewal Laws — The Operative Click-to-Cancel Layer

- **What**: With the FTC's 2024 Negative Option (Click-to-Cancel) Rule vacated in its entirety (Custom Communications, Inc. v. FTC, No. 24-3137 (8th Cir. July 8, 2025)), state auto-renewal statutes are now the operative source of click-to-cancel obligations. A 2024-26 wave of new and amended laws — California's AB 2863 (applying to contracts entered into, amended, or extended on or after July 1, 2025), plus New York, Colorado, Minnesota, Tennessee, Utah, and Virginia — imposes disclosure, documented-consent, renewal-reminder, and same-medium/online cancellation duties.
- **Threshold**: ROSCA still applies at the federal level; the strictest cancellation mechanics now come from state law. See `auto-renewal.md` for the per-state regimes.
- **Consequence**: Violations can trigger both auto-renewal-specific consequences (in California, the auto-renewal provision is void and goods delivered under it are an "unconditional gift") and the general UDAP remedies in this file.

### Preemption Considerations

- **What**: Certain federal statutes expressly preempt state consumer protection claims in specific areas. FCRA preempts state claims related to credit reporting furnisher conduct. TILA preempts some state disclosure requirements. CAN-SPAM preempts state email regulation (except fraud-based claims).
- **Threshold**: Preemption analysis is statute-specific. Express preemption provisions must be read narrowly. State claims based on general fraud or deception theories may survive preemption even when the underlying conduct touches a federally regulated area.
- **Consequence**: Improperly asserting preempted state claims wastes litigation resources. Failing to assert non-preempted state claims forfeits potentially significant remedies (treble damages, attorney's fees).

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection — FTC**: State UDAP statutes provide private enforcement and enhanced remedies that supplement federal FTC Act protections (which have no private right of action). Federal and state enforcement are complementary. State junk-fee laws now reach well beyond the FTC's ticket-and-lodging Fees Rule (16 C.F.R. Part 464).
- **Consumer Protection — Auto-Renewal**: Many states have enacted specific auto-renewal laws separate from general UDAP statutes, and after the Eighth Circuit vacated the FTC's Click-to-Cancel Rule (July 2025) they are the operative click-to-cancel layer. Violations may trigger both auto-renewal-specific penalties and general UDAP remedies. See `auto-renewal.md`.
- **Consumer Protection — TCPA**: State mini-TCPA laws provide additional private remedies for telemarketing violations. Florida and Washington have aggressive state telephone solicitation statutes.
- **Data Privacy**: State privacy laws (CCPA, CPRA, state comprehensive privacy acts) and state UDAP statutes may both apply to the same data practices, with different enforcement mechanisms, remedies, and standing requirements. Washington's My Health My Data Act is enforced through the CPA.
- **Corporate**: State consumer protection compliance must be factored into corporate structuring, multi-state registration, and entity selection.
- **Litigation**: State UDAP claims are common in consumer class actions and often accompany breach of contract, tort, and warranty claims. Fee-shifting provisions make these claims attractive to plaintiff's counsel.
