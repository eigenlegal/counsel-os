## State Consumer Laws

# State Consumer Protection Laws

## Applicability

This sub-file applies whenever a consumer-facing business operates in specific states, state AG enforcement is a concern, state-specific consumer rights are at issue, treble damages or fee-shifting exposure exists, multi-state consumer class action risk is present, or choice-of-law and forum selection clauses in consumer contracts need evaluation. Also load when assessing whether a contract provision may be unenforceable under state unconscionability doctrines.

## Key Requirements

### California — Unfair Competition Law (UCL, Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200)

- **What**: Prohibits any "unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent" business act or practice and any "unfair, deceptive, untrue, or misleading" advertising. The UCL has an extremely broad "unlawful" prong — any violation of any law can serve as the predicate for a UCL claim.
- **Threshold**: Standing requires "lost money or property" as a result of the unfair competition. No intent to deceive is required. The "unfair" prong under Cel-Tech (when competitors sue) requires the practice threaten an incipient antitrust violation or violate the spirit of an antitrust law; for consumers, courts apply a balancing test.
- **Consequence**: Remedies include restitution and injunctive relief, but no damages and no attorney's fees. A 4-year statute of limitations applies. Representative actions are available. Disgorgement of profits is not available after Kwikset v. Superior Court clarified standing requirements.

### California — Consumer Legal Remedies Act (CLRA, Civ. Code § 1750)

- **What**: Prohibits 27 specific deceptive practices in consumer transactions for goods or services, including misrepresenting the source, sponsorship, approval, characteristics, uses, benefits, or quantities of goods or services.
- **Threshold**: Applies to transactions intended to result primarily in the sale or lease of goods or services to a consumer. Requires a 30-day demand letter before filing suit for damages (Civ. Code § 1782). Injunctive relief can be sought without the demand letter.
- **Consequence**: Actual damages, punitive damages (for intentional violations), restitution, injunctive relief, and attorney's fees. Statutory minimum damages are not provided, but attorney's fee shifting is a powerful incentive for plaintiff's attorneys. Any waiver of CLRA rights is void and unenforceable.

### California — False Advertising Law (FAL, Bus. & Prof. Code § 17500)

- **What**: Prohibits untrue or misleading advertising, including any advertising that is "untrue or misleading, and which is known, or which by the exercise of reasonable care should be known, to be untrue or misleading."
- **Threshold**: Applies to any form of advertising, including online, print, broadcast, and social media. The "reasonable care" standard means negligence is sufficient — no intent required.
- **Consequence**: Injunctive relief and restitution. Civil penalties up to $2,500 per violation when brought by a prosecutor. No private right to damages, but available in representative actions.

### New York — General Business Law §§ 349 and 350

- **What**: GBL § 349 prohibits deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any business, trade, or commerce in New York. GBL § 350 specifically addresses false advertising. Both provide private rights of action.
- **Threshold**: § 349 requires (1) consumer-oriented conduct, (2) that is materially misleading, and (3) injury. Reliance is not required — the plaintiff need only show the act was "likely to mislead a reasonable consumer." § 350 requires actual reliance on the false advertising.
- **Consequence**: Actual damages or $50 statutory damages (whichever is greater) under § 349. Treble damages up to $1,000 for willful or knowing violations. Attorney's fees available. 3-year statute of limitations.

### Massachusetts — Chapter 93A

- **What**: Prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices" in trade or commerce. Uniquely applies to both consumer and business-to-business transactions (§ 11 covers B2B claims).
- **Threshold**: A 30-day demand letter is required before filing suit. The demand letter creates an opportunity for the defendant to make a reasonable settlement offer, which can limit subsequent liability. No reliance requirement — the standard is whether the practice is within "at least the penumbra of some common-law, statutory, or other established concept of unfairness."
- **Consequence**: Treble damages are mandatory for willful or knowing violations. Attorney's fees and costs awarded to prevailing plaintiffs. Up to 5x damages in egregious cases per some court interpretations. 4-year statute of limitations.

### Illinois — Consumer Fraud and Deceptive Business Practices Act (ICFA, 815 ILCS 505)

- **What**: Prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices, including the use of any deception, fraud, false pretense, false promise, or misrepresentation in the conduct of trade or commerce.
- **Threshold**: Private plaintiffs must show (1) a deceptive or unfair act or practice, (2) intent that the plaintiff rely on the deception, (3) the deception occurred in the course of trade or commerce, and (4) actual damages. The AG has broader standing and does not need to prove individual reliance.
- **Consequence**: Actual damages, punitive damages, attorney's fees and costs. Civil penalties up to $50,000 per violation when brought by the AG ($50,000 for acts targeting persons 65+ or disabled). Injunctive relief. 3-year statute of limitations.

### Texas — Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA, Bus. & Com. Code Ch. 17)

- **What**: Prohibits false, misleading, or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of trade or commerce. Enumerates a "laundry list" of 27+ specific prohibited practices. Also provides a cause of action for breach of warranty and unconscionable actions.
- **Threshold**: "Consumer" is defined as anyone who seeks or acquires goods or services by purchase or lease. Business entities can be consumers. A 60-day pre-suit notice is required. Damages must exceed $100 (the former $500 threshold was removed for certain claims).
- **Consequence**: Economic damages, plus treble damages for knowing violations. Attorney's fees to prevailing consumers. Mental anguish damages available for knowing violations. 2-year statute of limitations.

### Florida — Deceptive and Unfair Trade Practices Act (FDUTPA, Ch. 501)

- **What**: Prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices in trade or commerce. The statute is construed liberally in favor of consumers.
- **Threshold**: No individual reliance required. The standard is whether the practice was likely to deceive a consumer acting reasonably under the circumstances. No demand letter required before filing suit.
- **Consequence**: Actual damages, injunctive relief, and attorney's fees for prevailing party. No treble or punitive damages under FDUTPA itself (but other Florida statutes may provide them). Civil penalties up to $10,000 per violation in AG actions. 4-year statute of limitations.

### Preemption Considerations

- **What**: Certain federal statutes expressly preempt state consumer protection claims in specific areas. FCRA preempts state claims related to credit reporting furnisher conduct. TILA preempts some state disclosure requirements. CAN-SPAM preempts state email regulation (except fraud-based claims).
- **Threshold**: Preemption analysis is statute-specific. Express preemption provisions must be read narrowly. State claims based on general fraud or deception theories may survive preemption even when the underlying conduct touches a federally regulated area.
- **Consequence**: Improperly asserting preempted state claims wastes litigation resources. Failing to assert non-preempted state claims forfeits potentially significant remedies (treble damages, attorney's fees).

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection — FTC**: State UDAP statutes provide private enforcement and enhanced remedies that supplement federal FTC Act protections (which have no private right of action). Federal and state enforcement are complementary.
- **Consumer Protection — Auto-Renewal**: Many states have enacted specific auto-renewal laws separate from general UDAP statutes. Violations may trigger both auto-renewal-specific penalties and general UDAP remedies.
- **Consumer Protection — TCPA**: State mini-TCPA laws provide additional private remedies for telemarketing violations. Florida and Washington have aggressive state telephone solicitation statutes.
- **Data Privacy**: State privacy laws (CCPA, CPRA, state comprehensive privacy acts) and state UDAP statutes may both apply to the same data practices, with different enforcement mechanisms, remedies, and standing requirements.
- **Corporate**: State consumer protection compliance must be factored into corporate structuring, multi-state registration, and entity selection.
- **Litigation**: State UDAP claims are common in consumer class actions and often accompany breach of contract, tort, and warranty claims. Fee-shifting provisions make these claims attractive to plaintiff's counsel.
