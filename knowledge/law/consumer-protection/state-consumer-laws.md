# State Consumer Protection Laws

## Overview
Every US state has enacted consumer protection statutes prohibiting unfair or deceptive trade practices, commonly known as "UDAP" or "little FTC Acts." These laws vary significantly in scope, remedies, private rights of action, and enforcement mechanisms, and often provide protections beyond federal law.

## Key Requirements
- **Universal prohibition on deception:** All states prohibit deceptive acts or practices in consumer transactions, though the standard for what constitutes deception varies (some follow the FTC standard, others apply stricter tests).
- **Unfairness coverage varies:** Not all state UDAP statutes cover "unfair" practices — some are limited to "deceptive" acts only. States like Massachusetts (Chapter 93A), Illinois (Consumer Fraud Act), and California (UCL) have broad unfairness coverage.
- **Private right of action:** Most state UDAP statutes provide private rights of action for individual consumers, unlike the FTC Act. Many allow class actions.
- **Enhanced remedies:** Many states provide treble (3x) or statutory damages, attorney's fees, and injunctive relief. California's UCL allows disgorgement of profits and restitution. Some states provide minimum statutory damages per violation.
- **Attorney General enforcement:** State AGs have enforcement authority under state consumer protection statutes and often coordinate multi-state enforcement actions.
- **Key state statutes:**
  - California: Unfair Competition Law (UCL, Bus. & Prof. Code 17200), Consumer Legal Remedies Act (CLRA, Civ. Code 1750), False Advertising Law (FAL, Bus. & Prof. Code 17500)
  - New York: General Business Law 349 (deceptive practices) and 350 (false advertising)
  - Massachusetts: Chapter 93A (unfair or deceptive acts)
  - Illinois: Consumer Fraud and Deceptive Business Practices Act (815 ILCS 505)
  - Texas: Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA, Bus. & Com. Code Chapter 17)
  - Florida: Deceptive and Unfair Trade Practices Act (FDUTPA, Chapter 501)
- **Lemon laws:** Most states have specific consumer protection statutes for new vehicle purchases with mandatory remedies (replacement or refund) for defective vehicles.
- **Home solicitation and cooling-off rules:** State laws often supplement the FTC Cooling-Off Rule with additional protections for door-to-door sales, home improvement contracts, and similar transactions.

## Common Contract Issues
- Forum selection and choice-of-law clauses that attempt to circumvent protective state consumer protection statutes — courts frequently refuse to enforce these in consumer contracts when they would deprive the consumer of statutory protections.
- Mandatory arbitration clauses that conflict with state unconscionability doctrines or specific state prohibitions.
- Class action waivers that may be unenforceable under certain state consumer protection statutes.
- Limitation of liability provisions that attempt to disclaim liability for deceptive practices — many state UDAP statutes do not permit contractual limitation of statutory remedies.
- Fee-shifting provisions in consumer contracts that discourage consumers from exercising statutory rights.
- Unilateral modification clauses that allow the company to change material terms without adequate notice and opportunity to cancel.
- Warranty disclaimers in consumer sales contracts that conflict with state implied warranty protections or lemon law requirements.

## Interaction with Other Areas
- **Consumer Protection (FTC):** State UDAP statutes often provide broader coverage and private enforcement rights that supplement federal FTC Act protections. Preemption issues arise in some areas (e.g., FCRA, TILA).
- **Consumer Protection (Auto-Renewal):** Many states have enacted specific auto-renewal and subscription billing laws separate from or in addition to general UDAP statutes.
- **Data Privacy:** State privacy laws and state consumer protection statutes may both apply to the same data practices, with different enforcement mechanisms and remedies.
- **Corporate:** State consumer protection compliance must be factored into corporate structuring and multi-state business operations.
- **Litigation:** State UDAP claims are common in consumer class actions and often accompany breach of contract and tort claims.
