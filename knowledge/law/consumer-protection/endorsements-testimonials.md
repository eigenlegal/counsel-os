# Endorsements and Testimonials

## Applicability

This sub-file applies whenever a matter involves influencer marketing agreements, affiliate marketing programs, sponsored content, product reviews (paid or incentivized), testimonials in advertising, celebrity endorsements, employee advocacy or ambassador programs, material connection disclosures, user-generated content used in marketing, review generation campaigns, or any agreement that governs how third parties represent a company's products or services to consumers.

## Key Requirements

### FTC Endorsement Guides (16 C.F.R. Part 255)

- **What**: The FTC Endorsement Guides provide guidance on the application of FTC Act Section 5 to endorsements and testimonials in advertising. The Guides were significantly updated in June 2023 to address modern marketing practices including social media, influencer marketing, and online reviews. Endorsements that are deceptive — because they misrepresent the endorser's experience, fail to disclose material connections, or make unsubstantiated claims — violate Section 5.
- **Threshold**: An "endorsement" is any advertising message that consumers are likely to believe reflects the opinions, beliefs, findings, or experiences of a party other than the sponsoring advertiser. This includes social media posts, online reviews, blog posts, podcast mentions, video content, and any other communication where a third party appears to be expressing their own views about a product or service.
- **Consequence**: Violations are enforced under FTC Act Section 5 as deceptive practices. Civil penalties can reach $50,120+ per violation. Both the endorser and the advertiser may be liable. The 2023 update makes clear that individual social media influencers can face direct FTC enforcement.

### Material Connection Disclosure

- **What**: If there is a "material connection" between an endorser and the advertiser — any connection that might affect the weight or credibility a consumer gives to the endorsement — that connection must be "clearly and conspicuously" disclosed.
- **Threshold**: Material connections include: (1) monetary payment (including commissions, affiliate fees, revenue sharing), (2) free or discounted products, (3) employment relationships, (4) family or personal relationships, (5) business ownership or financial interests, (6) contest or sweepstakes entries, (7) any other benefit the endorser receives. Disclosure must be: (a) in clear and unambiguous language (e.g., "#ad," "Paid partnership," "Company X gave me this product"), (b) placed where consumers will notice it before engaging with the endorsement (not buried at the end of a post or in hashtag strings), (c) in the same medium as the endorsement (visual disclosure for video, audio disclosure for podcasts), and (d) unavoidable — not requiring the consumer to click "more" or scroll.
- **Consequence**: Failure to disclose material connections is a deceptive practice. The FTC has brought enforcement actions against both companies and individual influencers for inadequate disclosures.

### Honest Opinions and Genuine Experience

- **What**: Endorsements must reflect the honest opinions, findings, beliefs, or experiences of the endorser. An endorser may not make claims about a product that the endorser does not actually believe or has not actually experienced.
- **Threshold**: The endorser must have actually used the product or service in the manner described. The endorser's statements must be truthful and not misleading. If the endorser's experience is atypical, the advertisement must clearly disclose what results consumers can generally expect, or the endorser's experience must be accompanied by a disclaimer that results may vary — and that disclaimer must be clear and conspicuous, not fine print.
- **Consequence**: Deceptive endorsements are FTC Act violations. The advertiser is liable for deceptive endorsements if the advertiser knew or should have known the endorsement was deceptive — creating a duty to monitor endorser claims.

### Typical Results and "Results Not Typical" Disclaimers

- **What**: The 2023 Endorsement Guides update eliminated the prior safe harbor for "results not typical" disclaimers. Previously, an advertiser could use an atypical consumer testimonial with a "results not typical" disclosure. Now, if an endorsement conveys a performance claim, the advertiser must have substantiation for the claim as interpreted by the audience.
- **Threshold**: If a testimonial communicates a specific result (e.g., "I lost 30 pounds"), the advertiser must either (1) have substantiation that the results shown are typical, or (2) clearly and conspicuously disclose what results consumers can generally expect. A vague "results may vary" disclaimer is no longer sufficient. The advertiser must provide specific information about typical outcomes.
- **Consequence**: Failure to substantiate the results communicated through testimonials or failure to adequately disclose typical outcomes is a deceptive practice.

### Expert Endorsements

- **What**: When an advertisement represents that an endorser has expertise in a relevant area, the endorsement must be supported by an actual exercise of that expertise. The expert must have actually evaluated the product using the expert's professional judgment.
- **Threshold**: The expert must have qualifications sufficient to support the implied level of expertise. The expert must have performed an evaluation that an expert in the field would conduct. The advertiser must have a reasonable basis for claims the expert makes. An expert endorsement of a product that the expert has not actually tested or evaluated is deceptive.
- **Consequence**: Deceptive expert endorsements subject both the advertiser and the expert to FTC enforcement.

### Consumer Endorsements and Testimonials

- **What**: Consumer endorsements (testimonials from real consumers about their experience with a product) must be truthful and reflect genuine experiences. Fabricated or incentivized reviews that do not disclose the incentive are deceptive.
- **Threshold**: If a consumer was incentivized (free product, payment, discount, contest entry) to provide a review or testimonial, that incentive must be disclosed. Advertisements may not use fake consumer reviews or testimonials. The FTC's 2023 proposed rule on fake reviews would specifically prohibit: (1) buying, selling, or procuring fake reviews, (2) suppressing negative reviews, (3) using insider reviews without disclosure, and (4) creating or controlling review websites that appear independent.
- **Consequence**: The FTC has imposed penalties exceeding $4 million for fake review practices (Fashion Nova, $4.2 million in 2022 for suppressing negative reviews). Companies face both FTC enforcement and state AG actions.

### Influencer-Specific Disclosure Requirements

- **What**: Social media influencers who have material connections with brands must disclose those connections in each post or piece of content that mentions, promotes, or features the brand's products or services.
- **Threshold**: (1) The disclosure must use unambiguous language — "#ad" or "Paid partnership with [Brand]" are acceptable; "#sp," "#ambassador," or "#collab" alone are not sufficient. (2) The disclosure must appear at the beginning of the post, not after a "more" cut or buried in a long list of hashtags. (3) For video content (YouTube, TikTok, Instagram Reels), the disclosure must be both visual and verbal and must appear at the beginning of the video, not just in the description. (4) For stories or ephemeral content, the disclosure must appear in each individual story segment. (5) Platform-specific disclosure tools (Instagram "Paid partnership" tag, YouTube "Includes paid promotion") may supplement but should not replace text disclosures if the platform tool is not sufficiently conspicuous.
- **Consequence**: Both the influencer and the brand can face FTC enforcement. The brand has a duty to provide clear disclosure guidance to influencers and to monitor compliance. Failure to train and monitor influencers can result in brand liability even if the brand did not direct the specific non-compliant post.

### Brand Responsibility and Monitoring Obligations

- **What**: Advertisers are responsible for ensuring that endorsers comply with FTC requirements. This includes providing clear guidance, monitoring compliance, and taking corrective action when violations are identified.
- **Threshold**: Advertisers must: (1) clearly communicate disclosure requirements to endorsers in writing, (2) provide examples of compliant and non-compliant disclosures, (3) periodically monitor endorser posts and content for compliance, (4) take action when non-compliant content is identified (requesting correction or removal), and (5) terminate relationships with endorsers who repeatedly fail to comply. A written influencer/endorser agreement that includes disclosure requirements is necessary but not sufficient — the advertiser must also monitor compliance.
- **Consequence**: Advertisers who fail to adequately train and monitor endorsers may be held liable for the endorser's non-compliance. The FTC has brought enforcement actions against brands for failing to monitor influencer disclosures (Lord & Taylor, 2016; Sunday Riley, 2019).

### Reviews and Ratings

- **What**: The FTC proposed a rule in 2023 specifically addressing fake reviews and testimonials, which would complement the existing Endorsement Guides by creating specific prohibitions with civil penalty authority.
- **Threshold**: Under the proposed rule (and current enforcement practice), businesses may not: (1) create or procure fake consumer reviews (positive or negative), (2) pay for reviews without requiring disclosure of the payment, (3) suppress or hide negative reviews to manipulate overall ratings, (4) use company insiders (employees, officers, agents) to write reviews without disclosing the relationship, (5) create or control fake review websites, or (6) misrepresent that reviews represent independent consumer opinion when they are curated or influenced by the company.
- **Consequence**: The proposed rule would authorize civil penalties of $50,120+ per violation. Under current law, these practices are enforceable as deceptive acts under Section 5.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection — FTC/UDAP**: Endorsement and testimonial requirements are enforced under FTC Act Section 5. The Endorsement Guides are interpretive guidelines, not rules — but the FTC enforces them through Section 5 enforcement actions. See `ftc-udap.md`.
- **Consumer Protection — State Laws**: State UDAP statutes provide private rights of action for deceptive endorsement practices. Consumers deceived by undisclosed paid endorsements may bring state consumer protection claims with treble damages. See `state-consumer-laws.md`.
- **Consumer Protection — Dark Patterns**: Social proof manipulation (fake reviews, fabricated testimonials, deceptive ratings) is a category of dark pattern. See `dark-patterns.md`.
- **IP and Technology**: Influencer agreements must address intellectual property rights (content ownership, license grants, usage rights). Platform terms of service may impose additional disclosure requirements. App store review policies supplement FTC requirements.
- **Data Privacy**: Influencer and endorser agreements may involve personal data sharing, requiring privacy compliance. Review platforms collecting consumer data must comply with applicable privacy laws.
