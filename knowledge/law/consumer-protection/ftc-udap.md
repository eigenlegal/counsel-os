## Ftc Udap

# FTC Act Section 5 and UDAP

## Applicability

This sub-file applies whenever a matter involves advertising claims, marketing practices, consumer-facing product or service representations, endorsements, testimonials, FTC enforcement risk, or any allegation of unfair or deceptive commercial conduct. Also applies to privacy and data security representations made to consumers, as the FTC treats misleading privacy policies as deceptive acts.

## Key Requirements

### Deception Standard (FTC Policy Statement on Deception, 1983)

- **What**: A representation, omission, or practice is deceptive if (1) there is a representation, omission, or practice likely to mislead the consumer, (2) the consumer's interpretation is reasonable under the circumstances, and (3) the misleading representation, omission, or practice is material.
- **Threshold**: Materiality is presumed for express claims, claims involving health and safety, and claims where the seller knew or should have known the representation would be important to consumers. Implied claims must be evaluated by the overall net impression of the advertisement.
- **Consequence**: FTC may issue cease-and-desist orders, seek consumer redress, require corrective advertising, or impose civil penalties for violations of consent orders.

### Unfairness Standard (15 U.S.C. § 45(n))

- **What**: An act or practice is unfair if (1) it causes or is likely to cause substantial injury to consumers, (2) the injury is not reasonably avoidable by consumers themselves, and (3) the injury is not outweighed by countervailing benefits to consumers or to competition.
- **Threshold**: Substantial injury typically means monetary harm. Emotional harm alone is generally insufficient unless accompanied by other tangible injury. Trivial or speculative harm does not qualify.
- **Consequence**: Same enforcement powers as deception — administrative complaints, consent orders, injunctions, and monetary remedies.

### Advertising Substantiation Doctrine

- **What**: Advertisers must possess a reasonable basis for all objective advertising claims before disseminating them. The level of substantiation required depends on the type of claim.
- **Threshold**: Health and safety claims require "competent and reliable scientific evidence," which the FTC defines as tests, analyses, research, or studies conducted and evaluated by qualified persons using generally accepted procedures. General product performance claims require a reasonable basis, which may include consumer surveys, expert testing, or other reliable evidence appropriate to the claim.
- **Consequence**: Failure to have adequate substantiation at the time a claim is made constitutes a deceptive act. The FTC can require substantiation be produced and can challenge claims where substantiation is inadequate.

### FTC Enforcement Powers

- **What**: The FTC enforces Section 5 through administrative proceedings, federal court actions, and rulemaking.
- **Threshold**: Civil penalties for violations of FTC rules or consent orders are adjusted annually for inflation — currently exceeding $50,120 per violation (as adjusted post-2021 under the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act). Each day of a continuing violation or each affected consumer may constitute a separate violation.
- **Consequence**: Remedies include cease-and-desist orders, civil penalties, consumer redress (refunds), disgorgement of ill-gotten gains, corrective advertising, and compliance monitoring. After the Supreme Court's decision in AMG Capital Management v. FTC (2021), the FTC's ability to seek monetary relief under Section 13(b) is limited; monetary remedies now primarily come through Section 19 (following administrative proceedings) or through Penalty Offense Authority under Section 5(m)(1)(B).

### Holder Rule (16 C.F.R. Part 433)

- **What**: The FTC Holder Rule preserves consumers' claims and defenses when consumer credit contracts are assigned to third-party holders. Sellers must include a specific notice in consumer credit contracts.
- **Threshold**: Applies to all purchase money loans and credit sales involving consumer goods or services. The required notice must appear in 10-point bold type in the consumer credit contract.
- **Consequence**: Failure to include the Holder Rule notice is an unfair act or practice. The notice preserves all of the consumer's claims and defenses against subsequent holders of the credit contract, up to the amount paid under the contract.

### Health Claims Substantiation

- **What**: Health-related advertising claims — including claims about disease treatment, prevention, cure, or mitigation — require a higher level of substantiation than general product claims.
- **Threshold**: The FTC generally requires at least two adequate and well-controlled human clinical studies for disease-related claims. Structure/function claims (e.g., "supports immune health") require competent and reliable scientific evidence, though typically less rigorous than disease claims. Claims must not be misleading even if technically truthful.
- **Consequence**: The FTC has brought numerous enforcement actions against health claims, particularly for dietary supplements, homeopathic products, and health devices. Penalties can include full refunds to consumers and bans on future health claims without substantiation.

### Made in USA Claims (16 C.F.R. Part 323)

- **What**: Products advertised as "Made in USA" must be "all or virtually all" made domestically — meaning all significant parts, processing, and labor are of U.S. origin. The FTC Made in USA Rule codifies this standard.
- **Threshold**: "All or virtually all" means that all or virtually all significant inputs are domestic and the product was last substantially transformed in the U.S. Qualified claims (e.g., "Assembled in USA from imported parts") must be truthful and non-deceptive. The FTC Rule applies to product labels and implies penalties for violations.
- **Consequence**: Civil penalties up to $50,120 per violation for violations of the Made in USA Rule. The FTC can also pursue enforcement under Section 5 for misleading origin claims not covered by the specific Rule.

### Native Advertising and Sponsored Content

- **What**: Advertising that mimics editorial content must be clearly identifiable as advertising to avoid being deceptive. The FTC's Enforcement Policy Statement on Deceptively Formatted Advertising (2015) and the Endorsement Guides govern these practices.
- **Threshold**: Disclosure must be clear and conspicuous, using language the consumer will understand, placed where the consumer will see it before engaging with the content. "Sponsored" or "ad" labels must be prominent and unambiguous.
- **Consequence**: The FTC has brought enforcement actions against both advertisers and publishers for deceptive native advertising. Failure to disclose the commercial nature of content is treated as deceptive.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Data Privacy**: The FTC actively enforces data privacy and security practices under Section 5 unfairness and deception authority. Misleading privacy policies are deceptive acts. Inadequate data security can be an unfair practice. This overlaps with CCPA, state privacy laws, and sector-specific federal privacy laws.
- **Consumer Protection — State**: State UDAP statutes often provide broader protections, private rights of action, and treble damages not available under the FTC Act (which has no private right of action). Federal FTC enforcement and state AG enforcement are complementary, not exclusive.
- **Consumer Protection — Auto-Renewal**: ROSCA and FTC negative option rules directly govern subscription and auto-renewal practices under FTC Section 5 authority.
- **Consumer Protection — Endorsements/Testimonials**: The FTC Endorsement Guides (16 C.F.R. Part 255) are enforced through Section 5 deception authority. See `endorsements-testimonials.md` for detailed guidance.
- **Consumer Protection — Dark Patterns**: The FTC increasingly targets manipulative user interface design as unfair or deceptive practices. See `dark-patterns.md`.
- **IP and Technology**: FTC Endorsement Guides apply to technology reviews, app store descriptions, SaaS marketing, and software product claims.
- **Securities**: Advertising and marketing claims about investment products or financial performance may trigger both FTC and SEC scrutiny.
- **Financial Services**: The FTC enforces consumer financial protection through the Telemarketing Sales Rule, Holder Rule, and other trade regulation rules.
