# Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act

## Applicability

This sub-file applies whenever a matter involves consumer product warranties (written or implied), warranty disclaimers in consumer sales, extended warranty or service contract agreements, product return and refund policies for warranted goods, consumer product labeling, or any transaction where a written warranty is offered on a consumer product costing more than a de minimis amount. Also applies when evaluating warranty-related litigation risk, including class actions under the Act's private enforcement provisions.

## Key Requirements

### Statutory Framework (15 U.S.C. §§ 2301-2312)

- **What**: The Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act is the federal law governing consumer product warranties. It does not require any person to provide a written warranty, but if one is given, it must comply with the Act's disclosure, designation, and substantive requirements. The Act also restricts the ability to disclaim implied warranties when a written warranty is provided.
- **Threshold**: Applies to "consumer products" — tangible personal property normally used for personal, family, or household purposes. Applies to written warranties on products costing more than $15 (for disclosure requirements) and more than $5 (for designation as "full" or "limited"). The Act does not apply to services alone, real property, or commercial/industrial products not used for personal purposes.
- **Consequence**: The FTC enforces the Act's requirements. Consumers have a private right of action in state or federal court. Attorney's fees are available to prevailing consumers. Class actions are authorized with specific requirements.

### Written Warranty Disclosure Requirements (16 C.F.R. Part 701)

- **What**: Any written warranty on a consumer product costing more than $15 must clearly and conspicuously disclose specific information in a single document, in simple and readily understood language.
- **Threshold**: Required disclosures include: (1) the identity of the warrantor(s), (2) the identity of the party to whom the warranty is extended (e.g., original purchaser only or subsequent owners), (3) the products or parts covered, (4) what the warrantor will do in the event of a defect, malfunction, or failure to conform, including items or services the warrantor will pay for or provide, and the duration of such coverage, (5) the date the warranty term begins if different from the purchase date, (6) step-by-step procedures for obtaining warranty performance, (7) information about any informal dispute resolution mechanism, (8) any limitations on the duration of implied warranties (which may not be less than the duration of the written warranty), and (9) any exclusions or limitations on relief such as incidental or consequential damages, with a statement about whether such exclusions may be prohibited in the consumer's state.
- **Consequence**: Failure to make required disclosures is a violation enforceable by the FTC. Non-compliant warranty documents may also support consumer claims for breach of warranty under state law.

### Full vs. Limited Warranty Designation (15 U.S.C. § 2303)

- **What**: Written warranties on consumer products costing more than $5 must be designated as either "Full" or "Limited."
- **Threshold**: A "Full Warranty" must meet the Federal Minimum Standards for Warranty (15 U.S.C. § 2304): (1) the warrantor must remedy defects within a reasonable time and without charge, (2) no unreasonable duty may be imposed on the consumer as a precondition for receiving warranty service (e.g., requiring return of a heavy appliance), (3) the warranty may not be limited to the original purchaser, (4) if the warrantor cannot repair the product after a reasonable number of attempts, the consumer may elect either a replacement or a full refund ("lemon" provision), and (5) the warrantor may not impose any limitation on the duration of implied warranties. Any warranty that does not meet all of these standards must be designated "Limited."
- **Consequence**: Misdesignating a warranty as "Full" when it does not meet the Federal Minimum Standards is a violation of the Act. The designation affects the consumer's remedies and the warrantor's obligations.

### Implied Warranty Protections (15 U.S.C. § 2308)

- **What**: The Act prohibits the disclaimer of implied warranties (including the implied warranty of merchantability and the implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose) whenever a written warranty is provided or a service contract is sold.
- **Threshold**: If a supplier provides ANY written warranty on a consumer product, implied warranties on that product cannot be disclaimed. Implied warranties may be limited in duration to the duration of the written warranty, but only if the limitation is conscionable, set forth in clear and unmistakable language, and prominently displayed on the face of the warranty. The minimum implied warranty duration that can be set is the duration of the written warranty.
- **Consequence**: Any attempt to disclaim implied warranties when a written warranty is given is void and unenforceable. This is a critical constraint: offering even a minimal written warranty eliminates the ability to disclaim implied warranties, which could significantly expand the warrantor's liability exposure under the UCC.

### Anti-Tying Provision (15 U.S.C. § 2302(c))

- **What**: A warrantor may not condition a written or implied warranty on the consumer's use of any article or service identified by brand, trade, or corporate name, unless the article or service is provided free of charge or the FTC has specifically granted a waiver.
- **Threshold**: The warrantor cannot require consumers to use only branded replacement parts, authorized service providers, or specific maintenance products as a condition of warranty coverage — unless those items are provided at no cost. The warrantor may recommend branded products but cannot require them.
- **Consequence**: Violation renders the tying requirement unenforceable. The FTC can pursue enforcement. This provision is particularly relevant for technology products, automobiles, and equipment where manufacturers may try to restrict aftermarket parts or independent repair.

### Service Contracts (15 U.S.C. § 2306)

- **What**: Service contracts (extended warranties) are subject to specific Magnuson-Moss requirements. A service contract is defined as a contract in writing to perform services relating to the maintenance or repair of a consumer product over a fixed period of time.
- **Threshold**: Service contracts must be clearly identified as such and must state conspicuously that the service contract is not a warranty. If a supplier sells a service contract, the supplier may not disclaim implied warranties on the covered product. Service contracts are not subject to the Full/Limited designation requirement.
- **Consequence**: Selling a service contract triggers the same implied warranty protection as providing a written warranty — implied warranties cannot be disclaimed.

### Private Right of Action and Class Actions (15 U.S.C. § 2310)

- **What**: Consumers may bring individual or class actions in state or federal court for breach of written warranty, breach of implied warranty, or violation of the Act's obligations.
- **Threshold**: Federal court jurisdiction requires: (1) the amount in controversy of each individual claim must exceed $25 (effectively any warranty claim), (2) for class actions, there must be at least 100 named plaintiffs, the amount in controversy must exceed $50,000 (exclusive of interest and costs), and the matter must meet standard class action requirements. State court actions have no minimum thresholds under the Act.
- **Consequence**: Courts may award damages, equitable relief, and attorney's fees based on actual time expended. Attorney's fee shifting is a significant incentive for consumer warranty litigation. Informal dispute resolution may be required before filing suit if the warrantor has established an FTC-complying mechanism.

### Informal Dispute Settlement Mechanisms (16 C.F.R. Part 703)

- **What**: Warrantors may establish informal dispute settlement mechanisms (arbitration, mediation) and require consumers to use them before filing suit, but only if the mechanisms comply with FTC minimum requirements.
- **Threshold**: The mechanism must be adequately funded and staffed, must not charge the consumer, must render decisions within 40 days of receipt of the dispute, must be audited annually, and must comply with procedural requirements including the right to present evidence and receive a written decision with reasons. The mechanism's decision is not binding on the consumer — the consumer retains the right to file suit if unsatisfied.
- **Consequence**: If the warrantor requires use of an informal dispute settlement mechanism, the consumer must use it before filing suit. But if the mechanism does not comply with FTC Part 703 requirements, the warrantor cannot enforce the exhaustion requirement.

### Interaction with UCC Warranty Provisions

- **What**: The Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act supplements but does not replace state UCC warranty law. UCC Article 2 governs warranty creation, scope, and remedies in sales of goods. Magnuson-Moss adds federal requirements on top of UCC rules.
- **Threshold**: Under UCC § 2-316, implied warranties may generally be disclaimed with conspicuous language (e.g., "as is" or "with all faults"). However, Magnuson-Moss overrides UCC § 2-316 whenever a written warranty is provided — making implied warranty disclaimers void. This creates a critical interaction: a seller must decide whether to offer any written warranty at all, knowing that doing so eliminates the ability to disclaim implied warranties.
- **Consequence**: The strategic decision of whether to offer a written warranty must account for the Magnuson-Moss restriction on implied warranty disclaimers. Some sellers deliberately avoid written warranties to preserve the ability to disclaim implied warranties under the UCC.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection — FTC/UDAP**: The FTC enforces Magnuson-Moss and may also challenge deceptive warranty practices under Section 5. Warranty representations in advertising that exceed actual warranty coverage are deceptive acts.
- **Consumer Protection — State Laws**: State consumer protection statutes (CLRA, GBL § 349, 93A, DTPA) frequently provide additional remedies for warranty breaches, including treble damages and fee-shifting not available under Magnuson-Moss alone.
- **IP and Technology**: The anti-tying provision intersects with right-to-repair issues and aftermarket parts disputes, particularly for technology products, IoT devices, and connected products.
- **Litigation**: Magnuson-Moss class actions have specific numerosity and amount-in-controversy requirements that differ from Rule 23. The 100-named-plaintiff requirement for federal court class actions is a significant procedural hurdle.
