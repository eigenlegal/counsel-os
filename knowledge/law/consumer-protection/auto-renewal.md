# Automatic Renewal and Subscription Billing

## Applicability

This sub-file applies whenever a matter involves subscription services, recurring billing, free trial offers, auto-renewal terms, cancellation procedures, negative option offers, continuity plans, or click-to-cancel compliance. Also applies when reviewing SaaS agreements, mobile app subscriptions, membership agreements, or any contract with automatic payment continuation features — including B2B contracts where the counterparty may qualify as a "consumer" under applicable state law.

## Key Requirements

### ROSCA — Restore Online Shoppers' Confidence Act (15 U.S.C. §§ 8401-8405)

- **What**: Prohibits charging consumers for goods or services sold in transactions effected on the Internet through a negative option feature unless three conditions are met: (1) the seller clearly and conspicuously discloses all material terms of the transaction before obtaining billing information, (2) the seller obtains the consumer's informed affirmative consent to the agreement, and (3) the seller provides simple mechanisms for stopping recurring charges and canceling the purchase.
- **Threshold**: "Clearly and conspicuously" means the disclosure must be difficult to miss, easily understandable, and placed where consumers will look before making a purchase decision. "Material terms" include the cost, frequency of charges, and how to cancel. "Simple mechanism" requires the cancellation method be easy to find and use.
- **Consequence**: Violations are treated as unfair or deceptive acts under FTC Act Section 5. Enforcement by the FTC and state AGs. Penalties can include injunctions, consumer refunds, and civil penalties of up to $53,088 per violation (indexed annually for inflation; figure per the FTC's 2025 adjustment, carried unchanged into 2026). ROSCA is the FTC's primary federal enforcement tool for subscription practices — FTC v. Amazon settled in September 2025 for a $1 billion civil penalty plus $1.5 billion in consumer redress over Prime enrollment and cancellation flows.

### FTC Negative Option Rule (16 C.F.R. Part 425)

- **What**: Governs pre-notification negative option plans (e.g., book-of-the-month, record clubs). Requires sellers to clearly and conspicuously disclose the material terms of the plan, including the obligation to notify the seller to avoid receiving and being charged for merchandise.
- **Threshold**: Sellers must provide a form for rejecting merchandise, allow at least 10 days to respond after receipt of the announcement, and promptly credit returns of unwanted merchandise. If the seller ships merchandise after receiving a rejection or after the member cancels, the member may treat it as unordered merchandise.
- **Consequence**: Violations are unfair trade practices under FTC Act Section 5. The FTC can seek injunctions, civil penalties, and consumer redress. After the 2024 Click-to-Cancel amendments were vacated (see below), this 1973 prenotification rule is again the only operative FTC negative option regulation — the FTC formally restored the prior text to the CFR in February 2026 and opened a new ANPRM on negative option practices in March 2026 (comments closed April 13, 2026).

### FTC Click-to-Cancel Rule (Finalized October 2024 — VACATED July 2025)

- **What**: Comprehensive update to the FTC's negative option framework that would have required cancellation to be at least as easy as sign-up, across all negative option programs regardless of medium (online, phone, in-person). Finalized October 2024 and published at 89 FR 90476 (Nov. 15, 2024); effective January 14, 2025, with compliance for the core disclosure, consent, and cancellation provisions deferred to July 14, 2025. The Eighth Circuit vacated the rule **in its entirety** days before that compliance date — Custom Communications, Inc. v. FTC, No. 24-3137 (8th Cir. July 8, 2025) (per curiam) — holding that the FTC's failure to issue a preliminary regulatory analysis under FTC Act Section 22 was a fatal procedural error. The rule imposes no current obligations.
- **Threshold**: For reference (and as a signal of where FTC rulemaking may go), the vacated rule would have required: (1) no misrepresentation of any material fact made to induce payment through a negative option feature, (2) clear and conspicuous disclosure of all material terms before obtaining billing information, (3) the consumer's unambiguously affirmative consent to the negative option feature, separate from any other portion of the transaction, and (4) a simple cancellation mechanism in the same medium as sign-up — no live-agent requirement if sign-up did not require one. (The proposed annual-reminder requirement was dropped from the final rule.)
- **Consequence**: None directly — the operative federal regime is ROSCA, Section 5, and the 1973 prenotification rule, and the strictest current click-to-cancel obligations come from state auto-renewal laws (notably California's). The FTC restarted negative option rulemaking with a March 2026 ANPRM; monitor for a re-proposed rule before advising that the federal click-to-cancel benchmarks are gone for good.

### California Automatic Renewal Law (ARL, Bus. & Prof. Code §§ 17600-17606)

- **What**: Imposes specific disclosure, consent, and cancellation requirements on businesses making automatic renewal or continuous service offers to California consumers. Significantly strengthened by AB 2863 (2024), which applies to contracts entered into, amended, or extended on or after July 1, 2025.
- **Threshold**: (1) Clear and conspicuous disclosure of the auto-renewal offer terms, including the recurring charge amount and frequency, before the consumer completes the purchase. (2) The consumer must provide affirmative consent to the auto-renewal terms, which must be documented or recorded. (3) An acknowledgment/confirmation must be provided after sign-up that includes the auto-renewal terms, cancellation policy, and contact information for cancellation. (4) For offers that include a free trial or promotional rate, the disclosure must state the price that will be charged after the trial/promotion, the length of the trial/promotion, and the deadline and method for cancellation. (5) If the consumer signs up online, the business must provide an online cancellation mechanism — a "click to cancel" capability (effective July 1, 2022 amendments). (6) Businesses must provide a toll-free number, email address, or other mechanism for cancellation in the acknowledgment. (7) AB 2863 adds (from July 1, 2025): express affirmative consent specifically to the automatic renewal terms, with consent records retained at least three years (or one year after termination, whichever is longer); annual reminders of the automatic renewal; advance clear-and-conspicuous notice of material changes to pricing or service; same-medium cancellation, including a prominent online "click to cancel" and a voice equivalent, without obstruction or delay; and an express prohibition on misrepresenting any material fact related to the transaction.
- **Consequence**: Non-compliant auto-renewal provisions are void and unenforceable, meaning the business cannot enforce the renewal or collect payment. Goods or services provided under a void provision are deemed an unconditional gift to the consumer. The California AG can enforce with civil penalties. Private right of action under the UCL and CLRA. This is the strictest consequence in any U.S. jurisdiction — rendering the entire auto-renewal provision void.

### New York General Business Law § 527

- **What**: Requires clear disclosure and easy cancellation for automatic renewal and continuous service offers to New York consumers.
- **Threshold**: Disclosure of auto-renewal terms must be "clear and conspicuous." The consumer must affirmatively consent. Acknowledgment must be provided. Cancellation must be available through at least the same method used for enrollment.
- **Consequence**: AG enforcement. Violations may also support claims under GBL § 349 (deceptive practices), providing private rights of action with attorney's fees and treble damages.

### Illinois Automatic Contract Renewal Act (815 ILCS 601)

- **What**: Requires written disclosure of automatic renewal terms in consumer and some commercial contracts, and requires consumer acknowledgment.
- **Threshold**: Written disclosure of the automatic renewal provision must be presented "clearly and conspicuously" in the contract or contract offer. The disclosure must include the cancellation procedure. Notice of renewal must be provided 30-60 days before the renewal date for contracts with terms of 12 months or more.
- **Consequence**: If the seller fails to comply, the automatic renewal provision is not enforceable against the consumer. AG enforcement authority.

### Oregon Automatic Renewal Law (ORS 646A.295)

- **What**: Requires clear and conspicuous disclosures, affirmative consent, and easy cancellation for automatic renewal offers.
- **Threshold**: Disclosure must be presented in a manner that is "clear, conspicuous, and in visual proximity" to the request for consent. Must provide a toll-free number, email, or other easy mechanism for cancellation.
- **Consequence**: Non-compliant auto-renewal provisions are unenforceable. The consumer is not obligated to pay for goods or services under a non-compliant renewal. AG enforcement.

### Virginia Consumer Protection Act — Auto-Renewal (Va. Code § 59.1-207.45 et seq.)

- **What**: Requires disclosure, affirmative consent, and easy cancellation for automatic renewal offers to Virginia consumers.
- **Threshold**: Clear and conspicuous disclosure of automatic renewal terms before the consumer's initial order. Affirmative consent documented. Free trial offers must clearly state post-trial charges and cancellation methods.
- **Consequence**: AG enforcement. Violations may also trigger Virginia Consumer Protection Act remedies (actual damages, attorney's fees, treble damages for willful violations).

### Common Requirements Across All Jurisdictions

### Free Trial Conversion Rules

- **What**: Free trials that convert to paid subscriptions are subject to heightened disclosure requirements under ROSCA and most state auto-renewal laws.
- **Threshold**: The trial-to-paid conversion must be disclosed before the consumer provides billing information. The disclosure must include: the post-trial price, the date or event that triggers the first charge, and clear instructions for canceling before conversion. California ARL requires that free trial disclosures appear in visual proximity to the sign-up mechanism.
- **Consequence**: Failure to properly disclose free trial conversion terms may result in the auto-renewal being void (California), FTC enforcement, or state UDAP liability. Charges collected without proper disclosure may need to be refunded.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection — FTC**: ROSCA is enforced by the FTC under its Section 5 authority. FTC enforcement actions frequently target auto-renewal practices as deceptive or unfair. With the 2024 Click-to-Cancel Rule vacated, ROSCA plus Section 5 is the operative federal framework — and the FTC continues to enforce it aggressively, as the 2025 Amazon Prime settlement shows.
- **Consumer Protection — State Laws**: State auto-renewal laws supplement general state UDAP statutes. Violations may trigger both auto-renewal-specific consequences (voiding the provision) and general consumer protection remedies (treble damages, attorney's fees).
- **Consumer Protection — Dark Patterns**: Dark pattern prohibitions in cancellation flows directly intersect with auto-renewal compliance. See `dark-patterns.md` for restrictions on obstruction, nagging, and forced action in cancellation user flows.
- **Data Privacy**: Auto-renewal disclosures and cancellation mechanisms must be accessible and functional, which may interact with cookie consent, account deletion rights (e.g., CCPA's right to delete), and data retention obligations.
- **IP and Technology**: SaaS and digital subscription agreements must comply with auto-renewal laws even in B2B contexts when the customer may qualify as a consumer. Subscription billing logic must be designed to facilitate compliance with disclosure and cancellation requirements.
- **Litigation**: Auto-renewal violations are a frequent basis for consumer class actions, particularly in California under the ARL's void-and-unenforceable remedy. The "unconditional gift" consequence makes these cases attractive for plaintiff's counsel.
