## Key Constraints

These are non-overridable legal requirements from this area. They cannot be modified by practice/ or matters/ overrides.

- CPSC reporting obligations (Section 15(b) of the CPSA) require manufacturers, importers, distributors, and retailers to report products that contain defects that could create a substantial product hazard, or that create an unreasonable risk of serious injury or death. Reporting must occur within 24 hours of obtaining reportable information. Failure to report is a federal offense with civil penalties up to $120,000 per violation ($17.15 million cap) and potential criminal prosecution.
- Implied warranty of merchantability under UCC Article 2 cannot be disclaimed in consumer transactions in some jurisdictions. Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act prohibits disclaimer of implied warranties when a written warranty is provided.
- Strict product liability (in jurisdictions that follow it) cannot be contractually disclaimed or limited between the manufacturer and the injured consumer — limitation-of-liability clauses do not protect against third-party product liability claims.
- FDA pre-market approval or clearance requirements for regulated products (drugs, medical devices, biologics) cannot be waived or contracted around.
- Product recall obligations, once triggered, are regulatory mandates that cannot be deferred by contractual provisions between supply chain parties. Recall costs cannot be shifted to consumers.
- Failure-to-warn liability attaches when a manufacturer knows or should know of product risks and fails to provide adequate warnings, regardless of contractual disclaimers. Post-sale duty to warn (Restatement Third, Section 10) is an ongoing obligation triggered by newly discovered risks.
- CPSIA lead content limit (100 ppm in accessible parts of children's products) and phthalate limits (0.1% in children's toys and child care articles) are absolute limits — products exceeding these thresholds are banned hazardous substances.
- Children's products require third-party testing by a CPSC-accepted laboratory and a Children's Product Certificate (CPC) before sale or importation. Self-certification is not permitted for children's products.
- California Proposition 65 requires clear and reasonable warnings for products containing listed chemicals above safe harbor thresholds, with civil penalties up to $2,500 per day per violation enforceable through private bounty actions.

## Consumer Product Safety

# Consumer Product Safety

## Applicability

Load this file when a matter involves consumer product manufacturing, importing, or distribution subject to CPSC jurisdiction; children's product requirements; product testing and certification; lead or phthalate limits; tracking label requirements; product import compliance; or potential substantial product hazard determinations. Also load when reviewing supplier quality agreements, import contracts, or product compliance representations.

## Key Requirements

### Consumer Product Safety Act (CPSA)

- **Substantial product hazard**
  - **Standard:** A product defect which (because of the pattern of defect, the number distributed, severity of risk, or otherwise) creates a substantial risk of injury to the public, OR a product failing to comply with an applicable consumer product safety rule that creates a risk of injury.
  - **Consequence:** Triggers mandatory reporting under Section 15(b) within 24 hours (see recalls.md). CPSC may seek corrective action including recall, repair, replacement, or refund. Companies refusing voluntary corrective action face mandatory recall through administrative adjudication and civil penalties. CPSC evaluates by considering: pattern of defect, number of products distributed, severity of risk, and likelihood of injury.

- **CPSC jurisdiction**
  - **Standard:** Any article, or component part thereof, produced or distributed for sale to, or for the personal use, consumption, or enjoyment of a consumer in or around a household, residence, school, recreation, or otherwise (15 U.S.C. 2052(a)(5)).
  - **Consequence:** Broad jurisdiction covers most consumer goods including household products, sporting goods, electronics, children's products, and furniture. Statutory exclusions: tobacco, motor vehicles (NHTSA), pesticides (EPA), aircraft (FAA), boats (Coast Guard), drugs/medical devices/cosmetics/food (FDA). Dual-use products (consumer and commercial) are covered to the extent they reach consumers.

- **Banned hazardous products**
  - **Threshold:** Products presenting an unreasonable risk of injury that cannot be made safe through a safety standard.
  - **Consequence:** CPSC may ban outright under Section 8 of CPSA. Current bans include: lawn darts with elongated tips, unstable refuse bins, fireworks with certain chemical mixtures, products containing certain asbestos forms, and small high-powered magnets marketed to children (effective 2023). Manufacturing, importing, distributing, or selling a banned product is a prohibited act subject to civil and criminal penalties.

- **Consumer product safety rules**
  - **Threshold:** CPSC issues mandatory rules under Section 7 for product categories where voluntary standards are inadequate.
  - **Consequence:** Rules prescribe performance, composition, design, construction, packaging, or labeling requirements. Non-compliant products are prohibited from manufacture, import, and sale. Current mandatory rules cover: baby cribs (16 C.F.R. 1219-1220), mattress flammability (16 C.F.R. 1632-1633), clothing textiles (16 C.F.R. 1610), children's sleepwear (16 C.F.R. 1615-1616), bicycle helmets (16 C.F.R. 1203), and many others. CPSC frequently incorporates voluntary standards (ASTM, UL) by reference as mandatory.

### Consumer Product Safety Improvement Act (CPSIA 2008)

- **Lead content limits — children's products**
  - **Threshold:** Total lead content must not exceed 100 ppm (0.01%) in any accessible part of a children's product (designed or intended primarily for children 12 and under).
  - **Consequence:** Violation renders the product a banned hazardous substance. Third-party testing required by a CPSC-accepted laboratory before certification and importation. Inaccessible component parts (per 16 C.F.R. 1500.87) are exempt from the 100 ppm limit but not from accessibility testing. Functional exceptions available (16 C.F.R. Part 1307) where lead is necessary and no practical alternative exists. Lead in surface coatings (paint) limited to 90 ppm under FHSA — applies to all consumer products, not just children's.

- **Phthalate limits — children's products**
  - **Threshold:** Children's toys and child care articles must not contain more than 0.1% (1,000 ppm) of these permanently banned phthalates: DEHP, DBP, BBP, DINP, DIBP, DPENP, DHEXP, and DCHP.
  - **Consequence:** Permanent ban. Third-party testing required. Products exceeding limits are banned hazardous products subject to seizure, recall, and penalties. "Child care article" means a product designed to facilitate sleep, feeding, sucking, or teething by children 3 and under. The CPSC Chronic Hazard Advisory Panel (CHAP) periodically reviews restrictions and may recommend additional bans.

- **Third-party testing requirement**
  - **Threshold:** All children's products must be tested by a CPSC-accepted third-party conformity assessment body for compliance with all applicable children's product safety rules before certification and importation.
  - **Consequence:** CPSC maintains a public list of accepted laboratories. Testing must cover all applicable rules: lead, phthalates, flammability, small parts (16 C.F.R. 1501), sharp edges, ASTM F963, and all product-specific rules. Periodic testing required — at least annually or upon material change in design, manufacturing process, or component sourcing. Component part testing permitted if the component is not altered during assembly. Costs borne by manufacturer or importer.

- **Children's Product Certificate (CPC)**
  - **Threshold:** Required for all children's products (children 12 and under) manufactured or imported for sale in the U.S.
  - **Consequence:** Must accompany each product or shipment and be furnished to retailers and distributors. Required contents: product identification, each applicable safety rule, CPSC-accepted laboratory (name, address, phone), date and place of manufacture, date and place of testing, certifier contact information. Issued by the domestic manufacturer or importer — not by the testing laboratory. Must be in English. Available to CPSC, CBP, and state AGs on request. Failure to provide a valid CPC may result in port detention and civil penalties.

- **General Certificate of Conformity (GCC)**
  - **Threshold:** Required for all non-children's consumer products subject to a CPSC rule, ban, standard, or regulation.
  - **Consequence:** Manufacturer or importer certifies based on testing or a reasonable testing program. Does not require third-party testing — self-certification acceptable. Required contents: product identification, applicable CPSC rules, testing entity, date/place of manufacture and testing. Available to CPSC and CBP on request.

- **Tracking labels**
  - **Threshold:** All children's products manufactured on or after August 14, 2009 must bear a permanent, distinguishing tracking label on the product and packaging (to extent practicable).
  - **Consequence:** Must identify: manufacturer or private labeler, production location (factory), production date (at least month/year), and cohort information (batch, lot, run number). Purpose: facilitate recalls by enabling precise identification of affected production cohorts. Labels must be permanent (survive normal use), legible, and visible. Failure to include tracking labels is a CPSIA violation subject to civil penalties.

### Import Requirements

- **Port of entry compliance**
  - **Threshold:** All imported consumer products must comply with applicable CPSC regulations; children's products must be accompanied by a CPC.
  - **Consequence:** CPSC works with CBP to screen imports. CPSC's Risk Assessment Methodology (RAM) system targets high-risk shipments based on product type, country of origin, manufacturer, importer history, and prior violations. Non-compliant products may be detained, refused entry, seized, or destroyed. Importers bear the same obligations as domestic manufacturers.

- **Filing of certificates**
  - **Threshold:** Electronic filing of CPC or GCC with CBP may be required upon importation under CPSC's eFiling program.
  - **Consequence:** Filed electronically through CBP's Automated Commercial Environment (ACE). Must be in English. Products without proper certification may be denied entry. The eFiling program is expanding to require electronic submission for an increasing range of product categories.

- **Importer obligations**
  - **Threshold:** Importer of record is the responsible party for CPSC compliance of imported products.
  - **Consequence:** Treated as "manufacturer" for compliance — including Section 15(b) reporting, certification, and recall responsibility. If the foreign manufacturer is unreachable, the importer bears full liability. Importers should maintain contracts covering: compliance with U.S. safety rules, testing requirements, indemnification, right to audit facilities, and recall cooperation.

### Penalties and Enforcement

- **Civil penalties**
  - **Threshold:** Any person who knowingly violates the CPSA or a rule, regulation, standard, or ban issued under it.
  - **Consequence:** Up to $120,000 per individual violation; $17.15 million maximum for a related series (inflation-adjusted). Each product unit and each day may constitute a separate violation. CPSC considers: nature, severity, and number of violations; ability to pay; effect on continued business; violation history; degree of culpability; and whether prompt corrective action was taken.

- **Criminal penalties**
  - **Threshold:** Knowing and willful violation of CPSA after CPSC notice, or knowing and willful violation of Section 15(b) reporting.
  - **Consequence:** Individuals: up to $50,000 fine, 5 years imprisonment, or both. Second offense doubles penalties ($100,000, 10 years). Corporate entities subject to Sentencing Guidelines criminal fines. DOJ prosecutes on CPSC referral. Requires both knowing (awareness of conduct) and willful (intentional disregard of law).

- **Section 17 enforcement (seizure and injunction)**
  - **Threshold:** Product is imminently hazardous or violates a ban or standard.
  - **Consequence:** CPSC may file suit seeking seizure and/or injunction against further manufacture, distribution, or sale. Seized products may be condemned and destroyed at owner's expense. Preliminary injunctions available on emergency basis. CPSC may also seek disgorgement and consumer redress.

### ASTM F963 (Toy Safety Standard)

- **Mandatory toy safety standard**
  - **Threshold:** All toys intended for children 14 and under must comply with ASTM F963.
  - **Consequence:** Incorporated by reference as a mandatory CPSC standard under CPSIA Section 106. Covers: mechanical/physical requirements (small parts, sharp edges, projectiles, strings/cords, stability), flammability, chemical limits (heavy metals in surface coatings — Sb, As, Ba, Cd, Cr, Pb, Hg, Se; migration limits), electrical and thermal requirements, battery-operated products, ride-on toys, and labeling. Third-party testing required. Periodically updated — manufacturers must comply with the CPSC-incorporated version.

- **Age determination guidelines**
  - **Threshold:** Manufacturers must accurately determine and label the intended age group for toys.
  - **Consequence:** CPSC Age Determination Guidelines provide criteria based on developmental stages and play patterns. Mis-aging (marketing small-parts toys to under-3) may violate mandatory safety rules, trigger enforcement, and create product liability exposure. Age grading determines which CPSIA requirements apply — products for children 12 and under trigger full CPSIA obligations; toys for under-3 trigger small parts regulations (16 C.F.R. 1501).

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Product Liability (product-liability.md):** CPSC safety standard violations constitute evidence of defect in tort litigation — may establish per se or rebuttable presumption depending on jurisdiction. Compliance is evidence of due care but not a complete defense. Testing and certification records are discoverable.
- **Recalls (recalls.md):** Section 15(b) reporting is the primary intersection. Tracking labels facilitate recall identification. Recall cost allocation in supply chain agreements must account for CPSC compliance costs.
- **Warnings and Labels (warnings-labels.md):** CPSC-mandated warnings (small parts, choking hazard under FHSA) are a subset of the broader adequacy analysis. CPSIA tracking labels serve a different purpose (recall facilitation) than safety warnings.
- **Consumer Protection:** CPSC enforcement complements FTC and state AG enforcement. State AGs have independent authority to enforce CPSA in federal court.
- **International Trade:** Foreign certifications (EU CE, EN 71) do not satisfy CPSC requirements. CPSC-accepted labs may be located abroad but must meet accreditation requirements. EU and U.S. requirements differ significantly.
- **Data Privacy:** Connected toys collecting personal information must comply with COPPA in addition to product safety requirements. Smart toys with internet connectivity present cybersecurity risks.

## Product Liability

# Product Liability

## Applicability

Load this file when a matter involves product defect claims, product safety incidents, product design reviews, product launch legal reviews, supply chain liability allocation, or post-sale duty-to-warn obligations. Also load when drafting or reviewing indemnification, warranty, or limitation-of-liability provisions in manufacturing, distribution, or supplier agreements.

## Key Requirements

### Theories of Liability

- **Manufacturing defect**
  - **Standard:** Strict liability — product departs from its intended design.
  - **Consequence:** Manufacturer is liable regardless of the care exercised. No negligence showing required. A single unit that deviates from specifications triggers liability for the manufacturer and every entity in the distribution chain. The defect is measured against the manufacturer's own design intent — the product simply did not come out as planned (Restatement Third, Section 2(a)). This is the least contested theory because the manufacturer's own specifications define the benchmark.

- **Design defect — risk-utility test**
  - **Standard:** Foreseeable risks of harm outweigh product utility considering the availability of a reasonable alternative design (RAD).
  - **Consequence:** Manufacturer liable if a RAD existed that would have reduced the foreseeable risks and the omission renders the product not reasonably safe. Majority of modern jurisdictions apply risk-utility (Restatement Third, Section 2(b)). Factors: magnitude and probability of foreseeable risk, instructions and warnings, consumer expectations, relative advantages and disadvantages of the product and alternatives, feasibility and cost of alternative designs, and consumer choice. Plaintiff typically bears the burden of identifying a RAD.

- **Design defect — consumer expectations test**
  - **Standard:** Product fails to perform as safely as an ordinary consumer would expect when used in an intended or reasonably foreseeable manner.
  - **Consequence:** Available in a minority of states (notably California under Barker v. Lull Engineering, which uses a dual-test approach shifting the burden to the manufacturer under risk-utility). Some states apply consumer expectations for simple products and risk-utility for complex ones. A few states (e.g., Oregon under ORS 30.920) apply a hybrid test. The Restatement Third rejected a standalone consumer expectations test, but several states retain it.

- **Failure to warn**
  - **Standard:** Foreseeable risk of harm could have been reduced by reasonable warnings or instructions (Restatement Third, Section 2(c)).
  - **Consequence:** Adequacy evaluated on content (risk specificity, severity, instructions for safe use), prominence (location, conspicuousness, font size), and comprehensibility (plain language, symbols, bilingual where required). Over-warning can dilute effectiveness, itself a basis for inadequacy (see warnings-labels.md). Must warn of risks from both intended uses and reasonably foreseeable misuses.

- **Learned intermediary doctrine**
  - **Standard:** For prescription drugs and medical devices, the duty to warn runs to the prescribing physician, not the end consumer.
  - **Consequence:** Manufacturer discharges its duty by providing adequate warnings to the prescribing physician via package inserts, physician labeling, and Dear Healthcare Provider letters. Exceptions: direct-to-consumer advertising (NJ, under Perez v. Wyeth Laboratories), mass immunization programs, and contraceptive devices in some jurisdictions. Does not apply to OTC products.

- **Negligence**
  - **Standard:** Manufacturer's conduct fell below the standard of a reasonable manufacturer in design, production, inspection, testing, or warnings.
  - **Consequence:** Requires proof of duty, breach, causation, and damages. Available in all jurisdictions. Particularly important for professional services associated with products (installation, maintenance) where strict liability may not reach, post-sale negligence (failure to recall or warn), and negligent entrustment.

- **Breach of warranty**
  - **Standard:** Express warranty (UCC 2-313), implied warranty of merchantability (UCC 2-314 — fit for ordinary purposes), implied warranty of fitness for particular purpose (UCC 2-315).
  - **Consequence:** Privity requirements vary by state — most have relaxed horizontal privity (injured third parties can sue) but some still require vertical privity. Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act prohibits disclaiming implied warranties when a written warranty is provided for consumer products. Warranty claims may allow recovery of purely economic losses where tort claims would not (economic loss doctrine).

### Defenses and Limitations

- **Comparative fault / contributory negligence**
  - **Threshold:** Varies by state — pure comparative fault (13 states), modified 50% bar (10 states), modified 51% bar (23 states), pure contributory negligence (4 states + DC: AL, MD, NC, VA).
  - **Consequence:** Plaintiff's recovery reduced by percentage of fault. In modified states, plaintiff barred if fault exceeds threshold. Whether comparative fault applies to strict liability varies by state. Product misuse may be treated as comparative fault or as a separate defense.

- **Assumption of risk**
  - **Threshold:** Plaintiff must have known of the specific risk and voluntarily encountered it.
  - **Consequence:** Complete bar in some jurisdictions (express assumption); in others merged into comparative fault analysis. Express assumption via signed waivers is generally enforceable for recreational activities but not ordinary consumer products. Implied assumption requires subjective knowledge of the specific danger.

- **Statute of limitations**
  - **Threshold:** Typically 2-3 years from date of injury or discovery (varies by state and theory; 4 years for UCC warranty claims).
  - **Consequence:** Claim time-barred if not filed within period. Discovery rule tolls the statute in most jurisdictions. Tolled for minors until reaching age of majority. Fraudulent concealment may toll.

- **Statute of repose**
  - **Threshold:** 6-15 years from date of first sale or delivery (e.g., CT 10 years, NC 12 years, OR 8 years, IN 10 years, TN 10 years).
  - **Consequence:** Absolute bar on claims after the repose period, regardless of when injury occurred. No tolling, no discovery rule. Distinct from statutes of limitations — repose runs from the product's first sale, not from the injury. Some states exempt fraud or intentional concealment. Not all states have product liability statutes of repose.

- **Federal preemption**
  - **Threshold:** Federal regulatory scheme occupies the field or directly conflicts with state law claims.
  - **Consequence:** Express preemption: National Childhood Vaccine Injury Act (design defect claims for vaccines), FIFRA (state labeling requirements for pesticides). Implied preemption: Riegel v. Medtronic (2008) — Class III PMA medical devices preempt state tort claims imposing different requirements. Does not apply to 510(k) devices (Medtronic v. Lohr, 1996). Prescription drugs: FDA approval does not preempt failure-to-warn claims where the manufacturer could have unilaterally strengthened the label (Wyeth v. Levine, 2009).

- **Regulatory compliance defense**
  - **Threshold:** Product complied with all applicable government safety standards at time of manufacture.
  - **Consequence:** Evidence of due care but not a complete defense (majority rule). A few states provide statutory regulatory compliance defenses: MI (rebuttable presumption), NJ (rebuttable presumption if FDA-approved), TX (limited defense for drugs). Industry standards (ANSI, ASTM, UL, ISO) are relevant but not dispositive.

- **State-of-the-art defense**
  - **Threshold:** The risk was not known or knowable given the state of scientific and technical knowledge at the time of manufacture.
  - **Consequence:** Available in some states — manufacturer not liable for risks undiscoverable at time of sale. Particularly relevant to failure-to-warn claims. Does not protect against manufacturing defects (strict liability regardless of knowledge). Some states reject this defense entirely in strict liability.

### Damages

- **Compensatory — economic loss**
  - **Threshold:** Property damage, medical expenses, lost wages, loss of earning capacity, cost of repair or replacement.
  - **Consequence:** Recoverable in all product liability actions with personal injury or property damage. Economic loss doctrine bars recovery in tort for purely economic losses without physical harm — must proceed under warranty or contract theory.

- **Compensatory — personal injury**
  - **Threshold:** Physical injury to the plaintiff.
  - **Consequence:** Pain and suffering, emotional distress (with physical injury), disfigurement, disability, loss of enjoyment of life, loss of consortium. Some states cap non-economic damages (CO $642K adjusted, OH $350K-$500K). Medical monitoring costs recoverable in some jurisdictions without present injury if toxic exposure creates significantly increased disease risk.

- **Punitive damages**
  - **Threshold:** Malice, fraud, willful misconduct, conscious disregard for safety, or reckless indifference.
  - **Consequence:** Available in most states. Caps vary: TX (2x economic + non-economic up to $750K or $200K), NJ (5x compensatory or $350K), VA ($350K). Some states prohibit in product liability (NH, WA limited). Due process limits: BMW v. Gore (1996) and State Farm v. Campbell (2003) — single-digit ratio guideline. Many states require clear and convincing evidence. Some states allocate a portion to the state (OR 60%, GA 75%).

### Class Actions and Mass Torts

- **Class certification**
  - **Threshold:** Rule 23(a) — numerosity, commonality, typicality, adequacy; Rule 23(b)(3) — predominance and superiority.
  - **Consequence:** Product defect classes often defeated on predominance due to individual causation and damages issues. Most succeed only as settlement classes or issue classes (Rule 23(c)(4)). Certification for injunctive relief under Rule 23(b)(2) may be more attainable (recall, corrective advertising).

- **Multidistrict litigation (MDL)**
  - **Threshold:** Civil actions with common fact questions pending in different districts, transferred by JPML under 28 U.S.C. Section 1407.
  - **Consequence:** Consolidated before a single judge for pretrial proceedings. Bellwether trials test liability theories and assess case values. Primary mechanism for managing mass product liability litigation. Most cases settle; remainder remanded for trial.

- **Successor liability**
  - **Threshold:** Company acquires another company's product line or assets.
  - **Consequence:** General rule: asset purchaser does not assume predecessor's liabilities. Exceptions: express or implied assumption, fraudulent transfer, mere continuation, and product-line exception (adopted in some states). Critical in M&A due diligence for manufacturing companies.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Consumer Protection:** Product liability claims frequently accompany UDAP and consumer class action claims. Deceptive advertising about product safety supports both theories.
- **Recalls (recalls.md):** A recall does not eliminate tort liability — it may mitigate damages but serves as evidence of a known defect. Failure to recall after learning of a defect may support punitive damages.
- **Warnings and Labels (warnings-labels.md):** Failure-to-warn claims depend on the adequacy analysis in warnings-labels.md. Post-sale duty to warn interacts with recall obligations.
- **Consumer Product Safety (consumer-product-safety.md):** CPSC Section 15(b) reporting is triggered by the same defect analysis underlying product liability. CPSC enforcement actions and consent decrees are discoverable.
- **Insurance:** CGL policies cover product liability (Products-Completed Operations hazard) but exclude recall costs. Punitive damages coverage varies by state.
- **Data Privacy:** Connected/IoT products with security vulnerabilities causing physical harm create product liability exposure alongside data breach obligations.
- **International Trade:** Export markets impose separate product safety regimes (EU Product Liability Directive, EU General Product Safety Regulation 2023).
- **Litigation:** MDL, class actions, expert testimony (Daubert), extensive document discovery, and privilege issues are common in product liability.

## Recalls

# Product Recalls

## Applicability

Load this file when a matter involves a product safety incident, potential or actual product recall, CPSC reporting obligations, FDA recall classification, NHTSA vehicle safety defect reporting, recall cost allocation in supply chain agreements, or post-recall litigation risk assessment. Also load when drafting recall cooperation provisions in manufacturing, distribution, or supplier agreements.

## Key Requirements

### Voluntary vs. Mandatory Recalls

- **Voluntary recall (CPSC)**
  - **Threshold:** Company identifies or is notified of a substantial product hazard and initiates corrective action cooperatively with CPSC.
  - **Consequence:** Company works with CPSC to develop a corrective action plan (CAP) — refund, repair, or replacement at no cost to consumers. CPSC publishes recall notice on cpsc.gov with product photos, hazard description, incident count, and remedy instructions. Approximately 99% of CPSC recalls are voluntary. Company bears all costs. Cooperation typically results in more favorable enforcement terms and reduced civil penalty exposure. CPSC may require periodic progress reports on recall effectiveness and remedy completion rates.

- **Mandatory recall (CPSC)**
  - **Threshold:** CPSC determines through administrative proceedings that a product presents a substantial product hazard and the company refuses voluntary corrective action.
  - **Consequence:** CPSC issues an order under Section 15(c)-(d) of CPSA compelling recall, repair, replacement, or refund. Requires formal adjudication with hearing before an administrative law judge. Additional civil penalties for failure to comply. Mandatory recalls are rare (fewer than 1% of all CPSC recalls) but signal maximum regulatory confrontation. The mandatory process is slow — typically 12-18 months — during which the defective product may remain on the market, increasing liability exposure.

- **FDA Class I recall**
  - **Threshold:** Reasonable probability that use of or exposure to the product will cause serious adverse health consequences or death.
  - **Consequence:** Most urgent classification. Triggers public health notification, press releases, and depth-of-distribution checks. Company must account for all product in distribution and provide FDA with a recall strategy including scope, depth (wholesale, retail, consumer level), and public notification plan. FDA may request a health hazard evaluation. Examples: contaminated food with pathogen, mislabeled drugs with wrong active ingredient, medical devices with life-threatening malfunction. FDA has authority to order mandatory recall for food (FSMA) and devices (21 U.S.C. 360h).

- **FDA Class II recall**
  - **Threshold:** Use of or exposure may cause temporary or medically reversible adverse health consequences, or probability of serious consequences is remote.
  - **Consequence:** Requires product retrieval or correction. Company must conduct effectiveness checks to verify recall reaches sub-recipients — FDA specifies frequency (typically 2-3 checks). Most common FDA recall class by volume. Company must provide FDA with status reports at specified intervals.

- **FDA Class III recall**
  - **Threshold:** Use of or exposure is not likely to cause adverse health consequences.
  - **Consequence:** Typically involves minor labeling issues or technical violations. May not require public notification or effectiveness checks, but product must still be corrected or removed from distribution.

- **NHTSA vehicle recall**
  - **Threshold:** Motor vehicle or equipment contains a safety-related defect or does not comply with a Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standard (FMVSS).
  - **Consequence:** Manufacturer must notify NHTSA within 5 business days of determining a defect exists, then notify owners/purchasers/dealers by first-class mail within a reasonable time (60 days for vehicles, 70 days for tires). Must provide a free remedy. 49 U.S.C. Section 30118. NHTSA can initiate investigation even without manufacturer report. Recall campaigns must achieve adequate completion rates — NHTSA monitors and may require additional outreach.

### Reporting Obligations

- **CPSC Section 15(b) reporting**
  - **Timeline:** Within 24 hours of obtaining information reasonably supporting that a product: (1) fails to comply with a consumer product safety rule, (2) contains a defect creating a substantial product hazard, or (3) creates an unreasonable risk of serious injury or death.
  - **Consequence:** Failure to report is a federal offense — civil penalties up to $120,000 per violation, $17.15 million cap. Knowing failure can result in criminal prosecution ($50,000 fine, 5 years imprisonment). Applies to manufacturers, importers, distributors, and retailers. The 24-hour clock starts when any employee obtains reportable information — not just senior management. Late reporting is one of the most common bases for CPSC civil penalties.

- **CPSC Section 37 reporting (lawsuit patterns)**
  - **Timeline:** Within 30 days of becoming aware that 3+ civil actions filed in federal or state court within 24 months allege death or grievous bodily injury from a particular product model.
  - **Consequence:** Must report to CPSC even if no recall initiated and even if claims are believed meritless. Triggers CPSC investigation into potential substantial product hazard. Same civil and criminal penalties as Section 15(b) for failure to report.

- **FDA MedWatch / MDR reporting**
  - **Timeline:** 15 calendar days for serious adverse events involving medical devices (21 C.F.R. Part 803); 5 calendar days for events requiring remedial action to prevent unreasonable risk.
  - **Consequence:** Failure to report is a prohibited act under FDCA. FDA may issue warning letter, pursue injunction, or bring criminal prosecution. Importers and user facilities (hospitals, nursing homes) also have independent reporting obligations. Reported through FDA FAERS (drugs) and MAUDE database (devices).

- **NHTSA Early Warning Reporting (EWR)**
  - **Timeline:** Quarterly reports for manufacturers meeting production thresholds (500+ vehicles/year); defect and noncompliance reports within 5 business days of determination.
  - **Consequence:** Failure subject to civil penalties up to $26,315 per violation, $131.6 million maximum. TREAD Act requires quarterly reporting of: property damage claims, injuries, deaths, warranty claims for specified components, consumer complaints, and field reports. Must also report foreign safety recalls within 5 business days.

### Recall Process Steps

- **Investigation phase:**
  Identify scope — affected models, lot numbers, date codes, serial number ranges, production facilities, and time periods. Conduct root cause analysis. Compile injury/incident reports, consumer complaints, warranty returns, and field data. Investigation records are discoverable in subsequent litigation. Engage outside counsel early for privilege protections — but purely factual investigation records (testing results, engineering analyses) may not be privileged.

- **Determination phase:**
  Assess whether the defect constitutes a substantial product hazard (CPSC), safety defect (NHTSA), or Class I/II/III hazard (FDA). Factors: frequency of failure, severity of potential injury, foreseeability of misuse, affected population size, and existence of a feasible remedy. The determination decision should involve legal counsel, as it triggers reporting obligations and has significant litigation implications.

- **Notification phase:**
  For CPSC — submit a Full Report via saferproducts.gov. For FDA — submit recall report to appropriate district office. For NHTSA — file Part 573 Defect and Noncompliance Report. Direct consumer notification required: first-class mail (NHTSA), email, phone, social media (CPSC encourages multi-channel). Public notice through agency databases and press release. Recall notice must clearly describe: the product, the hazard, incident count, and the remedy.

- **Remedy phase:**
  Provide consumers a remedy at no cost — refund, repair, or replacement. Establish consumer contact channel (phone, website, email). Track completion rates — CPSC and NHTSA monitor effectiveness and may require additional outreach waves. CPSC may require effectiveness assessments at 3, 6, and 12 months. Retain records of all contacts, fulfillment, and returned/destroyed products. Disposal must prevent re-entry into commerce.

### Cost Allocation and Insurance

- **Manufacturer responsibility:**
  Recall costs — investigation, root cause analysis, notification, product retrieval, remedy, disposal/destruction, regulatory compliance, public relations, and customer service — are borne by the manufacturer as a regulatory obligation. Cannot be shifted to consumers.

- **Supply chain cost allocation:**
  Contractual indemnification provisions govern cost allocation between commercial parties. Absent express provisions, the party whose component or process caused the defect typically bears cost under common law indemnification. Key provisions: recall cooperation obligations, root cause investigation costs, indemnification for recall losses, insurance requirements, and duty to preserve evidence.

- **Insurance coverage:**
  Standard CGL policies exclude recall costs under the "sistership" exclusion (CGL exclusion (n)). Product recall insurance (product contamination/withdrawal insurance) is a separate line covering: notification costs, product retrieval, disposal/destruction, business interruption, third-party liability, replacement product costs, and crisis management expenses. Premiums vary by product type, distribution volume, and claims history.

### Liability Implications of Recalls

- **Recall as evidence of defect:**
  A recall announcement may be used by plaintiffs as evidence of a known and acknowledged defect. Some courts admit recall evidence; others exclude it as a subsequent remedial measure under FRE 407 (but FRE 407 does not apply to government-mandated actions). Careful drafting of recall language is essential.

- **Post-recall injuries:**
  Injuries occurring after a recall — particularly where the consumer was not effectively reached — may support enhanced damages including punitive damages. The adequacy of the recall effort (notification scope, remedy availability, completion rate) directly impacts litigation exposure.

- **Failure to recall:**
  A decision not to recall after learning of a defect may itself be evidence of conscious disregard for safety, supporting punitive damages. Document the analysis and rationale for any decision not to recall.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Product Liability (product-liability.md):** A recall does not shield against tort liability — it may mitigate damages but constitutes evidence of a known defect. Post-recall injuries may support punitive damages. Recall cooperation provisions should be coordinated with indemnification clauses.
- **Consumer Product Safety (consumer-product-safety.md):** Section 15(b) overlaps with the substantial product hazard analysis. CPSIA tracking labels facilitate recall effectiveness.
- **Warnings and Labels (warnings-labels.md):** Post-sale duty to warn (Restatement Third Section 10) may be triggered by the same information. Recall notices must meet warning adequacy standards.
- **Consumer Protection:** Recall communications must be accurate. Deceptive or inadequate notices may trigger FTC or state AG enforcement. State AGs may independently investigate and coordinate with CPSC.
- **Insurance:** Recall insurance coverage, CGL exclusions, and additional insured requirements are critical. Review policy triggers and limits before a recall event.
- **Data Privacy:** Recall notifications involving registered users raise data privacy considerations — generally permitted as a safety exception, but communications should be limited to recall content.

## Warnings Labels

# Warnings and Labels

## Applicability

Load this file when a matter involves product warning adequacy, labeling requirements, failure-to-warn claims, post-sale duty to warn, Proposition 65 compliance, OSHA hazard communication, product-specific labeling regulations (food, cosmetics, chemicals, drugs), or bilingual/multilingual labeling requirements. Also load when reviewing warning language in product documentation, packaging, or user manuals.

## Key Requirements

### Warning Adequacy (Tort Law)

- **Content adequacy**
  - **Standard:** Warning must identify the specific hazard, the nature and severity of potential harm, and instructions for safe use or avoidance.
  - **Consequence:** A warning that merely states "Danger" or "Use with caution" without specifying the nature and severity of the risk is legally inadequate. Vague or generic warnings are treated as equivalent to no warning under Restatement Third, Section 2(c). Must address foreseeable uses and reasonably foreseeable misuses. Must communicate: what the hazard is, how it can cause injury, what to do (or not do) to avoid injury, and what to do if exposure occurs. Failure to include any of these elements may render the warning inadequate even if a warning label exists.

- **Prominence and conspicuousness**
  - **Standard:** Warning must be positioned where a user will encounter it before exposure to the hazard, in a size, color, and format reasonably calculated to attract attention.
  - **Consequence:** A warning buried in a multi-page manual, printed in small type, or on discarded interior packaging may be inadequate regardless of content. ANSI Z535 series provides voluntary consensus standards for safety signs, labels, and product safety information. Signal word hierarchy: DANGER (red — will result in death or serious injury), WARNING (orange — could result in death or serious injury), CAUTION (yellow — could result in minor or moderate injury). Safety alert symbol (triangle with exclamation point) precedes the signal word. Courts increasingly reference ANSI Z535 as evidence of the standard of care.

- **Comprehensibility**
  - **Standard:** Warning must be understandable to the reasonably foreseeable user population, accounting for literacy, language, age, cognitive ability, and expertise.
  - **Consequence:** Consumer warnings must use plain language (6th-8th grade reading level recommended), pictograms/symbols, and avoid jargon. Technical warnings for professionals may use industry terminology. Where the foreseeable user population includes non-English speakers, bilingual or symbol-based warnings may be required. ISO 3864 and ANSI Z535.3 provide standardized safety symbols.

- **Over-warning dilution**
  - **Standard:** Excessive warnings about trivial, obvious, or scientifically unsupported risks can dilute the effectiveness of warnings about serious risks.
  - **Consequence:** Courts recognize that "warning fatigue" from over-warning can itself render a scheme inadequate. Cotton v. Buckeye Gas Products Co. (D.C. Cir. 1988). The goal is proportionate, risk-calibrated warnings that draw appropriate attention to the most significant risks.

- **State-of-the-art defense for warnings**
  - **Standard:** The risk was not known or reasonably knowable given the state of scientific and technical knowledge at the time of distribution.
  - **Consequence:** In recognizing jurisdictions, a manufacturer is not liable for failure to warn of a risk undiscoverable at time of sale. Once the risk becomes known, the post-sale duty to warn is triggered. Does not apply where the manufacturer had internal data suggesting the risk but failed to investigate.

### Post-Sale Duty to Warn

- **Restatement Third Section 10**
  - **Threshold:** Manufacturer learns or should learn of a product risk after sale, and a reasonable person in the seller's position would provide a warning.
  - **Consequence:** Manufacturer must take reasonable steps to warn current users. Four factors: (1) severity of the risk, (2) likelihood of harm, (3) feasibility of identifying and reaching affected users (product registration, distribution records, connected product channels), (4) burden of providing the warning relative to the risk. Does not always require a full recall — may be satisfied by targeted notification, updated manuals, safety bulletins, retrofit labels, or software updates for connected products. Interacts with CPSC Section 15(b) reporting (see recalls.md).

- **Continuing duty for known users**
  - **Threshold:** Manufacturer maintains a customer registry, warranty database, or reasonable means of contacting purchasers (including connected product channels).
  - **Consequence:** Where a direct communication channel exists (product registration, subscription, IoT connectivity), the post-sale duty is heightened. Failure to use an available channel for safety-critical information strengthens a failure-to-warn claim. Connected product capabilities (push notifications, firmware updates) may raise the standard of care.

### Learned Intermediary Doctrine

- **Prescription drugs and medical devices**
  - **Standard:** For Rx products, the manufacturer's duty to warn runs to the prescribing physician, not the end patient.
  - **Consequence:** Manufacturer satisfies its duty by providing the physician with adequate information about risks, side effects, contraindications, and interactions through the package insert, physician labeling, Dear Healthcare Provider letters, and medical literature. The physician evaluates and communicates relevant risks. Manufacturer must update warnings when new safety information emerges (FDA-required labeling supplements).

- **Exceptions to learned intermediary**
  - **Threshold:** Direct-to-consumer (DTC) advertising, mass immunization programs, contraceptive devices (some jurisdictions), oral contraceptives (FDA-required patient inserts).
  - **Consequence:** Where the manufacturer communicates directly with patients via DTC advertising, some courts hold the manufacturer assumes a direct duty to warn consumers (Perez v. Wyeth Laboratories, NJ 1999). Not universally adopted. For mass immunizations without individualized physician consultation, the manufacturer has a direct duty. FDA-mandated patient medication guides supplement the doctrine.

### Bilingual and Multilingual Requirements

- **Federal requirements**
  - **Threshold:** Specific product categories and workplace contexts with federal bilingual mandates.
  - **Consequence:** Pesticide labels must include Spanish-language safety information where used by a workforce with significant Spanish-speaking workers (EPA under FIFRA; 40 C.F.R. Part 156). OSHA requires hazard communication in a language workers understand — may require translated materials or oral training. No general federal mandate for bilingual consumer labeling, but failure to provide multilingual warnings where the foreseeable user population includes non-English speakers may support a failure-to-warn tort claim.

- **State and local requirements**
  - **Threshold:** Certain states and municipalities require bilingual labeling for specific products.
  - **Consequence:** Requirements vary by jurisdiction and product type. Failure to comply creates per se regulatory violation exposure and strengthens tort claims. Best practice: include symbol-based warnings (ISO 3864 pictograms) and bilingual text for products with foreseeable non-English-speaking users.

### California Proposition 65

- **Safe Drinking Water and Toxic Enforcement Act (Prop 65)**
  - **Threshold:** Product contains or exposes individuals to any chemical on the Prop 65 list (900+ chemicals including lead, cadmium, BPA, acrylamide, formaldehyde, certain phthalates) at levels exceeding safe harbor thresholds — NSRL (no significant risk level) for carcinogens, MADL (maximum allowable dose level) for reproductive toxicants.
  - **Consequence:** Requires a "clear and reasonable" warning before exposure. Since August 2018 amendments (27 CCR Section 25603 et seq.), warning must include: the name of at least one listed chemical, the specific health endpoint (cancer and/or reproductive harm), the triangular warning symbol, and URL (www.P65Warnings.ca.gov). Civil penalties up to $2,500 per day per violation. Enforced primarily through private bounty actions — plaintiff's attorneys receive 25% of penalties. Applies to any product sold in California regardless of where manufactured. No de minimis exception — either below safe harbor or must warn.

- **Prop 65 safe harbor warnings**
  - **Threshold:** Use of specific warning language prescribed in 27 CCR Section 25603 et seq., tailored by product type.
  - **Consequence:** Safe harbor warnings provide a rebuttable presumption of adequacy. Short-form and long-form options available. Must be prominently displayed on product, packaging, shelf tag, or at point of sale. Online sellers must provide warning before purchase — cannot be buried in post-purchase documentation. Catalog sellers must provide in a manner the purchaser is likely to see before completing the purchase.

### OSHA Hazard Communication Standard (HCS)

- **Safety Data Sheets (SDS)**
  - **Threshold:** All chemical manufacturers and importers must prepare a 16-section SDS for hazardous chemicals; employers must maintain accessible SDS for all workplace hazardous chemicals.
  - **Consequence:** GHS-aligned 16-section format: (1) identification, (2) hazard identification, (3) composition, (4) first-aid, (5) fire-fighting, (6) accidental release, (7) handling/storage, (8) exposure controls/PPE, (9) physical/chemical properties, (10) stability/reactivity, (11) toxicological, (12) ecological, (13) disposal, (14) transport, (15) regulatory, (16) other. Updated SDS required within 3 months of new significant information. Penalties: serious violation up to $16,131; willful up to $161,323.

- **Container labels (HCS)**
  - **Threshold:** All containers of hazardous chemicals shipped or in the workplace must bear HCS-compliant labels.
  - **Consequence:** Shipped containers must include: product identifier, signal word, hazard statements, precautionary statements, GHS pictograms (flame, skull and crossbones, exclamation mark, corrosion, health hazard, etc.), and supplier identification. Workplace containers may use alternative labeling (NFPA 704, HMIS) if equivalent and employees are trained. Labels must be legible, prominent, and in English.

### Product-Specific Labeling Requirements

- **Food labeling**
  - **Threshold:** All packaged food products sold in the U.S.
  - **Consequence:** Nutrition Facts panel (21 C.F.R. Part 101), ingredient list in descending order of predominance, allergen declaration under FALCPA — Top 9 allergens (milk, eggs, fish, shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat, soybeans, sesame under FASTER Act 2021) in plain English. Misbranding is a prohibited act under FDCA. FDA Warning Letters and recall authority. Country of origin labeling (COOL) for certain products.

- **Cosmetics labeling**
  - **Threshold:** All cosmetic products sold in the U.S.
  - **Consequence:** INCI ingredient names in descending order, net quantity, distributor/manufacturer identity, warnings for specific ingredients (coal tar dyes, sunscreen drug facts). MoCRA (2022) adds: mandatory serious adverse event reporting (15 business days), facility registration, product listing with ingredient disclosure to FDA, FDA mandatory recall authority, fragrance allergen disclosure (phased implementation).

- **Chemical labeling (TSCA / state laws)**
  - **Threshold:** Chemical substances manufactured, imported, or processed in the U.S.
  - **Consequence:** EPA may require labeling under TSCA Section 6 risk management rules. SNURs under TSCA Section 5(a)(2) may impose notification and labeling obligations. State laws add requirements: CA Cleaning Product Right to Know Act (SB 258) requires ingredient disclosure; WA Children's Safe Products Act requires reporting of chemicals of high concern.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Product Liability (product-liability.md):** Failure to warn is one of three product liability theories under Restatement Third. Warning adequacy analysis here directly supports the failure-to-warn analysis in product-liability.md. State-of-the-art and learned intermediary are defenses to failure-to-warn claims.
- **Recalls (recalls.md):** Post-sale duty to warn intersects with recall reporting — the same risk may trigger both. Recall notices are post-sale warnings and must meet adequacy standards.
- **Consumer Product Safety (consumer-product-safety.md):** CPSIA tracking labels facilitate post-sale warning delivery. FHSA-required warning labels (small parts, choking hazard) are mandatory product-specific warnings.
- **Consumer Protection:** False or misleading labeling triggers FTC and state AG enforcement. Advertising claims contradicting product warnings create inconsistency liability.
- **Data Privacy:** Prop 65 warnings on websites must be delivered before purchase. Connected product post-sale warnings raise questions about customer data use.
- **Employment:** OSHA HCS protects workers handling hazardous chemicals. Employer training obligations complement product warnings.
