## Current Posture (verify before relying — checked 2026-06-11)

Labor relations is in an unusually unstable period; confirm the live status of any Board doctrine before advising.

- **Board composition / quorum.** The Board lacked a quorum for most of 2025 after President Trump removed Member Gwynne Wilcox in January 2025. The Senate confirmed James Murphy and Scott Mayer on December 18, 2025; both were sworn in January 7, 2026, restoring a quorum alongside Member David Prouty. The Board now has a Republican-led majority. Quorum is again at risk when Prouty's term expires (August 27, 2026) unless a successor is seated. Confirm current membership at nlrb.gov before assuming the Board can issue binding decisions.
- **General Counsel.** GC Jennifer Abruzzo was removed in January 2025. Acting GC William Cowen issued GC Memorandum 25-05 rescinding 29 Abruzzo-era GC memoranda. Crystal Carey was confirmed (December 18, 2025) and sworn in as GC January 7, 2026, and has set new enforcement priorities. Treat pre-2025 GC memoranda as potentially rescinded; verify against the current GC memo list.
- **Removal-power litigation.** Trump v. Wilcox (SCOTUS stay May 22, 2025, 6-3) permits the removal pending litigation and signals the majority views the President as likely to prevail; the merits are not yet decided. The full D.C. Circuit declined en banc review of Wilcox's challenge (January 28, 2026), leaving the panel ruling upholding her removal in place. The Fifth Circuit (August 19, 2025) held that the dual for-cause removal protections for NLRB ALJs and Board members are likely unconstitutional and affirmed injunctions halting NLRB proceedings against SpaceX and others; this issue is likely headed to the Supreme Court.
- **Doctrine under reconsideration.** Recent pro-union Board doctrine (Cemex, Stericycle, the 2023 representation-election rule, Thryv consequential damages) remains Board law but is widely expected to be revisited by the Republican-majority Board. The Sixth Circuit rejected the Cemex bargaining-order framework in Brown-Forman Corp. v. NLRB (March 6, 2026), but the Board continues to apply Cemex until it is reversed by the Supreme Court or by a three-member Board majority. See the sub-files for current status of each doctrine.
- **State encroachment.** With the NLRB hampered, several states moved to assert state jurisdiction over private-sector labor disputes (New York S.8034A, signed September 5, 2025; California AB 288, signed September 30, 2025), and 10+ states have enacted captive-audience-meeting bans (e.g., California SB 399, effective January 1, 2025). All face NLRA-preemption and First Amendment challenges and have drawn preliminary injunctions or NLRB suits. See `union-organizing.md` and `nlra.md`.

## Key Constraints

These are non-overridable legal requirements from this area. They cannot be modified by practice/ or matters/ overrides.

- Employees covered by the NLRA have the right to engage in protected concerted activity for mutual aid or protection — in union and non-union workplaces alike — and an employer may not interfere with, restrain, or coerce them in exercising Section 7 rights (NLRA Section 8(a)(1), 29 U.S.C. 158(a)(1)).
- An employer may not discharge or discriminate against an employee because of union activity or for filing NLRB charges or giving testimony (NLRA Sections 8(a)(3) and 8(a)(4)); the remedy is reinstatement with back pay and make-whole relief.
- An employer and a union with majority support in an appropriate unit must bargain in good faith over wages, hours, and other terms and conditions of employment, and may not make unilateral changes to mandatory subjects without bargaining to agreement or lawful impasse (NLRA Sections 8(a)(5), 8(b)(3), 8(d)).
- Unfair labor practice charges must be filed with the NLRB within six months of the alleged violation (NLRA Section 10(b)); this limitations period is strict.
- A union may not engage in secondary strikes, picketing, or boycotts aimed at neutral employers to pressure the primary employer (NLRA Section 8(b)(4)).
- NLRB district-court injunctions under Section 10(j) are governed by the traditional four-factor equitable test, not a deferential "reasonable cause" standard (Starbucks Corp. v. McKinney, 602 U.S. 339 (2024)).
- The NLRA preempts state and local regulation of conduct that is arguably protected or prohibited by Sections 7 or 8 (Garmon preemption) and of activity Congress intended to leave to the free play of economic forces (Machinists preemption); state laws regulating non-coercive employer speech about unionization or asserting jurisdiction over NLRA-covered employers are vulnerable on these grounds.
