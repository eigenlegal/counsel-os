# National Labor Relations Act (NLRA)

## Applicability

Applies to most private-sector employers and employees in the United States. The National Labor Relations Act (29 U.S.C. 151-169) establishes the right of employees to organize, bargain collectively, and engage in concerted activity, while prohibiting unfair labor practices by employers and unions. Administered by the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB).

## Key Requirements

### Section 7 — Employee Rights

- **Right to Organize** / Employees have the right to self-organization, to form, join, or assist labor organizations / **Consequence**: Employer interference with organizational rights = unfair labor practice (ULP)
- **Right to Bargain Collectively** / Employees have the right to bargain collectively through representatives of their own choosing / **Consequence**: Employer refusal to bargain with certified representative = ULP under Section 8(a)(5)
- **Right to Engage in Concerted Activity** / Employees have the right to engage in other concerted activities for the purpose of collective bargaining or other mutual aid or protection / **Consequence**: Protects group complaints, walkouts, social media discussions about workplace conditions, and other collective action even without a union; discipline for protected concerted activity = ULP
- **Right to Refrain** / Employees also have the right to refrain from any or all of the above activities / **Consequence**: Union cannot coerce employees into joining or supporting; applies equally in union and non-union workplaces

### Section 8(a) — Employer Unfair Labor Practices (5 Types)

- **8(a)(1) — Interference, Restraint, Coercion** / Employer may not interfere with, restrain, or coerce employees in the exercise of Section 7 rights / **Threshold**: Broad catch-all; any employer conduct that tends to discourage Section 7 activity may violate / **Consequence**: Remedies include cease-and-desist order, notice posting, reinstatement, back pay; every other employer ULP also violates 8(a)(1)
- **8(a)(2) — Domination of Labor Organization** / Employer may not dominate or interfere with the formation or administration of any labor organization, or contribute financial or other support to it / **Consequence**: Employer-formed "employee involvement committees" that deal with terms and conditions of employment may be unlawful company unions; disestablishment ordered
- **8(a)(3) — Discrimination** / Employer may not discriminate in hiring, tenure, or any term of employment to encourage or discourage membership in a labor organization / **Consequence**: Discriminatory discharge for union activity = reinstatement with back pay; damages may be substantial for long delays
- **8(a)(4) — Retaliation for NLRB Activity** / Employer may not discharge or discriminate against an employee for filing charges or giving testimony under the NLRA / **Consequence**: Same remedies as 8(a)(3); retaliatory discharge is per se violation
- **8(a)(5) — Refusal to Bargain** / Employer may not refuse to bargain collectively with the representative designated by a majority of employees in an appropriate bargaining unit / **Consequence**: Includes failure to bargain in good faith, unilateral changes to mandatory subjects without bargaining, surface bargaining, failure to provide requested information; see `collective-bargaining.md`

### Section 8(b) — Union Unfair Labor Practices

- **8(b)(1) — Coercion of Employees** / Union may not restrain or coerce employees in the exercise of Section 7 rights (including right to refrain) / **Consequence**: Physical intimidation, threats of loss of employment, mass picketing blocking ingress/egress
- **8(b)(2) — Causing Employer Discrimination** / Union may not cause or attempt to cause an employer to discriminate against an employee in violation of 8(a)(3) / **Consequence**: Union demand to fire employee for non-membership reasons (beyond dues obligation) = ULP
- **8(b)(3) — Refusal to Bargain** / Union may not refuse to bargain collectively with the employer / **Consequence**: Same obligations as employer; must bargain in good faith
- **8(b)(4) — Secondary Boycotts and Strikes** / Union may not engage in strikes, picketing, or boycotts aimed at neutral (secondary) employers to pressure the primary employer / **Consequence**: Injunctive relief available (NLRB can seek 10(l) injunction); damages under Section 303; see `strikes-lockouts.md`
- **8(b)(7) — Recognitional Picketing** / Union may not picket for recognition for more than 30 days without filing an election petition / **Consequence**: NLRB may seek injunction; expedited election may be ordered

### NLRB Jurisdiction

- **Jurisdictional Standards (Annual Revenue)** / NLRB asserts jurisdiction based on annual business volume:
  - Non-retail: $50,000 outflow or inflow of goods across state lines
  - Retail: $500,000 gross annual volume
  - Office buildings: $100,000 gross annual revenue
  - Health care institutions: $250,000 gross annual revenue
  - Private universities: $1,000,000 gross annual revenue
  - US Postal Service: jurisdiction regardless of revenue
  / **Consequence**: Employers below thresholds = no NLRB jurisdiction; state labor boards may apply
- **Interstate Commerce** / NLRA applies to employers "affecting commerce"; essentially all private employers meeting jurisdictional thresholds / **Consequence**: Very broad reach; even indirect effect on interstate commerce sufficient

### Exclusions from Coverage

- **Supervisors** / Individuals with authority to hire, fire, discipline, assign, reward, or responsibly direct employees using independent judgment / **Consequence**: Supervisors have no Section 7 rights under NLRA; but employer may not discipline supervisors for refusing to commit ULPs
- **Managers** / Individuals who formulate and effectuate management policies by expressing and making operative the decisions of their employer / **Consequence**: Managers are excluded from bargaining units and Section 7 protections
- **Agricultural Laborers** / Excluded from NLRA coverage / **Consequence**: Some states have separate agricultural labor relations statutes (e.g., California Agricultural Labor Relations Act)
- **Independent Contractors** / Common law agency test determines employee vs. independent contractor status / **Consequence**: Misclassification = failure to recognize Section 7 rights. As of mid-2026 the Board applies the employee-favoring common-law standard of The Atlanta Opera, Inc., 372 NLRB No. 95 (2023), which de-emphasized "entrepreneurial opportunity" relative to the prior SuperShuttle (2019) test; this standard is among the doctrines a Republican-majority Board is expected to revisit — verify before relying.
- **Confidential Employees** / Employees who assist and act in a confidential capacity to persons who formulate, determine, and effectuate management labor relations policies / **Consequence**: Excluded from bargaining units; narrow definition — must relate to labor relations, not just access to confidential business information
- **Federal, State, and Local Government Employees** / Excluded from NLRA; covered by separate statutes (Federal Service Labor-Management Relations Statute; state public employee labor laws) / **Consequence**: Different frameworks apply; some states have no public sector bargaining rights
- **Railway and Airline Employees** / Covered by Railway Labor Act, not NLRA / **Consequence**: Different election, bargaining, and strike rules apply

### NLRB Procedures

- **ULP Charge Filing** / Filed with regional NLRB office within 6 months of alleged violation / **Timeline**: 6-month statute of limitations; strict deadline / **Consequence**: Late filing = charge dismissed; no tolling for continuing violations in most circumstances
- **Investigation and Complaint** / Regional Director investigates; if meritorious, issues complaint; settlement encouraged; if not settled, hearing before Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) / **Timeline**: Investigation typically 7-14 weeks; complaint to hearing 6-12 months / **Consequence**: ALJ decision appealable to full NLRB Board; Board decisions appealable to federal circuit court
- **Remedies** / Cease and desist; notice posting (physical and electronic); reinstatement with back pay (less interim earnings); make-whole relief; bargaining order in egregious cases / **Consequence**: No punitive damages under NLRA; but back pay liability accumulates during proceedings; the Board expanded make-whole remedies to include consequential damages in Thryv, Inc., 372 NLRB No. 22 (2022) — still Board law but among the doctrines the Republican-majority Board is expected to revisit; verify current status
- **Section 10(j) Injunctions** / While a ULP case is pending, the NLRB may seek a preliminary injunction in federal district court / **Standard**: District courts apply the traditional four-factor equitable test (likelihood of success, irreparable harm, balance of equities, public interest), not a deferential "reasonable cause" standard (Starbucks Corp. v. McKinney, 602 U.S. 339 (2024)) / **Consequence**: 10(j) relief is harder for the Board to obtain than under the prior reasonable-cause approach
