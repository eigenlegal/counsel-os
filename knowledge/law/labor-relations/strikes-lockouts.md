# Strikes and Lockouts

## Applicability

Applies to work stoppages, economic actions, and related activities by unions and employers. Covers the legal framework for economic strikes, unfair labor practice strikes, lockouts, picketing, replacement workers, secondary activity, and related doctrines. Critical intersection of labor law, constitutional law (First Amendment), and tort law.

## Key Requirements

### Economic Strikes

- **Definition** / Strike called to pressure employer on mandatory bargaining subjects (wages, hours, conditions) after impasse or to obtain a first contract / **Consequence**: Economic strikers are "employees" under NLRA; retain certain rights but may be permanently replaced
- **Permanent Replacement — Mackay Radio Doctrine** / Employer may hire permanent replacements for economic strikers (NLRB v. Mackay Radio, 1938) / **Consequence**: Permanently replaced economic strikers retain reinstatement rights only to substantially equivalent positions as they become available; must make unconditional offer to return; placed on preferential recall list (Laidlaw, 1968)
- **Reinstatement Rights** / Permanently replaced economic strikers who make unconditional offer to return must be placed on preferential recall list; recalled as positions become available, in order of seniority (unless business justification for different order) / **Timeline**: Recall rights continue for reasonable period (12 months typically; NLRB has extended in some cases) / **Consequence**: Failure to recall when position available = ULP; employer cannot hire new employees while replaced strikers are on recall list
- **Temporary Replacement** / Employer may also hire temporary replacements; economic strikers whose replacements are temporary have right to reinstatement when strike ends / **Consequence**: Distinction between permanent and temporary replacement is crucial; employer must clearly communicate status of replacement at time of hire

### Unfair Labor Practice (ULP) Strikes

- **Definition** / Strike caused in whole or in part by employer's unfair labor practices / **Consequence**: ULP strikers have stronger protections than economic strikers
- **No Permanent Replacement** / Employer may not permanently replace ULP strikers; may only use temporary replacements / **Consequence**: Upon unconditional offer to return, ULP strikers are entitled to immediate reinstatement, even if temporary replacements must be discharged
- **Reinstatement Right** / ULP strikers entitled to immediate reinstatement upon unconditional offer to return to work / **Timeline**: Employer must reinstate within reasonable time (typically 5 business days) after unconditional offer / **Consequence**: Failure to reinstate = additional ULP (8(a)(3)); back pay accrues from date of offer to return
- **Conversion** / An initially economic strike may convert to a ULP strike if employer commits ULPs during the strike that prolong or aggravate it / **Consequence**: Conversion retroactively changes replacement workers' status from permanent to temporary; significantly increases employer exposure

### Sympathy Strikes

- **Definition** / Refusal by employees to cross a picket line established by another union or at another employer's facility / **Consequence**: Generally protected activity under Section 7 unless the employee's CBA contains a no-strike clause that encompasses sympathy strikes
- **No-Strike Clause Interpretation** / Broad no-strike clauses ("employees will not engage in any strike") may waive sympathy strike rights; narrow clauses may not / **Threshold**: Clear and unmistakable waiver standard; ambiguous no-strike clause does not waive sympathy strike right / **Consequence**: Arbitrability of sympathy strike issue may be contested; discipline of sympathy strikers requires clear contractual waiver

### Partial and Intermittent Strikes

- **Partial Strike** / Strike by some but not all bargaining unit employees; or slowdowns, work-to-rule actions / **Consequence**: Partial strikes and slowdowns are generally unprotected activity; employer may discipline participants without committing ULP
- **Intermittent Strike** / Repeated short-duration work stoppages designed to disrupt operations without full strike / **Consequence**: Intermittent strikes are unprotected; employees engaging in intermittent strikes may be discharged; distinguishable from single protected strike followed by return to work

### Reasonable-Precautions Duty and State Tort Exposure

- **Duty to Protect Employer Property** / The NLRA does not protect strikers who fail to take "reasonable precautions" to protect the employer's property from foreseeable, aggravated, and imminent danger caused by the sudden cessation of work (Glacier Northwest, Inc. v. Teamsters Local 174, 598 U.S. 771 (2023)) / **Consequence**: Where a strike is timed or conducted so as to destroy perishable products or damage equipment, the conduct is at least arguably unprotected, so Garmon does not preempt the employer's state-law tort claim; the union may face state-court damages liability. Timing a work stoppage to maximize property damage is a litigation risk for unions and a potential affirmative claim for employers.

### Lockouts

- **Offensive/Economic Lockout** / Employer locks out employees to apply economic pressure during bargaining / **Threshold**: Permitted after genuine impasse in bargaining (American Ship Building v. NLRB, 1965); may also be permitted pre-impasse in multi-employer bargaining (NLRB v. Brown, 1965) / **Consequence**: Employer may use temporary replacements during lockout; lockout must be for legitimate business purpose, not to destroy union
- **Defensive Lockout** / Employer locks out to prevent harm from threatened or anticipated strike (e.g., orderly shutdown to protect perishable inventory, coordinate with business operations) / **Consequence**: Defensive lockout permitted when employer has reasonable basis to believe strike is imminent; may act preemptively
- **Permanent Replacements During Lockout** / Employer generally cannot hire permanent replacements during lockout (unlike economic strike) / **Consequence**: Temporary replacements during lockout are permitted; but permanent replacements may convert lawful lockout into ULP
- **Lockout Duration** / No time limit on lawful lockout; continues until parties reach agreement or employer terminates lockout / **Consequence**: Extended lockout may create financial pressure on both parties; locked-out employees may seek other employment

### Picketing

#### Primary Picketing

- **Protected Activity** / Picketing at the struck employer's premises to publicize the dispute and discourage employees and public from entering / **Consequence**: Protected by First Amendment and NLRA; employer cannot enjoin peaceful primary picketing (Norris-LaGuardia Act limits federal injunctions)
- **Restrictions** / Must be peaceful; no violence, threats, mass picketing blocking ingress/egress, property damage / **Consequence**: Violence or blocking = unprotected; state courts may enjoin; criminal prosecution possible

#### Secondary Picketing (Section 8(b)(4))

- **Prohibition** / Union may not picket, strike, or boycott a neutral (secondary) employer to pressure the primary employer / **Consequence**: Secondary activity = ULP; NLRB may seek expedited injunction (Section 10(l)); union liable for damages under Section 303
- **Publicity Proviso** / Permitted to distribute handbills (but not picket) at secondary employer if: (1) truthfully advises public; (2) does not induce work stoppage by secondary employer's employees / **Consequence**: Handbilling is protected; picketing at secondary site is generally prohibited; distinction between handbilling and picketing is important

#### Common Situs Picketing

- **Multi-Employer Worksites** / When primary and neutral employers share a common worksite (e.g., construction site), picketing may inadvertently affect neutral employers / **Threshold**: Moore Dry Dock standards: picket only when primary employer is present; picket as close to primary employer's operations as practical; clearly identify dispute is with primary employer / **Consequence**: Picketing that meets Moore Dry Dock criteria is lawful primary picketing; failure to meet standards = unlawful secondary picketing

#### Reserved Gate Doctrine (General Electric)

- **Separate Gates** / When an employer establishes separate gates for its employees and for neutral contractor employees, union must limit picketing to the gate used by the struck employer's employees / **Consequence**: Picketing at gate reserved for neutral contractors = unlawful secondary activity; reserved gate must be clearly marked and consistently used
- **Exception** / If neutral contractor's work is "related to" the struck employer's normal operations, picketing at all gates is permissible / **Consequence**: Distinction between related and unrelated contractor work is frequently litigated

### Ally Doctrine

- **Definition** / Neutral employer that performs struck work (work normally done by striking employees) loses its neutral status and becomes an "ally" subject to primary picketing and boycott / **Consequence**: Struck employer's subcontracting of work to third party during strike makes third party an ally; union may picket ally
- **Single Employer / Alter Ego** / Entities that are commonly owned and controlled and share interrelated operations may be treated as a single employer for labor law purposes / **Consequence**: Picketing at alter ego entity is primary, not secondary activity

### Notice Requirements

- **FMCS Notice** / 60 days notice before contract expiration for existing CBA; 30 days for initial contract; must notify FMCS and state mediation agencies / **Consequence**: Strike or lockout during 60-day notice period = unprotected; employees lose Section 7 protections
- **Healthcare Institutions** / 10-day advance notice of strike required at healthcare institutions (Section 8(g)) / **Consequence**: Strike without 10-day notice = unprotected; notice must specify date and time

### No-Strike Obligations

- **CBA No-Strike Clause** / Most CBAs include no-strike clause prohibiting work stoppages during contract term / **Consequence**: Strike in violation of no-strike clause = unprotected; employer may discipline; union may be liable for damages under Section 301 (Boys Markets injunction available)
- **Implied No-Strike Obligation** / Arbitration clause may imply no-strike obligation even without express no-strike clause (Gateway Coal v. UMWA) / **Consequence**: Broad arbitration clause creates implied obligation not to strike over arbitrable grievances
