# Union Organizing

## Applicability

Applies to employers facing or anticipating union organizing campaigns, including election petitions, voluntary recognition, card-check campaigns, and neutrality agreements. Covers NLRB election procedures, employer campaign rules, the Cemex doctrine, and Gissel bargaining orders.

## Key Requirements

### Election Petitions

- **RC Petition (Certification of Representative)** / Filed by union or employees seeking to certify a union as bargaining representative / **Threshold**: Must demonstrate 30% showing of interest (authorization cards or petition signatures) among employees in proposed bargaining unit / **Consequence**: NLRB conducts investigation; if appropriate unit exists and showing of interest is valid, election ordered
- **RD Petition (Decertification)** / Filed by employees seeking to remove an existing union as bargaining representative / **Threshold**: 30% showing of interest among bargaining unit employees; cannot be filed during contract bar period (except during 30-day window) / **Consequence**: Decertification election conducted; if majority votes against representation, union decertified
- **RM Petition (Employer-Filed)** / Filed by employer when presented with a claim of representation from a union / **Threshold**: Employer must have objective reasonable doubt about union's majority status; or union demands recognition / **Consequence**: Election ordered if valid question concerning representation exists

### Election Timeline and Process

- **NLRB Quickie Election Rule (2014/2023)** / Streamlined election procedures reducing time from petition to election / **Timeline**: Pre-election hearing within 8 days of petition filing; election typically 20-25 days after direction of election (can be as short as 11 days after hearing) / **Consequence**: Compressed timeline reduces employer campaign period; employer must be prepared for rapid election. **Status (verify)**: The 2023 rule (eff. Dec. 26, 2023) remains in effect, but a return to longer pre-election timelines is among the rules a Republican-majority Board is expected to revisit; confirm the current rule before advising on timing.
- **Voter Eligibility** / Employees in the bargaining unit employed during the eligibility period (payroll period immediately preceding direction of election) / **Consequence**: Unit determination (who is included/excluded) is critical; contested unit issues resolved at pre-election hearing or post-election
- **Excelsior List** / Employer must provide union with list of eligible voters (names, home addresses, phone numbers, email addresses, work locations, shifts, job classifications) within 2 business days of direction of election / **Consequence**: Failure to provide timely and accurate list = grounds for setting aside election; expanded from names/addresses to include electronic contact information (2014 rule)
- **Election Conduct** / Secret ballot election at worksite (or by mail ballot in certain circumstances); NLRB agent supervises; observers from both sides permitted / **Consequence**: Election results certified if no objections; challenged ballots resolved if determinative

### Employer Campaign Rules — TIPS

- **No Threats** / Employer may not threaten employees with adverse consequences for supporting a union (e.g., plant closure, job loss, benefit reduction, more onerous conditions) / **Consequence**: Threats = 8(a)(1) ULP; may result in election being set aside; in egregious cases, Gissel bargaining order
- **No Interrogation** / Employer may not systematically interrogate employees about their union sympathies, activities, or the sympathies of coworkers / **Consequence**: Coercive interrogation = 8(a)(1) ULP; even casual questioning may be unlawful depending on context (Rossmore House factors: timing, relationship, circumstances)
- **No Promises** / Employer may not promise benefits to employees to discourage union support (e.g., wage increase, promotion, improved conditions if employees vote against union) / **Consequence**: Promise of benefit = 8(a)(1) ULP; timing of benefit changes during campaign period is heavily scrutinized
- **No Surveillance** / Employer may not surveil or create impression of surveillance of employee union activities (monitoring union meetings, photographing picketers, tracking who attends organizing events) / **Consequence**: Surveillance or impression thereof = 8(a)(1) ULP; social media monitoring during campaigns raises similar concerns

### Employer Permitted Activity — FORE

- **Facts** / Employer may communicate factual information about unionization, costs of dues, union history, contract negotiation process / **Consequence**: Factual communications are protected employer speech under Section 8(c)
- **Opinions** / Employer may express opinions about unionization as long as they do not contain threats or promises / **Consequence**: Fine line between opinion ("I don't think a union would be good for us") and threat ("If the union comes in, we'll have to consider closing")
- **Rules** / Employer may enforce existing workplace rules consistently (no-solicitation during working time, no-distribution in work areas) / **Consequence**: Rules must be facially neutral and consistently enforced; rules adopted in response to organizing may be unlawful
- **Examples** / Employer may share examples of negative union experiences, unfulfilled union promises at other companies, strike history / **Consequence**: Must be accurate; misleading examples may be viewed as threats

### Captive-Audience Meetings and State Speech Restrictions

- **NLRB posture** / Mandatory employer meetings on unionization ("captive-audience meetings") were long permitted under Babcock & Wilcox (1948); the Biden-era Board overruled that in Amazon.com Services LLC, 373 NLRB No. 136 (2024), holding such mandatory meetings unlawful prospectively. **Status (verify)**: Amazon.com is among the doctrines a Republican-majority Board is expected to revisit; confirm whether mandatory captive-audience meetings are currently treated as a ULP before advising.
- **State bans** / 10+ states have enacted statutes barring adverse action against employees who decline to attend employer meetings on political or religious matters, including unionization (e.g., California SB 399 / Cal. Lab. Code 1137, effective January 1, 2025) / **Consequence**: These bans face NLRA-preemption and First Amendment challenges. A federal court preliminarily enjoined California SB 399 on September 30, 2025, finding it likely preempted under Machinists and Garmon and an unconstitutional content-based speech restriction; an appeal to the Ninth Circuit is expected. Check the litigation status of any captive-audience ban in the relevant state before relying on it.

### State Mini-NLRA Jurisdiction (Verify)

- **Context** / With the NLRB unable to assert jurisdiction during prolonged quorum gaps, some states enacted laws empowering state agencies to regulate private-sector labor relations when the NLRB "cannot successfully assert jurisdiction" / **Examples**: New York S.8034A (signed September 5, 2025), expanding PERB authority over private-sector ULPs, elections, and CBA enforcement; California AB 288 (signed September 30, 2025), expanding PERB jurisdiction over NLRA-covered employees / **Consequence**: Both laws drew NLRA-preemption challenges. The NLRB sued both states; Amazon also sued in New York and California. A federal court granted a partial preliminary injunction against California AB 288 in late December 2025, allowing enforcement only where the NLRB is truly unable to act. Treat these state-jurisdiction regimes as contested and unsettled; verify the current injunction and preemption posture before advising that a state board has authority over an NLRA-covered employer.

### Gissel Bargaining Orders

- **Standard** / NLRB may order employer to bargain with a union without an election when employer ULPs are so egregious that a fair election is unlikely / **Threshold**: Union must demonstrate majority support (authorization cards); employer misconduct must be "outrageous and pervasive" (Category I) or make fair election "unlikely" (Category II) / **Consequence**: Employer loses right to election; must bargain immediately with union; remedy for severe employer misconduct

### Voluntary Recognition and Card Check

- **Authorization Cards** / Union collects signed cards from majority of employees authorizing the union as bargaining representative / **Threshold**: Majority (50% + 1) of bargaining unit employees must sign cards / **Consequence**: Cards alone do not compel recognition (employer may insist on election under Linden Lumber); but see Cemex doctrine below
- **Voluntary Recognition** / Employer may voluntarily recognize union based on demonstrated majority support (card check) without election / **Consequence**: Once voluntarily recognized, employer must bargain; recognition bar doctrine provides reasonable period of bargaining without challenge to representation (typically 6-12 months)
- **Neutrality Agreements** / Employer agrees not to campaign against union; may include card-check recognition provision / **Consequence**: Enforceable as private contract; increasingly common in organized sectors; may include access agreements and expedited procedures

### NLRB 2023 Cemex Doctrine

**Status (verify before relying — checked 2026-06-11)**: Cemex remains Board law, but it is contested and widely expected to be revisited by the Republican-majority Board (which regained a quorum January 7, 2026). The Sixth Circuit rejected the Cemex bargaining-order framework in Brown-Forman Corp. v. NLRB (March 6, 2026), characterizing it as "rulemaking under the guise of an adjudication" and citing Loper Bright. The Board has stated it will continue to apply Cemex outside that circuit until the Supreme Court or a three-member Board majority overrules it. Where employer misconduct supports a bargaining order, the Board and reviewing courts may instead rest on the traditional Gissel standard. Confirm the live status before advising.

- **Rule** / When a union requests recognition based on majority support (authorization cards) and the employer commits ULPs that would require setting aside an election, the NLRB will issue a bargaining order rather than re-running the election / **Threshold**: (1) Union demonstrates majority in appropriate unit; (2) union requests recognition; (3) employer refuses and commits ULPs that would prevent a fair election; OR (4) employer fails to petition for election within 2 weeks of demand / **Consequence**: Fundamentally changes the organizing landscape; employer that commits ULPs during an election campaign risks automatic bargaining order; employer that receives card-check demand should promptly petition for election if it does not wish to voluntarily recognize
- **Impact on Employer Strategy** / Employer that commits even moderate ULPs during election campaign may face bargaining order instead of re-run election / **Consequence**: Dramatically increases stakes of employer misconduct; training of supervisors and managers is critical before any campaign
- **Election Petition Obligation** / Employer that receives recognition demand based on majority cards must either recognize the union or promptly file RM petition (within 2 weeks) / **Consequence**: Failure to file petition = may be ordered to bargain; eliminates historical employer strategy of simply refusing to recognize

### Bargaining Unit Determination

- **Community of Interest** / NLRB determines appropriate unit based on: similarity of skills, duties, wages, benefits, working conditions; common supervision; degree of interchange; geographic proximity; desires of employees; history of bargaining / **Consequence**: Unit determination affects election outcome; broader units may dilute union support; narrower units may increase it
- **Micro-Units** / NLRB has permitted smaller, specialized units within larger workforces (Specialty Healthcare, 2011) / **Consequence**: Employer opposing micro-unit bears burden of showing overwhelming community of interest with excluded employees
- **Accretion** / Addition of new employees or positions to existing bargaining unit without election / **Threshold**: New positions share sufficient community of interest with existing unit; no separate identity / **Consequence**: Employer must apply CBA to accreted employees; avoid inadvertent accretion by maintaining separate supervision and conditions
