## Key Constraints

These are non-overridable legal requirements from this area. They cannot be modified by practice/ or matters/ overrides.

- Minimum wage and overtime requirements under FLSA and state law cannot be waived by agreement between employer and employee.
- Anti-discrimination and anti-harassment protections under Title VII, ADA, ADEA, and state laws are non-waivable and cannot be prospectively released (though past claims can be released with adequate consideration and knowing/voluntary consent).
- WARN Act notice requirements (60 days for covered plant closings and mass layoffs) cannot be shortened by contract.
- Workers' compensation coverage is mandatory in nearly all states and cannot be waived by agreement.
- Employee classification (exempt vs. non-exempt, employee vs. contractor) is determined by the economic reality/totality of circumstances under applicable law, not by contract labels.
- NLRA Section 7 rights (to organize, engage in concerted activity) cannot be waived, and employer policies that chill protected activity are unlawful.
- ADEA release requirements (OWBPA) mandate specific provisions including a 21-day consideration period (45 days for group terminations), 7-day revocation period, and advice-to-consult-counsel language.
- OSHA general duty clause requires employers to provide a workplace free from recognized hazards likely to cause death or serious physical harm.
- I-9 verification must be completed within 3 business days of the employee's first day of work; no exceptions by contract.
- ACA employer shared responsibility applies to all applicable large employers (50+ FTEs) and cannot be contracted around.
- ERISA fiduciary duties cannot be disclaimed or indemnified against by plan sponsors.
- COBRA election rights (60-day election period, 18/36-month continuation) are statutory minimums that cannot be reduced.

## At Will

# At-Will Employment

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting employment agreements, offer letters,
termination provisions, employee handbooks, separation agreements, or any matter
involving wrongful discharge claims. It governs the default termination framework
in all US states except Montana.

## Key Requirements

### Default Rule

- **What**: At-will employment means either party may terminate the relationship at any
  time, for any lawful reason or no reason, with or without notice.
- **Threshold**: Applies in 49 of 50 US states plus the District of Columbia.
  Montana is the sole exception.
- **Consequence**: Employers that fail to establish clear at-will status risk
  implied-contract claims that convert the relationship to termination-for-cause-only.

### Three Exception Categories

**1. Implied Contract Exception (recognized in ~38 states)**

- **What**: Employer statements, policies, or conduct can create an implied promise of
  continued employment or termination-only-for-cause, overriding at-will status.
- **Threshold**: Any written or oral representation suggesting job security, guaranteed
  duration, or progressive discipline before termination can trigger this exception.
  Employee handbooks are the most common source of implied contract claims.
- **Consequence**: Employee may sue for breach of implied contract. Damages typically
  include lost wages, lost benefits, and potentially front pay. Juries frequently
  award substantial damages where the employee relied on specific handbook promises.
- **Key states**: California (Foley v. Interactive Data Corp., 1988), Michigan
  (Toussaint v. Blue Cross, 1980), Illinois, New Jersey, and Arizona broadly
  recognize implied contract claims. New York recognizes this exception only for
  express limitations on at-will status, not general handbook provisions.
- **Risk factors**: Handbook progressive discipline policies, orientation materials
  promising "permanent" employment, supervisor emails referencing job security,
  practices of always following progressive discipline before termination.

**2. Public Policy Exception (recognized in ~44 states)**

- **What**: Termination that violates a clear mandate of public policy is actionable
  as wrongful discharge in tort, regardless of at-will status.
- **Threshold**: Employee must show termination was motivated by one of four categories:
  (a) refusal to commit an illegal act (e.g., perjury, fraud, safety violations),
  (b) exercise of a statutory right (e.g., filing workers' comp claim, voting),
  (c) performance of a public obligation (e.g., jury duty, military service), or
  (d) reporting illegal employer conduct (whistleblowing).
- **Consequence**: Tort damages including compensatory damages, emotional distress,
  and potentially punitive damages. Broader remedy than contract claims and not
  limited to lost wages. Statutes of limitation for tort claims vary by state
  (typically 1-3 years).
- **California breadth**: California recognizes all four categories broadly under
  Tameny v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (1980). Public policy sources include statutes,
  constitutional provisions, administrative regulations, and judicial decisions.
  This is one of the broadest public policy exceptions in the nation.
- **States not recognizing**: Georgia, Louisiana, Rhode Island, and Maine do not
  recognize a general public policy exception. Florida and New York recognize it only
  in narrow statutory contexts, relying instead on specific whistleblower statutes.

**3. Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing (recognized in ~11 states)**

- **What**: An implied covenant prevents termination made in bad faith, typically
  to deprive the employee of compensation already substantially earned.
- **Threshold**: Requires showing the employer terminated the employee specifically to
  avoid paying commissions, bonuses, pensions, or other accrued benefits that were
  substantially earned. This is the narrowest of the three exceptions.
- **Consequence**: Contract damages in most states. Some jurisdictions (Montana, Alaska)
  allow tort damages including punitive damages for bad-faith discharge.
- **Key states**: California (narrow — Fortune v. National Cash Register Co. principle,
  limited to preventing employers from terminating to deny already-earned compensation),
  Massachusetts (Fortune v. National Cash Register Co., 1977), Alaska (Mitford v.
  de Lasala, 1983), Arizona, Delaware, Idaho, Montana, Nevada, Utah, Wyoming.

### Montana Wrongful Discharge from Employment Act (WDEA)

- **What**: Montana is the only state requiring "good cause" for termination after a
  probationary period. Good cause means reasonable job-related grounds for dismissal
  based on failure to satisfactorily perform, disruption of operations, or other
  legitimate business reason.
- **Probationary period**: Defaults to 6 months unless extended by written agreement.
  Some positions may have up to 12 months. During probation, at-will rules apply.
- **Consequence**: Wrongful discharge damages capped at 4 years of lost wages and
  benefits. Punitive damages available only if employer acted with actual fraud or
  malice. Claim must be filed within 1 year of discharge or within 1 year of
  exhausting internal remedies. Jury trial is available.
- **Practical impact**: Montana employers must document cause before terminating
  post-probationary employees. No other state has enacted a comparable statute,
  though similar proposals have been introduced in multiple state legislatures.

### Handbook as Contract Risk

- **What**: Employee handbooks containing progressive discipline policies, termination
  procedures, or benefit promises can create binding contractual obligations.
- **Threshold**: Handbooks without conspicuous, prominently placed disclaimers are
  at risk in the ~38 states recognizing implied contract claims. Disclaimers must be
  consistent throughout — a disclaimer on page 1 can be undermined by specific
  promises on page 30.
- **Consequence**: Courts in California (Guz v. Bechtel National, 2000), Michigan
  (Toussaint, 1980), and other states have held handbook provisions create enforceable
  implied contracts. Once an implied contract is found, the employer must show cause
  for termination.
- **Best practice requirements**:
  1. Prominent at-will disclaimer on the acknowledgment signature page
  2. Separate at-will disclaimer within the body of the handbook
  3. Statement that the handbook does not create a contract of employment
  4. Reservation of the right to modify, revoke, or supplement any policy at any time
  5. Progressive discipline policies explicitly reserving the right to skip steps
  6. Avoidance of terms like "permanent employee," "job security," or "guaranteed"
  7. Annual re-acknowledgment by all employees

### Constructive Discharge

- **What**: If an employer makes working conditions so intolerable that a reasonable
  person would feel compelled to resign, the resignation is treated as an involuntary
  termination.
- **Threshold**: Conditions must be objectively intolerable under a "reasonable person"
  standard. Mere dissatisfaction, disagreement with management, or a single unpleasant
  event is generally insufficient. Factors: severity and pervasiveness of conditions,
  duration, whether employer was notified and failed to correct.
- **Consequence**: Triggers all claims available for involuntary termination, including
  wrongful discharge, discrimination, retaliation, and may activate severance
  provisions tied to involuntary termination. Also relevant for WARN Act analysis
  if part of a mass departure.

### Documentation Requirements

- **What**: While at-will employers are not legally required to document reasons for
  termination, the absence of documentation creates significant litigation risk.
- **Threshold**: An undocumented termination of an employee in a protected class, or
  who recently engaged in protected activity, creates a strong inference of pretext.
- **Consequence**: Undocumented terminations are substantially more difficult to defend.
  Contemporaneous documentation of performance issues, warnings, and the legitimate
  business reason for termination is the single most effective defense to wrongful
  termination claims.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (Non-Competes):** At-will termination without cause may undermine
  non-compete enforcement in Massachusetts, Washington, and Oregon.
- **Employment (Contractor Classification):** Reclassified workers gain at-will
  protections and wrongful termination rights retroactively.
- **Employment (Severance):** Separation agreements must comply with OWBPA for
  employees aged 40+. Constructive discharge may trigger WARN Act analysis.
- **Employment (Discrimination-Harassment):** At-will status does not authorize
  discriminatory termination. Every firing must be evaluated against anti-discrimination
  statutes regardless of at-will status.
- **Corporate (Governance):** Officer and director termination must follow corporate
  governance requirements (board approval, charter/bylaws) in addition to employment law.
- **Litigation:** Wrongful termination claims are the most common employment litigation.
  Proper documentation of at-will status and legitimate reasons is essential for defense.

## Contractor Classification

# Independent Contractor Classification

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting independent contractor agreements,
consulting agreements, freelance arrangements, staffing agency agreements, or any
matter involving worker classification disputes. It governs the distinction between
employees and independent contractors for purposes of tax, benefits, labor
protections, and liability.

## Key Requirements

### Three Primary Classification Tests

**1. ABC Test (California AB5, Massachusetts, New Jersey, Illinois, and others)**

- **What**: Presumes the worker is an employee unless the hiring entity proves ALL
  three prongs:
  - (A) The worker is free from control and direction in performing the work, both
    under the contract and in fact.
  - (B) The work performed is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business.
  - (C) The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade,
    occupation, or business of the same nature.
- **Threshold**: Prong B is the most restrictive and most frequently failed — a
  software company hiring a freelance developer will likely fail Prong B because
  software development is within its usual course of business. All three prongs must
  be satisfied simultaneously. The burden is on the hiring entity.
- **Consequence**: Failure on any single prong means the worker is an employee.
  California AB5 (Labor Code 2775, effective January 1, 2020) applies this test for
  wage orders, the Labor Code, unemployment insurance, and workers' compensation.
  California PAGA penalties reach up to $200 per pay period per worker. Massachusetts
  imposes treble damages for misclassification (Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 149, 148B).
  New Jersey penalties include $250 per misclassified worker for first violation.
- **Exemptions**: California AB5 includes professional exemptions for certain
  occupations (lawyers, doctors, accountants, real estate agents, direct sales) that
  revert to the Borello multi-factor test. California Proposition 22 (2020) exempts
  app-based transportation and delivery platform workers from AB5, creating a third
  category with minimum earnings guarantees (120% of local minimum wage plus
  $0.30/mile for engaged driving time).

**2. Economic Reality Test (DOL / FLSA)**

- **What**: Six-factor totality-of-circumstances analysis determining whether the
  worker is economically dependent on the employer (employee) or in business for
  themselves (independent contractor).
- **Factors**: (1) Opportunity for profit or loss depending on managerial skill,
  (2) investments by the worker and the employer, (3) degree of permanence of the
  work relationship, (4) nature and degree of employer control, (5) extent the work
  is an integral part of the employer's business, (6) worker's skill and initiative.
  No single factor is determinative; the totality controls.
- **Threshold**: The DOL 2024 final rule (effective March 11, 2024) restored this
  multi-factor totality-of-circumstances test, replacing the 2021 rule that gave
  disproportionate weight to control and opportunity for profit/loss. Economic
  dependence, not control alone, is the touchstone.
- **Consequence**: Misclassified workers are entitled to FLSA minimum wage ($7.25/hour
  federal; higher in most states), overtime (1.5x for hours over 40/week), and
  recordkeeping protections. Liquidated damages (double back pay) plus attorneys'
  fees. Statute of limitations: 2 years (3 years if willful).

**3. Common Law (Right-to-Control) Test (IRS / Federal Tax)**

- **What**: Examines three categories of evidence — behavioral control, financial
  control, and type of relationship — to determine whether the hiring entity has
  the right to control how the worker performs their services.
- **Behavioral control**: Does the company dictate when, where, and how work is done?
  Does it provide training? Does it set the sequence of work?
- **Financial control**: Does the worker have unreimbursed business expenses? Does the
  worker invest in their own equipment? Can the worker realize a profit or suffer
  a loss? Does the worker market services to others? How is the worker paid
  (by project vs. by hour/salary)?
- **Type of relationship**: Is there a written contract? Are benefits provided? Is the
  relationship permanent or project-based? Is the work a key aspect of the business?
- **Threshold**: No single factor is determinative. The IRS applies the totality of
  circumstances. Form SS-8 may be filed by either party for an IRS determination.
- **Consequence**: Misclassification triggers liability for the employer's share of
  FICA (7.65%), plus back withholding. Under IRC 3509, reduced penalties apply if
  Form 1099 was filed: 20% of employee's FICA share plus 1.5% of wages for income
  tax. Without 1099 filing, full 100% liability under IRC 3509(b).

### Penalties for Misclassification

- **Federal tax**: Employer liable for employer's FICA share (7.65%), plus employee
  FICA share (reduced rates under IRC 3509 if 1099 filed), plus income tax
  withholding. Total exposure can reach 30%+ of total compensation paid.
- **Willful misclassification**: $50 per Form W-2 that should have been filed per
  worker (IRC 6721). Full 100% of un-withheld taxes. Criminal penalties under
  IRC 7202: up to $10,000 fine and 5 years imprisonment per occurrence for willful
  failure to collect or pay employment taxes.
- **State penalties**: California AB5 — penalties of $5,000-$25,000 per violation
  under Labor Code 226.8 for willful misclassification. Massachusetts — treble
  damages. New York — up to $50,000 for knowing misclassification. New Jersey —
  $250 per worker first offense, additional penalties for repeat violations.
- **Back benefits**: Misclassified workers may claim retroactive entitlement to health
  insurance, retirement plans, paid leave, workers' compensation, and unemployment
  insurance for the entire period of misclassification.
- **Joint liability**: Staffing agencies and client companies may face joint employer
  liability. Multiple states impose joint and several liability for misclassification
  by staffing intermediaries.

### Section 530 Safe Harbor

- **What**: Provides relief from federal employment tax liability if the employer had
  a "reasonable basis" for treating workers as independent contractors and met
  reporting and consistency requirements.
- **Three requirements (all must be met)**:
  1. **Reasonable basis**: Judicial precedent, published IRS ruling, past IRS audit
     that did not reclassify similar workers, or long-standing recognized practice
     in the industry.
  2. **Substantive consistency**: Treated all workers in substantially similar positions
     the same way (all as contractors or all as employees).
  3. **Reporting consistency**: Filed all required Form 1099-NEC for the workers.
- **Consequence**: If all three requirements are met, the employer is not liable for
  federal employment taxes for that class of workers, even if later determined to be
  employees. However, Section 530 does NOT protect against state-law claims, FLSA
  claims, benefits claims, or discrimination claims.

### IRS Voluntary Classification Settlement Program (VCSP)

- **What**: Allows employers to voluntarily reclassify workers as employees with
  substantially reduced tax liability for past misclassification.
- **Eligibility**: Must not be under IRS employment tax audit. Must have consistently
  treated workers as contractors. Must have filed all required 1099 forms. Apply
  using Form 8952.
- **Consequence**: Pay only 10% of the employment tax liability for the most recent
  year, with no penalties, no interest, and no audits of prior years for that
  worker class. Employer must treat workers as employees prospectively.

### Gig Economy and Platform Workers

- **What**: Platform-based workers (rideshare, delivery, freelance marketplaces)
  are subject to intensive classification scrutiny across all three tests.
- **California Proposition 22**: Exempts app-based transportation and delivery workers
  from AB5, creating a hybrid category with minimum earnings guarantees (120% of
  local minimum wage for engaged time), healthcare stipends, and accident insurance.
  The California Supreme Court upheld Proposition 22 in 2024 (Castellanos v. State).
- **Federal status**: No comprehensive federal legislation addressing gig worker
  classification. The DOL 2024 rule applies the economic reality test to platform
  workers without a platform-specific carve-out.
- **Consequence**: A single platform's business model may result in different worker
  classifications across different states and under different legal regimes.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (At-Will):** Reclassified workers gain at-will employment protections
  and wrongful termination rights retroactively.
- **Employment (Non-Competes):** Non-compete enforcement for contractors may be
  weaker; overly broad restrictions may indicate misclassification.
- **Employment (Wage-and-Hour):** Reclassified workers are entitled to FLSA and state
  overtime, minimum wage, and break requirements retroactively.
- **Employment (Discrimination-Harassment):** Reclassified workers gain Title VII, ADA,
  and ADEA protections. Contractor headcount may affect coverage thresholds.
- **IP and Technology:** Copyright work-for-hire differs for employees (automatic for
  scope-of-employment works) and contractors (requires written agreement and qualifying
  work category under 17 USC 101).
- **Corporate:** Contractor headcount affects regulatory thresholds (50+ FTEs for ACA,
  100+ for EEO-1, WARN Act).
- **Data Privacy:** Classification affects GDPR data processing roles and access
  control requirements.

## Discrimination Harassment

# Discrimination and Harassment

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting employment policies, hiring
practices, termination decisions, accommodation requests, workplace conduct policies,
or any matter involving complaints of discrimination, harassment, or retaliation.
It governs federal anti-discrimination statutes (Title VII, ADA, ADEA, EPA, PDA,
GINA, PWFA) and key state variations.

## Key Requirements

### Coverage Thresholds by Statute

| Statute | Min. Employees | Protected Characteristic |
|---------|---------------|--------------------------|
| **Title VII** (42 USC 2000e) | 15+ | Race, color, religion, sex (incl. sexual orientation & gender identity per Bostock v. Clayton County, 2020), national origin |
| **ADA** (42 USC 12101) | 15+ | Disability (physical/mental impairment substantially limiting a major life activity) |
| **ADEA** (29 USC 621) | 20+ | Age (40 and older only) |
| **Equal Pay Act** (29 USC 206(d)) | Virtually all employers | Sex-based wage disparities (no minimum employee count; covered under FLSA) |
| **Pregnancy Discrimination Act** (42 USC 2000e(k)) | 15+ (amends Title VII) | Pregnancy, childbirth, related medical conditions |
| **PWFA** (Pregnant Workers Fairness Act, 2023) | 15+ | Requires reasonable accommodation for known limitations related to pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions |
| **GINA** (42 USC 2000ff) | 15+ | Genetic information including family medical history |
| **Section 1981** (42 USC 1981) | No minimum | Race and ethnicity in contracts (no administrative exhaustion required; 4-year SOL) |

- **State laws often go further**: California FEHA covers employers with 5+ employees
  for discrimination and 1+ for harassment. New York State HRL covers 4+ employees.
  New York City HRL covers 4+ employees and is interpreted under the most liberal
  standard. Many states protect additional classes: sexual orientation, gender
  identity, marital status, political affiliation, criminal history, military status,
  and reproductive health decisions.
- **Consequence**: An employer below federal thresholds may still be fully covered
  under state or local laws with broader protections and lower thresholds.

### Theories of Discrimination

**Disparate Treatment (Intentional Discrimination)**

- **What**: An employer intentionally treats an employee less favorably because of
  a protected characteristic. Requires proof of discriminatory intent.
- **Threshold**: Under McDonnell Douglas burden-shifting (1973): (1) employee
  establishes prima facie case (member of protected class, qualified, adverse action,
  circumstances suggesting discrimination), (2) employer articulates legitimate
  nondiscriminatory reason, (3) employee shows that stated reason is pretextual.
  Direct evidence of intent bypasses this framework.
- **Consequence**: Full Title VII remedies: back pay, front pay, compensatory damages
  (emotional distress), punitive damages, injunctive relief, and attorneys' fees.
  Compensatory + punitive damages capped by employer size:
  - 15-100 employees: **$50,000**
  - 101-200 employees: **$100,000**
  - 201-500 employees: **$200,000**
  - 500+ employees: **$300,000**
  Section 1981 race claims and state law claims are not subject to these federal caps.

**Disparate Impact (Unintentional but Disproportionate Effect)**

- **What**: A facially neutral employment practice (testing, hiring criteria, degree
  requirement, algorithm) that disproportionately excludes a protected group.
  No intent to discriminate is required.
- **Threshold**: Plaintiff demonstrates statistically significant adverse impact
  (commonly using the 4/5ths rule as a screening tool). Employer defends by showing
  the practice is job-related and consistent with business necessity. Plaintiff may
  rebut by identifying a less discriminatory alternative.
- **Consequence**: Same remedies as disparate treatment except punitive damages are
  generally unavailable for pure disparate impact claims. Applies to hiring criteria,
  standardized tests, promotion requirements, criminal background policies, and
  algorithmic hiring tools.

### Harassment and Hostile Work Environment

- **What**: Unwelcome conduct based on a protected characteristic that is sufficiently
  severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of employment.
- **Standard**: Both subjective (the employee found it hostile) and objective
  (a reasonable person in the same position would find it hostile). Factors: frequency,
  severity, whether physically threatening or humiliating, whether it unreasonably
  interferes with work performance. A single incident can suffice if severe enough
  (e.g., physical assault, use of a racial epithet by a supervisor).
- **Supervisor harassment**: Employer is vicariously liable for supervisor harassment
  resulting in tangible employment action (termination, demotion, undesirable transfer).
  For harassment without tangible action, employer may assert the Faragher/Ellerth
  affirmative defense: (1) employer exercised reasonable care to prevent and promptly
  correct, and (2) employee unreasonably failed to use available corrective
  opportunities (complaint procedure).
- **Co-worker harassment**: Employer is liable only if it knew or should have known
  about the harassment and failed to take prompt, effective remedial action.
- **Consequence**: Full Title VII remedies. Absence of a complaint procedure and
  anti-harassment policy effectively eliminates the Faragher/Ellerth defense.
  California FEHA imposes strict liability for supervisor harassment with no
  affirmative defense available.

### ADA Reasonable Accommodation

- **What**: Employers must provide reasonable accommodations to qualified individuals
  with disabilities unless doing so would cause undue hardship on the operation of
  the business.
- **Interactive process**: Mandatory once the employer is aware (or should be aware)
  of the need for accommodation. The employer must engage in a good-faith, timely
  dialogue to identify effective accommodations. Neither party may obstruct or
  delay the process.
- **Examples**: Modified work schedule, telecommuting, reassignment to a vacant
  position, equipment modification, leave of absence beyond FMLA entitlement,
  removal of marginal job functions, modified break schedules.
- **Undue hardship**: Considers the accommodation's net cost, the employer's overall
  financial resources, the number of employees, the nature of the operations, and
  the impact on the ability to conduct business. Small employers face a lower bar
  for demonstrating undue hardship.
- **Consequence**: Failure to engage in the interactive process is itself an ADA
  violation, even if no reasonable accommodation ultimately exists. Remedies: back
  pay, compensatory and punitive damages (within Title VII caps), reinstatement,
  attorneys' fees.

### EEOC Administrative Process

- **Filing deadlines**: **180 days** from the discriminatory act in states without
  a state fair employment agency. **300 days** in states with such an agency (the
  majority). ADEA follows the same 180/300-day framework. Equal Pay Act: no EEOC
  filing required; 2-year SOL (3 years if willful).
- **Process**: Charge filed with EEOC. Investigation and possible mediation.
  Determination of reasonable cause or dismissal. Right-to-sue letter issued.
  Employee has **90 days** from receipt of right-to-sue letter to file suit.
- **Consequence**: Failure to timely file an EEOC charge bars the federal lawsuit
  (subject to limited equitable tolling). State-law claims have separate filing
  deadlines (e.g., California DFEH/CRD: 3 years; New York: 3 years state, 1 year
  city). EEOC charge requirement does not apply to Section 1981 race claims or
  Equal Pay Act claims.

### Retaliation

- **What**: Employers may not retaliate against employees who oppose unlawful
  practices, file charges, participate in investigations, or testify in proceedings.
- **Protected activity**: Formal EEOC charges, internal complaints (even if informal),
  refusing to participate in discriminatory conduct, cooperating with investigations,
  serving as a witness. The employee's belief of unlawfulness need only be reasonable,
  not correct.
- **Adverse action standard**: Any action that would dissuade a reasonable worker
  from engaging in protected activity (Burlington Northern v. White, 2006). Broader
  than the adverse action standard for discrimination claims — includes schedule
  changes, increased scrutiny, negative references, and exclusion from meetings.
- **Consequence**: Retaliation is the most frequently filed EEOC charge category
  (over 55% of all charges in recent years). Full remedies available. Retaliation
  claims frequently survive even when the underlying discrimination claim does not.

### Pay Equity

- **What**: Equal Pay Act prohibits sex-based wage disparities for substantially
  equal work. Title VII also covers compensation discrimination.
- **Threshold**: Jobs need not be identical, but must involve substantially equal
  skill, effort, and responsibility under similar working conditions. Defenses:
  seniority system, merit system, quantity/quality of production system, or any
  factor other than sex (narrowly construed in most jurisdictions).
- **Consequence**: EPA: 2-year SOL (3 years willful), liquidated damages (double
  back pay), attorneys' fees, no EEOC charge required. States have enacted broader
  laws covering additional protected characteristics: California (all protected
  classes), Massachusetts (gender), Colorado (all protected classes), New York
  (all protected classes). Several states ban salary history inquiries to prevent
  perpetuation of pay gaps (California, New York, Massachusetts, Colorado, and
  approximately 20 other jurisdictions).

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (At-Will):** At-will status does not authorize discriminatory
  termination. Every termination of a protected-class member must be evaluated.
- **Employment (Severance):** Releases must satisfy OWBPA for ADEA claims and
  knowing/voluntary standards for Title VII and ADA claims. Defective releases
  are unenforceable as to the specific statute.
- **Employment (Wage-and-Hour):** EPA claims frequently accompany FLSA wage claims.
  Pay audits should address both compliance frameworks.
- **Employment (Immigration):** National origin discrimination in hiring and
  visa-related employment decisions must be managed under both Title VII and INA 274B.
- **Data Privacy:** ADA medical information must be in separate confidential files.
  GINA prohibits requesting genetic information. Background check processes must
  comply with FCRA and state ban-the-box laws.
- **Litigation:** Burden-shifting frameworks (McDonnell Douglas), statistical
  evidence, and class certification under Rule 23 for pattern-or-practice claims.

## Employee Benefits

# Employee Benefits

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting employment agreements with benefits
provisions, employee benefit plan documents, summary plan descriptions, severance
plans, or any matter involving ERISA compliance, retirement plans, health insurance
obligations, COBRA continuation coverage, ACA employer mandate requirements, or
HIPAA portability and nondiscrimination rules.

## Key Requirements

### ERISA Framework

- **What**: The Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA, 29 USC 1001 et seq.)
  governs employer-sponsored retirement plans (defined benefit, defined contribution,
  401(k), profit-sharing) and welfare benefit plans (health, disability, life
  insurance, severance).
- **Preemption**: ERISA preempts virtually all state laws that "relate to" an employee
  benefit plan. This means state-law contract, tort, and statutory claims for benefit
  denials are displaced, channeling all claims to federal court under ERISA Section 502.
- **Exemptions**: Government plans, church plans (unless they elect coverage), plans
  maintained solely to comply with workers' compensation, unemployment, or disability
  insurance laws, and plans maintained outside the United States for nonresident aliens.
- **Fiduciary duties**: Any person who exercises discretionary authority over plan
  management, administration, or asset disposition is a fiduciary. Fiduciaries must:
  (1) act solely in the interest of participants and beneficiaries, (2) act with the
  care, skill, prudence, and diligence of a prudent person, (3) diversify plan
  investments, and (4) act in accordance with plan documents (to the extent consistent
  with ERISA).
- **Consequence**: Personal liability for plan losses caused by fiduciary breach.
  DOL enforcement actions, participant lawsuits under ERISA 502(a)(2), and equitable
  relief under 502(a)(3). Fiduciary liability cannot be disclaimed, indemnified,
  or insured away by the plan sponsor (though fiduciary liability insurance is
  permissible if the insurer has subrogation rights).

### 401(k) and Retirement Plans

- **Annual contribution limits (2024)**:
  - Employee elective deferral: **$23,000** ($30,500 for age 50+)
  - Employer + employee combined (415 limit): **$69,000** ($76,500 for age 50+)
  - Highly compensated employee (HCE) threshold: earnings above **$155,000**
- **Nondiscrimination testing**: HCEs cannot defer disproportionately more than
  non-HCEs. Actual Deferral Percentage (ADP) test for employee contributions.
  Actual Contribution Percentage (ACP) test for employer matching. Testing is
  required annually unless the plan qualifies for safe harbor status.
- **Safe harbor provisions** (avoids nondiscrimination testing):
  - **Basic match**: 100% of first 3% of compensation + 50% of next 2%
  - **Enhanced match**: At least as generous as basic match (e.g., 100% of first 4%)
  - **Non-elective contribution**: 3% of compensation to all eligible employees
    regardless of whether they contribute
  - Annual safe harbor notice must be provided 30-90 days before plan year start.
    Safe harbor contributions must be **100% immediately vested**.
- **SECURE Act 2.0 (December 2022)**:
  - Mandatory automatic enrollment for new 401(k) and 403(b) plans established
    after December 29, 2022, at 3-10% deferral with annual 1% auto-escalation
    up to 10-15%.
  - Exempt: employers with 10 or fewer employees, businesses less than 3 years old,
    church plans, and government plans.
  - New Roth catch-up requirement: employees with wages exceeding $145,000 must
    make catch-up contributions on a Roth (after-tax) basis only (effective 2026).
- **Consequence**: Nondiscrimination test failure requires corrective distributions
  to HCEs (taxable) or qualified nonelective contributions (QNECs) to non-HCEs.
  Plan disqualification results in loss of tax-deferred status for all participants.
  DOL and IRS audit risk increases with plan asset size.

### ACA Employer Mandate (Employer Shared Responsibility)

- **What**: Applicable large employers (ALEs) must offer minimum essential coverage
  (MEC) that is affordable and provides minimum value (MV) to full-time employees
  (30+ hours/week) and their dependents (children under 26).
- **ALE threshold**: **50+ full-time equivalent employees** in the prior calendar year.
  FTEs are calculated by: (total monthly hours of all part-time employees) / 120,
  added to the count of full-time employees.
- **Penalties (2024)**:
  - **4980H(a)** ("sledgehammer"): If employer fails to offer MEC to at least
    **95% of full-time employees** and any employee receives a premium tax credit:
    **$2,970/year** per full-time employee (minus first 30). For an employer with
    200 full-time employees: up to $504,900 per year.
  - **4980H(b)** ("tack hammer"): If employer offers coverage but it is unaffordable
    (employee cost exceeds **8.39% of household income** in 2024) or does not provide
    minimum value (**60% actuarial value**): **$4,460/year** per employee who
    actually receives a premium tax credit.
- **Reporting**: Forms 1094-C and 1095-C filed annually with the IRS and furnished
  to employees. Late filing penalty: **$310/return** (2024), maximum
  **$3,783,500/year**.
- **Consequence**: ACA penalties are not deductible as business expenses. No penalty
  for failing to cover part-time employees (under 30 hours/week).

### COBRA Continuation Coverage

- **What**: The Consolidated Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act (29 USC 1161 et seq.)
  requires employers with **20+ employees** to offer continuation of group health
  plan coverage to qualified beneficiaries after qualifying events.
- **Qualifying events and continuation periods**:
  - Voluntary or involuntary termination (not gross misconduct) or reduction in
    hours: **18 months**
  - Employee entitled to Medicare: **18 months** for spouse/dependents
  - Divorce or legal separation: **36 months** for spouse/dependents
  - Death of covered employee: **36 months** for dependents
  - Dependent child losing eligibility: **36 months**
  - Social Security disability determination within first 60 days: extends to
    **29 months**
- **Election period**: Qualified beneficiary has **60 days** from the later of the
  qualifying event or the date the COBRA election notice is provided.
- **Premium**: Employer may charge up to **102%** of the full premium cost. For
  disability extension months 19-29: up to **150%**.
- **Notice requirements**: Employer must notify the plan administrator within
  **30 days** of a qualifying event (for employer-triggered events). Plan administrator
  must provide the election notice within **14 days** of receiving notice.
- **Consequence**: Failure to provide timely COBRA notice: excise tax of **$100/day**
  per affected individual (IRC 4980B, up to $200/day if more than one qualified
  beneficiary in the family), statutory penalty of up to **$110/day** per participant
  under ERISA 502(c), and liability for medical expenses incurred during the gap.
- **State mini-COBRA**: Many states extend COBRA-like rights to smaller employers
  and/or provide longer periods: California Cal-COBRA (2-19 employees, 36 months),
  New York (36 months), Connecticut (36 months for employers with 2+), Illinois
  (12 months for under 20 employees), Massachusetts (36 months for under 20).

### HIPAA Portability and Nondiscrimination

- **What**: HIPAA's group health plan provisions prohibit discrimination in enrollment
  eligibility or premiums based on health factors, and ensure portability when
  employees change jobs.
- **Health factors**: Health status, medical condition, claims experience, receipt of
  health care, medical history, genetic information, evidence of insurability,
  disability. Plans may not use these factors to deny enrollment or charge higher
  premiums to individuals.
- **Portability**: Group health plans that impose waiting periods must credit prior
  creditable coverage (reducing or eliminating the waiting period).
- **Consequence**: Violations of HIPAA nondiscrimination: excise tax of **$100/day**
  per affected individual under IRC 4980D, up to **$500,000/year** for failures
  that are not due to reasonable cause.

### FMLA and Benefits Continuation

- **What**: During FMLA leave, employers must maintain group health insurance on the
  same terms as if the employee continued working.
- **Threshold**: Employers with **50+ employees within 75 miles**. Eligible employees:
  12 months of service and 1,250 hours worked in prior 12 months. Up to 12 weeks
  unpaid leave per year (26 weeks for military caregiver leave).
- **Consequence**: Failure to maintain health benefits during FMLA leave violates the
  FMLA and may create independent ERISA claims. The employee must continue paying
  their share of premiums. If the employee does not return from leave (for reasons
  other than a serious health condition or other circumstances beyond control),
  the employer may recover its share of premiums paid during leave.

### Vesting Requirements

- **What**: ERISA mandates minimum vesting schedules for employer contributions to
  qualified retirement plans.
- **Schedules**: (1) **3-year cliff vesting** (0% until 3 years, then 100%), or
  (2) **6-year graded vesting** (20% at year 2, increasing 20%/year to 100% at
  year 6). Safe harbor contributions and employee elective deferrals must be
  **100% immediately vested**. Top-heavy plans must use 3-year cliff or 6-year
  graded (2-year/20% increments).
- **Consequence**: Plans that fail to meet minimum vesting schedules risk
  disqualification. Accelerated vesting is required upon plan termination or
  partial termination. A reduction in force affecting 20%+ of plan participants
  may constitute a partial termination triggering full vesting.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (Severance):** ERISA may govern severance plans if they constitute
  employee welfare benefit plans. COBRA is triggered by all termination events.
- **Employment (Discrimination-Harassment):** ADA requires reasonable accommodation
  in benefits. GINA restricts genetic information use in benefits eligibility.
- **Employment (Wage-and-Hour):** ACA full-time (30 hrs/week) differs from FLSA
  overtime (40 hrs/week). Time in mandatory benefits enrollment may be compensable.
- **Employment (Contractor Classification):** Reclassified workers may claim
  retroactive benefits eligibility. ACA FTE calculations must include them.
- **Data Privacy:** HIPAA privacy and security rules govern group health plan
  administration. Plan sponsors need administrative, technical, and physical safeguards.
- **Corporate (M&A):** Benefits plan succession, COBRA transition, and ACA reporting
  are critical M&A deal items. 401(k) plan mergers require DOL/IRS compliance.

## Immigration

# Immigration and Work Authorization

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting employment agreements for foreign
nationals, sponsoring work visas, conducting I-9 compliance audits, implementing
E-Verify, or any matter involving hiring authorization verification, visa-dependent
employment terms, or immigration-related compliance obligations.

## Key Requirements

### I-9 Employment Eligibility Verification

- **What**: The Immigration Reform and Control Act (IRCA, 8 USC 1324a) requires ALL
  employers to verify the identity and employment authorization of every person hired
  in the United States, regardless of citizenship or national origin, using Form I-9.
- **Timeline**:
  - Section 1 (employee portion): Must be completed by the employee **on or before
    the first day of employment for pay**.
  - Section 2 (employer portion): Must be completed by the employer **within 3
    business days** of the employee's first day of work. The employer must physically
    examine original documents (or use an authorized alternative procedure for
    remote hires under DHS authorization).
- **Acceptable documents**: Employee presents either one List A document (establishing
  both identity and work authorization, e.g., US passport, permanent resident card,
  EAD) OR a combination of one List B document (identity, e.g., driver's license)
  plus one List C document (work authorization, e.g., unrestricted Social Security
  card).
- **Anti-discrimination rule**: Employers must accept any document that appears
  reasonably genuine and relates to the employee. Employers must NOT request specific
  documents, reject facially valid documents, require additional documents beyond
  what is listed, or treat employees differently based on citizenship or national
  origin in the I-9 process (INA Section 274B, 8 USC 1324b).
- **Retention**: I-9 forms must be retained for the longer of **3 years after the
  date of hire** or **1 year after the date employment ends**.
- **Reverification**: Required when an employee's work authorization document expires.
  Must be completed on or before the expiration date. Do NOT reverify US citizens
  or permanent residents, or employees who presented List B identity documents that
  expired.

### I-9 Penalties (2024 Adjusted Amounts)

| Violation | Penalty Range |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Paperwork violations (first offense)** | **$252 to $2,507** per I-9 form |
| **Paperwork violations (second offense)** | **$1,161 to $2,507** per form |
| **Paperwork violations (third+ offense)** | **$1,740 to $2,507** per form |
| **Knowingly hiring unauthorized worker (first)** | **$698 to $5,579** per worker |
| **Knowingly hiring (second offense)** | **$5,579 to $13,946** per worker |
| **Knowingly hiring (third+ offense)** | **$8,369 to $27,894** per worker |
| **Document fraud (first offense)** | **$529 to $4,236** per document |
| **Document fraud (subsequent)** | **$4,236 to $10,591** per document |

- **Consequence**: ICE Notices of Inspection (I-9 audits) have increased significantly.
  Penalties are assessed per violation (per form), so a company with 500 employees
  and systemic errors across all forms faces exposure exceeding **$1.25 million**
  for paperwork violations alone. Criminal penalties apply for pattern or practice
  of knowingly hiring unauthorized workers: up to **$3,000 per worker** and **6 months
  imprisonment** per count.

### E-Verify

- **What**: Electronic system operated by DHS and SSA that compares I-9 information
  against government records to confirm employment eligibility.
- **Mandatory use requirements**:
  - **Federal contractors**: Executive Order 12989 (as amended by E.O. 13465)
    requires E-Verify for federal contracts above **$150,000** with a performance
    period exceeding **120 days** (FAR 52.222-54).
  - **State mandates**: States requiring E-Verify for all or most employers include
    Alabama, Arizona, Georgia, Mississippi, North Carolina, South Carolina,
    Tennessee, and Utah. Requirements vary by employer size and sector.
  - **Voluntary**: All other employers may voluntarily enroll.
- **Timeline**: E-Verify query must be initiated no later than **3 business days**
  after the employee's start date (concurrent with I-9 Section 2 completion).
- **Tentative Nonconfirmation (TNC)**: Employer must NOT terminate or take adverse
  action based on a TNC. Employee has **8 federal workdays** to begin the
  resolution process. Employer must continue employment during resolution.
- **Consequence**: Failure to follow E-Verify procedures can result in loss of federal
  contracts, referral for I-9 violations, and state-specific penalties. Arizona
  mandates license revocation for employers that knowingly employ unauthorized workers
  after E-Verify check (Legal Arizona Workers Act, upheld in Chamber of Commerce v.
  Whiting, 563 U.S. 582 (2011)).

### Common Work Visa Categories

**H-1B (Specialty Occupation)**
- **What**: Permits employment in a "specialty occupation" requiring a bachelor's
  degree or equivalent in a specific field directly related to the position.
- **Wage**: Employer must pay the **higher of the actual wage paid to similarly
  situated workers or the prevailing wage** for the occupation in the area. The
  statutory floor for H-1B dependent employers seeking exemption from displacement
  attestations is **$60,000/year**.
- **Annual cap**: **65,000** regular cap plus **20,000** for beneficiaries with a
  US master's degree or higher (total **85,000**). Cap-exempt employers: universities,
  nonprofit research organizations, government research organizations.
- **Duration**: Initial period up to **3 years**; extendable to a maximum of
  **6 years**. Extensions beyond 6 years available under AC21 Section 106 if a
  PERM labor certification or I-140 petition has been pending or approved for
  **365+ days**.
- **Labor Condition Application (LCA)**: Employer must file LCA with DOL attesting
  to: prevailing wage payment, no adverse effect on working conditions, no
  strike/lockout, and notice to workers (posted at worksite for 10 days or provided
  to bargaining representative).
- **Consequence**: LCA violations: up to **$1,000 per violation** for non-willful
  failures. Willful violations or misrepresentation: up to **$35,000 per violation**
  and **debarment from H-1B program for 2+ years**. H-1B dependent employers
  (15%+ of workforce are H-1B) face additional displacement and recruitment
  attestation obligations.

**L-1 (Intracompany Transferee)**
- **L-1A** (managers/executives): Up to **7 years**. Requires 1 continuous year of
  employment with qualifying related entity abroad within the preceding 3 years.
  No annual cap. May qualify for EB-1C green card without PERM labor certification.
- **L-1B** (specialized knowledge): Up to **5 years**. Same 1-year foreign
  employment requirement. High denial rates due to USCIS restrictive interpretation
  of "specialized knowledge."
- **No prevailing wage requirement** (unlike H-1B).

**O-1 (Extraordinary Ability/Achievement)**
- **What**: For individuals with extraordinary ability in sciences, arts, education,
  business, or athletics (O-1A), or extraordinary achievement in the motion picture
  or television industry (O-1B).
- **Threshold**: Must demonstrate sustained national or international acclaim by
  meeting at least **3 of 8 evidentiary criteria** (major awards, published material,
  scholarly articles, judging, original contributions, high salary, etc.) or
  providing comparable evidence.
- **Duration**: Up to **3 years** initially, with **1-year extensions** without limit.
  No annual cap. No prevailing wage requirement. Requires peer group advisory opinion.

**E-2 (Treaty Investor)**
- **What**: For nationals of treaty countries investing a "substantial" amount of
  capital in a US enterprise.
- **Investment**: No fixed statutory minimum. Must be substantial relative to the
  total enterprise cost. Typical minimum: **$100,000-$200,000** for service
  businesses; higher for capital-intensive enterprises. Investor must have
  operational control and the investment must be at risk.
- **Duration**: Up to **5 years** initially, with unlimited **2-year or 5-year
  extensions** as long as the enterprise is operational.
- **Limitation**: Does not lead directly to permanent residence. Available only to
  nationals of countries with an E-2 treaty with the United States.

**TN (USMCA/NAFTA Professionals)**
- **What**: For Canadian and Mexican citizens in specified professional occupations
  listed in the USMCA agreement (formerly NAFTA).
- **Duration**: Up to **3 years**, renewable indefinitely. Canadian citizens may
  apply directly at the port of entry (no petition required).
- **Limitation**: Limited to the USMCA profession list. Extended stays may raise
  immigrant intent concerns.

### PERM Labor Certification (Green Card Process)

- **What**: Most employment-based green card categories (EB-2, EB-3) require the
  employer to first obtain a PERM labor certification from DOL, demonstrating no
  qualified, willing, and able US workers are available for the position.
- **Recruitment period**: Mandatory recruitment must occur within **30-180 days**
  before PERM filing. Professional positions require at least 2 print advertisements,
  30-day job order, and 3 additional recruitment steps from a prescribed list.
- **Processing timeline**: Prevailing wage determination: **6-12 months**. PERM
  processing: **6-12+ months**. Audit rates are significant (approximately 30-40%
  of filings).
- **Cost allocation**: The employer must bear ALL costs of the PERM process. The
  employer may NOT require the employee to reimburse PERM-related costs, including
  attorney fees, recruitment expenses, and filing fees.
- **Consequence**: Filing errors, misrepresentations, or failure to document
  recruitment may result in denial, supervised recruitment (DOL monitors the process),
  or debarment from the PERM program. Fraudulent filings can result in criminal
  prosecution.

### Immigration-Related Discrimination

- **What**: INA Section 274B (8 USC 1324b) prohibits citizenship status discrimination
  and national origin discrimination in hiring, firing, and recruitment. It also
  prohibits unfair documentary practices and retaliation.
- **Coverage**: **4+ employees** for citizenship discrimination. **4-14 employees**
  for national origin (employers with 15+ employees are covered by Title VII instead).
  Enforced by the DOJ Immigrant and Employee Rights Section (IER).
- **Filing deadline**: Charge must be filed within **180 days** of the discriminatory
  act.
- **Common violations**: Requiring specific documents during I-9 (e.g., demanding a
  green card when a driver's license and Social Security card are presented),
  refusing to hire work-authorized non-citizens, imposing citizenship requirements
  not mandated by law or regulation, and reverifying documents that do not require
  reverification (e.g., US passport).
- **Consequence**: Civil penalties of **$2,000-$5,000** per individual for first
  offense (hiring/firing discrimination), escalating to **$10,000-$25,000** for
  third and subsequent offenses. Back pay, front pay, attorneys' fees, and
  injunctive relief also available.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (At-Will):** Employment authorization is a lawful condition of
  employment. Reverification on document expiration is required, but over-verification
  is discriminatory.
- **Employment (Contractor Classification):** I-9 is required only for employees.
  However, companies using staffing agencies must ensure the agency completes I-9
  for assigned workers.
- **Employment (Discrimination-Harassment):** I-9 document abuse violates INA 274B
  and may violate Title VII. National origin discrimination must be carefully
  managed in visa sponsorship decisions.
- **Employment (Wage-and-Hour):** H-1B LCA wage requirements may exceed minimum wage.
  Employers must pay H-1B workers the full required wage even during non-productive
  time ("benching" is prohibited).
- **Corporate (M&A):** Corporate restructuring may require amended or new H-1B
  petitions. Successor-in-interest rules determine whether employees need new
  visa petitions after an acquisition.
- **Data Privacy:** I-9 and visa documents contain sensitive PII requiring secure
  storage, limited access, and proper destruction after retention period.

## Non Competes

# Non-Competition and Post-Employment Restrictive Covenants

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting employment agreements, separation
agreements, equity award agreements, or any document containing non-competition,
non-solicitation, no-hire, or forfeiture-for-competition provisions. It also applies
when evaluating enforceability of existing restrictive covenants, choice-of-law
issues, or compliance with rapidly evolving state and federal restrictions.

## Key Requirements

### Types of Restrictive Covenants

**Non-Competition Agreements**
- **What**: Restrict the employee from working for a competitor or starting a
  competing business for a specified period after termination.
- **Threshold**: Must be reasonable in scope (duration, geography, activity), supported
  by adequate consideration, and necessary to protect a legitimate business interest
  (trade secrets, confidential information, customer relationships, specialized training).
- **Consequence**: Unenforceable covenants may be voided entirely (red pencil states)
  or reformed by the court (blue pencil/reformation states). Some states impose
  penalties on employers that attempt to enforce void non-competes.

**Non-Solicitation Agreements**
- **What**: Restrict solicitation of the employer's customers, clients, or employees.
- **Threshold**: Customer non-solicitation must typically be limited to customers with
  whom the employee had a material relationship during a defined look-back period
  (typically 12-24 months). Employee non-solicitation restricts recruiting former
  colleagues for a competing endeavor.
- **Consequence**: Generally more enforceable than non-competes because narrower. Courts
  in some jurisdictions treat overly broad non-solicitation as de facto non-competes.

**No-Hire / No-Raid Agreements**
- **What**: Restrict hiring or recruiting the employer's current employees.
- **Threshold**: Narrower than non-solicitation — applies to active recruitment, not to
  colleagues who independently seek out the departing employee.
- **Consequence**: Generally enforceable. Must be distinguished from employer-to-employer
  no-poach agreements, which are per se antitrust violations subject to DOJ
  criminal prosecution.

**Forfeiture-for-Competition Provisions**
- **What**: Employee forfeits unvested equity, deferred compensation, or other benefits
  upon engaging in competition.
- **Threshold**: Delaware (under the "employee choice doctrine") treats these as
  permissible conditions on at-will compensation, not restrictive covenants. California,
  Illinois, and Colorado treat them as non-competes subject to the same enforceability
  requirements and restrictions.
- **Consequence**: Jurisdictional analysis is essential. A forfeiture provision valid
  in Delaware may be entirely unenforceable in California.

### FTC Non-Compete Rule

- **What**: The FTC finalized a rule in April 2024 banning most non-competes nationwide.
- **Scope**: Existing non-competes for "senior executives" (earning >$151,164 annually
  AND in policy-making positions) would remain enforceable. No new non-competes
  could be entered for any worker. Does not apply to non-solicitation, NDA, or
  sale-of-business non-competes.
- **Status**: The Northern District of Texas (Ryan v. FTC, August 2024) issued a
  nationwide order setting aside the rule. The Eastern District of Pennsylvania
  (ATS Tree Services) reached the opposite conclusion. As of late 2024, the rule
  is NOT in effect. The matter is on appeal.
- **Consequence**: Counsel must monitor the appellate litigation continuously. If
  ultimately upheld, employers would need to provide notice to current employees
  that existing non-competes (for non-senior-executives) are no longer enforceable.

### Blue Pencil / Reformation Doctrine

- **Blue pencil states** (Texas, Georgia, Wisconsin): Courts may strike overbroad
  portions and enforce the remainder, but only by deleting — not rewriting — language.
- **Reformation states** (Massachusetts, New York, Ohio, Tennessee, Michigan): Courts
  may rewrite the covenant to make it reasonable and then enforce the modified version.
  Employers benefit from reformation but may face a "chilling effect" concern — overly
  broad drafting deters employees even if ultimately reformed.
- **Red pencil states** (Virginia, Nebraska, historically): Courts void the entire
  restriction if any portion is overbroad. No partial enforcement is permitted.
- **Consequence**: In red pencil states, an overbroad non-compete is worse than having
  no restriction. Drafting must be narrowly tailored.

### Choice-of-Law Issues

- **What**: Employers frequently select favorable governing law. Courts evaluate
  whether the chosen law has a substantial relationship to the parties and whether
  application would violate a fundamental policy of the employee's home state.
- **California rule**: California courts will not enforce non-competes against
  California-based employees under any governing law. Bus. & Prof. Code 16600 is a
  fundamental public policy that overrides contractual choice-of-law provisions.
  SB 699 (2024) codifies this and creates a private right of action. Application
  Group v. Hunter Group (1998); Edwards v. Arthur Andersen (2008).
- **Other restrictive states**: Colorado, Washington, and Oregon courts generally
  apply their own restrictive non-compete laws to in-state employees regardless
  of the contractual choice of law.
- **Consequence**: Choice-of-law clauses selecting Delaware, Texas, or other
  non-compete-friendly states will not save a non-compete when the employee resides
  and works in a restrictive state. The employee's work location typically controls.

### Consideration Requirements

- **At hire**: In most states, the offer of employment is sufficient consideration
  for a non-compete signed at the time of hiring.
- **After hire**: Many states require independent consideration beyond continued
  employment. Examples: promotion, raise, bonus, equity grant, access to confidential
  information. Illinois requires 2 years of continued employment as adequate
  consideration unless other independent consideration is provided. Texas requires
  the employer to provide confidential information, specialized training, or
  other enforceable obligations.
- **Consequence**: A non-compete signed mid-employment without new, independent
  consideration is unenforceable in many jurisdictions.

### Notice Requirements

- **Massachusetts**: Must be provided at earlier of formal offer or 10 business
  days before employment start (or at change in terms with garden leave).
- **Oregon**: Must be signed at least 2 weeks before employment start or upon
  bona fide advancement.
- **Washington**: Must disclose terms in writing at or before the time of hire,
  or with independent consideration upon a bona fide change in conditions.
- **Colorado**: Must be provided to the prospective worker before the worker
  accepts the offer of employment.
- **Consequence**: Failure to provide required notice renders the non-compete void
  in these states regardless of all other factors.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (At-Will):** Termination without cause voids non-competes in
  Massachusetts, Washington, and Oregon.
- **Employment (Contractor Classification):** Restrictive covenants for contractors
  may indicate misclassification and face weaker enforcement.
- **Employment (Severance):** Enhanced severance can serve as consideration for new
  or expanded non-competes in separation agreements.
- **Employment (Wage-and-Hour):** Illinois requires minimum $75K compensation;
  Washington $116,593; Colorado $123,750.
- **IP and Technology:** Trade secret protection (DTSA, UTSA) may provide alternative
  protection without non-compete enforceability issues.
- **Corporate (M&A):** Sale-of-business non-competes are broadly enforceable, typically
  allowing 3-5 year restrictions. FTC rule expressly exempts them.
- **Antitrust:** Employer-to-employer no-poach agreements are per se antitrust
  violations subject to DOJ criminal prosecution.
- **Litigation:** Disputes require expedited TRO/preliminary injunction proceedings.
  Forum and choice-of-law analysis are critical threshold issues.

## Severance

# Severance and Separation Agreements

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting separation agreements, release-of-claims
provisions, severance plans, reduction-in-force packages, WARN Act compliance, or any
matter involving employee termination with consideration in exchange for a release.
It governs OWBPA requirements for ADEA releases, WARN Act notice obligations, and
tax treatment of severance payments.

## Key Requirements

### OWBPA Release Requirements (Age 40+ Employees)

- **What**: The Older Workers Benefit Protection Act (OWBPA, 29 USC 626(f)) imposes
  mandatory requirements for a "knowing and voluntary" release of Age Discrimination
  in Employment Act (ADEA) claims. A release that fails any single OWBPA requirement
  is void as to ADEA claims — no ratification or cure is possible.
- **Individual termination requirements** (ALL must be satisfied):
  1. Written in a manner calculated to be understood by the individual employee
     (or the average individual eligible to participate)
  2. Specifically refers to rights or claims arising under the ADEA
  3. Does not waive rights or claims that may arise after the date of execution
  4. Supported by consideration beyond anything the employee is already entitled to
  5. Contains written advice to consult with an attorney before signing
  6. **21-day consideration period** before the employee signs
  7. **7-day revocation period** after signing (agreement is not effective until
     this period expires without revocation)
- **Group termination / exit incentive requirements** (all individual requirements
  above, plus):
  - **45-day consideration period** (replaces 21-day period)
  - Written disclosure identifying the **decisional unit** (the group from which
    selections were made), the **eligibility factors** and **time limits** for
    the program, and the **job titles and ages** of all individuals who were
    eligible or selected for the program and those who were not (the "disclosure
    schedule")
- **Consequence**: A release that fails any OWBPA requirement is void and
  unenforceable as to ADEA claims. Under Oubre v. Entergy Operations, 522 U.S. 422
  (1998), the employee may retain the severance payment and still bring an ADEA
  claim. The employer has no "tender back" or "ratification" defense. This makes
  OWBPA compliance a strict, non-negotiable requirement.

### General Release Requirements (Non-ADEA Claims)

- **What**: For claims other than ADEA (Title VII, ADA, state discrimination, breach
  of contract, tort claims), a valid release requires knowing and voluntary consent,
  adequate consideration, and compliance with applicable state law.
- **Totality-of-circumstances factors**: Clarity and specificity of the agreement,
  the employee's education and experience, the time given to consider, whether the
  employee was advised to consult an attorney, adequacy of consideration, whether
  the employee had meaningful input in negotiating terms.
- **Non-waivable claims**: Certain claims cannot be released regardless of the
  agreement: workers' compensation rights, unemployment insurance benefits, FLSA
  claims (in most circuits, without DOL or court approval), prospective discrimination
  claims, rights under NLRA Section 7, and OSHA whistleblower rights.
- **Consequence**: Overly broad or ambiguous releases may be challenged as
  unconscionable or void as against public policy. Courts closely scrutinize
  releases obtained in coercive circumstances (e.g., employee given no time to
  review, told they must sign immediately or lose the offer).

### WARN Act (Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification)

- **What**: Federal WARN (29 USC 2101 et seq.) requires covered employers to provide
  **60 calendar days** advance written notice before plant closings or mass layoffs.
- **Covered employers**: **100+ employees** (excluding part-time workers who average
  fewer than 20 hours/week). Part-time workers are counted only for determining
  triggering thresholds, not for coverage.
- **Triggering events**:
  - **Plant closing**: Shutdown of a single site or operating unit resulting in
    employment loss for **50+ employees** during any 30-day period
  - **Mass layoff**: Employment loss at a single site for **500+ employees**, OR
    **50-499 employees** constituting **33%+ of the active workforce**, during any
    30-day period
  - **90-day aggregation**: Separate layoffs within a 90-day period are aggregated
    and treated as a single event unless the employer demonstrates each was caused
    by a separate and distinct action or cause
- **Notice must be provided to**: (1) affected workers or their union representative,
  (2) the state rapid response dislocated worker unit, and (3) the chief elected
  official of the local government unit.
- **Limited exceptions** (employer bears the burden of proof):
  - **Faltering company**: Actively seeking capital, and giving notice would
    jeopardize the capital-raising effort (plant closings only, not mass layoffs)
  - **Unforeseeable business circumstances**: The closing/layoff is caused by
    business circumstances not reasonably foreseeable at the time notice would
    have been required
  - **Natural disaster**: Flood, earthquake, drought, storm, tidal wave, etc.
  Even under exceptions, the employer must provide as much notice as practicable
  and explain why the full 60 days was not given.
- **Consequence**: Employers who violate WARN owe **back pay and benefits for each
  day of the violation, up to 60 days**. Civil penalty of up to **$500/day**
  payable to the local government (curable by paying employees within 3 weeks of
  the closing/layoff). Attorneys' fees to prevailing plaintiffs. WARN claims are
  frequently brought as class actions.

### State Mini-WARN Acts

- **California (Cal-WARN)**: **75+ employees**; **60-day notice**; covers relocations
  of 100+ miles. Penalties: 60 days of back pay and benefits per employee.
- **New York**: **50+ employees** (at plant or within state); **90-day notice**.
  Penalties: 60 days of back pay plus $500/day to local government.
- **New Jersey**: **100+ employees**; **90-day notice**. Unique: mandatory severance
  of **1 week of pay per full year of service** if employer fails to give required
  notice (effective 2023).
- **Illinois**: **75+ full-time employees**; **60-day notice**. Applies to mass
  layoffs of 250+ employees or plant closings of 75+.
- **Massachusetts**: **50+ employees**; **90-day notice**. Director of Labor and
  Workforce Development may extend to 1 year for large layoffs.
- **Consequence**: State WARN remedies are cumulative with federal WARN. An employer
  may owe damages under both federal and state law for the same layoff event.

### Tax Treatment of Severance

- **What**: Severance payments are ordinary income subject to federal income tax
  withholding, FICA (Social Security and Medicare), and FUTA.
- **Key authority**: United States v. Quality Stores, Inc., 572 U.S. 141 (2014) —
  the Supreme Court held that severance payments to involuntarily separated employees
  are "wages" for FICA purposes, regardless of payment method (lump sum or
  installments). This resolved a circuit split.
- **Section 409A**: Severance may constitute "deferred compensation" subject to
  IRC 409A timing rules. Payments must comply with one of:
  - **Short-term deferral exception**: Paid by March 15 of the year following the
    year the right to payment is no longer subject to a substantial risk of forfeiture
  - **Two-year separation pay exception**: Total payments do not exceed the lesser
    of (a) 2x the employee's annual compensation, or (b) 2x the 401(a)(17) limit
    ($345,000 in 2024, meaning the exception covers up to **$690,000**)
  - **Specified employee delay**: Payments to "specified employees" of publicly
    traded companies triggered by separation from service must be delayed **6 months**
    following separation (or structured to avoid this delay under a permissible
    exception)
- **Consequence**: Section 409A violations trigger **20% additional tax** plus
  premium interest on the employee. Employer withholding obligations attach.
  Structuring severance within the safe harbors is critical.

### Restrictive Covenants in Severance Agreements

- **What**: Separation agreements frequently include or modify non-competition,
  non-solicitation, non-disparagement, confidentiality, and cooperation provisions.
- **NLRA Section 7 limits**: The NLRB's McLaren Macomb decision (2023) held that
  broad non-disparagement and confidentiality provisions in severance agreements
  may violate NLRA Section 7 by chilling employees' rights to engage in concerted
  activity. This applies to non-supervisory employees covered by the NLRA.
- **Consequence**: Severance agreements for non-supervisory employees should include
  carve-outs preserving Section 7 rights (right to discuss wages, working conditions,
  and terms of employment with co-workers, unions, government agencies, and in legal
  proceedings). Overly broad provisions may result in unfair labor practice charges
  and may void the restrictive provisions.

### Cooperation and Non-Disparagement Clauses

- **What**: Cooperation clauses require the departing employee to assist with
  litigation, investigations, and transition. Non-disparagement clauses restrict
  negative statements about the employer.
- **Threshold**: Cooperation clauses should specify reasonable scope (e.g., during
  business hours, with reimbursement of expenses). Non-disparagement must be mutual
  (employer and employee) and must carve out testimony under oath, communications
  with government agencies, and Section 7 protected activity.
- **Consequence**: One-sided non-disparagement (binding only the employee) may be
  unenforceable under McLaren Macomb for non-supervisory employees. SEC Rule 21F-17
  prohibits provisions that impede SEC whistleblower communications.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (At-Will):** Separation agreements formalize the end of at-will
  relationships. Constructive discharge may trigger WARN analysis.
- **Employment (Discrimination-Harassment):** Releases must satisfy OWBPA for ADEA
  claims. Discriminatory selection for layoff may accompany WARN claims.
- **Employment (Non-Competes):** Enhanced severance serves as consideration for
  restrictive covenants in jurisdictions requiring it.
- **Employment (Employee Benefits):** COBRA notice must accompany termination.
  Severance plans may be ERISA-governed. 401(k) distribution rules apply.
- **Corporate (M&A):** Change-of-control severance (golden parachutes) subject to
  IRC 280G: 20% excise tax on "excess parachute payments" exceeding 3x base amount.
- **Litigation:** Release adequacy is frequently litigated. WARN claims are often
  class actions with aggregate exposure in the millions.

## Wage And Hour

# Wage and Hour

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting compensation provisions, employment
agreements, independent contractor agreements, or any matter involving exempt/non-exempt
classification, overtime calculations, minimum wage compliance, meal and rest breaks,
tip credits, or wage payment practices. It governs federal requirements under the
Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) and key state variations.

## Key Requirements

### FLSA Exempt vs. Non-Exempt Classification

- **What**: Employees must be classified as exempt or non-exempt from FLSA overtime
  and minimum wage requirements. Exempt employees are not entitled to overtime pay;
  non-exempt employees must receive overtime for all hours worked over 40 per week.
- **Salary threshold**: $35,568/year ($684/week) under the current effective rule.
  The DOL proposed increasing to $55,068/year ($1,059/week) in 2024; the rule was
  vacated by the Eastern District of Texas in November 2024. Counsel must confirm
  the effective threshold at the time of classification.
- **Salary basis test**: Exempt employees must be paid on a salary basis — a
  predetermined, fixed amount not subject to reduction based on quality or quantity
  of work performed. Impermissible deductions from salary (other than safe harbor
  exceptions) can destroy the exemption for the entire class of employees in that
  job classification.
- **Duties tests** (salary alone is never sufficient):
  - **Executive**: Primary duty is management of the enterprise or a recognized
    department/subdivision. Regularly directs the work of 2+ other employees. Has
    authority to hire or fire, or significant influence on those decisions.
  - **Administrative**: Primary duty is office or non-manual work directly related to
    management or general business operations of the employer or its customers.
    Exercises discretion and independent judgment on matters of significance.
  - **Professional (Learned)**: Primary duty requires advanced knowledge in a field
    of science or learning customarily acquired through prolonged specialized study.
  - **Professional (Creative)**: Primary duty requires invention, imagination,
    originality, or talent in a recognized field of artistic or creative endeavor.
  - **Computer Employee**: Systems analyst, programmer, or software engineer. Must
    be paid at least $684/week salary OR $27.63/hour. Duties must involve systems
    analysis, design, testing, or programming.
  - **Outside Sales**: Primary duty is making sales or obtaining orders/contracts
    away from the employer's place of business. No salary threshold.
  - **Highly Compensated Employee (HCE)**: Total annual compensation of $107,432+.
    Must customarily and regularly perform at least one exempt duty of an executive,
    administrative, or professional employee. The DOL proposed increasing to
    $143,988; this was vacated in 2024.
- **Consequence**: Misclassification results in liability for unpaid overtime for
  up to 2 years (3 years if willful), plus liquidated damages equal to unpaid
  overtime (effectively doubling recovery), plus attorneys' fees. FLSA collective
  actions (opt-in) and state class actions (opt-out) create enterprise-wide risk.

### Overtime Requirements

- **Federal (FLSA)**: Non-exempt employees must receive at least 1.5x their regular
  rate for all hours worked over 40 in a single workweek. The workweek is a fixed,
  recurring 168-hour period. Employers may not average hours across multiple weeks
  (except in healthcare under the 8/80 exception, 29 USC 207(j)).
- **California daily overtime**: 1.5x after 8 hours in a single workday. 2x after
  12 hours in a workday. 1.5x for the first 8 hours on the 7th consecutive day
  worked in a workweek. 2x after 8 hours on the 7th consecutive day.
- **Other state variations**: Colorado requires overtime after 12 hours per day or
  40 hours per week. Alaska requires daily overtime after 8 hours. Nevada requires
  overtime after 8 hours per day at 1.5x for employees earning less than 1.5x the
  minimum wage.
- **Regular rate calculation**: Includes base hourly rate plus non-discretionary
  bonuses, commissions, shift differentials, and piece rates. Excludes: truly
  discretionary bonuses, gifts, holiday premium pay, employer contributions to
  bona fide benefit plans, and paid time off.
- **Consequence**: Overtime violations carry FLSA liquidated damages (equal to unpaid
  overtime, effectively doubling liability), plus mandatory attorneys' fees. California
  Labor Code 510 and 1194 provide additional penalties and fee-shifting.

### Minimum Wage

- **Federal**: $7.25/hour (unchanged since July 24, 2009).
- **State minimums (selected, 2024)**: California $16.00, Washington $16.28,
  New York $15.00 (NYC $16.00), Massachusetts $15.00, Colorado $14.42,
  Oregon $14.70 (Portland metro $15.45), Connecticut $15.69.
  Over 30 states and numerous cities exceed the federal minimum.
- **Tipped employees**: Federal tipped minimum wage is $2.13/hour, provided tips
  bring total compensation to at least $7.25/hour (tip credit of up to $5.12/hour).
  Seven states prohibit tip credits entirely (employer must pay full minimum wage
  before tips): California, Oregon, Washington, Alaska, Montana, Minnesota, Nevada.
- **Youth/training wage**: FLSA allows $4.25/hour for workers under 20 during their
  first 90 consecutive calendar days of employment. Rarely used in practice.
- **Consequence**: Minimum wage violations trigger back pay, FLSA liquidated damages
  (double back pay), attorneys' fees, and potential state penalties. California
  waiting time penalties of up to 30 days' wages for final pay violations
  (Labor Code 203) can multiply exposure significantly.

### Meal and Rest Breaks

- **Federal (FLSA)**: No meal or rest break requirement. However, short breaks
  (5-20 minutes) that are provided must be compensated as hours worked. Bona fide
  meal periods (typically 30+ minutes) may be unpaid if the employee is completely
  relieved of all duties.
- **California (most stringent)**:
  - 30-minute unpaid meal period before end of 5th hour of work.
  - Second 30-minute meal before end of 10th hour.
  - 10-minute paid rest break per 4 hours (or major fraction thereof).
  - **Penalty**: 1 additional hour of pay at the employee's regular rate for each
    workday a meal or rest break is missed. For 100 employees missing one meal break
    daily over a year, exposure exceeds $2.6M in premium pay alone.
- **New York**: 30-minute meal for shifts of 6+ hours spanning 11am-2pm. Additional
  20-minute meal for shifts before 11am through after 7pm. Additional 45-minute
  meal for shifts of 6+ hours between 1pm-6am in factories.
- **Washington**: 30-minute meal for 5+ hour shifts. 10-minute rest per 4 hours.
  Meal must be no later than 5 hours into the shift.
- **Illinois**: 20-minute meal break for 7.5+ hour shifts, no later than 5 hours
  after the start of the shift.
- **Colorado**: 30-minute uninterrupted meal for 5+ hour shifts. 10-minute paid
  rest per 4 hours.
- **Consequence**: Missed-break penalties accumulate rapidly, especially in California
  where they are assessed per employee per day. Class action exposure for systematic
  break violations is among the highest-value employment litigation categories.

### Wage Payment and Final Pay

- **What**: States regulate the timing, manner, and frequency of wage payments, and
  impose strict deadlines for final pay upon termination.
- **California**: Final pay due immediately upon involuntary termination. Due within
  72 hours if the employee quits without advance notice, or immediately if 72+ hours
  of notice was given. Waiting time penalty: average daily wages for each day late,
  up to a maximum of 30 days (Labor Code 201-203).
- **New York**: Final pay by the next regular payday. Wage payment frequency: weekly
  for manual workers, semimonthly for clerical and other workers.
- **Illinois**: Final pay due by the next regular payday.
- **Massachusetts**: Final pay due on the day of involuntary termination. Treble
  damages for late payment (Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 149, 150).
- **Consequence**: Missed final-pay deadlines trigger per-day penalties that can
  quickly exceed the underlying wages owed. California and Massachusetts have the
  most severe penalty structures.

### Recordkeeping

- **What**: FLSA requires employers to maintain payroll records for non-exempt
  employees for at least 3 years (general records) and 2 years (supplemental records
  including time cards and work schedules).
- **Required records**: Employee name, address, birth date (if under 19), sex,
  occupation, time/day workweek begins, hours worked per day and per week, basis
  of pay and regular rate, total daily or weekly straight-time earnings, total
  overtime earnings, all additions to or deductions from wages, total wages paid
  each pay period, date of payment and period covered.
- **Consequence**: Failure to maintain records shifts the burden of proof to the
  employer in wage disputes. Employees may estimate their hours worked using the
  "just and reasonable inference" standard (Anderson v. Mt. Clemens Pottery, 1946),
  which the employer must then disprove with records.

### Statute of Limitations and Damages

- **FLSA**: 2-year SOL for non-willful violations; 3 years for willful. Liquidated
  damages (equal to back pay, effectively doubling recovery) are presumed unless
  employer demonstrates good faith and reasonable grounds. Attorneys' fees mandatory
  for prevailing employees.
- **California**: 3-year SOL for statutory wage claims (Labor Code); 4 years under
  UCL (Bus. & Prof. Code 17200). PAGA penalties: $100/employee for initial violation
  per pay period, $200/employee for subsequent violations per pay period.
- **Collective / class actions**: FLSA permits collective actions (opt-in); state
  laws often permit Rule 23 class actions (opt-out). Aggregate exposure across
  hundreds or thousands of affected employees can reach tens of millions.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (Contractor Classification):** Reclassified workers are entitled to
  retroactive minimum wage, overtime, and break protections for the full period
  of misclassification.
- **Employment (At-Will):** Termination in retaliation for filing wage complaints
  violates FLSA 15(a)(3) anti-retaliation provisions and state whistleblower laws.
- **Employment (Employee Benefits):** Non-exempt employees must be compensated for
  mandatory training, meetings, and benefit-enrollment activities.
- **Employment (Discrimination-Harassment):** Equal Pay Act claims often accompany
  FLSA claims. Pay equity audits should address both frameworks simultaneously.
- **Data Privacy:** Biometric time-tracking systems may trigger BIPA (Illinois) or
  state biometric privacy laws requiring notice, consent, and data retention policies.

## Workplace Safety

# Workplace Safety

## Applicability

This sub-topic applies when reviewing or drafting workplace safety policies, employment
agreements with safety provisions, vendor/contractor safety requirements, or any
matter involving OSHA compliance, workplace injuries, recordkeeping obligations,
reporting requirements, or whistleblower retaliation claims related to safety concerns.

## Key Requirements

### OSHA General Duty Clause

- **What**: Section 5(a)(1) of the Occupational Safety and Health Act (29 USC 654(a)(1))
  requires each employer to furnish a place of employment free from recognized hazards
  that are causing or are likely to cause death or serious physical harm to employees.
- **Coverage**: Virtually all private-sector employers with **1+ employees**. Does not
  cover self-employed individuals, family farm workers employing only immediate family,
  or workplaces regulated exclusively by other federal agencies (mining under MSHA,
  nuclear under NRC, transportation under DOT/FAA/FRA).
- **State and local government employees**: Covered only in states with OSHA-approved
  state plans (28 states and territories operate approved plans).
- **"Recognized hazard" standard**: The hazard must be known to the employer or
  recognized by the employer's industry. Evidence includes: industry consensus
  standards (ANSI, NFPA), employer's own safety manuals and policies, prior incidents
  or near-misses at the same workplace, employee complaints, expert testimony, and
  common knowledge among safety professionals.
- **Consequence**: Citations under the General Duty Clause carry the same penalty
  structure as specific-standard violations. Compliance with specific OSHA standards
  is not a defense if additional recognized hazards exist beyond those standards.

### OSHA Penalties (2024 Adjusted Amounts)

| Violation Type | Penalty Range |
|---------------|---------------|
| **Serious** (substantial probability of death/serious harm; employer knew or should have known) | Up to **$16,131** per violation |
| **Other-than-serious** (related to safety/health but unlikely to cause death/serious harm) | Up to **$16,131** per violation |
| **Willful** (intentional disregard or plain indifference to the law) | **$11,524** minimum to **$161,323** maximum per violation |
| **Repeat** (substantially similar violation within prior 5 years) | Up to **$161,323** per violation |
| **Failure to abate** (not corrected by abatement date) | Up to **$16,131 per day** beyond the abatement date |
| **Posting requirement** (failure to post OSHA poster or citations) | Up to **$16,131** |

- **Penalty adjustment**: OSHA adjusts penalty amounts annually for inflation under
  the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act Improvements Act of 2015.
  The amounts above are the 2024 adjusted figures.
- **Penalty factors**: OSHA considers gravity of the violation, employer size (fewer
  employees may reduce penalties), good faith efforts, and history of violations.
- **Criminal penalties**: Willful violation causing death of an employee: up to
  **$250,000** (individual) or **$500,000** (organization) and up to **6 months
  imprisonment** (first offense). Doubled for repeat offenses. Making false
  statements in OSHA records: up to **$10,000** and **6 months imprisonment**
  (18 USC 1001). Advance notice of an OSHA inspection to the employer: up to
  **$1,000** and **6 months imprisonment** (29 USC 666(f)).

### Recordkeeping Requirements (OSHA 300 Log)

- **What**: Employers must maintain records of work-related injuries and illnesses
  on OSHA Forms 300 (Log of Work-Related Injuries and Illnesses), 300A (Summary),
  and 301 (Injury and Illness Incident Report).
- **Coverage threshold**: Required for employers with **10+ employees** at any time
  during the prior calendar year. Certain low-hazard industries (e.g., retail, finance,
  insurance, real estate) are partially exempt from routine recordkeeping but must
  still report fatalities and severe injuries.
- **Electronic submission**: Establishments with **250+ employees** in industries
  covered by the recordkeeping regulation must electronically submit Form 300A data
  to OSHA annually. Establishments with **20-249 employees** in designated
  high-hazard industries must also submit Form 300A data electronically.
- **Recordable criteria**: An injury or illness is recordable if it is work-related
  and results in: (1) death, (2) days away from work, (3) restricted work activity
  or job transfer, (4) medical treatment beyond first aid, (5) loss of consciousness,
  or (6) a significant injury/illness diagnosed by a physician or licensed healthcare
  professional (e.g., cancer, chronic irreversible disease, fractured bone, punctured
  eardrum).
- **Retention**: Records must be maintained for **5 years** following the end of the
  calendar year they cover. Employees and their representatives have the right to
  access the 300 Log and 301 forms.
- **Annual posting**: Form 300A (Annual Summary) must be posted in a conspicuous
  location from **February 1 through April 30** of each year.
- **Consequence**: Failure to maintain or post: up to **$16,131** per violation.
  Intentional falsification of records: criminal prosecution under 18 USC 1001.
  OSHA has increasingly used recordkeeping violations as a basis for programmed
  inspections and willful citations.

### Reporting Requirements

- **Fatality**: Must be reported to OSHA within **8 hours** of the employer learning
  of the death. Applies to any work-related fatality, including those occurring within
  30 days of the incident.
- **In-patient hospitalization**: Must be reported within **24 hours** of the
  employer's knowledge. Applies to hospitalization for treatment, not just observation.
- **Amputation**: Must be reported within **24 hours**. Includes amputations,
  including fingertip amputations with or without bone loss.
- **Loss of an eye**: Must be reported within **24 hours**.
- **How to report**: Telephone to the nearest OSHA Area Office, the OSHA 24-hour
  hotline (1-800-321-OSHA / 1-800-321-6742), or online at www.osha.gov. The report
  must include business name, names of affected employees, location, time, brief
  description, and contact person.
- **Consequence**: Failure to report: up to **$161,323** if classified as willful,
  or **$16,131** for other-than-serious violations. Late reporting is one of the
  most common bases for OSHA citations.

### OSHA Inspections

- **Priorities** (in order): (1) imminent danger situations, (2) fatality/catastrophe
  investigations, (3) employee complaints and referrals, (4) programmed/planned
  inspections targeting high-hazard industries.
- **Employer rights**: Employer may require an inspection warrant (Marshall v.
  Barlow's, Inc., 436 U.S. 307 (1978)). However, requesting a warrant may escalate
  the inspection scope and does not prevent OSHA from obtaining the warrant.
  Employer has the right to participate in the opening conference, accompany the
  compliance officer during the walk-around, and attend the closing conference.
- **Employee rights**: Employees may file confidential complaints triggering an
  inspection. An authorized employee representative has the right to participate
  in the walk-around inspection. Employers may not retaliate against employees who
  file complaints, participate in inspections, or exercise any right under the Act.
- **Contesting citations**: Employer must file a Notice of Contest within **15
  working days** of receiving the citation and proposed penalty. Failure to timely
  contest makes the citation final and unappealable. Contested cases are heard by
  the Occupational Safety and Health Review Commission (OSHRC).
- **Consequence**: An uncontested citation becomes a final order of OSHRC.
  Subsequent violations of the same or substantially similar standard within 5 years
  may be classified as "repeat" violations with penalties up to $161,323.

### Whistleblower Protection — Section 11(c)

- **What**: Section 11(c) of the OSH Act (29 USC 660(c)) prohibits any person from
  discharging or discriminating against an employee because the employee exercised
  rights under the Act.
- **Filing deadline**: **30 days** from the adverse retaliatory action. This is one
  of the shortest filing periods in all of employment law.
- **Protected activities**: Filing an OSHA complaint or requesting an inspection.
  Reporting a workplace injury or illness. Refusing to perform work that the employee
  reasonably believes poses an imminent danger of death or serious injury (and there
  is no reasonable alternative and insufficient time to address through regular
  channels). Participating in an OSHA inspection. Exercising any right under the Act,
  including requesting access to OSHA records and exposure/medical records.
- **Enforcement**: OSHA investigates and, if it finds merit, files suit on the
  employee's behalf in federal district court. There is no private right of action
  under Section 11(c) — the employee cannot sue independently; the claim must go
  through OSHA.
- **Remedies**: Reinstatement to former position, back pay with interest,
  compensatory damages, and reasonable attorneys' fees.
- **Consequence**: The 30-day window is strictly enforced. Employees who miss it
  lose their Section 11(c) claim, though state whistleblower laws (with longer
  filing periods) may provide alternative remedies.

### Frequently Cited OSHA Standards

- **Hazard Communication (29 CFR 1910.1200)**: Most frequently cited. Requires
  Safety Data Sheets (SDS), container labeling, and employee training for
  hazardous chemicals.
- **Fall Protection (29 CFR 1926.501)**: Construction: protection at **6+ feet**.
  General industry (29 CFR 1910.28): protection at **4+ feet**.
- **Scaffolding (29 CFR 1926.451)**: Requirements for scaffold design, erection,
  and use in construction.
- **Respiratory Protection (29 CFR 1910.134)**: Medical evaluation, fit testing,
  training required before employees use respirators.
- **Lockout/Tagout (29 CFR 1910.147)**: Procedures for controlling hazardous energy
  during equipment servicing and maintenance.
- **Ladders (29 CFR 1926.1053)**: Requirements for ladder use, design, and condition.

### State OSHA Plans

- **What**: **22 states and territories** operate OSHA-approved state plans covering
  both private-sector and state/local government employees. An additional **6 states**
  cover only state and local government employees.
- **Requirement**: State plans must be "at least as effective as" federal OSHA.
  Many states impose stricter requirements. California Cal/OSHA has additional
  standards for heat illness prevention (outdoor and indoor), injury and illness
  prevention programs (IIPP, mandatory for all employers), and aerosol transmissible
  diseases.
- **Federal OSHA monitoring**: Federal OSHA retains authority to monitor state plans,
  conduct federal enforcement if the state fails to act, and revoke state plan
  approval.
- **Consequence**: In state-plan states, the state agency (not federal OSHA) conducts
  inspections and enforces standards. Employers operating in multiple states must
  comply with whichever standard (federal or state) is more protective.

## Interaction with Other Areas

- **Employment (At-Will):** Terminating an employee for reporting safety concerns
  violates Section 11(c) and state whistleblower statutes regardless of at-will.
- **Employment (Contractor Classification):** Multi-employer worksite doctrine creates
  obligations for host employers, controlling employers, and exposing employers
  regarding contractor worker safety.
- **Employment (Wage-and-Hour):** Time spent on mandatory safety training is
  compensable under FLSA. Unpaid safety training may create wage-hour violations.
- **Employment (Discrimination-Harassment):** Safety-related retaliation may overlap
  with Title VII or state anti-retaliation protections.
- **Data Privacy:** OSHA recordkeeping and electronic reporting involve employee
  medical information subject to confidentiality requirements.
- **Litigation:** OSHA citations are not per se evidence of negligence in tort
  litigation but may be admitted as evidence of the standard of care. Workers'
  compensation generally provides the exclusive remedy, with exceptions for
  intentional employer misconduct in some states.
