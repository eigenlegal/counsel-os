## Audit Rights

# Audit Rights — Clause Library

## General Audit Rights

### Standard
> Customer (or its designated independent third-party auditor) may audit Vendor's compliance with this Agreement no more than once per twelve (12) month period, upon at least thirty (30) days' prior written notice. The audit may include review of Vendor's records, processes, systems, and facilities relevant to the Services. Vendor shall cooperate with the audit and provide reasonable access during normal business hours. The audit shall be conducted in a manner that minimizes disruption to Vendor's operations.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Customer may audit Vendor's compliance with this Agreement at any time upon fifteen (15) days' notice. Audits may include on-site inspections, record reviews, system testing, and interviews with Vendor personnel. The frequency shall not be limited if: (a) a prior audit revealed material non-compliance; (b) a security incident or data breach has occurred; or (c) a regulatory authority requires or requests an audit. Customer may use its own personnel or independent third-party auditors. Vendor shall provide full cooperation and access to all relevant records, systems, and personnel without restriction.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Customer may audit Vendor's compliance with this Agreement no more than once per twelve (12) month period, upon at least sixty (60) days' prior written notice, during normal business hours, and conducted by an independent third-party auditor mutually agreed upon by the parties and bound by confidentiality obligations. The audit scope shall be limited to records directly related to the Services. Customer shall bear all costs of the audit unless the audit reveals material non-compliance by Vendor.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Customer may audit Vendor's compliance with this Agreement once per year upon reasonable notice. Vendor shall cooperate and provide access to relevant records.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language requiring mutual agreement on auditors, limiting to annual audits regardless of circumstances, and imposing costs on Customer creates barriers to exercising audit rights. Push for customer-selected auditors and trigger-based additional audits (post-breach, regulatory request). Cost allocation should default to Vendor when non-compliance is found.

## Scope of Audit

### Standard
> The audit may cover Vendor's compliance with all obligations under this Agreement, including but not limited to: (a) data protection and security obligations; (b) service level commitments; (c) financial records related to pricing and fee calculations; (d) sub-processor management and oversight; (e) regulatory compliance obligations; and (f) insurance coverage.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> The audit may cover any and all aspects of Vendor's performance, including: (a) data protection, security, and privacy compliance; (b) service levels and uptime; (c) financial records, invoicing accuracy, and fee calculations; (d) sub-processor compliance and oversight; (e) regulatory compliance; (f) insurance coverage and claims history; (g) business continuity and disaster recovery readiness; (h) employee training and background checks; (i) physical and logical access controls; and (j) incident response capabilities. Vendor shall not assert trade secret, proprietary, or other objections to limit the scope of the audit.

### Vendor-Favorable
> The audit shall be limited to Vendor's compliance with the data protection obligations set forth in this Agreement and shall not extend to Vendor's financial records, proprietary processes, trade secrets, or information relating to other customers. The audit scope shall be agreed in writing by the parties prior to the audit.

### Minimum Acceptable
> The audit may cover Vendor's compliance with data protection, security, and service level obligations under this Agreement.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language restricting audit scope to data protection only and excluding financial records eliminates the ability to verify fee accuracy and pricing compliance. Push for at least data protection, security, SLA, and financial record coverage. Pre-approval of scope by vendor creates a veto right over the audit.

## Cost Allocation

### Standard
> Customer shall bear the costs of conducting the audit, including fees of any third-party auditor, unless the audit reveals a material breach of Vendor's obligations, in which case Vendor shall reimburse Customer for reasonable audit costs. Vendor shall bear its own internal costs of cooperating with the audit.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall bear all costs of facilitating the audit, including making personnel and records available. Customer shall bear the costs of its own auditors. If the audit reveals material non-compliance, Vendor shall reimburse Customer for all reasonable audit costs, including third-party auditor fees, travel, and internal time. If the audit reveals a security deficiency, Vendor shall remediate at its own expense within thirty (30) days.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Customer shall bear all costs of the audit, including third-party auditor fees, travel expenses, and compensation for Vendor's time spent facilitating the audit at Vendor's standard professional services rates. If the audit exceeds two (2) business days, Customer shall reimburse Vendor for additional time at Vendor's hourly rates.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Each party shall bear its own costs of the audit, except that if material non-compliance is found, Vendor shall reimburse Customer's reasonable audit costs.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language charging Customer for Vendor's time at professional services rates effectively imposes a penalty for exercising audit rights. Push for Vendor to bear its own facilitation costs and for cost-shifting to Vendor upon finding material non-compliance.

## Remediation

### Standard
> If the audit identifies non-compliance with Vendor's obligations, Vendor shall prepare a remediation plan within fifteen (15) business days of receiving the audit findings. Vendor shall implement the remediation plan within a mutually agreed timeframe, not to exceed sixty (60) days. Vendor shall provide Customer with evidence of remediation upon completion. If Vendor fails to remediate within the agreed timeframe, Customer may exercise its termination rights under this Agreement.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall remediate any non-compliance identified in the audit within thirty (30) days at Vendor's sole expense. Vendor shall provide Customer with a written remediation plan within five (5) business days, weekly progress updates, and evidence of completion. Customer may conduct a follow-up audit within sixty (60) days to verify remediation at Vendor's expense. Material non-compliance that is not remediated within the specified period shall constitute a material breach. Critical security deficiencies shall be remediated immediately.

### Vendor-Favorable
> If the audit identifies areas of non-compliance, Vendor shall use commercially reasonable efforts to address such areas within a reasonable timeframe. Vendor shall not be required to implement any remediation that would require material changes to its standard operating procedures or infrastructure. The parties shall discuss any findings in good faith and agree on appropriate next steps.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor shall remediate any material non-compliance identified in the audit within a commercially reasonable timeframe and provide evidence of remediation.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language with no firm remediation timeline and no obligation to make material changes essentially makes the audit right toothless. Push for specific remediation deadlines, verification audits, and consequences for failure to remediate. Critical security deficiencies should require immediate action, not "commercially reasonable efforts."

## Records Retention

### Standard
> Vendor shall maintain complete and accurate records related to its performance of the Services, including records of data processing activities, security measures, incident logs, and financial transactions, for the duration of the Agreement and for a period of three (3) years thereafter. Such records shall be maintained in a format that permits audit and inspection.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall maintain complete and accurate records related to the Services for the Term and five (5) years thereafter, including: (a) data processing logs; (b) access logs; (c) security incident records; (d) sub-processor compliance documentation; (e) employee training records; (f) financial and invoicing records; and (g) all correspondence with regulators. Records shall be maintained in electronic, searchable format. Vendor shall not destroy any records during the retention period without Customer's prior consent. Vendor shall provide records or copies to Customer upon request within ten (10) business days.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Vendor shall maintain records related to the Services in accordance with Vendor's standard records retention policies. Vendor's standard retention period for operational records is twelve (12) months following the end of each calendar year. Vendor shall have no obligation to maintain records beyond its standard retention period.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor shall maintain records related to the Services for the Term and at least two (2) years thereafter, and make them available for audit upon reasonable request.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable 12-month retention is insufficient for most regulatory requirements (GDPR requires records during processing plus a reasonable period; financial regulations typically require 5-7 years). Push for at minimum a 3-year post-termination retention period. Ensure records are in auditable format, not just whatever Vendor's standard system produces.

## Financial Audit

### Standard
> Customer may audit Vendor's invoicing accuracy and usage metering no more than once per twelve (12) month period, upon at least thirty (30) days' prior written notice. The financial audit may include review of: (a) invoices and supporting calculations; (b) usage metering data and methodology; (c) billing system records; and (d) any variable fee calculations (e.g., per-user, per-transaction, consumption-based). Vendor shall provide reasonable access to billing data and knowledgeable personnel. If the audit reveals that Customer has been overcharged by more than five percent (5%) in any audited period, Vendor shall promptly refund the overpayment and reimburse Customer for reasonable audit costs. If the audit reveals underpayment, Customer shall promptly pay the deficiency.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Customer may audit Vendor's invoicing, fee calculations, and usage metering at any time upon fifteen (15) days' notice, and without frequency limitation if a prior financial audit revealed a discrepancy exceeding three percent (3%). The audit scope shall include: (a) all invoices issued during the audit period; (b) raw usage and metering data, including logs, timestamps, and the algorithms or formulas used to calculate fees; (c) any rate changes, discount applications, and credit calculations; (d) sub-contractor and third-party costs passed through to Customer; and (e) reconciliation of actual usage against contracted commitments. Vendor shall provide Customer with self-service access to real-time usage dashboards and downloadable billing data in machine-readable format (CSV or JSON). If the audit reveals any overpayment, regardless of amount, Vendor shall refund the full overpayment within fifteen (15) business days plus interest at the lesser of 1.5% per month or the maximum legal rate from the date of overpayment. If the audit reveals overcharges exceeding five percent (5%) in any twelve-month period, Vendor shall also reimburse all reasonable audit costs and, at Customer's election, convert variable-fee components to fixed pricing for the remainder of the Term. Vendor shall maintain complete billing records for the Term and three (3) years thereafter.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Customer may request a financial review of invoicing accuracy once per year by submitting a written request to Vendor's finance department. Vendor shall provide a summary reconciliation within sixty (60) days. If Customer disputes the reconciliation, the parties shall attempt to resolve the dispute in good faith. Customer shall not have direct access to Vendor's billing systems, metering tools, or internal financial records. Any overpayment confirmed by Vendor shall be credited against future invoices. Underpayments shall be invoiced immediately and are due within fifteen (15) days.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Customer may audit Vendor's invoices and usage metering once per year upon reasonable notice. If the audit reveals overcharges exceeding five percent (5%), Vendor shall refund the overpayment and reimburse reasonable audit costs.

### Notes
Financial audit rights are essential for consumption-based and variable-fee contracts, where metering errors can result in significant overcharges. Key issues: (1) Access to metering data — Vendor-Favorable language providing only a "summary reconciliation" without access to underlying data makes it impossible to independently verify charges. Push for access to raw usage data and metering methodology. (2) Overpayment threshold — a 5% threshold for cost-shifting is standard, but any confirmed overpayment should be refunded regardless of percentage. (3) Refund vs. credit — refunds should be in cash, not credits against future invoices; credits lock the customer into continued spending. (4) Metering transparency — real-time usage dashboards reduce the need for formal audits and catch errors earlier. (5) Underpayment discovery — ensure that underpayment obligations are symmetric with overpayment protections. (6) Interest on overpayments — vendors have held customer money improperly; interest provides a deterrent. (7) Billing record retention — ensure records exist long enough to audit historical periods.

## Regulatory Audit

### Standard
> Customer's regulatory and supervisory authorities (including but not limited to banking regulators, data protection authorities, and securities regulators) shall have the right to audit, examine, and inspect Vendor's facilities, systems, records, and personnel relevant to the Services, to the extent required by applicable law or regulation. Vendor shall cooperate fully with such regulatory audits and shall not restrict or impede the regulatory authority's access. Customer shall provide Vendor with reasonable advance notice of a regulatory audit to the extent permitted by the regulatory authority. Vendor shall treat the existence and content of regulatory audits as confidential.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor acknowledges that Customer is subject to oversight by regulatory and supervisory authorities, and that such authorities may require direct access to Vendor's systems, facilities, records, and personnel. Vendor agrees that: (a) Customer's regulators (including, without limitation, any federal or state banking regulator, the SEC, FINRA, OCC, FDIC, CFPB, state attorneys general, data protection supervisory authorities, and any successor agencies) shall have the right to audit, examine, and inspect Vendor to the same extent as if the Services were performed by Customer directly; (b) Vendor shall not require any regulatory authority to sign an NDA, execute an access agreement, or satisfy any preconditions as a prerequisite to conducting an audit; (c) Vendor shall provide the regulatory authority with unrestricted access to all relevant facilities, systems, records, personnel, and sub-processors; (d) Vendor shall respond to regulatory requests within the timeframe specified by the authority, or if none is specified, within five (5) business days; (e) Vendor shall designate a regulatory affairs contact to coordinate audit requests; and (f) Vendor's obligations under this section shall survive termination for a period of five (5) years. Vendor shall not assert that any record, system, or facility is outside the scope of a regulatory audit based on proprietary or trade secret claims. Any contractual limitation on audit frequency shall not apply to audits conducted by or at the direction of a regulatory authority.

### Vendor-Favorable
> To the extent required by applicable law, Customer's regulators may audit Vendor's compliance with data protection obligations. Vendor shall cooperate with regulatory audits to the extent legally required, subject to: (a) at least sixty (60) days' advance notice; (b) a mutually agreed scope limited to records directly related to the Services; (c) the regulatory authority's execution of Vendor's standard confidentiality agreement; and (d) Vendor's right to have legal counsel present during all audit activities. Customer shall bear all costs associated with facilitating a regulatory audit, including Vendor's internal costs at standard professional services rates.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Customer's regulatory authorities shall have the right to audit Vendor to the extent required by applicable law. Vendor shall cooperate with such audits and provide access to relevant records and personnel.

### Notes
Regulatory audit rights are non-negotiable for customers in regulated industries. Key issues: (1) Scope of access — regulators typically expect the same access they would have if the customer performed the function in-house. Vendor-Favorable limitations on scope or preconditions (NDAs, agreed scope) are often unacceptable to regulators and may put the customer in violation of its regulatory obligations. (2) Advance notice — while reasonable notice is preferred, regulators sometimes conduct unannounced examinations; the contract should not prohibit this. (3) Cost allocation — charging the customer for regulatory audit facilitation is inappropriate; regulatory cooperation is a cost of doing business as a service provider to regulated entities. (4) Sub-processor access — regulators may need to audit sub-processors; ensure the contract provides for this. (5) Survival — regulatory obligations typically extend beyond the contract term. (6) OCC Bulletin 2013-29 — for bank customers, the OCC requires that third-party contracts provide for direct regulatory access, and the contract must not restrict the bank regulators' authority in any way.

## Remote vs. On-Site

### Standard
> Audits may be conducted remotely or on-site at Vendor's facilities, at Customer's election. Remote audits may include review of records transmitted electronically, video conference interviews with Vendor personnel, and remote access to relevant systems. On-site audits may be conducted at Vendor's principal offices or any facility where Customer Data is stored or processed. Customer shall conduct audits in a manner that minimizes disruption to Vendor's operations. On-site audits shall be conducted during normal business hours unless otherwise agreed.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Customer may conduct audits remotely or on-site, at Customer's sole election. Remote audits may include: (a) review of electronically transmitted records; (b) video conference or telephone interviews with Vendor personnel; (c) remote access to Vendor's monitoring and logging systems; and (d) automated security scanning of Vendor's externally facing systems (with reasonable coordination). On-site audits may be conducted at any Vendor facility where Customer Data is stored, processed, or accessible, including sub-processor facilities. On-site audits shall be triggered without limitation upon: (i) a security incident or data breach involving Customer Data; (ii) a material audit finding in a prior remote audit; (iii) a request by Customer's regulatory authority; or (iv) Vendor's failure to provide adequate documentation for a remote audit. Vendor shall provide reasonable workspace, network access, and access to relevant personnel during on-site audits. On-site audits may be conducted during or outside business hours if the nature of the audit requires it (e.g., physical security assessments). Vendor shall not require Customer's auditors to sign any waiver of liability, limitation of access, or additional agreement beyond a reasonable confidentiality undertaking as a condition of on-site access.

### Vendor-Favorable
> All audits shall be conducted remotely unless Customer demonstrates that a remote audit is insufficient to achieve the audit objectives, in which case on-site access may be granted subject to Vendor's prior written approval. On-site audits shall be limited to Vendor's principal office, conducted during normal business hours, and shall not exceed two (2) business days. Customer's auditors must comply with Vendor's facility access policies, including background checks, and must sign Vendor's standard visitor NDA and liability waiver. Vendor may designate specific areas as restricted and may redact or withhold information relating to other customers or proprietary technology.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Audits may be conducted remotely or, when reasonably necessary, on-site at Vendor's facilities during normal business hours upon reasonable notice.

### Notes
The choice between remote and on-site audits involves practical and legal considerations. Key issues: (1) Default mode — remote audits are more cost-effective and less disruptive; they should be the default for routine compliance checks. However, the right to conduct on-site audits must be preserved for situations where remote review is insufficient. (2) On-site triggers — Vendor-Favorable language requiring the customer to justify on-site access creates an unnecessary barrier. On-site audits should be available at customer election, with specific triggers (breach, regulatory request, material findings) that override any frequency limitations. (3) Facility scope — limiting on-site access to the "principal office" (Vendor-Favorable) may exclude data centers, disaster recovery sites, and sub-processor facilities where Customer Data actually resides. (4) Access conditions — requiring background checks and liability waivers for auditors is reasonable for physical security but should not be used as a barrier. (5) Restricted areas — vendors may legitimately restrict access to shared infrastructure areas, but restrictions should not prevent assessment of controls relevant to Customer Data. (6) Sub-processor facilities — for critical sub-processors, the right to audit on-site should flow through to the sub-processor's facilities.
