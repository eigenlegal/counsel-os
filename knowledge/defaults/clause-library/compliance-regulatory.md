## Anti-Corruption / FCPA

### Standard
> Each party represents, warrants, and covenants that it shall comply with all applicable anti-corruption laws, including the U.S. Foreign Corrupt Practices Act ("FCPA") and the UK Bribery Act 2010. Neither party shall, directly or indirectly, offer, pay, promise to pay, or authorize the payment of any money or anything of value to any government official, political party, or candidate for the purpose of influencing any act or decision or securing any improper advantage. Each party shall maintain accurate books and records in accordance with applicable law.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor represents, warrants, and covenants that it shall comply with all applicable anti-corruption laws, including the FCPA, UK Bribery Act, and all local anti-bribery laws. Vendor has not and shall not offer, pay, or authorize payment of anything of value to any government official to influence any act or decision in connection with the Services. Vendor shall maintain an anti-corruption compliance program. Vendor shall promptly notify Customer of any actual or suspected violation and shall cooperate fully with any investigation. A breach of this section shall constitute a material, incurable breach entitling Customer to immediate termination.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Each party shall comply with applicable anti-corruption laws in connection with this Agreement. Each party represents that it has not been convicted of or pleaded guilty to any offense involving bribery or corruption.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Each party shall comply with all applicable anti-corruption laws, including the FCPA and UK Bribery Act, in the performance of its obligations under this Agreement.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable brevity may be insufficient in high-risk geographies or when using agents, distributors, or sub-contractors. Push for notification obligations and termination rights for violations. The Aggressive version is appropriate when vendor operations touch government customers or high-corruption-risk jurisdictions. Immediate termination for FCPA violations should be non-negotiable.

## Export Controls and Sanctions

### Standard
> Each party shall comply with all applicable export control laws and regulations, including the U.S. Export Administration Regulations ("EAR"), the International Traffic in Arms Regulations ("ITAR"), and all applicable economic sanctions programs administered by the U.S. Office of Foreign Assets Control ("OFAC"). Neither party shall export, re-export, or transfer any technical data, software, or services provided under this Agreement to any country, entity, or person prohibited or restricted under applicable export control or sanctions laws.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor represents and warrants that: (a) it is not located in, organized under the laws of, or owned or controlled by nationals of any country subject to comprehensive U.S. sanctions; (b) it is not listed on any U.S. government denied or restricted party list; (c) the Services do not contain encryption or other technology subject to export licensing requirements that would restrict Customer's intended use; and (d) it shall comply with all applicable export controls and sanctions. Vendor shall screen all sub-processors and sub-contractors against applicable denied-party lists. A breach of this section shall be grounds for immediate termination.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Customer shall comply with all applicable export control and sanctions laws and shall not access or use the Services from any embargoed country or in violation of any applicable sanctions. Customer represents that it is not listed on any U.S. government denied or restricted party list. Vendor shall have no liability arising from Customer's failure to comply with export controls or sanctions.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Each party shall comply with all applicable export control laws and economic sanctions in connection with this Agreement.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language placing all export/sanctions compliance burden on Customer is one-sided. Both parties should bear compliance obligations appropriate to their role. Customer-side representations are reasonable, but push for reciprocal vendor representations, particularly regarding denied-party screening. Critical for cross-border transactions and cloud services with global infrastructure.

## ADA / Accessibility Compliance

### Standard
> Vendor shall use commercially reasonable efforts to ensure that the Services comply with the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 Level AA, as published by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C). Vendor shall provide a current Voluntary Product Accessibility Template (VPAT) or equivalent accessibility conformance report upon Customer's request. Vendor shall remediate material accessibility deficiencies identified by Customer within a commercially reasonable timeframe.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor represents and warrants that the Services comply with WCAG 2.1 Level AA and all applicable accessibility laws, including Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act. Vendor shall provide an updated VPAT annually and upon each major release. Vendor shall remediate accessibility deficiencies at no additional charge within sixty (60) days of notification. Vendor shall indemnify Customer against third-party accessibility claims arising from the Services.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Vendor shall use reasonable efforts to address accessibility in the development of the Services. Customer acknowledges that no software can guarantee full compliance with all accessibility standards. Vendor shall provide a VPAT upon written request, which shall be provided for informational purposes only and shall not constitute a warranty of compliance.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor shall use commercially reasonable efforts to make the Services accessible in accordance with WCAG 2.1 Level AA standards.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language with "reasonable efforts" and an express disclaimer that the VPAT is not a warranty provides minimal protection. Push for at least a WCAG 2.1 Level AA commitment with a remediation obligation. Accessibility claims are increasing, and public-sector or regulated customers may have mandatory accessibility requirements. The Aggressive version with indemnification is appropriate for customer-facing applications.

## Anti-Money Laundering / KYC

### Standard
> Each party shall comply with all applicable anti-money laundering laws and regulations, including the Bank Secrecy Act, the USA PATRIOT Act, and applicable "know your customer" requirements. Each party shall maintain an anti-money laundering compliance program appropriate to the nature and scope of its business. Upon reasonable request, each party shall provide information and documentation necessary to satisfy the other party's KYC obligations.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor represents and warrants that it maintains a comprehensive anti-money laundering program that includes customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, suspicious activity reporting, and employee training. Vendor shall promptly notify Customer if it becomes aware of any suspicious activity involving Customer's accounts or transactions processed through the Services. Vendor shall cooperate with Customer's AML compliance program and regulatory examinations. A material failure to comply with AML obligations shall constitute grounds for immediate termination.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Customer shall comply with all applicable anti-money laundering and KYC laws in connection with its use of the Services. Customer shall be solely responsible for conducting due diligence on its own customers and transactions. Vendor shall have no AML compliance obligation with respect to Customer's end users or transactions.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Each party shall comply with all applicable anti-money laundering laws and regulations in connection with this Agreement.

### Notes
AML provisions are primarily relevant for fintech, payment processing, and financial services agreements. Vendor-Favorable language disclaiming all responsibility for AML compliance may be unacceptable for regulated financial institutions. Push for mutual compliance obligations and cooperation commitments. The Aggressive version is appropriate for payment processors or platforms that handle financial transactions.

## Data Localization

### Standard
> Vendor shall process and store Customer Data within the geographic regions specified in the applicable Order Form or Data Processing Agreement. Vendor shall not transfer or process Customer Data outside the specified regions without Customer's prior written consent. If applicable law requires data localization, Vendor shall comply at no additional cost to Customer.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall process and store all Customer Data exclusively within [specified country/region]. Vendor shall not transfer, access, or make Customer Data accessible from outside the specified region, including for support, maintenance, or troubleshooting purposes, without Customer's prior written consent. Vendor shall implement technical controls to enforce geographic restrictions. Vendor shall provide evidence of data localization compliance upon Customer's request.

### Vendor-Favorable
> The Services are provided using a global cloud infrastructure. Vendor shall use commercially reasonable efforts to store primary copies of Customer Data in the region specified in the Order Form, but may process Customer Data in other regions as necessary for service delivery, disaster recovery, and maintenance. Customer acknowledges that metadata, logs, and support data may be processed outside the specified region.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor shall store Customer Data within the geographic region specified in the Order Form, subject to Vendor's disaster recovery and business continuity requirements.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language allowing global processing "as necessary for service delivery" with carve-outs for metadata, logs, and support data may undermine data localization commitments. Push for clearly defined exceptions and technical controls. Data localization requirements are becoming more common (GDPR, China, Russia, India) and should be addressed explicitly.

## Regulatory Investigation Cooperation

### Standard
> In the event that Customer becomes subject to a regulatory inquiry, investigation, examination, or audit that relates to the Services or to data processed by Vendor on Customer's behalf, Vendor shall: (a) cooperate reasonably with Customer and the applicable regulatory authority; (b) preserve all documents, records, and data relevant to the inquiry; (c) make knowledgeable personnel available for interviews or testimony upon reasonable notice; and (d) provide relevant records and documentation within the timeframes required by the regulatory authority or, if no timeframe is specified, within ten (10) business days of Customer's request. Vendor shall maintain the confidentiality of the investigation to the extent permitted by law. The costs of Vendor's cooperation shall be borne by Vendor for its own personnel and records, and by Customer for any extraordinary or third-party costs.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall fully cooperate with any regulatory inquiry, investigation, examination, enforcement action, or audit that relates to the Services, Customer Data, or Vendor's performance under this Agreement, whether initiated by Customer's regulators, Vendor's regulators, or any governmental authority with jurisdiction. Vendor's cooperation obligations include: (a) preserving all documents, records, communications, logs, and data relevant to the inquiry, including implementing litigation holds as directed by Customer or the regulator; (b) providing all requested records and documentation within five (5) business days, or such shorter period as required by the regulatory authority; (c) making personnel with knowledge of the relevant systems, processes, and incidents available for interviews, depositions, or testimony at times reasonably convenient for the regulatory authority; (d) providing written responses to regulatory inquiries as directed by Customer; and (e) granting the regulatory authority direct access to relevant systems and facilities if required by applicable law or regulation. Vendor shall not destroy, alter, or conceal any records that may be relevant to a regulatory investigation. Vendor shall promptly notify Customer if Vendor receives any regulatory inquiry or subpoena related to the Services or Customer Data, unless prohibited by law. All cooperation obligations shall be provided at Vendor's expense, except that Customer shall reimburse reasonable travel costs for Vendor personnel required to appear in person at a location more than 100 miles from their regular workplace. Vendor's cooperation obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of three (3) years.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Vendor shall cooperate with Customer's regulatory inquiries to the extent legally required and commercially reasonable. Vendor shall respond to regulatory requests within thirty (30) business days, unless a shorter period is required by law. Vendor shall not be required to disclose proprietary information, trade secrets, or information relating to other customers. Customer shall reimburse Vendor for all costs of regulatory cooperation, including internal personnel time at Vendor's standard professional services rates. Vendor shall have no obligation to cooperate with regulatory inquiries that do not specifically name Vendor or the Services.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor shall cooperate with regulatory inquiries related to the Services or Customer Data, including preserving relevant documents and making personnel reasonably available. Each party shall bear its own costs unless otherwise required by law.

### Notes
Regulatory cooperation obligations are essential for regulated customers (financial services, healthcare, government contractors) and increasingly important for any company subject to data protection enforcement. Key issues: (1) Scope of cooperation — Vendor-Favorable language limiting cooperation to inquiries that "specifically name Vendor" is too narrow; regulators often investigate the customer and need vendor cooperation. (2) Response timeline — 30 business days (Vendor-Favorable) is far too slow for most regulatory investigations; push for 5-10 business days or alignment with regulatory deadlines. (3) Document preservation — explicit litigation hold obligations are critical; destruction of relevant records during an investigation can create severe legal liability for the customer. (4) Cost allocation — vendor should bear its own internal costs of cooperation; charging professional services rates penalizes the customer for regulatory requirements. (5) Personnel availability — ensure vendor will make knowledgeable personnel available, not just designate a single point of contact. (6) Survival — cooperation obligations must survive termination, as regulatory investigations often extend beyond the contract term.

## Sanctions Screening

### Standard
> Vendor represents and warrants that neither it nor any of its officers, directors, or principals is listed on any U.S. government sanctions or denied-party list, including the OFAC Specially Designated Nationals and Blocked Persons List ("SDN List"), the Bureau of Industry and Security Entity List, or equivalent lists maintained by the EU, UK, or United Nations. Vendor shall screen all sub-processors and material sub-contractors against applicable sanctions lists prior to engagement and at least annually thereafter. If Vendor or any sub-processor becomes listed on a sanctions or denied-party list, Vendor shall notify Customer within forty-eight (48) hours and cooperate with Customer to implement remediation measures, which may include immediate termination of the affected sub-processor.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor represents, warrants, and covenants that: (a) neither Vendor nor any of its officers, directors, shareholders holding more than 10% ownership, employees with access to Customer Data, sub-processors, or material sub-contractors is currently listed on any sanctions, denied-party, or restricted-party list maintained by the U.S. (including OFAC SDN List, Sectoral Sanctions Identifications List, Entity List, Denied Persons List, and Unverified List), the EU, the UK, the United Nations, or any other applicable jurisdiction; (b) Vendor shall screen all personnel with access to Customer Data or systems, all sub-processors, and all material sub-contractors against all applicable sanctions lists at onboarding and no less frequently than quarterly; (c) Vendor shall promptly notify Customer (within twenty-four (24) hours) if Vendor, any sub-processor, or any individual with access to Customer Data or systems is identified on any sanctions or restricted-party list, and shall immediately suspend such person's or entity's access to Customer Data and systems pending remediation; (d) Vendor shall provide Customer with an annual certification of compliance with this section, signed by an authorized officer; and (e) Vendor shall maintain records of all sanctions screening activities for the duration of the Agreement and three (3) years thereafter, and make such records available for audit. A breach of this section shall constitute a material, incurable breach entitling Customer to immediate termination without liability.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Each party represents that it is not listed on the OFAC SDN List as of the Effective Date. Each party shall comply with applicable sanctions laws. Vendor shall have no obligation to conduct ongoing screening of its personnel, sub-processors, or sub-contractors unless specifically required by applicable law. Vendor shall not be liable for any sanctions violation by a sub-processor unless Vendor had actual knowledge of the violation.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor represents that it is not listed on any applicable sanctions or denied-party list and shall notify Customer promptly if it or any sub-processor becomes listed. Vendor shall screen sub-processors against applicable sanctions lists at least annually.

### Notes
Sanctions compliance has become a critical contract provision as enforcement has intensified globally. Key issues: (1) Scope of screening — limiting screening to the entity itself (Vendor-Favorable) misses the risk of sanctioned individuals within the organization or sanctioned sub-processors. Push for screening of sub-processors and key personnel. (2) Frequency — annual screening is a minimum; quarterly is preferred for high-risk contexts. Real-time screening against updated lists is ideal. (3) Lists covered — OFAC SDN is the most common, but EU, UK, and UN lists should also be covered for international deals. The Entity List and Denied Persons List (Bureau of Industry and Security) cover export control restrictions. (4) Certification — annual officer certification provides accountability and an audit trail. (5) Remediation — if a sub-processor is listed, the contract should require immediate suspension of access and provide the customer with termination rights. (6) Actual knowledge standard — Vendor-Favorable "actual knowledge" standard is too permissive; due diligence obligations should not depend on stumbling upon information.

## Compliance Certifications

### Standard
> Vendor shall maintain the following compliance certifications applicable to the Services: (a) SOC 2 Type II (Trust Services Criteria: Security, Availability, Confidentiality); (b) ISO 27001 certification for information security management; and (c) PCI DSS certification (if Vendor processes, stores, or transmits payment card data). Vendor shall renew all certifications annually and provide copies of current certification reports or attestation letters to Customer upon request. Vendor shall notify Customer within fifteen (15) business days if any certification lapses, is revoked, or if the certifying body identifies material findings or qualifications.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall obtain and maintain throughout the Term, at its sole expense, the following compliance certifications: (a) SOC 2 Type II report covering Security, Availability, Confidentiality, and Processing Integrity trust services criteria, issued by a nationally recognized independent auditing firm; (b) ISO 27001 certification for information security management systems covering the infrastructure, personnel, and processes used to deliver the Services; (c) PCI DSS Level 1 certification (if Vendor processes, stores, or transmits payment card data on Customer's behalf); and (d) such additional certifications as may be required by applicable law or regulation for the Services provided (e.g., HITRUST, FedRAMP, ISO 27701). Vendor shall: (i) provide Customer with complete, unredacted copies of all certification reports (not merely summary letters) within thirty (30) days of issuance and upon request; (ii) notify Customer within five (5) business days of any certification lapse, revocation, material qualification, or adverse finding; (iii) provide a remediation plan for any material findings within fifteen (15) business days; (iv) allow Customer's auditors to review the full SOC 2 report under NDA; and (v) not materially reduce the scope of any certification without Customer's prior written consent. If any required certification lapses and is not renewed within sixty (60) days, Customer may terminate the affected Services without penalty. Vendor shall provide an annual compliance certification summary to Customer's security team without requiring a separate request.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Vendor shall use commercially reasonable efforts to maintain SOC 2 Type II certification. Vendor may provide a summary letter or executive summary of the SOC 2 report upon Customer's written request, subject to Vendor's standard NDA for audit reports. Vendor shall not be required to maintain any specific certification unless expressly required by applicable law. A lapse in certification shall not constitute a breach of this Agreement provided Vendor is actively pursuing renewal.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor shall maintain SOC 2 Type II certification (or equivalent) and provide certification reports to Customer upon request. Vendor shall notify Customer if any material certification lapses or is revoked.

### Notes
Compliance certifications serve as third-party validation of a vendor's security and operational controls. Key issues: (1) SOC 2 Type II vs. Type I — Type II covers a period of time (typically 12 months) and is far more meaningful than Type I (a point-in-time assessment). Always require Type II. (2) Report access — summary letters are insufficient; customers and their auditors need the full report to assess control effectiveness and review any exceptions or qualified opinions. Push for full report access under NDA. (3) Scope — ensure the certification covers the specific systems and infrastructure used to deliver the Services, not just the vendor's corporate IT environment. (4) Lapse notification — a lapsed certification may indicate financial distress, operational issues, or a failed audit. Timely notification is essential. (5) PCI DSS applicability — required only if the vendor handles payment card data; confirm whether this applies to the specific engagement. (6) Additional certifications — regulated industries may require HITRUST (healthcare), FedRAMP (US government), or ISO 27701 (privacy). (7) Certification reduction — vendors sometimes reduce the scope of certifications to avoid audit findings; push for consent requirements before scope reduction.
