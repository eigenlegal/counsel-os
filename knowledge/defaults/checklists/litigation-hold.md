# Litigation Hold Checklist

## Trigger Assessment

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Identify the triggering event (lawsuit, demand letter, regulatory inquiry, anticipated claim) — preservation obligations attach when litigation is reasonably anticipated, not just when filed
- [ ] Assess whether preservation obligations have been triggered — failure to preserve once obligations attach can result in spoliation sanctions
- [ ] Determine the preservation obligation start date — the start date defines the scope of what must be preserved
- [ ] Engage outside counsel (if applicable) — early engagement protects privilege and ensures proper scope

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Determine the scope of the dispute (parties, claims, relevant time period)
- [ ] Identify the legal basis for the hold (pending litigation, reasonably anticipated, regulatory investigation)
- [ ] Assess potential exposure and severity
- [ ] Determine whether insurance carriers should be notified

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Review applicable court rules for preservation obligations
- [ ] Assess whether opposing party has been notified of preservation expectations

## Scope Definition

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Define the subject matter of the hold — overbroad holds are costly; too narrow creates spoliation risk
- [ ] Identify relevant time period (start date through present, or specific date range) — time boundaries determine the volume of data to preserve
- [ ] Identify relevant parties, entities, and counterparties — missing a key entity can result in destruction of critical evidence
- [ ] Define relevant document categories and types — specificity reduces burden while ensuring completeness

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Identify relevant projects, transactions, or business matters
- [ ] Identify relevant geographic locations and offices
- [ ] Define relevant keywords and search terms for electronic discovery
- [ ] Determine whether international data privacy laws affect preservation scope

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Assess proportionality of preservation scope to the claims
- [ ] Consider whether a phased preservation approach is appropriate

## Key Custodian Identification

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Identify all individuals likely to have relevant documents or information — missing custodians is the most common discovery failure
- [ ] Include current employees, former employees, contractors, and executives — former employees' data may be at risk of deletion
- [ ] Identify custodians across all relevant departments (business, legal, finance, IT, HR, sales, support, executive) — siloed preservation misses cross-functional evidence
- [ ] Document the custodian list and the basis for inclusion — documentation protects against spoliation claims

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Prioritize custodians by likely relevance
- [ ] Identify custodians who are flight risks or soon departing
- [ ] Confirm custodian contact information is current
- [ ] Assess whether any custodians are outside the jurisdiction

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Interview key custodians to understand their data landscape
- [ ] Map custodian relationships to identify additional custodians

## Data Source Identification

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Email systems (Exchange, Gmail, etc.) — email is the most requested discovery source in litigation
- [ ] Instant messaging and chat platforms (Slack, Teams, etc.) — chat messages are increasingly discoverable and often contain candid communications
- [ ] Document management systems (SharePoint, Google Drive, Dropbox, etc.) — primary repositories for business documents
- [ ] Mobile devices (phones, tablets, personal BYOD devices) — text messages and mobile apps are frequently relevant

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Project management tools (Jira, Asana, etc.)
- [ ] CRM systems (Salesforce, HubSpot, etc.)
- [ ] Financial systems (ERP, billing, accounting)
- [ ] Source code repositories (if relevant)
- [ ] Laptops and desktops
- [ ] Shared network drives and file servers
- [ ] Cloud storage and SaaS applications
- [ ] Backup tapes and archives

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Voicemail systems
- [ ] Video conferencing recordings
- [ ] Social media accounts (corporate)
- [ ] Physical files and records
- [ ] IoT devices and access logs

## Litigation Hold Notice

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Draft the hold notice with: description of matter, scope of preservation, types of materials, instruction to cease destruction, specific examples, contact person, acknowledgment requirement — the notice is the primary defense against spoliation claims
- [ ] Have the notice reviewed by legal counsel — ensures the notice is legally sufficient
- [ ] Distribute the notice to all identified custodians — documented distribution is essential evidence of compliance
- [ ] Collect acknowledgments from all custodians — unacknowledged notices create enforcement gaps

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Distribute to IT for system-level preservation
- [ ] Follow up with custodians who have not acknowledged within 5 business days
- [ ] Include instruction to preserve auto-delete settings and suspend retention policies
- [ ] Provide foreign-language versions if custodians are non-English speakers

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Offer a Q&A session for custodians with complex data landscapes
- [ ] Provide department-specific examples of relevant documents

## Technical Preservation

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Suspend auto-deletion policies for relevant custodians and data sources — auto-deletion is the most common cause of inadvertent spoliation
- [ ] Place legal holds in email/collaboration systems (Exchange, Google Vault, etc.) — technical holds are more reliable than relying on individual compliance
- [ ] Preserve departing employee devices and accounts — departing employees' data is permanently lost if not preserved before offboarding

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Suspend email retention policies for relevant mailboxes
- [ ] Preserve relevant backup tapes or archives from recycling
- [ ] Image or preserve relevant mobile devices
- [ ] Preserve relevant cloud storage and SaaS data
- [ ] Document all preservation steps taken and dates

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Preserve database snapshots if structured data is relevant
- [ ] Engage forensic vendor for complex data sources

## Ongoing Management

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Maintain a litigation hold log (issuance date, custodians, acknowledgments, reminders, data sources) — the log is your compliance record if preservation is challenged
- [ ] Send periodic reminders to custodians (quarterly recommended) — reminder frequency shows ongoing diligence
- [ ] Manage custodian departures (preserve accounts and devices before offboarding) — the single highest-risk moment for data loss

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Update the custodian list as new relevant individuals are identified
- [ ] Issue supplemental hold notices if the scope changes
- [ ] Coordinate with IT on system changes that could affect preserved data
- [ ] Document any preservation failures or gaps and steps to address them

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Conduct periodic compliance audits of preservation
- [ ] Track costs associated with preservation for budgeting

## Hold Release

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Confirm the triggering matter has been fully resolved — premature release can result in sanctions if the matter is revived
- [ ] Obtain legal counsel approval to release the hold — only legal should authorize release

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Issue a hold release notice to all custodians
- [ ] Coordinate with IT to release technical holds
- [ ] Resume normal retention and deletion policies

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Document the hold release in the litigation hold log
- [ ] Retain the hold log and notices as part of the matter file
- [ ] Conduct lessons-learned review for process improvement
