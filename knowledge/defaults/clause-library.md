### Notes
The Vendor-Favorable version contains several problematic provisions. The "sole discretion" content determination (item (b)) gives the vendor unchecked authority to restrict use without objective criteria. The prohibition on publishing benchmarks or security assessments (item (k)) can conflict with regulatory obligations and security disclosure norms. The "intended use case" restriction (item (l)) is vague and gives the vendor broad authority to restrict legitimate use. The "excessive" usage standard (item (i)) without quantitative thresholds is unacceptable — usage limits should be specified numerically in the Order Form. The Aggressive version preserves Customer's rights to conduct security testing, benchmark for internal purposes, use the Services for clients, and exercise reverse engineering rights under applicable law. Always push for objective, quantitative usage limits rather than vendor-discretionary standards.

## Usage Limits

### Standard
> Customer's use of the Services is subject to the usage limits specified in the applicable Order Form (e.g., number of authorized users, API calls per month, storage capacity, transaction volumes). If Customer exceeds the specified usage limits:
>
> (a) Vendor shall notify Customer in writing within five (5) business days of detecting the overage;
>
> (b) Customer shall have thirty (30) days to reduce usage to within the applicable limits or to purchase additional capacity at then-current rates;
>
> (c) if Customer does not reduce usage or purchase additional capacity within the cure period, Vendor may charge overage fees at the per-unit rates specified in the Order Form (or, if no per-unit rate is specified, at Vendor's then-current list price less the percentage discount reflected in the Order Form); and
>
> (d) Vendor shall not throttle, suspend, or degrade the Services due to overages without first providing the notice and cure period described above.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Customer's use is subject to the usage limits in the Order Form. Customer shall be entitled to exceed the specified limits by up to fifteen percent (15%) (the "Burst Allowance") in any calendar month without incurring additional fees or triggering enforcement action. If Customer's usage exceeds the Burst Allowance:
>
> (a) Vendor shall notify Customer within three (3) business days, providing specific usage data and applicable overage rates;
>
> (b) Customer shall have forty-five (45) days to either reduce usage or execute an order for additional capacity;
>
> (c) overage fees shall be calculated at the effective per-unit rate in the Order Form (i.e., reflecting all negotiated discounts, not list price), and shall be invoiced in arrears;
>
> (d) Vendor shall not throttle, suspend, degrade, or otherwise restrict the Services due to overages unless Customer has exceeded the Burst Allowance for three (3) or more consecutive months and has failed to cure within the cure period;
>
> (e) Vendor shall provide Customer with real-time or near-real-time usage dashboards and monitoring tools to enable Customer to track consumption against limits; and
>
> (f) usage limits shall be measured and reported on a monthly basis; instantaneous or daily peaks shall not constitute overages.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Customer's use is subject to the usage limits in the Order Form. Vendor shall monitor Customer's usage and, if Customer exceeds the limits: (a) Vendor may charge overage fees at Vendor's then-current list price for the excess usage, calculated daily and invoiced monthly; (b) Vendor may throttle or restrict access to bring Customer within limits; or (c) if overages persist for more than five (5) business days, Vendor may suspend the Services until Customer purchases additional capacity. Customer is responsible for monitoring its own usage. Vendor shall have no obligation to provide advance notice of overage charges.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Usage is subject to Order Form limits. If Customer exceeds limits, Vendor shall provide notice and a thirty (30) day cure period before charging overages or restricting service. Overages are charged at the per-unit rate in the Order Form (or a reasonable rate if not specified). Vendor shall not suspend Services solely due to overages unless they persist beyond the cure period.

### Notes
The Vendor-Favorable approach of charging list-price overages without notice and suspending after five days is punitive and creates significant business risk. Overages should always be at the negotiated rate, not list price — the customer's volume discount should apply to incremental usage. The burst allowance (Aggressive) reflects the reality that usage fluctuates and that occasional spikes should not trigger enforcement. Usage monitoring tools are essential because the customer often has no visibility into consumption until an invoice arrives. Measurement methodology matters — daily or hourly peaks can trigger overages even if monthly average usage is within limits, which is unfair for workloads with variable demand. Always ensure the Order Form specifies per-unit overage rates rather than leaving this to future negotiation.

## Enforcement

### Standard
> If Vendor believes Customer has violated the Acceptable Use Policy, Vendor shall follow this enforcement process:
>
> (a) **Notice**: Vendor shall provide Customer with written notice specifying the nature of the violation, the evidence supporting the determination, and the remedial action required;
>
> (b) **Cure Period**: Customer shall have fifteen (15) days to cure the violation or provide a written explanation of why the use does not constitute a violation;
>
> (c) **Suspension**: If Customer fails to cure within the cure period, Vendor may suspend the affected portion of the Services (but not unaffected Services) until the violation is cured. Vendor shall restore access within twenty-four (24) hours of receiving confirmation that the violation has been cured;
>
> (d) **Emergency Suspension**: Notwithstanding the foregoing, Vendor may immediately suspend access without prior notice if: (i) continued use poses an imminent threat to the security or integrity of the Services or other customers' data; (ii) continued use would violate applicable law; or (iii) Vendor is required to do so by a court order or regulatory authority. In the event of an emergency suspension, Vendor shall provide notice as soon as practicable, and in no event later than twenty-four (24) hours;
>
> (e) **Termination**: Vendor may terminate this Agreement only if the violation is material, Customer fails to cure within the cure period, and the violation is not susceptible to cure.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall enforce the Acceptable Use Policy through the following tiered process:
>
> (a) **Written Warning**: Vendor shall first provide a written warning specifying the alleged violation in detail, including the specific provision violated, the factual basis, and the required remediation. Customer shall have thirty (30) days to cure or dispute the allegation;
>
> (b) **Second Notice**: If the violation recurs or is not cured, Vendor shall provide a second written notice, and Customer shall have an additional fifteen (15) days to cure;
>
> (c) **Partial Suspension**: If Customer fails to cure after two notices, Vendor may suspend only the specific functionality or user account directly involved in the violation. Vendor shall not suspend unrelated Services, functionality, or user accounts;
>
> (d) **Emergency Suspension**: Vendor may suspend without prior notice only if continued use creates an imminent, demonstrable threat of material harm to the Services' infrastructure, other customers' data, or third parties, or if required by law or court order. Vendor shall provide written notice within twelve (12) hours, including the specific factual basis for the emergency determination. Customer may immediately dispute the emergency determination, and Vendor shall escalate to senior management for review within twenty-four (24) hours;
>
> (e) **Appeal**: Customer may appeal any enforcement action to a designated senior executive of Vendor. Vendor shall respond within five (5) business days. During the appeal, suspension shall be limited to the narrowest scope necessary;
>
> (f) **Termination**: Vendor may terminate only after following the full enforcement process (warnings, cure periods, and suspension), and only for material, repeated violations that Customer has failed to cure. Vendor's right to terminate for AUP violations shall be subject to the termination-for-cause cure periods in this Agreement; and
>
> (g) **No Liability Reduction**: Suspension or termination for an AUP violation shall not relieve Vendor of any data return, transition assistance, or refund obligations.

### Vendor-Favorable
> If Vendor determines, in its sole discretion, that Customer has violated the Acceptable Use Policy, Vendor may take any of the following actions without prior notice: (a) issue a warning; (b) remove or disable access to offending content; (c) throttle or limit Customer's access; (d) suspend the Services in whole or in part; or (e) terminate this Agreement immediately. Vendor shall not be liable for any damages, losses, or costs resulting from enforcement of the Acceptable Use Policy. Customer shall remain obligated to pay all fees during any suspension period.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor shall provide written notice of alleged AUP violations and a fifteen (15) day cure period before suspension (except in genuine emergencies threatening imminent harm). Suspension shall be limited to the affected Services. Emergency suspensions require notice within twenty-four (24) hours. Vendor may terminate only for material, uncured violations following the for-cause termination process.

### Notes
The Vendor-Favorable approach of granting the vendor unilateral, discretionary authority to suspend or terminate without notice, without liability, while requiring continued payment is the most aggressively one-sided AUP enforcement model and should never be accepted. Even the most reasonable AUP violations (which may be inadvertent or caused by a single user) should not result in immediate termination of enterprise services without due process. At minimum, insist on written notice, a cure period, proportional response (affect only the offending use, not the entire service), and the right to dispute. The emergency suspension carve-out is necessary but should be narrowly defined and require prompt notice. The Aggressive version's appeal process and no-liability-reduction provision protect against weaponized AUP enforcement used to avoid contractual obligations.

## AUP Modifications

### Standard
> Vendor may update the Acceptable Use Policy from time to time. Vendor shall provide Customer with at least thirty (30) days' prior written notice of any material changes. For purposes of this Section, a "material change" is one that would: (a) prohibit a use that Customer is currently engaged in; (b) impose materially more restrictive usage limits; or (c) expand the enforcement remedies available to Vendor. If a material change would adversely affect Customer's existing use, Customer may, within the thirty-day notice period, provide written objection, and the parties shall negotiate in good faith to resolve the issue. If the parties cannot reach agreement within thirty (30) days, Customer may terminate the affected Order Form without penalty and receive a pro-rata refund of prepaid fees.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall not modify the Acceptable Use Policy in any manner that is material to Customer's use without Customer's prior written consent. A modification is "material" if it would: (a) restrict, limit, or prohibit any use in which Customer is currently engaged; (b) impose new usage limits or reduce existing limits; (c) expand the categories of prohibited content or activity; (d) expand Vendor's enforcement remedies or reduce Customer's cure periods; or (e) impose new obligations on Customer not contemplated by this Agreement. Non-material changes (such as clarifications that do not change the substance of obligations) may be made with thirty (30) days' notice. If Vendor makes a material change without Customer's consent, the prior version of the AUP shall continue to govern Customer's use for the remainder of the Term. In no event shall any AUP modification: (x) be applied retroactively to penalize Customer for conduct that was permitted at the time it occurred; or (y) override or conflict with the express terms of this Agreement or any Order Form, which shall control in the event of any inconsistency.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Vendor may update the Acceptable Use Policy at any time by posting the revised version on its website. Continued use of the Services following an update constitutes acceptance. Customer is responsible for regularly reviewing the AUP. Vendor shall use commercially reasonable efforts to notify Customer of significant changes but shall have no liability for failure to provide notice. In the event of a conflict between the AUP and this Agreement, the AUP shall control.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor may update the AUP with thirty (30) days' prior written notice of material changes. No modification shall be applied retroactively. If a material change adversely affects Customer's existing use, Customer may terminate without penalty and receive a pro-rata refund. In the event of a conflict between the AUP and this Agreement, this Agreement controls.

### Notes
The Vendor-Favorable approach — unilateral modification by website posting, deemed acceptance through continued use, and AUP controls over the Agreement — is fundamentally incompatible with a negotiated contract. It allows the vendor to effectively rewrite key terms of the agreement without consent. The provision that the AUP controls over the Agreement is particularly dangerous because it means the vendor can override negotiated protections by modifying the AUP. At minimum: require advance written notice of material changes, prohibit retroactive enforcement, ensure the Agreement controls over the AUP, and provide a termination right if changes are unacceptable. The consent-based approach (Aggressive) is appropriate for enterprise agreements where the customer has significant investment in the platform and cannot easily terminate.

## Security Testing

### Standard
> Notwithstanding any prohibition on reverse engineering or interference with the Services, Customer may conduct or commission authorized security testing of the Services, including vulnerability scanning and penetration testing, subject to the following conditions:
>
> (a) Customer shall provide Vendor with at least fifteen (15) business days' prior written notice, including the scope, methodology, timing, and personnel or firms conducting the testing;
>
> (b) testing shall be conducted during mutually agreed windows to minimize impact on other customers;
>
> (c) Customer shall not access, copy, or exfiltrate other customers' data during testing;
>
> (d) Customer shall promptly report any vulnerabilities discovered, and Vendor shall remediate critical and high-severity vulnerabilities within the timeframes set forth in the SLA (or, if no SLA is specified, within thirty (30) days for critical and sixty (60) days for high-severity);
>
> (e) testing results shall be treated as Confidential Information of both parties; and
>
> (f) Customer shall not publicly disclose vulnerabilities until Vendor has had a reasonable opportunity to remediate.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Customer shall have the right to conduct or commission, at any time and without limitation on frequency, authorized security assessments of the Services, including:
>
> (a) automated vulnerability scanning and static/dynamic analysis;
>
> (b) penetration testing (black box, gray box, and white box);
>
> (c) social engineering testing related to access to Customer's data or account;
>
> (d) application security assessments, including code review of customer-facing components;
>
> (e) network and infrastructure security assessments of components used to deliver the Services; and
>
> (f) compliance assessments against applicable regulatory frameworks.
>
> Customer shall provide reasonable advance notice (no less than five (5) business days), and Vendor shall cooperate by providing: (i) a dedicated test environment where available; (ii) test accounts with appropriate access levels; (iii) documentation of architecture and security controls; and (iv) a technical point of contact during testing.
>
> Vendor shall remediate vulnerabilities as follows: critical severity within seventy-two (72) hours (or immediately if actively exploited); high severity within fifteen (15) days; medium severity within thirty (30) days; and low severity within ninety (90) days. If Vendor fails to remediate within the specified timeframes, Customer may: (x) implement compensating controls at Vendor's expense; (y) reduce payment by the amount necessary to procure alternative security measures; or (z) terminate for cause if the vulnerability poses an unacceptable risk to Customer's data.
>
> Testing results are Confidential Information. Customer may share results with regulators as required. Vendor shall not restrict Customer's ability to comply with regulatory obligations regarding security assessments.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Customer shall not conduct or commission any security testing, penetration testing, vulnerability scanning, or similar assessment of the Services without Vendor's prior written consent, which may be withheld in Vendor's sole discretion. If Vendor grants consent, testing shall be conducted only by a firm approved by Vendor, at Customer's sole expense, during a window designated by Vendor, and using only methodologies approved by Vendor. Customer shall share all results exclusively with Vendor before any other disclosure. Vendor shall have no obligation to remediate findings within any specified timeframe.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Customer may conduct authorized security testing (including vulnerability scanning and penetration testing) with at least ten (10) business days' prior notice. Vendor shall cooperate reasonably and provide a test environment where available. Vendor shall remediate critical vulnerabilities within thirty (30) days and high-severity vulnerabilities within sixty (60) days. Results are confidential.

### Notes
The Vendor-Favorable requirement of prior written consent "in sole discretion" effectively prohibits security testing, which is increasingly unacceptable to customers with regulatory obligations (NYDFS, DORA, SOX, HIPAA) that require vendor risk assessments. Many customers have contractual or regulatory obligations to test their service providers, and a consent-based restriction conflicts with these obligations. At minimum, require the vendor to consent to reasonable testing with advance notice. The Aggressive remediation timeframes align with industry standards (e.g., PCI-DSS, CISA guidelines) and create accountability for timely patching. The dedicated test environment is important to avoid impacting production. Always ensure the testing carve-out is explicitly referenced in the AUP's prohibition on "interference" and "unauthorized access" to prevent the vendor from arguing that authorized security testing violates the AUP.

## AI Training Restrictions

### Standard
> Vendor shall not use Customer Data, Customer Confidential Information, or any data derived from Customer's use of the Services to train, develop, improve, or fine-tune any machine learning model, artificial intelligence system, or algorithm, whether owned by Vendor or any third party. This prohibition applies regardless of whether the data is aggregated, anonymized, or de-identified. Vendor shall ensure that its sub-processors comply with this restriction.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall not use Customer Data or any data derived from Customer's use of the Services (including usage patterns, queries, inputs, outputs, feedback, and metadata) to train, develop, improve, validate, benchmark, or fine-tune any machine learning model, AI system, algorithm, or similar technology. This restriction applies to all forms of the data, including aggregated, anonymized, de-identified, and synthetic data derived from Customer Data. Vendor represents that no Customer Data has been or will be included in any training dataset. If Vendor uses AI or ML components in the Services, Vendor shall disclose all such components and confirm they were not trained on Customer Data.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Customer acknowledges that the Services incorporate machine learning technology that improves through usage. Vendor may use aggregated, anonymized data derived from all customers' use of the Services (including Customer's use) to train, improve, and develop the Services, new features, and Vendor's products. Vendor shall not use identifiable Customer Data for training purposes without Customer's prior consent.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor shall not use Customer Data to train or develop machine learning models or AI systems without Customer's prior written consent.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language permitting use of "aggregated, anonymized data" for model training is the most contested AI-era provision. Whether anonymization is sufficient depends on the data type, volume, and re-identification risk. Push for an outright prohibition in sensitive data contexts. Even "anonymized" training data can embed patterns attributable to specific customers. The Aggressive version closes loopholes around metadata and synthetic data.

AI output ownership is evolving as IP law catches up with generative AI. The Aggressive version includes an honest acknowledgment that AI-generated outputs may lack IP protection. Vendor-Favorable language retaining rights to "underlying algorithms" while allowing use of "anonymized outputs" blurs the line between model IP and customer content. Push for clear customer ownership of outputs with no vendor retention.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable blanket disclaimers on AI accuracy with full liability shift to Customer may be acceptable for general productivity tools but are inappropriate for high-stakes applications (hiring, lending, healthcare). Push for transparency, bias testing, and human review capabilities. EU AI Act and state AI legislation are creating mandatory requirements for AI transparency and accountability.

>
> If Customer consents to fine-tuning: (a) Ownership — the fine-tuned model, including all weights, parameters, LoRA adapters, and other adaptations derived from Customer Data, shall be owned by Customer or, at minimum, Customer shall have an exclusive, perpetual, irrevocable license to use the fine-tuned model. (b) Isolation — the fine-tuned model shall be logically isolated from Vendor's base models and other customers' fine-tuned models. Customer's fine-tuning data shall not be used to improve the base model or any model serving other customers. (c) Portability — upon Customer's request, Vendor shall export the fine-tuned model weights in a standard format (e.g., ONNX, SafeTensors, or equivalent) to enable Customer to deploy the model independently. (d) Deletion — upon termination of the Agreement or upon Customer's request, Vendor shall permanently delete the fine-tuned model, all associated weights, all training checkpoints, all cached inference states, and all copies (including backups) within fifteen (15) days, and provide signed officer certification of deletion. (e) No knowledge extraction — Vendor shall not use distillation, knowledge transfer, or any other technique to extract information from the fine-tuned model for use in other models, products, or services. (f) Audit — Customer may audit Vendor's compliance with this section, including inspection of training pipelines and model registries.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Customer acknowledges that the Services may improve through usage, including through automated fine-tuning and optimization processes. Vendor may use aggregated usage data, feedback signals, and interaction patterns from all customers (including Customer) to fine-tune, improve, and optimize the models underlying the Services. Vendor retains all rights in any fine-tuned, improved, or derivative models. Customer shall have no ownership interest, license, or portability right in any model, regardless of the extent to which Customer Data contributed to its development. Upon termination, Vendor shall have no obligation to delete any model or model weights, as Customer Data shall have been incorporated into the model in a manner that is not practically extractable.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor shall not fine-tune models on Customer Data without Customer's prior written consent. If fine-tuning occurs with consent, Vendor shall delete the fine-tuned model weights upon termination and certify deletion.

### Notes
Fine-tuning provisions are among the most commercially consequential AI-era contract terms. Key issues: (1) Consent granularity — blanket consent to fine-tuning in the Terms of Service is insufficient; push for per-activity consent specifying what data is used and for what purpose. (2) Ownership of fine-tuned models — this is the central dispute. Vendors argue the base model is theirs and fine-tuning merely adjusts it; customers argue the fine-tuning data is the primary source of value. The answer often depends on the degree of customization. (3) Feedback as training data — ratings, corrections, and preference signals are forms of training data that vendors often collect without explicit consent. Ensure feedback is covered by the fine-tuning restrictions. (4) Deletion feasibility — vendors sometimes argue that fine-tuned weights cannot be practically separated from the base model. This is technically inaccurate for adapter-based fine-tuning (LoRA, QLoRA) but may be harder for full fine-tuning. Push for adapter-based approaches that facilitate clean deletion. (5) Knowledge distillation — even after deleting the fine-tuned model, knowledge can be extracted through distillation techniques. An express prohibition on distillation is necessary. (6) Portability — the ability to export fine-tuned weights in a standard format prevents vendor lock-in and preserves the customer's investment in training data.

### Notes
Third-party AI provider transparency is a rapidly evolving contract issue as the AI supply chain becomes more complex. Key issues: (1) Disclosure obligation — many SaaS vendors build on third-party AI APIs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google Cloud AI) without disclosing this to customers. The customer has a right to know where its data flows. (2) Training opt-outs — major AI providers have different policies on whether API data is used for training. OpenAI's API terms (as of 2024-2025) default to not training on API inputs, but this policy can change. Push for contractual commitments, not just reliance on provider policies. (3) Liability chain — Vendor-Favorable language disclaiming liability for third-party providers creates a gap where the customer has no recourse if the provider misuses data. The vendor should remain liable for its sub-processors. (4) Provider substitution — switching from one AI provider to another can materially affect output quality, accuracy, and compliance. Push for advance notice and objection rights. (5) Terms of service changes — AI providers frequently update their terms; the vendor should monitor and notify the customer of material changes. (6) Data flow transparency — customers need to understand the full data path: what data goes to the provider, in what form, and what the provider retains or returns.

## Bias & Fairness Testing

### Standard
> If the Services use AI or machine learning to make or support decisions that affect individuals (including but not limited to hiring, lending, insurance, pricing, content moderation, or access to services), Vendor shall: (a) conduct bias and fairness testing on the relevant models and algorithms at least annually and upon any material model change; (b) test for disparate impact across legally protected characteristics (including race, gender, age, disability, and national origin) to the extent feasible given the data available; (c) document testing methodology, metrics used, and results; (d) disclose known limitations, error rates, and demographic performance disparities in the documentation; and (e) provide a summary of testing results to Customer upon request. Vendor shall use commercially reasonable efforts to remediate material bias identified through testing.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall implement a comprehensive bias and fairness testing program for all AI and ML components of the Services that process Customer Data or generate outputs affecting individuals. Vendor's program shall include:
>
> (a) **Testing frequency.** Bias and fairness testing shall be conducted: (i) prior to initial deployment of any model; (ii) upon any material model update, retraining, or fine-tuning; (iii) at least semi-annually for all production models; and (iv) within thirty (30) days of Customer's written request.
>
> (b) **Testing methodology.** Vendor shall test for: (i) disparate impact across all legally protected characteristics under applicable law (including race, color, national origin, sex, gender identity, sexual orientation, age, disability, religion, and veteran status); (ii) proxy discrimination (where ostensibly neutral features serve as proxies for protected characteristics); (iii) intersectional bias (where bias manifests at the intersection of multiple characteristics); and (iv) performance disparities across demographic groups (including accuracy, false positive rates, and false negative rates).
>
> (c) **Fairness metrics.** Vendor shall report on recognized fairness metrics appropriate to the use case, which may include demographic parity, equalized odds, predictive parity, calibration across groups, and individual fairness measures.
>
> (d) **Disclosure.** Vendor shall provide Customer with: (i) a detailed bias testing report following each testing cycle, including methodology, metrics, results, and identified limitations; (ii) model cards or equivalent documentation disclosing training data characteristics, known biases, and intended use parameters; and (iii) prompt notification of any material bias or fairness issue discovered through testing, monitoring, or third-party reports.
>
> (e) **Remediation.** Vendor shall remediate material bias identified through testing within sixty (60) days and provide Customer with a remediation plan within fifteen (15) business days of identification. Customer may suspend use of the affected AI component pending remediation without penalty.
>
> (f) **Customer testing rights.** Customer shall have the right to conduct its own bias and fairness testing of the Services (including red-teaming and adversarial testing) and Vendor shall cooperate and provide reasonable access and documentation to facilitate such testing.
>
> (g) **Indemnification.** Vendor shall indemnify Customer against third-party claims alleging that the Services produced discriminatory or biased outputs, subject to the limitations of liability in this Agreement.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Vendor shall use commercially reasonable efforts to design the Services to minimize algorithmic bias. Customer acknowledges that no AI system can guarantee the absence of bias, and that bias testing is an evolving field without universally accepted standards. Vendor may, in its discretion, conduct bias testing and share high-level results with Customer. Vendor shall have no liability for biased or discriminatory outputs, and Customer is solely responsible for evaluating the suitability of AI-generated outputs for its use case, including compliance with anti-discrimination laws. Customer shall indemnify Vendor against claims arising from Customer's use of AI-generated outputs.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor shall conduct bias and fairness testing on AI components of the Services at least annually and disclose known limitations and material biases. Vendor shall provide testing results to Customer upon request and use commercially reasonable efforts to remediate material bias.

### Notes
Bias and fairness testing is transitioning from a best practice to a legal requirement as AI regulation matures. Key issues: (1) Legal landscape — the EU AI Act classifies certain AI systems as "high-risk" and imposes mandatory bias testing and documentation requirements. US state laws (Colorado AI Act, NYC Local Law 144 for automated employment decisions, Illinois BIPA) and the EEOC's guidance on AI in hiring impose specific testing obligations. (2) Testing methodology — there is no single accepted standard for bias testing, but recognized frameworks include NIST AI RMF, IEEE 7003, and the EU's Assessment List for Trustworthy AI. Push for vendor commitment to recognized frameworks. (3) Protected characteristics — testing should cover all characteristics protected under applicable law, not just a vendor-selected subset. Intersectional testing (e.g., Black women, elderly disabled individuals) catches biases that single-characteristic testing misses. (4) Proxy discrimination — bias can manifest through facially neutral features (ZIP code as proxy for race, name as proxy for national origin). Sophisticated testing must address proxies. (5) Customer testing rights — customers in regulated industries may need to conduct their own testing to satisfy regulatory obligations. Push for vendor cooperation with customer-directed testing. (6) Liability allocation — Vendor-Favorable language shifting all liability to the customer is inappropriate when the vendor controls the model and the customer has no ability to inspect or modify it. (7) Ongoing monitoring — one-time testing is insufficient; bias can emerge over time as data distributions shift. Push for continuous monitoring in addition to periodic testing.

The customer's ability to assign to a business unit acquirer (Aggressive, clause (c)) is important in divestitures.

## Change of Control Definition

### Standard
> "Change of Control" means: (a) the acquisition, directly or indirectly, by any person or group of persons acting in concert, of beneficial ownership of fifty percent (50%) or more of the outstanding voting securities of a party; (b) a merger, consolidation, or similar transaction in which the party's pre-transaction equity holders hold less than fifty percent (50%) of the voting power of the surviving entity immediately after the transaction; or (c) the sale, lease, or other disposition of all or substantially all of a party's assets.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> "Change of Control" means the occurrence of any of the following with respect to Vendor: (a) the acquisition, directly or indirectly, by any person, entity, or group (as defined in Section 13(d)(3) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934) of beneficial ownership of forty percent (40%) or more of the outstanding voting securities or equity interests of Vendor; (b) a merger, consolidation, share exchange, or similar transaction involving Vendor in which Vendor's pre-transaction equity holders hold less than fifty percent (50%) of the voting power of the surviving or resulting entity; (c) the sale, transfer, exclusive license, or other disposition of all or substantially all of Vendor's assets, or all or substantially all of the assets used in connection with the Services; (d) the replacement of a majority of the members of Vendor's board of directors (or equivalent governing body) over any twelve (12) month period, other than replacements approved by a majority of the incumbent directors; or (e) a transaction or series of related transactions that results in Vendor becoming a subsidiary of, or controlled by, another entity. For purposes of this definition, indirect acquisitions through parent entities, holding companies, or multi-tier structures shall be considered.

### Vendor-Favorable
> "Change of Control" means a single transaction (and not a series of transactions) resulting in a third party acquiring more than fifty percent (50%) of a party's voting shares and simultaneously replacing a majority of the party's board of directors. Internal reorganizations, mergers among affiliated entities, transfers to holding companies, private equity transactions where management continuity is maintained, and initial public offerings shall not constitute a Change of Control.

### Minimum Acceptable
> "Change of Control" means: (a) the acquisition of fifty percent (50%) or more of the voting securities of a party; (b) a merger or consolidation in which the party is not the surviving entity or its equity holders hold less than fifty percent (50%) of the survivor's voting power; or (c) the sale of all or substantially all of a party's assets.

### Notes
The Vendor-Favorable definition is intentionally narrow — requiring both a 50%+ share acquisition AND simultaneous board replacement in a single transaction excludes most real-world change of control scenarios, including creeping acquisitions, asset sales, and private equity transactions. The extensive carve-outs (internal reorgs, PE transactions, IPOs) render the provision nearly meaningless. The Aggressive definition addresses common evasion techniques: the 40% threshold captures significant minority control, the board replacement trigger catches management buyouts, and the look-through provision prevents structuring around the definition via holding companies. "Substantially all of the assets used in connection with the Services" is an important variation that captures product line sales that would not meet the "substantially all assets" threshold at the entity level.

## Competitor Termination Right

### Standard

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> If Vendor undergoes a Change of Control and the acquiring or controlling entity: (a) is a Competitor of Customer (as defined below); (b) is subject to any legal, regulatory, or compliance restriction that would impair Customer's ability to use the Services; or (c) presents a material risk to Customer's confidential information, data security, or competitive position, then Customer may, at its option: (i) terminate this Agreement upon thirty (30) days' written notice, delivered within one hundred twenty (120) days of Customer's actual knowledge of the Change of Control; and (ii) receive a pro-rata refund of all prepaid fees for the unused portion of the Term. Vendor shall provide Customer with written notice of any Change of Control within ten (10) business days of closing. "Competitor" means any entity identified in Exhibit [X] (updated annually by mutual agreement), or any entity that directly competes with Customer in [description of Customer's market], as reasonably determined by Customer.
>
> In addition to the termination right, upon a Change of Control to a Competitor, Vendor shall: (x) immediately implement information barriers to prevent the Competitor from accessing Customer's Confidential Information and Customer Data; and (y) provide Customer with written certification that such barriers are in place within five (5) business days.

The parties agree that a Change of Control does not affect the rights or obligations of either party under this Agreement, and this Agreement shall be binding upon and inure to the benefit of the parties and their respective successors and permitted assigns.

### Minimum Acceptable
> If Vendor undergoes a Change of Control to a direct competitor of Customer, Customer may terminate this Agreement upon sixty (60) days' notice, provided such notice is given within ninety (90) days of becoming aware of the Change of Control. Customer shall receive a pro-rata refund of prepaid fees for the unused Term.

### Notes
The Vendor-Favorable approach of eliminating the competitor termination right entirely is increasingly untenable given the pace of industry consolidation. Customers have a legitimate interest in not sharing confidential data and business information with a competitor that acquires their vendor. The definition of "competitor" is the most heavily negotiated aspect — vendors prefer a narrow, exhaustive list while customers prefer a functional definition. The exhibit-based approach with annual updates (Aggressive) offers a practical compromise. The information barrier requirement in the Aggressive version addresses the immediate data security concern independent of the termination right. Always pair this clause with an obligation for the acquired party to provide prompt notice of the Change of Control — without notice, the termination window is meaningless.

The Vendor-Favorable automatic release upon assignment is problematic because it eliminates the customer's recourse against the original contracting party even if the assignee defaults. The Aggressive provision preventing the assignee from materially diminishing the Services addresses a common post-acquisition concern where the acquirer downgrades or sunsets the acquired product. The 24-month joint liability period should be calibrated to the agreement's remaining term and the customer's transition timeline.

## Financial Audit

### Standard

Vendor shall maintain complete billing records for the Term and three (3) years thereafter.

### Notes
Financial audit rights are essential for consumption-based and variable-fee contracts, where metering errors can result in significant overcharges. Key issues: (1) Access to metering data — Vendor-Favorable language providing only a "summary reconciliation" without access to underlying data makes it impossible to independently verify charges. (2) Overpayment threshold — a 5% threshold for cost-shifting is standard, but any confirmed overpayment should be refunded regardless of percentage. (3) Refund vs. credit — refunds should be in cash, not credits against future invoices; credits lock the customer into continued spending. (4) Metering transparency — real-time usage dashboards reduce the need for formal audits and catch errors earlier. (5) Underpayment discovery — ensure that underpayment obligations are symmetric with overpayment protections. (6) Interest on overpayments — vendors have held customer money improperly; interest provides a deterrent. (7) Billing record retention — ensure records exist long enough to audit historical periods.

### Notes
Regulatory audit rights are non-negotiable for customers in regulated industries. ## Remote vs. On-Site

### Standard
> Audits may be conducted remotely or on-site at Vendor's facilities, at Customer's election. Remote audits may include review of records transmitted electronically, video conference interviews with Vendor personnel, and remote access to relevant systems. On-site audits may be conducted at Vendor's principal offices or any facility where Customer Data is stored or processed. Customer shall conduct audits in a manner that minimizes disruption to Vendor's operations. On-site audits shall be conducted during normal business hours unless otherwise agreed.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Customer may conduct audits remotely or on-site, at Customer's sole election. Remote audits may include: (a) review of electronically transmitted records; (b) video conference or telephone interviews with Vendor personnel; (c) remote access to Vendor's monitoring and logging systems; and (d) automated security scanning of Vendor's externally facing systems (with reasonable coordination). On-site audits may be conducted at any Vendor facility where Customer Data is stored, processed, or accessible, including sub-processor facilities. On-site audits shall be triggered without limitation upon: (i) a security incident or data breach involving Customer Data; (ii) a material audit finding in a prior remote audit; (iii) a request by Customer's regulatory authority; or (iv) Vendor's failure to provide adequate documentation for a remote audit. Vendor shall provide reasonable workspace, network access, and access to relevant personnel during on-site audits. On-site audits may be conducted during or outside business hours if the nature of the audit requires it (e.g., physical security assessments). Vendor shall not require Customer's auditors to sign any waiver of liability, limitation of access, or additional agreement beyond a reasonable confidentiality undertaking as a condition of on-site access.

### Vendor-Favorable
> All audits shall be conducted remotely unless Customer demonstrates that a remote audit is insufficient to achieve the audit objectives, in which case on-site access may be granted subject to Vendor's prior written approval. On-site audits shall be limited to Vendor's principal office, conducted during normal business hours, and shall not exceed two (2) business days. Customer's auditors must comply with Vendor's facility access policies, including background checks, and must sign Vendor's standard visitor NDA and liability waiver. Vendor may designate specific areas as restricted and may redact or withhold information relating to other customers or proprietary technology.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Audits may be conducted remotely or, when reasonably necessary, on-site at Vendor's facilities during normal business hours upon reasonable notice.

### Notes
The choice between remote and on-site audits involves practical and legal considerations. Key issues: (1) Default mode — remote audits are more cost-effective and less disruptive; they should be the default for routine compliance checks. However, the right to conduct on-site audits must be preserved for situations where remote review is insufficient. (2) On-site triggers — Vendor-Favorable language requiring the customer to justify on-site access creates an unnecessary barrier. On-site audits should be available at customer election, with specific triggers (breach, regulatory request, material findings) that override any frequency limitations. (3) Facility scope — limiting on-site access to the "principal office" (Vendor-Favorable) may exclude data centers, disaster recovery sites, and sub-processor facilities where Customer Data actually resides. (4) Access conditions — requiring background checks and liability waivers for auditors is reasonable for physical security but should not be used as a barrier. (5) Restricted areas — vendors may legitimately restrict access to shared infrastructure areas, but restrictions should not prevent assessment of controls relevant to Customer Data. (6) Sub-processor facilities — for critical sub-processors, the right to audit on-site should flow through to the sub-processor's facilities.

Sanctions compliance has become a critical contract provision as enforcement has intensified globally. ## Force Majeure Events

### Standard
> Neither party shall be liable for any failure or delay in performing its obligations under this Agreement (other than payment obligations) to the extent such failure or delay results from a Force Majeure Event. "Force Majeure Event" means any event beyond a party's reasonable control, including acts of God, natural disasters, fire, flood, earthquake, epidemic or pandemic, war, terrorism, civil unrest, government actions or orders, labor disputes (other than those involving the affected party's own employees), power failures, internet or telecommunications failures, or cyberattacks of a severity and nature that could not be reasonably prevented by industry-standard security measures.

"Force Majeure Event" means acts of God, natural disasters, war, terrorism, government orders, and pandemic, but shall not include: (i) general economic conditions, market fluctuations, or financial difficulties; (ii) changes in law or regulation foreseeable at execution; (iii) failures of the affected party's suppliers or sub-contractors unless caused by a qualifying event; or (iv) cyberattacks or system failures attributable to the affected party's failure to maintain industry-standard security.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Vendor shall not be liable for any failure or delay in providing the Services to the extent caused by events beyond Vendor's reasonable control, including but not limited to acts of God, natural disasters, pandemics, epidemics, government actions, war, terrorism, cyberattacks, denial-of-service attacks, internet disruptions, power outages, telecommunications failures, third-party service provider failures, labor disputes, and supply chain disruptions. During any Force Majeure Event, Vendor's SLA obligations and service credit obligations shall be suspended.

### Minimum Acceptable

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language suspending SLA and service credit obligations during force majeure and including broad triggers like "third-party service provider failures" and "supply chain disruptions" creates significant accountability gaps. Push for narrow, enumerated triggers and ensure data protection obligations are not excused. Cyberattacks should only qualify if they exceeded industry-standard defenses.

### Minimum Acceptable

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language with no specific notice timeline and no obligation to incur expense to mitigate allows open-ended performance excusal. Push for specific notice deadlines and meaningful mitigation obligations. The Aggressive version with a 48-hour notice requirement and weekly updates ensures visibility.

The "no material additional costs" limitation in the Vendor-Favorable version excuses Vendor from activating disaster recovery or engaging backup providers -- precisely the measures that should be required during a force majeure event. Vendor represents that it maintains: (a) alternative or backup providers for all critical service components, including hosting, infrastructure, and data processing; (b) contractual protections with its suppliers sufficient to ensure continuity of the Services; and (c) a tested business continuity plan that addresses supplier failure scenarios. If a Vendor supplier failure prevents performance, such failure shall be treated as a Vendor service outage subject to the SLA provisions, and Vendor shall activate its disaster recovery plan within the timeframes specified in this Agreement.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Failures of Vendor's third-party suppliers, hosting providers, cloud infrastructure providers (including AWS, Azure, Google Cloud), sub-processors, telecommunications providers, and other service providers shall constitute Force Majeure Events. Vendor shall use reasonable efforts to restore the Services but shall not be liable for delays or failures caused by the acts, omissions, or failures of its suppliers. Customer acknowledges that the Services depend on third-party infrastructure and that Vendor cannot guarantee uninterrupted availability of such infrastructure.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor's supplier failures shall constitute a Force Majeure Event only if the supplier's failure was itself caused by an independently qualifying Force Majeure Event and Vendor had taken reasonable steps to mitigate supplier dependencies.

### Notes
Supply chain provisions are where the force majeure clause can become a blanket performance excuse. Vendor-Favorable language treating all third-party provider failures (including major cloud providers) as force majeure essentially transfers Vendor's infrastructure risk to Customer. This is particularly problematic because Vendor chose its suppliers and is in the best position to manage those relationships. The Aggressive position (supplier failures never qualify) reflects the principle that vendors should bear the risk of their own supply chain. The Standard middle ground (supplier failures qualify only if caused by an independent qualifying event and Vendor maintained alternatives) is the most balanced approach. Always push Vendor to identify critical single-source dependencies and maintain tested business continuity plans.

### Notes
Multi-jurisdictional issues arise in virtually every SaaS and cloud services agreement with users or data in multiple countries. Key considerations: (1) Mandatory law carve-out — most jurisdictions have laws that apply regardless of contractual choice of law (GDPR is the most prominent example). A contract governed by Delaware law does not eliminate GDPR obligations if EU personal data is processed. (3) Vendor-Favorable risk — language placing all local-law compliance burden on the customer (Vendor-Favorable) ignores that many mandatory laws impose direct obligations on processors and service providers, not just controllers. (4) Practical enforceability — a Delaware governing law clause is largely irrelevant if a French data subject files a complaint with the CNIL. (5) Change of law risk — consider how changes to local mandatory law during the contract term will be handled.

### Notes
International arbitration is the standard dispute resolution mechanism for cross-border contracts because arbitral awards are enforceable in 170+ countries under the New York Convention, whereas foreign court judgments often are not. (5) Confidentiality — arbitration is private by default but not always confidential; include an express confidentiality provision. (6) Interim relief — preserve the right to seek injunctive relief from courts without waiving arbitration. (7) Costs — "loser pays" provisions incentivize meritorious claims; equal sharing regardless of outcome (Vendor-Favorable) removes this incentive. (8) Arbitrator selection — unilateral selection by one party (Vendor-Favorable) undermines the neutrality of the process.

## Tail Coverage

### Standard
> If any required insurance is written on a claims-made basis, Vendor shall maintain such coverage (or purchase an extended reporting period endorsement, commonly known as "tail coverage") for a period of three (3) years following the termination or expiration of this Agreement, with retroactive dates no later than the Effective Date of this Agreement.

> Claims-made policies shall be maintained for at least three (3) years following termination, or Vendor shall purchase tail coverage for such period. Retroactive dates shall be no later than the Effective Date.

### Notes
This is one of the most commonly overlooked insurance provisions. Professional liability and cyber liability policies are almost always written on a claims-made basis, meaning they cover claims made during the policy period regardless of when the underlying act occurred. If coverage lapses after the engagement ends, there is no coverage for claims arising from the vendor's work — even if the wrongful act occurred during the engagement. Three years is market standard; five years (Aggressive) is appropriate for engagements involving sensitive data or high-value professional services where claims may take years to surface. The retroactive date requirement prevents the vendor from switching to a new carrier with a later retroactive date that would create a gap in coverage. The self-help remedy in the Aggressive version (Customer may purchase at Vendor's expense) addresses the practical problem that the customer has no leverage to compel tail coverage after termination.

# IP & Confidentiality — Clause Library

## IP Ownership — Work Product

### Standard
> All work product and deliverables created by Vendor specifically for Customer under a Statement of Work ("Custom Deliverables") shall be owned by Customer. Vendor hereby assigns to Customer all right, title, and interest in such Custom Deliverables, including all intellectual property rights. Vendor retains all right, title, and interest in its pre-existing intellectual property, tools, and methodologies ("Vendor IP"). To the extent Vendor IP is incorporated in or necessary to use the Custom Deliverables, Vendor grants Customer a perpetual, irrevocable, non-exclusive, worldwide, royalty-free license to use such Vendor IP solely as incorporated in or necessary to use the Custom Deliverables.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> All work product, inventions, discoveries, improvements, and materials conceived or created by Vendor in connection with the Services ("Work Product") shall be the sole and exclusive property of Customer. Vendor irrevocably assigns to Customer all worldwide right, title, and interest, including all patent, copyright, trade secret, and trademark rights. Such assignment is effective upon creation. To the extent any assignment is not effective under applicable law, Vendor grants Customer an exclusive, irrevocable, perpetual, worldwide, royalty-free license. Vendor waives all moral rights to the fullest extent permitted by law. Vendor shall execute documents reasonably requested to perfect Customer's ownership.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Vendor retains all right, title, and interest in and to the Services, all deliverables, and all intellectual property developed in connection with this Agreement, including all modifications, improvements, and derivative works. Customer is granted a non-exclusive, non-transferable, non-sublicensable license to use deliverables solely for Customer's internal business purposes during the Term. Upon termination, Customer's license to deliverables shall terminate, except for fully paid deliverables expressly designated as perpetual in the applicable Statement of Work.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor grants Customer a perpetual, irrevocable, non-exclusive, worldwide, royalty-free license to use, modify, and distribute all deliverables created under a Statement of Work. Vendor retains ownership of its pre-existing intellectual property and generalized tools or methodologies.

### Notes
Standard language with assignment plus license-back for Vendor IP is the most common market approach. Vendor-Favorable language retaining all IP and granting only a limited use license is typical of vendor paper -- push for assignment of custom work product at minimum. Aggressive language with belt-and-suspenders (assignment + fallback license, moral rights waiver, further assurances) is appropriate for high-value custom development.

## Customer Data Ownership

### Standard
> As between the parties, Customer retains all right, title, and interest in Customer Data, including all intellectual property rights. "Customer Data" means all data provided by or on behalf of Customer to the Services, and all data generated by the Services derived from Customer's use. Nothing in this Agreement transfers ownership of Customer Data to Vendor.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Customer is the sole and exclusive owner of all Customer Data, including: (a) all data uploaded or transmitted by Customer or its users; (b) all data generated, derived, or collected by the Services from Customer's use, including usage data, metadata, logs, and analytics; and (c) all outputs and reports generated using Customer Data as input. Vendor acquires no right except to process Customer Data solely to provide the Services. Vendor shall not use Customer Data (including in aggregated or anonymized form) for any other purpose without Customer's prior written consent.

### Vendor-Favorable
> As between the parties, Customer owns Customer Data uploaded to the Services. Vendor shall own all aggregated, anonymized, or statistical data derived from Customer's use of the Services ("Service Data"), and may use Service Data for any lawful business purpose, including improving the Services, benchmarking, and developing new products. Vendor shall own all usage data, telemetry, and performance metrics generated by the Services.

### Minimum Acceptable
> As between the parties, Customer owns all Customer Data. Vendor may use Customer Data solely to provide the Services under this Agreement.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language carving out ownership of aggregated, anonymized, and derived data is increasingly common and can be acceptable for non-sensitive data if the anonymization is robust. However, push back on broad "Service Data" definitions that capture Customer-attributable insights. The Aggressive version is critical for AI/ML-era agreements where vendors may use customer data to train models.

## Confidentiality Obligations

### Standard
> Each party ("Receiving Party") agrees to: (a) hold the other party's ("Disclosing Party") Confidential Information in strict confidence; (b) not disclose to third parties except to employees, contractors, and advisors bound by confidentiality obligations at least as protective; and (c) not use for any purpose other than performing under this Agreement. The Receiving Party shall protect Confidential Information using at least the same degree of care as its own confidential information, but no less than reasonable care.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Each party ("Receiving Party") shall: (a) hold all Confidential Information in strictest confidence; (b) restrict access to those with a demonstrable need to know who are bound by written confidentiality obligations at least as restrictive; (c) implement appropriate technical and organizational safeguards; (d) not disclose to any third party without prior written consent; (e) use solely for performing under this Agreement; and (f) promptly notify the Disclosing Party of any unauthorized access or disclosure. Obligations survive for five (5) years after termination, or indefinitely for trade secrets.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Each party shall maintain the confidentiality of the other party's Confidential Information using commercially reasonable measures. The Receiving Party may disclose Confidential Information to its employees, contractors, advisors, affiliates, and potential acquirers who have a need to know, provided such recipients are bound by confidentiality obligations. The Receiving Party's obligations shall terminate three (3) years after the expiration or termination of this Agreement.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Each party shall maintain the confidentiality of the other party's Confidential Information and shall not disclose it to third parties or use it for purposes other than performing under this Agreement. Each party shall exercise reasonable care.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language with "commercially reasonable measures" and a short survival period (3 years) provides weaker protection. Push for "at least the same degree of care as its own" plus a floor of "reasonable care." Disclosure to potential acquirers without consent is concerning -- require prior notice and additional NDA obligations. Always pair with robust definitions and standard exclusions.

## Confidentiality Exclusions

### Standard
> Obligations of confidentiality shall not apply to information that: (a) is or becomes publicly available without fault of the Receiving Party; (b) was in the Receiving Party's possession before receipt, as evidenced by its records; (c) is independently developed without reference to Confidential Information; (d) is rightfully received from a third party without restriction; or (e) is required to be disclosed by law, provided the Receiving Party gives prompt written notice and cooperates with efforts to obtain a protective order.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> [Same as Standard. These exclusions are well-established and should not be narrowed. Resist attempts to add a "residuals" clause permitting use of retained ideas or concepts.]

### Vendor-Favorable
> Obligations of confidentiality shall not apply to information that: (a) is or becomes publicly available; (b) was previously known to the Receiving Party; (c) is independently developed; (d) is received from a third party without restriction; (e) is required to be disclosed by law or regulation; or (f) is retained in the unaided memories of the Receiving Party's personnel who had access to such information ("Residuals"), and such personnel may use such Residuals for any purpose without restriction.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Confidential Information does not include information that is publicly available, independently developed, previously known, or rightfully received from a third party.

### Notes
The standard exclusions are well-established and should not be negotiated. The Vendor-Favorable residuals clause (item (f)) is a significant carve-out that effectively allows a party to use any information its employees can remember. Reject residuals clauses in nearly all circumstances. The compelled disclosure exclusion should always include notice and cooperation obligations.

## License Grant (SaaS)

### Standard
> Subject to the terms of this Agreement and payment of applicable fees, Vendor grants Customer a non-exclusive, non-transferable, worldwide right to access and use the Services during the Term for Customer's internal business purposes, in accordance with the documentation and Order Form. Customer may permit its employees, contractors, and agents to access the Services as authorized users.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Subject to payment of applicable fees, Vendor grants Customer a non-exclusive, worldwide right to access and use the Services during the Term for Customer's and its Affiliates' internal and external business purposes, including for the benefit of Customer's clients. Customer may designate authorized users including employees, contractors, and consultants of Customer and its Affiliates. Affiliates may use the Services without executing separate agreements.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Subject to the terms of this Agreement and timely payment of all fees, Vendor grants Customer a limited, non-exclusive, non-transferable, non-sublicensable right to access and use the Services during the Term solely for Customer's internal business purposes and solely within the territory specified in the Order Form. Customer's use is limited to the number of authorized users, transaction volumes, or other usage metrics specified in the Order Form. Any use exceeding the permitted scope shall constitute a material breach and shall be subject to Vendor's then-current excess usage fees.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor grants Customer a non-exclusive right to access and use the Services during the Term in accordance with the Order Form.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language imposing strict usage limitations with automatic excess fees and "material breach" characterization for overages is aggressive. Push for a reasonable true-up process rather than treating overages as breach. Ensure the license scope aligns with the pricing model and covers affiliate use where needed.

## Feedback & Suggestions

### Standard
> If Customer provides Vendor with suggestions, enhancement requests, recommendations, or other feedback regarding the Services ("Feedback"), Customer grants Vendor a non-exclusive, perpetual, irrevocable, worldwide, royalty-free, fully paid-up, sublicensable, and transferable license to use, reproduce, modify, create derivative works from, distribute, and otherwise exploit such Feedback for any purpose without restriction, attribution, or compensation to Customer. Customer acknowledges that Vendor may have ideas similar to Feedback and that this provision does not create any obligation for Vendor to implement any Feedback.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor acknowledges that Customer may, from time to time, voluntarily provide suggestions, ideas, or feedback regarding the Services ("Feedback"). To the extent Customer provides Feedback, Customer grants Vendor a non-exclusive, royalty-free license to use such Feedback solely for the purpose of improving the Services generally available to all customers. Vendor shall not: (a) use Feedback to develop a separate product or service that competes with Customer's business; (b) disclose that specific Feedback originated from Customer; or (c) use Feedback in a manner that reveals Customer's Confidential Information, business strategy, or proprietary processes. Feedback shall not include any Customer Data, trade secrets, or proprietary methodologies, and any such materials provided by Customer in connection with Feedback shall remain Customer's Confidential Information and shall not be treated as Feedback. Nothing in this Agreement shall be construed as requiring Customer to provide Feedback.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Any Feedback, ideas, suggestions, feature requests, or materials submitted by Customer through any channel (including support tickets, product forums, beta programs, advisory boards, or informal communications) shall become the sole and exclusive property of Vendor. Customer irrevocably assigns to Vendor all right, title, and interest in all Feedback, including all intellectual property rights. Customer waives any moral rights in Feedback to the fullest extent permitted by law. Vendor may use Feedback for any purpose without restriction, attribution, or compensation.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Customer grants Vendor a non-exclusive, royalty-free license to use Feedback provided by Customer for the purpose of improving the Services. Feedback shall not include Customer's Confidential Information or trade secrets.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language assigning full ownership of all Feedback -- especially when the definition sweeps in support tickets, advisory board input, and "informal communications" -- is overreaching. Push back on assignment in favor of a license. Ensure the Feedback definition excludes Confidential Information, trade secrets, and Customer Data. The Aggressive version's restriction on using Feedback to develop competing products is appropriate when Customer operates in a market adjacent to Vendor's. Standard language with a broad license is the most common market approach and is generally acceptable for most SaaS agreements.

## Residuals

### Standard
> Nothing in this Agreement shall restrict either party from using the general knowledge, skills, experience, ideas, concepts, know-how, and techniques that are retained in the unaided memories of its personnel who had authorized access to the other party's Confidential Information during the course of this Agreement ("Residuals"), provided that this right does not constitute a license to any patent, copyright, or trade secret and shall not excuse any breach of the confidentiality obligations herein. "Unaided memory" means information retained in a person's mind without reference to Confidential Information in tangible or electronic form. This Residuals provision shall not apply to: (a) Customer Personal Data or any data subject to Data Protection Laws; (b) source code, algorithms, or proprietary formulas; or (c) information that is clearly marked or identified as a trade secret.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> This Agreement does not include a residuals clause. All Confidential Information, regardless of how it is retained or recalled, remains subject to the confidentiality obligations set forth herein for the full duration of the confidentiality period. Neither party may use or disclose the other party's Confidential Information based on unaided memory or any other basis outside the express permissions granted in this Agreement.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Each party shall be free to use for any purpose the Residuals resulting from access to the other party's Confidential Information. "Residuals" means information retained in the unaided memories of the Receiving Party's personnel who had access to Confidential Information, regardless of whether such information constitutes trade secrets or was marked as confidential. Neither party shall have any obligation to limit the scope of work assignments of personnel who have had access to the other party's Confidential Information.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Either party may use Residuals -- general knowledge, ideas, and concepts retained in the unaided memories of its personnel -- provided that this right does not extend to trade secrets, source code, personal data, or information specifically identified as subject to enhanced protection.

### Notes
Residuals clauses are among the most controversial provisions in IP-heavy agreements. Vendor-Favorable language with a broad Residuals definition that explicitly includes trade secrets and removes any obligation to restrict work assignments effectively creates a loophole in the entire confidentiality framework. Reject residuals clauses entirely (Aggressive position) when dealing with highly proprietary information, source code, or algorithms. If a residuals clause must be accepted, insist on: (a) narrow definition limited to general knowledge and concepts; (b) explicit exclusion of trade secrets, source code, personal data, and proprietary formulas; (c) no permission to intentionally memorize; and (d) no waiver of the obligation to restrict work assignments. The Standard version with robust carve-outs represents a reasonable middle ground.

## Trademark & Brand Usage

### Standard
> Neither party may use the other party's name, logo, or trademarks without prior written consent. Notwithstanding the foregoing, Vendor may identify Customer as a customer of Vendor in Vendor's customer lists and marketing materials (e.g., "Company X is a customer of Vendor"), provided that Vendor does not imply endorsement by Customer and uses Customer's trademarks in accordance with Customer's trademark usage guidelines. Customer may revoke this permission at any time upon written notice. Any use of a party's trademarks shall comply with such party's trademark guidelines and shall inure to the benefit of the trademark owner. Upon termination of this Agreement, each party shall promptly cease all use of the other party's trademarks.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall not use Customer's name, logo, trademarks, or any reference to Customer's identity in any marketing materials, case studies, press releases, presentations, website content, social media, or any other public or private communication without Customer's prior written consent for each specific use. Customer may withhold consent for any reason. Any approved use shall: (a) comply strictly with Customer's brand guidelines as provided to Vendor; (b) be subject to Customer's right to review and approve the specific content prior to publication; (c) be revocable by Customer at any time upon written notice, in which case Vendor shall remove such materials within five (5) business days; and (d) not imply endorsement, partnership, or affiliation beyond the actual customer relationship. Vendor shall submit all proposed uses to Customer at least fifteen (15) business days before intended publication. Customer grants no trademark license under this Agreement.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Customer grants Vendor a non-exclusive, royalty-free license to use Customer's name, logo, and trademarks during the Term for purposes of identifying Customer as a Vendor customer in marketing materials, case studies, presentations, press releases, and on Vendor's website. Vendor may develop case studies based on Customer's use of the Services, subject to Customer's review and approval (not to be unreasonably withheld or delayed). If Customer does not respond to a review request within ten (10) business days, approval shall be deemed given. Vendor may continue to reference Customer for a period of twelve (12) months following termination.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Neither party may use the other party's trademarks without prior written consent. Vendor may identify Customer by name in customer lists, provided no endorsement is implied. Any approved use must comply with the trademark owner's guidelines and shall cease upon termination or revocation of consent.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language with deemed approval after 10 business days of silence and a 12-month post-termination usage right gives Vendor significant marketing latitude. Push back on deemed-approval mechanisms and any post-termination usage rights. The Aggressive version with per-use approval and 15-day advance submission is appropriate for regulated industries or companies with strict brand management. Standard language permitting customer-list inclusion is the most common market approach and is generally acceptable. Always ensure brand usage rights terminate upon Agreement termination.

# Liability & Indemnification — Clause Library

## Limitation of Liability — Cap

### Standard
> EXCEPT FOR THE EXCLUDED CLAIMS (DEFINED BELOW), NEITHER PARTY'S AGGREGATE LIABILITY ARISING OUT OF OR RELATED TO THIS AGREEMENT SHALL EXCEED THE TOTAL AMOUNTS PAID OR PAYABLE BY CUSTOMER TO VENDOR UNDER THIS AGREEMENT DURING THE TWELVE (12) MONTH PERIOD IMMEDIATELY PRECEDING THE EVENT GIVING RISE TO THE CLAIM.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> EXCEPT FOR THE EXCLUDED CLAIMS (DEFINED BELOW), NEITHER PARTY'S AGGREGATE LIABILITY SHALL EXCEED THE GREATER OF (A) THE TOTAL AMOUNTS PAID OR PAYABLE BY CUSTOMER UNDER THE APPLICABLE ORDER FORM DURING THE TWELVE (12) MONTH PERIOD PRECEDING THE FIRST EVENT GIVING RISE TO THE CLAIM, OR (B) [DOLLAR AMOUNT]. THIS LIMITATION APPLIES REGARDLESS OF WHETHER SUCH PARTY HAS BEEN ADVISED OF THE POSSIBILITY OF SUCH DAMAGES AND REGARDLESS OF WHETHER ANY REMEDY FAILS OF ITS ESSENTIAL PURPOSE.

### Vendor-Favorable
> IN NO EVENT SHALL VENDOR'S AGGREGATE LIABILITY ARISING OUT OF OR RELATED TO THIS AGREEMENT EXCEED THE AMOUNTS ACTUALLY PAID BY CUSTOMER TO VENDOR DURING THE THREE (3) MONTH PERIOD PRECEDING THE CLAIM. CUSTOMER'S LIABILITY SHALL NOT BE LIMITED UNDER THIS SECTION WITH RESPECT TO CUSTOMER'S PAYMENT OBLIGATIONS, BREACH OF LICENSE RESTRICTIONS, OR INFRINGEMENT OF VENDOR'S INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY RIGHTS.

### Minimum Acceptable
> NEITHER PARTY'S AGGREGATE LIABILITY UNDER THIS AGREEMENT SHALL EXCEED THE AMOUNTS PAID BY CUSTOMER DURING THE TWELVE (12) MONTHS PRECEDING THE CLAIM.

### Notes
The cap should be based on "paid or payable" rather than just "paid" to ensure recovery early in the contract. Vendor-Favorable language with a 3-month lookback dramatically reduces the cap; push for 12 months at minimum. Asymmetric caps (low cap for vendor, no cap for customer payment obligations) should be resisted. A dollar-amount floor (Aggressive version) protects against low-value contracts with high-risk exposure.

## Excluded Claims / Super Cap

### Standard
> "Excluded Claims" means: (a) indemnification obligations under Section [X]; (b) breach of confidentiality obligations; (c) Vendor's breach of data protection obligations; (d) infringement or misappropriation of intellectual property; and (e) gross negligence, willful misconduct, or fraud. Excluded Claims shall be subject to a separate aggregate cap equal to two (2) times the general liability cap.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> The following obligations shall not be subject to the limitation of liability and shall be uncapped: (a) Vendor's IP indemnification obligations; (b) Vendor's breach of data protection and security obligations, including any Personal Data Breach; (c) Vendor's breach of confidentiality; (d) either party's liability for fraud, willful misconduct, or gross negligence; (e) liability for death or bodily injury caused by negligence; and (f) Customer's obligation to pay fees.

### Vendor-Favorable
> The limitation of liability shall not apply to: (a) either party's liability for fraud or intentional misconduct; (b) either party's liability for death or bodily injury caused by gross negligence; and (c) Customer's obligation to pay all fees due under this Agreement. All other claims, including claims for breach of data protection, confidentiality, or indemnification obligations, shall be subject to the general liability cap.

### Minimum Acceptable
> The limitation of liability shall not apply to: (a) either party's liability for fraud or willful misconduct; (b) either party's liability for death or bodily injury; or (c) Customer's obligation to pay undisputed fees.

### Notes
A super-cap (2x or 3x) for excluded claims is a common middle ground between capped and uncapped liability. Vendor-Favorable language subjecting data breach and IP indemnification to the general cap provides minimal protection for Customer's most significant risk areas. Push for at least a super-cap on data breach and IP claims. At minimum, fraud, willful misconduct, and bodily injury should always be carved out.

## Consequential Damages Exclusion

### Standard
> EXCEPT FOR THE EXCLUDED CLAIMS, IN NO EVENT SHALL EITHER PARTY BE LIABLE FOR ANY INDIRECT, INCIDENTAL, SPECIAL, CONSEQUENTIAL, OR PUNITIVE DAMAGES, OR ANY LOSS OF PROFITS, REVENUE, DATA, BUSINESS OPPORTUNITIES, OR GOODWILL, REGARDLESS OF THE THEORY OF LIABILITY AND EVEN IF ADVISED OF THE POSSIBILITY.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> IN NO EVENT SHALL CUSTOMER BE LIABLE TO VENDOR FOR INDIRECT, INCIDENTAL, SPECIAL, CONSEQUENTIAL, OR PUNITIVE DAMAGES. VENDOR'S EXCLUSION OF CONSEQUENTIAL DAMAGES SHALL NOT APPLY TO: (A) VENDOR'S BREACH OF DATA PROTECTION OR SECURITY OBLIGATIONS; (B) VENDOR'S BREACH OF CONFIDENTIALITY; (C) VENDOR'S INFRINGEMENT OF CUSTOMER'S INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY; OR (D) VENDOR'S GROSS NEGLIGENCE OR WILLFUL MISCONDUCT.

### Vendor-Favorable
> IN NO EVENT SHALL EITHER PARTY BE LIABLE TO THE OTHER FOR ANY INDIRECT, INCIDENTAL, SPECIAL, CONSEQUENTIAL, EXEMPLARY, OR PUNITIVE DAMAGES, INCLUDING ANY LOSS OF PROFITS, REVENUE, DATA, GOODWILL, OR BUSINESS OPPORTUNITIES, ARISING OUT OF OR RELATED TO THIS AGREEMENT, REGARDLESS OF THE CAUSE OF ACTION OR THEORY OF LIABILITY, EVEN IF ADVISED OF THE POSSIBILITY, AND REGARDLESS OF WHETHER ANY REMEDY FAILS OF ITS ESSENTIAL PURPOSE. THIS EXCLUSION SHALL APPLY TO ALL CLAIMS INCLUDING INDEMNIFICATION, DATA BREACH, AND CONFIDENTIALITY CLAIMS.

### Minimum Acceptable
> NEITHER PARTY SHALL BE LIABLE FOR INDIRECT, INCIDENTAL, SPECIAL, OR CONSEQUENTIAL DAMAGES ARISING UNDER THIS AGREEMENT, EXCEPT FOR DAMAGES ARISING FROM FRAUD OR WILLFUL MISCONDUCT.

### Notes
The consequential damages exclusion is one of the most negotiated provisions. Vendor-Favorable language applying the exclusion to all claims with no carve-outs eliminates recovery for the most likely damages in a data breach or IP infringement scenario. Push for carve-outs aligned with the Excluded Claims. The Aggressive version makes carve-outs asymmetric, which is appropriate when the vendor holds most of the risk.

## Vendor IP Indemnification

### Standard
> Vendor shall defend, indemnify, and hold harmless Customer from any third-party claim alleging that the Services, as provided by Vendor and used in accordance with this Agreement, infringe or misappropriate any third party's intellectual property rights ("IP Claim"). Vendor's obligations are conditioned on Customer: (a) providing prompt written notice; (b) granting Vendor sole control of the defense and settlement; and (c) providing reasonable cooperation at Vendor's expense.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall defend, indemnify, and hold harmless Customer, its Affiliates, and their respective officers, directors, employees, agents, successors, and assigns from any claim that the Services, Vendor's technology, deliverables, or materials provided by Vendor infringe or violate any third party's intellectual property rights worldwide. This obligation applies regardless of whether Customer's use conforms to documentation, provided Customer's use does not involve unauthorized modification. Vendor's obligations are conditioned on: (a) notice within thirty (30) days (late notice only reduces Vendor's obligations to the extent of material prejudice); (b) Vendor controls the defense (Customer may participate with its own counsel); and (c) no settlement imposing liability on Vendor without Vendor's consent.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Vendor shall defend and indemnify Customer against third-party claims that the Services, as delivered by Vendor and used by Customer strictly in accordance with the documentation, infringe a valid United States patent or registered copyright. Vendor shall have no obligation for claims arising from: (a) modifications by Customer; (b) combination with non-Vendor products; (c) use not in accordance with documentation; (d) open-source components; (e) any version other than the most current release; or (f) Customer's specifications or instructions. This section states Vendor's sole obligation and Customer's exclusive remedy for IP infringement.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor shall indemnify Customer against third-party claims alleging that the Services infringe a third party's intellectual property rights, subject to prompt notice and reasonable cooperation.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language narrows the indemnity to US patents and registered copyrights, excludes a long list of scenarios, limits to current-version use only, and makes it the exclusive remedy. Push to broaden coverage to all IP rights (including trade secrets), eliminate the current-version limitation, and ensure the indemnity is not the exclusive remedy.

## IP Indemnification — Cure Path

### Standard
> If the Services become or are likely to become the subject of an IP Claim, Vendor shall at its option and expense: (a) procure for Customer the right to continue using the Services; (b) modify the Services to be non-infringing without materially reducing functionality; (c) replace with a functionally equivalent non-infringing alternative; or (d) if (a)-(c) are not commercially practicable, terminate the affected Services and refund prepaid fees for the unused portion of the Term.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> If the Services become or are likely to become the subject of an IP Claim, Vendor shall: (a) procure the right to continue use on terms no less favorable; (b) modify the Services to be non-infringing without diminishing functionality, performance, or security; or (c) replace with a non-infringing alternative acceptable to Customer. If none are commercially practicable within sixty (60) days, Customer may terminate and receive all prepaid fees plus all fees paid during the preceding twelve (12) months.

### Vendor-Favorable
> If the Services are found to infringe by a final, non-appealable court order, Vendor may, at its sole option: (a) obtain a license for continued use; (b) modify or replace the Services to make them non-infringing; or (c) terminate the affected Services and refund prepaid, unused fees on a pro-rata basis. This section states Customer's sole and exclusive remedy for any intellectual property infringement claim.

### Minimum Acceptable
> If the Services are found to infringe, Vendor shall use commercially reasonable efforts to modify or replace the Services, or procure the right to continue use. If unable, Customer may terminate and receive a pro-rata refund.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language requiring a "final, non-appealable court order" before triggering the cure path means Customer could face years of uncertainty. Push for a trigger based on Vendor's reasonable opinion or any claim, not just a final judgment. Ensure the cure path does not allow Vendor to materially degrade the Services under the guise of making them non-infringing.

## Customer Indemnification

### Standard
> Customer shall defend, indemnify, and hold harmless Vendor from third-party claims arising from: (a) Customer Data that infringes a third party's intellectual property; (b) Customer's use of the Services in violation of this Agreement or applicable law; or (c) Customer's gross negligence or willful misconduct.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Customer shall indemnify Vendor against third-party claims directly arising from Customer Data that infringes a third party's intellectual property, provided Vendor provides prompt notice and Customer controls the defense. Customer's indemnification shall be subject to the general limitation of liability cap.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Customer shall defend, indemnify, and hold harmless Vendor and its affiliates from any and all third-party claims arising from or related to: (a) Customer's use of the Services; (b) Customer Data; (c) Customer's products or services; (d) Customer's breach of this Agreement; (e) Customer's violation of applicable law; or (f) Customer's negligence or misconduct. Customer's indemnification obligations shall not be subject to the limitation of liability.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Customer shall indemnify Vendor against third-party claims arising from Customer-provided content that infringes third-party intellectual property rights.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language with broad indemnification covering "any claim arising from Customer's use of the Services" is overreaching and could capture vendor-caused issues. Push for narrow scope limited to customer-provided content and willful misuse. Reject uncapped customer indemnification and overly broad triggers.

## Indemnification Procedures

### Standard
> The party seeking indemnification ("Indemnified Party") shall: (a) provide the indemnifying party ("Indemnifying Party") with prompt written notice of the claim, provided that failure to provide prompt notice shall not relieve the Indemnifying Party of its obligations except to the extent it is materially prejudiced by such failure; (b) grant the Indemnifying Party sole control of the defense and settlement of the claim, provided that the Indemnifying Party shall not settle any claim that imposes any liability, obligation, or restriction on the Indemnified Party without the Indemnified Party's prior written consent (not to be unreasonably withheld); and (c) provide reasonable cooperation at the Indemnifying Party's expense. The Indemnified Party may participate in the defense with its own counsel at its own expense.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> The Indemnified Party shall notify the Indemnifying Party of the claim in writing within thirty (30) days of becoming aware of it. Late notice shall reduce the Indemnifying Party's obligations only to the extent of actual, demonstrated material prejudice caused by the delay. The Indemnifying Party shall assume sole control of the defense using counsel reasonably acceptable to the Indemnified Party. The Indemnifying Party shall: (a) keep the Indemnified Party informed of the status of the defense at least monthly; (b) not consent to any settlement without the Indemnified Party's prior written consent if such settlement (i) imposes any liability, obligation, admission of fault, or equitable relief on the Indemnified Party, (ii) does not include a full and unconditional release of the Indemnified Party, or (iii) exceeds available insurance coverage; and (c) pay all costs of the defense, including the Indemnified Party's reasonable costs of cooperation. The Indemnified Party may participate in the defense with its own counsel at its own expense. If the Indemnifying Party fails to assume the defense within fifteen (15) days of notice, the Indemnified Party may assume the defense at the Indemnifying Party's expense.

### Vendor-Favorable
> The Indemnified Party shall provide the Indemnifying Party with prompt written notice of any claim within ten (10) business days of becoming aware of it. Failure to provide timely notice shall relieve the Indemnifying Party of its indemnification obligations with respect to such claim. The Indemnifying Party shall have sole and exclusive control of the defense and settlement, including selection of counsel, litigation strategy, and settlement decisions. The Indemnifying Party may settle any claim without the Indemnified Party's consent, provided the settlement does not require the Indemnified Party to pay money damages (other than amounts covered by the indemnity). The indemnification obligations in this Agreement are the Indemnified Party's sole and exclusive remedy for the claims covered herein.

### Minimum Acceptable
> The Indemnified Party shall provide prompt written notice and reasonable cooperation. The Indemnifying Party shall control the defense at its expense. No settlement imposing liability on the Indemnified Party without consent. Failure to provide timely notice reduces obligations only to the extent of material prejudice.

### Notes
The notice requirement is the most litigated aspect of indemnification procedures. Vendor-Favorable language with a strict 10-business-day deadline that completely extinguishes the indemnity upon late notice is a trap -- push for a "material prejudice" standard that reduces (not eliminates) the obligation proportionally. Defense control by the Indemnifying Party is standard, but ensure the Indemnified Party retains consent over settlements that impose obligations. The "sole and exclusive remedy" limitation in the Vendor-Favorable version means the indemnity replaces all other remedies for covered claims -- resist this in favor of indemnification as an additional remedy.

## Regulatory Fines & Penalties

### Standard
> Vendor's indemnification obligations shall extend to regulatory fines and penalties imposed on Customer by a governmental authority to the extent directly resulting from Vendor's breach of its data protection, security, or compliance obligations under this Agreement, provided that: (a) such fines and penalties are legally insurable in the applicable jurisdiction; (b) Customer has used commercially reasonable efforts to mitigate the amount of such fines and penalties; and (c) Customer has provided Vendor with reasonable opportunity to participate in regulatory proceedings. Vendor's obligation to indemnify for regulatory fines shall be subject to the super-cap set forth in the Excluded Claims provision. This provision shall not apply to fines or penalties resulting from Customer's own acts or omissions, Customer's failure to follow Vendor's reasonable recommendations, or Customer's independent regulatory violations.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall indemnify and hold harmless Customer from all regulatory fines, penalties, assessments, corrective action costs, and consent decree obligations imposed on Customer by any governmental or regulatory authority to the extent arising from or related to Vendor's breach of this Agreement, including breach of data protection, security, privacy, or compliance obligations. Vendor's obligation shall apply regardless of whether such fines or penalties are characterized as compensatory or punitive under applicable law, and regardless of insurability, provided that Customer cooperates with Vendor in challenging or mitigating such fines through available administrative or judicial processes. Vendor's obligation to indemnify for regulatory fines shall not be subject to the general limitation of liability and shall be uncapped. Vendor represents that it maintains insurance coverage sufficient to cover regulatory fine exposure arising from its services.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Vendor's indemnification obligations shall not extend to regulatory fines, penalties, or assessments of any kind imposed on Customer. Customer acknowledges that regulatory fines are the responsibility of the regulated entity and that Vendor is not a substitute for Customer's own compliance obligations. Vendor shall have no obligation to indemnify Customer for any fine, penalty, or sanction imposed by a governmental authority, regardless of the underlying cause.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor shall indemnify Customer for regulatory fines directly resulting from Vendor's breach of its data protection or security obligations under this Agreement, to the extent such fines are insurable and legally permissible to indemnify in the applicable jurisdiction. This obligation is subject to the super-cap.

### Notes
Regulatory fine indemnification is one of the most contested provisions in data-heavy agreements. Key considerations: (a) In some jurisdictions (particularly the EU), it is unsettled whether GDPR fines can be contractually indemnified -- include a "to the extent legally permissible" qualifier; (b) Insurability matters because if fines are uninsurable, the indemnity may be uncollectible; (c) Vendor-Favorable language refusing any fine coverage shifts all regulatory risk to Customer even when the fine results from Vendor's breach -- push back with a "to the extent caused by Vendor's breach" formulation; (d) Always exclude fines resulting from Customer's own independent violations. The Standard version with super-cap coverage and insurability/mitigation conditions represents a balanced approach.

## Indemnification Survival

### Standard
> Indemnification obligations shall survive the expiration or termination of this Agreement for a period of two (2) years, provided that the underlying claim (a) arose from acts or omissions occurring during the Term, and (b) written notice of the claim was provided to the Indemnifying Party within such two (2) year survival period. Claims for which proper notice is provided within the survival period shall continue to be subject to the indemnification obligations until final resolution, regardless of whether resolution occurs after the survival period expires.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Indemnification obligations shall survive the expiration or termination of this Agreement for a period of three (3) years following the later of (a) the effective date of expiration or termination, or (b) the date of completion of any transition or migration services. For indemnification obligations relating to data protection, security breaches, or intellectual property infringement, the survival period shall be the longer of three (3) years or the applicable statute of limitations. Claims for which written notice is provided within the survival period shall be fully indemnified regardless of when they are finally resolved. Discovery of a claim after the survival period shall not bar indemnification if the Indemnified Party demonstrates that the underlying act or omission occurred during the Term and the claim could not reasonably have been discovered within the survival period.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Indemnification obligations shall survive for twelve (12) months following the effective date of termination. Claims for which written notice is not provided within such twelve (12) month period shall not be subject to indemnification regardless of when the underlying act or omission occurred. No indemnification obligation shall extend beyond the general limitation of liability cap in effect at the time of termination.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Indemnification obligations shall survive termination for two (2) years. Claims noticed within the survival period remain subject to indemnification until final resolution.

### Notes
The survival period should be long enough to capture claims that may not surface immediately -- data breach claims, in particular, often emerge months or years after the breach occurs. Vendor-Favorable language with a 12-month survival period is insufficient for data protection and IP claims where discovery is often delayed. Push for 2-3 years at minimum. The Aggressive version's "later of termination or transition completion" start date is important because many data protection risks persist during migration. Always ensure that claims noticed within the survival period are resolved fully, even if resolution extends beyond the survival period -- otherwise the survival period is effectively a statute of repose.

## Most Favored Nation

# Most Favored Nation — Clause Library

## MFN Commitment

### Standard
> Vendor represents and warrants that the pricing and material commercial terms set forth in this Agreement are no less favorable than the pricing and material commercial terms offered by Vendor to any other customer of comparable size receiving comparable services and scope during the Term. If at any time during the Term Vendor enters into an agreement with a Comparable Customer (as defined below) on more favorable pricing or material commercial terms, Vendor shall promptly notify Customer and, upon Customer's request, adjust this Agreement to provide Customer with equivalent pricing or terms, retroactive to the date such more favorable terms first became effective.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor represents, warrants, and covenants that: (a) the pricing set forth in this Agreement is the lowest pricing Vendor has offered or will offer to any Comparable Customer during the Term; (b) the commercial terms of this Agreement are no less favorable in any material respect than those offered to any Comparable Customer; and (c) if Vendor offers more favorable pricing or terms to any Comparable Customer at any time during the Term, Vendor shall: (i) notify Customer in writing within fifteen (15) days, specifying the more favorable pricing or terms (without identifying the other customer); and (ii) automatically adjust this Agreement to provide Customer with equivalent pricing or terms, retroactive to the date such more favorable pricing or terms first became effective. Vendor shall issue a credit or refund for the difference between the amounts actually paid by Customer and the amounts that would have been payable under the more favorable pricing for the period from the effective date of the more favorable terms through the date of adjustment. This obligation applies to list pricing, negotiated discounts, volume commitments, bundled pricing, credits, free periods, and any other pricing mechanism. Vendor shall not circumvent this provision by restructuring pricing through separate agreements, side letters, or non-standard arrangements.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Vendor shall offer Customer pricing that is competitive with the pricing offered to Vendor's other customers, taking into account volume, scope, term, and other commercially relevant factors. This commitment is aspirational and does not create a binding obligation to match or adjust pricing. Vendor shall have no obligation to disclose the terms of its other customer agreements.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor represents that the pricing in this Agreement is no less favorable than that offered to Comparable Customers. If Vendor offers more favorable pricing to a Comparable Customer during the Term, Vendor shall, upon Customer's request, adjust Customer's pricing to match, effective from the date of Customer's request.

### Notes
The Vendor-Favorable "aspirational" language provides zero protection — if the MFN is not binding, it is not worth having. The key distinction between Standard and Aggressive is retroactivity: the Standard version adjusts upon request, while the Aggressive version adjusts automatically and retroactively with a refund obligation, which provides meaningful economic protection. The anti-circumvention provision (Aggressive) is critical because vendors can easily structure around MFN commitments through side letters, volume-based credits, extended free trials, or bundled pricing that is not directly comparable. Always pair the MFN commitment with audit/verification rights, otherwise it is unenforceable.

## Comparable Customer Definition

### Standard
> "Comparable Customer" means a customer of Vendor that: (a) is in the same or a similar industry as Customer; (b) has contracted for services that are substantially similar in scope, functionality, and technical requirements to the Services provided to Customer; (c) has a contract term within twelve (12) months (longer or shorter) of Customer's Term; and (d) has contracted for a volume of services (measured by users, transactions, or other applicable metrics) within twenty-five percent (25%) of Customer's contracted volume.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> "Comparable Customer" means a customer of Vendor that meets at least two (2) of the following four criteria: (a) is in the same or a related industry as Customer; (b) has contracted for services that include at least seventy-five percent (75%) of the core modules or features included in Customer's Services; (c) has a minimum contract term of at least twelve (12) months; or (d) has contracted for a volume of services that is at least fifty percent (50%) of Customer's volume. The determination of comparability shall be made in good faith, and Vendor shall not narrowly construe the criteria to avoid application of the MFN commitment. For the avoidance of doubt, differences in implementation services, professional services, or add-on features shall not render a customer non-comparable if the core subscription pricing is lower.

### Vendor-Favorable
> "Comparable Customer" means a customer of Vendor in the identical industry, purchasing the identical services and modules, with the identical contract term, volume, and payment terms, under a contract executed within six (6) months of Customer's Effective Date.

### Minimum Acceptable
> "Comparable Customer" means a customer of Vendor purchasing substantially similar services at a comparable volume and term length.

### Notes
The definition of "Comparable Customer" is where MFN clauses succeed or fail in practice. The Vendor-Favorable "identical" standard is designed to ensure that no customer ever qualifies as comparable — there will always be some difference in modules, timing, or terms. Push for "substantially similar" rather than "identical" and use a multi-factor test where not all factors need to be met. The Aggressive version requiring only two of four criteria prevents the vendor from disqualifying comparisons based on a single distinguishing factor. Be cautious about industry-specific requirements: for horizontal SaaS products, industry is less relevant than size and scope. The carve-out for implementation and add-on differences (Aggressive) addresses a common vendor tactic of differentiating based on ancillary services to avoid MFN triggers.

## Verification & Audit

### Standard
> Customer may, no more than once per year, request that Vendor certify in writing that it is in compliance with the MFN commitment. Vendor shall provide such certification within thirty (30) days. In addition, Customer may, upon reasonable notice and no more than once per year, engage an independent third-party auditor (acceptable to Vendor, acting reasonably) to audit Vendor's records solely to verify compliance with the MFN commitment. The auditor shall report to Customer only whether Vendor is in compliance and the amount of any adjustment owed, without disclosing the identity or specific terms of other customers. Audit costs shall be borne by Customer unless the audit reveals non-compliance resulting in an adjustment of five percent (5%) or more of Customer's annual fees, in which case Vendor shall bear the audit costs.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Customer may, at any time and upon thirty (30) days' written notice, exercise any of the following verification rights: (a) request that Vendor certify in writing its compliance with the MFN commitment, which Vendor shall provide within fifteen (15) days; (b) engage an independent third-party auditor (selected by Customer, subject to the auditor executing Vendor's standard NDA) to examine Vendor's pricing records, contracts, order forms, side letters, amendments, and discount approvals solely to verify compliance; or (c) request that Vendor provide a written summary of the pricing terms (without customer identification) offered to the five (5) most comparable customers during the preceding twelve (12) months. Vendor shall preserve all records necessary for verification for at least three (3) years. Audit costs shall be borne by Customer unless non-compliance is found, in which case Vendor shall bear all audit costs and shall pay the adjustment owed plus interest at the lesser of 1.5% per month or the maximum rate permitted by law, calculated from the date of non-compliance. Vendor's refusal or failure to cooperate with a verification request within the specified timeframes shall create a rebuttable presumption of non-compliance.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Upon reasonable written request (no more than once per year), Vendor shall certify in writing that, to the best of its knowledge, it is in compliance with the MFN commitment. Customer shall have no right to audit Vendor's records, contracts, or pricing with other customers. Vendor's certification shall be Customer's sole verification remedy.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Customer may request annual written certification of MFN compliance. Customer may engage an independent auditor, subject to confidentiality obligations, to verify compliance. Vendor shall cooperate with reasonable verification requests. Audit costs follow the non-compliant party.

### Notes
Without verification rights, an MFN clause is purely aspirational — the customer has no way to determine whether the vendor is offering better terms to others. The Vendor-Favorable "self-certification" without audit rights is inadequate because the vendor has every incentive to certify compliance. The independent auditor approach is the market standard and appropriately balances confidentiality (the auditor sees other contracts, the customer does not) with verification. The presumption of non-compliance for failure to cooperate (Aggressive) is a powerful enforcement mechanism but may be resisted. The record preservation requirement (Aggressive) is important because vendors may not retain the records needed for a meaningful audit. Always ensure the auditor's NDA protects other customers' identities.

## Adjustment Mechanics

### Standard
> If an MFN adjustment is required, Vendor shall: (a) reduce Customer's fees prospectively to match the more favorable pricing, effective from the date of Customer's written request; and (b) issue a credit to Customer's account equal to the difference between amounts actually paid and amounts that would have been payable at the adjusted rate for the period from the date of the more favorable agreement through the date of adjustment. Credits shall be applied against future invoices or, upon termination, refunded within thirty (30) days.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> If Vendor offers more favorable pricing or terms to a Comparable Customer, the following adjustments shall apply automatically (without requiring a request from Customer): (a) Customer's pricing shall be retroactively adjusted to the more favorable rate, effective from the date the more favorable terms first became effective; (b) Vendor shall issue a refund (not merely a credit) within thirty (30) days for the difference between amounts actually paid and amounts that would have been payable at the adjusted rate; (c) the more favorable pricing shall apply for the remainder of the Term, including any renewal periods; (d) if the more favorable terms include non-pricing benefits (e.g., additional features, more favorable SLAs, longer support hours, broader indemnification), Customer shall receive equivalent benefits; and (e) the adjusted pricing shall become the new baseline for future MFN comparisons. Vendor shall not satisfy an MFN adjustment by offering Customer a one-time credit, discount, or concession in lieu of a permanent rate reduction.

### Vendor-Favorable
> If Vendor determines that an MFN adjustment is warranted, Vendor shall reduce Customer's fees prospectively, effective from the next renewal date. Vendor shall have no obligation to provide retroactive adjustments, refunds, or credits for prior periods. Adjustments shall apply to base subscription fees only and shall not include discounts, credits, promotional pricing, or non-standard arrangements.

### Minimum Acceptable
> MFN adjustments shall reduce Customer's pricing effective from the date of Customer's request. Vendor shall issue a credit for the difference between amounts paid and amounts that would have been payable from the date the more favorable terms became effective.

### Notes
The adjustment mechanics determine the economic value of the MFN clause. Prospective-only adjustments (Vendor-Favorable) significantly reduce the MFN's value because the vendor has little incentive to proactively disclose better pricing — the longer the customer remains unaware, the more the vendor benefits. Retroactive adjustments (Standard and Aggressive) create meaningful incentive for vendor compliance and disclosure. The distinction between credits and refunds matters at termination — credits against future invoices are worthless if the relationship is ending. The "new baseline" provision (Aggressive) prevents a ratchet effect where the vendor offers better pricing, adjusts Customer's rate, then raises pricing to a new customer, resetting the MFN comparison. Always specify that adjustments cover the full fee structure, not just base subscription — vendors commonly offer better terms through free add-ons, waived implementation fees, or extended free periods that would not be captured by a narrow adjustment mechanism.

## Duration & Scope

### Standard
> The MFN commitment shall apply during the Initial Term and any Renewal Terms. The MFN commitment covers all pricing terms set forth in the Order Form, including subscription fees, professional services rates, and support fees. The MFN commitment does not apply to: (a) promotional or introductory pricing with a duration of ninety (90) days or less; (b) pricing offered to government or educational institutions pursuant to statutory requirements; or (c) pricing for materially different products or services.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> The MFN commitment shall apply during the Initial Term and all Renewal Terms, and shall survive termination with respect to any adjustment obligations that accrued during the Term. The MFN commitment covers all commercial terms, including but not limited to: (a) subscription and license fees; (b) professional services and implementation rates; (c) support and maintenance fees; (d) data storage and overage rates; (e) API access and transaction fees; (f) renewal pricing caps and escalation terms; (g) payment terms and early payment discounts; (h) service level commitments and associated credits; (i) warranty scope and duration; (j) indemnification scope; and (k) limitation of liability terms. The only exclusions shall be: (x) government or educational pricing mandated by law; and (y) bona fide, documented one-time promotional pricing for new customers with a duration not exceeding sixty (60) days. Vendor shall not create separate product SKUs, bundles, or pricing structures for the purpose of circumventing this MFN commitment.

### Vendor-Favorable
> The MFN commitment applies to base subscription pricing during the Initial Term only and does not extend to Renewal Terms. The MFN does not apply to: professional services, implementation, support, training, custom development, overage fees, transaction fees, promotional pricing, pricing offered through channel partners or resellers, pricing negotiated in connection with a competitive bid or RFP, volume discounts, multi-year discounts, enterprise agreements, or any pricing that reflects factors not present in Customer's arrangement. Vendor may discontinue the MFN commitment upon thirty (30) days' notice.

### Minimum Acceptable
> The MFN commitment applies during the Initial Term and any Renewal Terms. It covers subscription fees, professional services rates, and support fees. Reasonable exclusions for short-term promotional pricing and government-mandated pricing are acceptable.

### Notes
The Vendor-Favorable exclusion list is so extensive that it carves out virtually every pricing scenario, rendering the MFN meaningless. Common vendor tactics to circumvent MFN clauses include: offering discounts through channel partners (excluded from MFN), packaging services as a different "product" or SKU, offering extended free periods rather than lower rates, bundling services so that per-unit pricing is not directly comparable, and limiting the MFN to the initial term so it expires before the first renewal. The Aggressive version extending MFN to all commercial terms (not just pricing) is appropriate for strategic relationships where the customer's leverage diminishes over time. The anti-circumvention provision is essential. Be realistic about enforceability — MFN clauses are difficult to enforce even with audit rights, so they work best as negotiation leverage and a deterrent rather than as a litigation tool.

## Employee Non-Solicitation

### Standard
> During the Term and for a period of twelve (12) months following termination or expiration (the "Restricted Period"), neither party shall, directly or indirectly, solicit, recruit, or hire any employee of the other party who was involved in the performance or management of this Agreement, without the prior written consent of the other party. For purposes of this Section, "solicit" means direct, targeted outreach to a specific individual for the purpose of employment, and shall not include: (a) general advertising or job postings not specifically targeted at the other party's employees; or (b) hiring an individual who responds to such general advertising without encouragement or direction from the hiring party.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> During the Term and for a period of twelve (12) months following termination or expiration, Vendor shall not, directly or indirectly, through its officers, directors, employees, agents, Affiliates, or any third party (including recruiters, staffing agencies, or contractors): (a) solicit, recruit, induce, or encourage any employee, contractor, or consultant of Customer to leave Customer's employment or engagement; or (b) hire or engage any employee, contractor, or consultant of Customer who was involved in or had access to information relating to this Agreement, Customer's business operations, or Customer's confidential information, in each case without Customer's prior written consent. This restriction applies regardless of whether the solicited individual is at-will or subject to an employment agreement. Customer shall have the right, but not the obligation, to solicit or hire Vendor's employees at any time without restriction.

### Vendor-Favorable
> During the Term only, neither party shall directly solicit for employment any of the other party's employees who are actively performing work under this Agreement, provided that this restriction shall not apply to: (a) general advertising; (b) unsolicited inquiries; (c) use of recruiting firms, provided the recruiting firm was not specifically directed to target the other party's employees; (d) employees who have been terminated or given notice of termination by the other party; or (e) employees who have been on the other party's engagement for fewer than six (6) months.

### Minimum Acceptable
> During the Term and for twelve (12) months following termination, neither party shall directly solicit for employment any employee of the other party who was materially involved in this Agreement. General advertising and unsolicited inbound inquiries are excluded.

### Notes
The Aggressive version with asymmetric restrictions (Vendor cannot solicit Customer's employees, but Customer can solicit Vendor's) is appropriate when the customer has invested significantly in training employees who interact with the vendor's platform or when the vendor gains access to key customer personnel through the engagement. However, many jurisdictions view non-solicitation restrictions skeptically, particularly when they restrict employee mobility. California, for example, generally does not enforce non-solicitation provisions that effectively function as non-competes. The definition of "solicit" is critical — without the general advertising carve-out, posting a job on LinkedIn could technically constitute a violation. The Vendor-Favorable version limiting restrictions to the Term only is common in staffing and consulting agreements where there is an inherent tension between the parties' talent needs. Always verify enforceability under applicable law, as several states have enacted restrictions on non-solicitation provisions.

## Customer/Client Non-Solicitation

### Standard
> During the Term and for a period of twelve (12) months following termination, Vendor shall not, directly or indirectly, solicit, contact, or market to any customer, client, or prospective client of Customer for the purpose of offering products or services that are competitive with Customer's business, where Vendor's knowledge of or access to such customer or client was obtained through the performance of this Agreement. This restriction does not prohibit Vendor from conducting business with any person or entity that contacts Vendor independently and without solicitation.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> During the Term and for a period of twenty-four (24) months following termination, Vendor shall not, directly or indirectly, through its officers, employees, agents, Affiliates, or subcontractors: (a) solicit, contact, market to, or engage with any Customer Client (as defined below) for the purpose of offering products or services that are the same as or substantially similar to the Services; (b) use any Customer Confidential Information, including customer lists, pricing, business strategies, or relationship information, to identify, target, or pursue any Customer Client; or (c) interfere with or disrupt any business relationship between Customer and a Customer Client. "Customer Client" means any person or entity that: (i) is a customer or client of Customer at any time during the Term; (ii) was a customer or client within the twelve (12) months preceding termination; or (iii) was a prospective customer with whom Customer was in active negotiations or discussions, and whose identity was known to Vendor through the engagement. Vendor acknowledges that breach of this Section would cause irreparable harm and that Customer shall be entitled to seek injunctive relief without bond.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Vendor shall not use Customer's confidential customer lists or proprietary business information to directly solicit Customer's existing customers during the Term. This provision shall not restrict Vendor from marketing its products and services generally, conducting business with any person or entity in the ordinary course, or pursuing business opportunities that arise independently of this Agreement.

### Minimum Acceptable
> During the Term and for twelve (12) months following termination, Vendor shall not use information obtained through this Agreement to solicit Customer's customers or clients for competitive services. General marketing activities are excluded.

### Notes
Client non-solicitation is most important in professional services, outsourcing, and staffing engagements where the vendor gains direct access to and relationships with the customer's clients. The enforceability of these provisions depends heavily on jurisdiction and the specificity of the restriction. Courts are more likely to enforce restrictions that are tied to information obtained through the engagement (rather than broad prohibitions) and that have reasonable geographic and temporal scope. The "independently arising" carve-out in the Vendor-Favorable version can swallow the restriction if broadly interpreted. The Aggressive version's inclusion of prospective clients and 24-month duration is appropriate for high-stakes engagements but may face enforceability challenges in some jurisdictions. Always pair with strong confidentiality provisions covering customer lists and relationship information.

## Carve-Outs

### Standard
> The non-solicitation restrictions set forth herein shall not apply to:
>
> (a) general advertising, including public job postings on the party's website, job boards, or social media platforms, that are not specifically targeted at the other party's employees;
>
> (b) unsolicited inquiries from individuals who contact the hiring party on their own initiative, without encouragement, inducement, or direction from the hiring party;
>
> (c) individuals whose employment or engagement with the other party has been terminated by the other party (voluntarily or involuntarily) prior to the date of solicitation;
>
> (d) individuals who were already in substantive discussions with the hiring party prior to the Effective Date of this Agreement, as documented in writing; and
>
> (e) temporary staffing or secondment arrangements expressly agreed upon by both parties in writing.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> The non-solicitation restrictions on Customer shall not apply to:
>
> (a) general advertising not specifically targeted at Vendor's employees;
>
> (b) unsolicited inbound inquiries from individuals who contact Customer without encouragement from Customer.
>
> No additional carve-outs shall apply to Vendor's non-solicitation obligations.

### Vendor-Favorable
> The non-solicitation restrictions shall not apply to:
>
> (a) general advertising, job postings, career fairs, and marketing activities;
>
> (b) unsolicited inquiries or applications;
>
> (c) engagement of recruiting firms, headhunters, or staffing agencies, even if such firms contact the other party's employees, provided the hiring party did not specifically direct the firm to target the other party;
>
> (d) individuals who have been terminated, given notice of termination, or provided notice of resignation to the other party;
>
> (e) individuals who respond to social media posts, including LinkedIn messages, InMail, or similar outreach, where such outreach is part of a broader campaign not exclusively targeting the other party's employees;
>
> (f) individuals previously employed by the hiring party within the preceding twenty-four (24) months (i.e., "boomerang" employees); and
>
> (g) any hire made more than six (6) months after the individual's last contact with the hiring party's engagement team.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Carve-outs for general advertising and genuinely unsolicited inbound inquiries. Additional carve-outs for individuals already terminated by the other party and pre-existing recruitment discussions documented in writing.

### Notes
The carve-outs define the practical scope of the non-solicitation restriction. The Vendor-Favorable LinkedIn/social media carve-out (item (e)) is particularly problematic because targeted LinkedIn InMail campaigns are one of the primary mechanisms for poaching specific employees — characterizing them as part of a "broader campaign" is often pretextual. The recruiting firm carve-out (item (c)) can also undermine the restriction because parties frequently use recruiters to create plausible deniability. The Aggressive version with minimal carve-outs applied only to Customer reflects the reality that in most vendor-customer relationships, the greater risk is the vendor poaching customer employees who have domain expertise. Always document any pre-existing recruitment discussions (item (d) in Standard) at the outset of the engagement to avoid disputes.

## Remedies

### Standard
> In the event of a breach of the non-solicitation obligations, the breaching party shall pay the non-breaching party, as liquidated damages and not as a penalty, an amount equal to six (6) months of the solicited individual's annual compensation (base salary plus target bonus), in addition to any other remedies available at law or in equity. The parties agree that the actual damages resulting from a breach would be difficult to calculate, and that this liquidated damages amount is a reasonable estimate of the anticipated harm.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> In the event Vendor breaches its non-solicitation obligations:
>
> (a) **Liquidated Damages**: Vendor shall pay Customer an amount equal to twelve (12) months of the solicited individual's total annual compensation (including base salary, target bonus, equity vesting value, and benefits), payable within thirty (30) days of the breach. The parties agree that this amount represents a reasonable estimate of the damages that would be suffered by Customer, which would be difficult to calculate precisely.
>
> (b) **Injunctive Relief**: Customer shall be entitled to seek immediate injunctive relief, including temporary restraining orders and preliminary injunctions, without the necessity of proving actual damages, posting a bond, or showing irreparable harm (which Vendor acknowledges and stipulates). Such relief shall be in addition to, and not in lieu of, any other remedies available to Customer.
>
> (c) **Extension of Restricted Period**: Any period during which Vendor is in breach shall not count toward the Restricted Period, and the Restricted Period shall be extended by the duration of the breach.
>
> (d) **Prevailing Party Fees**: If Customer prevails in any action to enforce this Section, Vendor shall reimburse Customer's reasonable attorneys' fees and costs.

### Vendor-Favorable
> If either party breaches the non-solicitation obligations, the sole and exclusive remedy shall be payment of liquidated damages equal to three (3) months of the solicited individual's base salary (excluding bonus, equity, and benefits). This liquidated damages provision is the non-breaching party's sole remedy, and the non-breaching party expressly waives any right to seek injunctive relief, specific performance, or consequential damages for breach of this Section.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Liquidated damages equal to six (6) months of the solicited individual's annual compensation (base salary plus target bonus). The liquidated damages provision does not limit the non-breaching party's right to seek injunctive relief where appropriate.

### Notes
The Vendor-Favorable approach of limiting remedies to three months' base salary as the sole and exclusive remedy, with an express waiver of injunctive relief, makes the non-solicitation provision essentially unenforceable for valuable employees — the cost of hiring a key employee away from a counterparty far exceeds three months' base salary, making breach economically rational. At minimum, insist on six months' total compensation and preserve the right to seek injunctive relief. The enforceability of liquidated damages provisions depends on their reasonableness — courts may refuse to enforce amounts that are clearly punitive. The twelve-month amount in the Aggressive version approaches the upper bound of what courts typically accept as reasonable. The tolling provision (extension of Restricted Period during breach) is a useful enforcement mechanism but is not universally enforced. Always include a "cumulative remedies" statement to prevent the liquidated damages from being construed as an exclusive remedy cap.

## Duration

### Standard
> The non-solicitation restrictions shall apply during the Term and for a period of twelve (12) months following the termination or expiration of this Agreement for any reason. The restricted period shall run from the effective date of termination or expiration.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor's non-solicitation obligations shall apply during the Term and for a period of twenty-four (24) months following termination or expiration for any reason. If Vendor breaches its non-solicitation obligations during the restricted period, the period shall be extended by the duration of the breach (i.e., the restricted period is tolled during any period of non-compliance). Customer's non-solicitation obligations, if any, shall apply during the Term and for twelve (12) months following termination.

### Vendor-Favorable
> The non-solicitation restrictions shall apply during the Term only and shall cease immediately upon termination or expiration for any reason. If this Agreement is terminated by the restricted party for cause or due to the other party's breach, the non-solicitation restrictions shall terminate immediately upon such termination.

### Minimum Acceptable
> During the Term and for twelve (12) months following termination or expiration. Restrictions apply regardless of the reason for termination.

### Notes
Duration is the most litigated aspect of non-solicitation provisions. Twelve months post-termination is the most widely accepted market standard, with courts in most jurisdictions finding this reasonable. Twenty-four months (Aggressive) is enforceable in many jurisdictions but faces greater scrutiny, particularly for lower-level employees. Duration during the Term only (Vendor-Favorable) provides minimal protection because the greatest risk of solicitation typically arises after the relationship ends, when one party no longer has an incentive to maintain goodwill. The termination-for-cause carve-out in the Vendor-Favorable version (restrictions terminate if the restricted party terminates for cause) has some logical appeal — a party should not be bound by restrictions when the other party has breached — but it can be gamed. In California, Oklahoma, North Dakota, and increasingly other states, post-termination non-solicitation provisions face significant enforceability challenges, particularly if they effectively restrain employee mobility.

## Uptime Commitment

### Standard
> Vendor shall make the Services available with an uptime of at least 99.9% during each calendar month, measured as total minutes in the month minus Downtime, divided by total minutes. "Downtime" means any period during which the Services are unavailable or materially degraded, excluding Scheduled Maintenance, force majeure events, and outages caused by Customer's systems or actions.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall make the Services available with an uptime of at least 99.95% during each calendar month, measured using Customer-accessible monitoring tools or an agreed independent third-party service. "Downtime" means any period during which the Services are unavailable, materially degraded (response times exceeding 2x the documented baseline), or any critical functionality is impaired, regardless of cause, except for: (a) Scheduled Maintenance conducted within permitted windows; and (b) outages directly caused by Customer's unauthorized modification. Outages caused by Vendor's infrastructure providers, third-party dependencies, or security incidents shall count as Downtime.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Vendor shall use commercially reasonable efforts to make the Services available at least 99.5% of the time during each calendar month. "Downtime" means any period during which the Services are completely unavailable to all users, excluding: (a) Scheduled Maintenance; (b) force majeure events; (c) failures of Customer's equipment, networks, or third-party services not provided by Vendor; (d) actions or inactions of Customer or its users; and (e) any beta, trial, or free-tier features. Uptime shall be measured solely by Vendor's monitoring systems.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor shall use commercially reasonable efforts to make the Services available at least 99.5% of the time during each calendar month, excluding scheduled maintenance.

### Notes
The 99.9% standard allows approximately 43 minutes of downtime per month; 99.95% allows approximately 22 minutes; 99.5% allows approximately 3.6 hours. Vendor-Favorable language narrowing Downtime to "completely unavailable to all users" excludes partial outages and degraded performance. Push for a definition that captures material degradation. Measurement by vendor's own tools creates a conflict of interest -- push for independent or customer-accessible monitoring.

### Notes
Measurement methodology is often more important than the uptime percentage itself. Key negotiation points: (1) Who controls the measurement tool — vendor tools create a conflict of interest; independent or customer-accessible tools are strongly preferred. (2) Measurement granularity — 5-minute intervals can miss brief but impactful outages. (3) Exclusions — broad exclusions for "third-party services" or "customer actions" can swallow the SLA. (4) Partial outages — requiring "completely unavailable to all users" before counting Downtime allows significant degradation to go uncredited. (5) Geographic scope — measuring from a single region may miss regional outages affecting the customer.

> Vendor shall provide Customer with a monthly SLA performance report within five (5) business days after the end of each calendar month, delivered to Customer's designated contact by email and available via API. The report shall include: (a) actual uptime percentage calculated in accordance with the agreed methodology; (b) itemized listing of all Downtime events, including start time, end time, duration, root cause, and affected components; (c) performance metrics including average and 95th-percentile response times; (d) error rates by category; (e) service credits earned and applied; (f) Scheduled Maintenance summary; and (g) trend analysis comparing the current month to the trailing six-month period. For Severity 1 incidents, Vendor shall provide a preliminary root cause analysis within twenty-four (24) hours of resolution and a final post-incident report within three (3) business days. For Severity 2 incidents, Vendor shall provide a post-incident report within five (5) business days. All post-incident reports shall include: root cause, contributing factors, timeline with timestamps, customer impact assessment, corrective actions with owners and deadlines, and preventive measures. Vendor shall conduct quarterly SLA review meetings with Customer to discuss trends and improvement initiatives.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Vendor shall make uptime statistics available through its standard customer portal on a monthly basis. Reports shall include aggregate uptime percentage for the reporting period. Detailed incident information shall be available upon Customer's written request, subject to Vendor's reasonable ability to provide such information without disclosing proprietary operational details. Vendor shall provide root cause information for Severity 1 incidents upon request, on a best-efforts basis.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor shall provide a monthly uptime report showing the uptime percentage and any Downtime events. Vendor shall provide a post-incident report for Severity 1 incidents within ten (10) business days of resolution.

### Notes
SLA reporting is the mechanism by which customers verify vendor performance and trigger credit or termination rights. Without reliable, timely reporting, SLA commitments are unenforceable in practice. Key issues: (1) Delivery timeline — 10 business days is standard; longer delays reduce the value of the data. (2) Report content — aggregate percentages alone are insufficient; push for itemized incident data. (3) Post-incident reports — root cause analysis for S1/S2 incidents is essential for understanding systemic risk. (4) Access method — portal-only access (Vendor-Favorable) may lack the detail available via direct reports. (5) Trend data — month-over-month comparisons help identify deteriorating performance before it triggers chronic failure clauses.

## Non-SLA Services

### Standard
> The uptime commitments, service credits, incident response obligations, and chronic failure remedies in this Agreement shall not apply to: (a) beta, preview, or early-access features designated as such in writing; (b) free-tier or trial Services; (c) non-production environments (development, staging, sandbox); and (d) professional services, consulting, or advisory services. Vendor shall use commercially reasonable efforts to maintain the availability of non-SLA Services but shall have no liability for downtime or performance issues affecting such services. Vendor shall clearly designate which Services and features are excluded from SLA coverage in the applicable Order Form or documentation.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> The SLA shall apply to all Services and features included in the Order Form and made generally available by Vendor. The following categories are excluded from SLA coverage only if explicitly designated in the Order Form at the time of purchase: (a) features clearly labeled as "beta" or "preview" in both the documentation and the user interface; (b) free-tier or trial Services provided at no charge; and (c) non-production environments expressly designated as such in the Order Form. Vendor shall not unilaterally reclassify any production feature or Service as beta, preview, or non-SLA after it has been included in Customer's production environment. Professional services shall be governed by separate service levels specified in the applicable Statement of Work. If Vendor introduces a new feature as a replacement for an SLA-covered feature, the replacement shall be subject to the same SLA. Vendor shall provide at least ninety (90) days' notice before discontinuing SLA coverage for any feature or Service.

### Vendor-Favorable
> The SLA applies only to the core production Services as designated by Vendor in its standard documentation. The following are excluded from SLA coverage: (a) beta, preview, experimental, or early-access features; (b) free, trial, developer, or evaluation-tier Services; (c) non-production, sandbox, staging, or development environments; (d) APIs and integrations; (e) mobile applications; (f) any feature or Service not expressly listed as SLA-eligible in Vendor's current documentation; and (g) any third-party services or integrations. Vendor may designate or reclassify features as beta or non-SLA at any time in its sole discretion. Customer's use of non-SLA features is at Customer's sole risk.

### Minimum Acceptable
> The SLA shall not apply to beta features, free-tier services, or non-production environments. Vendor shall identify which Services are covered by the SLA in the Order Form or documentation.

### Notes
Non-SLA carve-outs are a common vector for eroding SLA value. The critical issues: (1) Scope of exclusions — Vendor-Favorable language excluding APIs, mobile apps, and integrations can gut the SLA for customers who rely heavily on those components. (2) Reclassification risk — the ability to unilaterally reclassify features as "beta" allows vendors to remove SLA coverage from underperforming features. Push for a prohibition on reclassification or at minimum require customer consent. (3) Feature replacement — if a vendor replaces an SLA-covered feature with a new version, the new version should inherit SLA coverage. (4) Professional services — if advisory or implementation services have time-sensitive deliverables, they should have their own service levels in the SOW, not simply be excluded. (5) Documentation clarity — require explicit identification of SLA-covered vs. excluded services in the Order Form, not buried in vendor documentation that can change unilaterally.

## Source Code Escrow

# Source Code Escrow — Clause Library

## Escrow Deposit

### Standard
> Vendor shall deposit with the Escrow Agent, within thirty (30) days of the Effective Date and quarterly thereafter, a complete and current copy of: (a) the source code for the Software; (b) all build scripts, compilers, and tools necessary to compile the source code into executable form; (c) technical documentation sufficient for a reasonably skilled developer to understand, maintain, and modify the source code; and (d) a list of all third-party dependencies and libraries, together with applicable license terms. Each deposit shall be accompanied by a written description of changes since the prior deposit. Vendor represents and warrants that each deposit is complete and sufficient to build, deploy, and maintain the Software without reliance on Vendor.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall deposit with the Escrow Agent, within fifteen (15) days of the Effective Date and monthly thereafter (or within five (5) business days of any major release, patch, or update), a complete and current copy of: (a) all source code for the Software, including all modules, libraries, APIs, and microservices; (b) all build scripts, makefiles, CI/CD configurations, container images, infrastructure-as-code templates, and tools required to compile, build, test, and deploy the Software; (c) comprehensive technical documentation including architecture diagrams, database schemas, API specifications, deployment guides, and administrator manuals; (d) all third-party dependencies, libraries, and components, together with applicable license agreements and evidence that such licenses permit Customer's use upon release; and (e) a complete copy of the test suite, including automated tests and test data. Vendor shall certify in writing with each deposit that the materials are complete and current and that the source code can be compiled into a fully functional version of the Software identical in all material respects to the production version. Failure to make timely or complete deposits shall constitute a material breach.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Vendor shall deposit with the Escrow Agent a copy of the source code for the Software within sixty (60) days of the Effective Date and semi-annually thereafter. The deposit shall include the source code and such documentation as Vendor determines, in its reasonable discretion, to be appropriate. Vendor shall not be required to deposit third-party components or tools that Vendor does not own or have the right to deposit. Deposits shall be made in a format determined by Vendor.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Vendor shall deposit with the Escrow Agent, within thirty (30) days of the Effective Date and quarterly thereafter, the source code for the Software and documentation reasonably necessary to compile and maintain the Software. Deposits shall include a list of material third-party dependencies.

### Notes
The critical gap in Vendor-Favorable language is the absence of third-party dependencies and build tooling — source code alone is useless if the customer cannot compile it. Always insist on build scripts and a dependency manifest at minimum. Monthly deposits (Aggressive) are appropriate for rapidly evolving SaaS platforms; quarterly is adequate for stable on-premise software. The certification of completeness is important because incomplete deposits are a common problem — pair this with verification rights.

## Release Conditions

### Standard
> The Escrow Agent shall release the deposited materials to Customer upon the occurrence of any of the following events (each a "Release Condition"): (a) Vendor files for bankruptcy, is adjudicated bankrupt, or makes a general assignment for the benefit of creditors; (b) a receiver, trustee, or liquidator is appointed for a substantial portion of Vendor's assets and is not dismissed within sixty (60) days; (c) Vendor materially breaches this Agreement and fails to cure within sixty (60) days of written notice; (d) Vendor discontinues the Software or ceases to provide maintenance and support for the Software; or (e) Vendor ceases to conduct business in the ordinary course. Customer shall deliver written notice to both Vendor and the Escrow Agent specifying the Release Condition. Vendor shall have thirty (30) days from receipt of such notice to dispute the release by providing written objection to the Escrow Agent, in which case the dispute shall be resolved in accordance with the Escrow Agreement's dispute resolution procedures. If Vendor does not timely dispute, the Escrow Agent shall release the deposited materials to Customer.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> The Escrow Agent shall release the deposited materials to Customer upon written notice from Customer certifying the occurrence of any of the following: (a) Vendor files for or is subject to any insolvency, bankruptcy, receivership, or similar proceeding; (b) Vendor materially breaches this Agreement and fails to cure within thirty (30) days of notice; (c) Vendor discontinues the Software, announces end-of-life, or fails to provide a major update for twelve (12) consecutive months; (d) Vendor ceases to conduct business or is dissolved; (e) Vendor undergoes a change of control to a direct competitor of Customer (as identified in Exhibit [X] or as reasonably determined by Customer); or (f) Vendor fails to maintain the escrow deposit in accordance with Section [X]. Vendor shall have fifteen (15) days to dispute the release by delivering a written objection to the Escrow Agent. If Vendor disputes, the Escrow Agent shall release the materials into a neutral holding environment pending resolution. Under no circumstances shall a pending dispute delay release for more than sixty (60) days. The occurrence of a Release Condition and subsequent release shall not limit any other rights or remedies available to Customer.

### Vendor-Favorable
> The Escrow Agent shall release the deposited materials to Customer only upon: (a) a final, non-appealable adjudication that Vendor is bankrupt; or (b) a final, non-appealable judgment or arbitral award confirming that Vendor has materially breached its maintenance and support obligations and failed to cure within ninety (90) days. No release shall occur based on Customer's unilateral assertion. Vendor shall have sixty (60) days to dispute any release request, during which time the Escrow Agent shall not release the materials. Vendor's discontinuation of the Software or cessation of any particular feature shall not constitute a Release Condition if Vendor offers a commercially reasonable replacement or migration path.

### Minimum Acceptable
> The Escrow Agent shall release the deposited materials to Customer upon: (a) Vendor's bankruptcy or insolvency; (b) Vendor's material breach that remains uncured for sixty (60) days after notice; or (c) Vendor's discontinuation of the Software without a reasonable migration alternative. Vendor shall have thirty (30) days to dispute any release request.

### Notes
The Vendor-Favorable language requiring "final, non-appealable" judgments before release effectively makes escrow useless — by the time litigation concludes, the customer has already suffered the harm escrow was designed to prevent. Insist on a notice-and-dispute mechanism with a reasonable (15-30 day) dispute window and a cap on how long disputes can delay release. Change of control to a competitor is an increasingly important trigger for technology companies. The dispute resolution mechanism in the escrow agreement itself (typically arbitration with a 30-60 day timeline) is critical — review the escrow agreement, not just the software license.

## License Upon Release

### Standard
> Upon release of the deposited materials, Customer shall have a non-exclusive, non-transferable, royalty-free license to use, compile, modify, and create derivative works of the source code solely for the purpose of maintaining, supporting, and operating the Software for Customer's internal business purposes. Customer shall not distribute, sublicense, or make the source code available to any third party, except to Customer's contractors who are bound by confidentiality obligations at least as protective as those in this Agreement and who require access solely to assist Customer with maintenance and support. This license shall survive termination of this Agreement.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Upon release, Customer shall have a perpetual, irrevocable, non-exclusive, worldwide, royalty-free, fully paid-up license to use, reproduce, compile, modify, enhance, and create derivative works of all deposited materials for any purpose related to Customer's use, maintenance, support, enhancement, or operation of the Software, including: (a) bug fixes and security patches; (b) modifications for compatibility with Customer's systems; (c) enhancements to add functionality; and (d) integration with Customer's other systems. Customer may engage third-party contractors to exercise these rights on Customer's behalf, provided such contractors are bound by confidentiality obligations. Customer may sublicense to its Affiliates for their internal use. This license shall be perpetual and irrevocable and shall not be affected by any subsequent termination or expiration of any agreement between the parties.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Upon release, Customer shall have a limited, non-exclusive, non-transferable, non-sublicensable license to use the source code solely to maintain the Software in its then-current form for Customer's internal business purposes. Customer shall not modify, enhance, or create derivative works of the source code, except for bug fixes necessary to maintain the Software's existing functionality. Customer shall not engage third parties to access the source code without Vendor's prior written consent. This license shall terminate three (3) years after the date of release.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Upon release, Customer shall have a non-exclusive, non-transferable, perpetual, royalty-free license to use, compile, and modify the source code solely for maintaining and operating the Software for Customer's internal business purposes. Customer may engage third-party contractors under appropriate confidentiality obligations.

### Notes
The Vendor-Favorable language prohibiting modifications beyond bug fixes and imposing a three-year time limit defeats the purpose of escrow — if the vendor has failed, the customer needs meaningful rights to keep the software running and adapt it over time. At minimum, insist on perpetual duration and the right to modify for maintenance purposes. The right to engage third-party contractors is essential since the customer's internal team may not have the skills to work with the code. Watch for third-party component licenses that may restrict Customer's rights upon release — the escrow should include evidence that embedded third-party licenses permit this use.

## Verification

### Standard
> Customer shall have the right, no more than once per year, to request that the Escrow Agent (or a mutually agreed independent technical expert) verify that the deposited materials are complete and can be compiled into a functional version of the Software. Verification shall be conducted at Customer's expense, unless the verification reveals a material deficiency, in which case Vendor shall bear the cost of the verification and shall cure the deficiency within thirty (30) days. Vendor shall cooperate with the verification process and provide reasonable technical assistance.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Customer may request verification of the deposited materials at any time, and no less than once per year. Verification shall be conducted by an independent technical expert selected by Customer (and reasonably acceptable to Vendor) and shall confirm that: (a) the deposited source code is complete and corresponds to the current production version of the Software; (b) the source code can be compiled into a fully functional executable; (c) all third-party dependencies are included and properly licensed; and (d) the documentation is sufficient for a competent developer unfamiliar with the Software to understand, build, and maintain it. All verification costs shall be borne by Vendor. If verification reveals any deficiency, Vendor shall cure within fifteen (15) days, and Customer may re-verify at Vendor's expense. Repeated verification failures (two or more within any twelve-month period) shall constitute a material breach.

### Vendor-Favorable
> Customer may request the Escrow Agent to confirm that a deposit has been received, but shall have no right to inspect, test, compile, or otherwise access the deposited materials prior to a valid release. Any technical verification must be mutually agreed in writing and shall be conducted at Customer's sole expense by a technical expert acceptable to Vendor.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Customer may request annual verification by the Escrow Agent (or an independent technical expert) to confirm that the deposited materials are complete and can be compiled. Verification costs are borne by Customer unless material deficiencies are found, in which case Vendor bears the cost and cures deficiencies within thirty (30) days.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language prohibiting any pre-release verification is a serious red flag — numerous escrow disputes have revealed that deposits were incomplete, outdated, or entirely non-functional. Annual verification is the market standard and should be non-negotiable. The cost allocation approach (customer pays unless deficient) is fair and widely accepted. For mission-critical software, consider requiring Vendor to conduct an internal build test with each deposit and certify success.

### Notes
Vendor-Favorable language with asymmetric cure periods (15 days for customer, 60 days for vendor), immediate vendor termination for payment and acceptable use, and exclusion of SLA failures from material breach is heavily one-sided. ### Standard

Vendor-Favorable language with a 15-day window, vendor-standard format only, and retention of anonymized/aggregated data provides weak protection. ## Renewal Notice Mechanics

### Standard
> Either party may elect not to renew this Agreement by providing written notice of non-renewal at least sixty (60) days prior to the end of the then-current Term. Notice shall be delivered in accordance with the notice provisions of this Agreement. If neither party provides timely non-renewal notice, the Agreement shall automatically renew for successive one (1) year renewal terms. Vendor shall send Customer a written renewal reminder at least ninety (90) days prior to the end of the then-current Term, identifying the renewal date, applicable pricing, and the deadline for non-renewal notice.

### Aggressive (Customer-Favorable)
> Vendor shall provide Customer with written notice of the upcoming renewal at least one hundred twenty (120) days prior to the end of the then-current Term. Such notice shall include: (a) the renewal date; (b) the fees for the renewal term, including any proposed increases; (c) any changes to terms or service scope; and (d) the deadline for Customer's non-renewal notice. Customer may elect not to renew by providing written notice at least sixty (60) days prior to the end of the then-current Term. If Vendor fails to provide the 120-day renewal notice, the Agreement shall renew at the then-current pricing with no fee increase, and Customer's non-renewal notice period shall be extended to thirty (30) days prior to the renewal date. Auto-renewal shall not take effect if Customer has an unresolved material dispute with Vendor at the time of renewal.

### Vendor-Favorable
> This Agreement shall automatically renew unless Customer provides written notice of non-renewal at least one hundred twenty (120) days prior to the end of the then-current Term. Vendor shall have no obligation to provide a renewal reminder. Non-renewal notices must be sent to Vendor's designated contracts address by certified mail or overnight courier; email notice shall not be sufficient. If Customer fails to provide timely non-renewal notice, Customer shall be obligated to pay all fees for the full renewal term regardless of actual usage.

### Minimum Acceptable
> Either party may provide written notice of non-renewal at least sixty (60) days prior to the end of the then-current Term. If neither party provides notice, the Agreement shall automatically renew for successive one-year terms.

### Notes
Renewal notice mechanics are one of the most common sources of contract disputes and inadvertent lock-in. Key issues: (1) Notice period length — 60 days is standard and reasonable; 120 days (Vendor-Favorable) creates a wide window for missed deadlines. (2) Vendor reminder obligation — requiring the vendor to send a proactive renewal reminder is a critical customer protection. Many customers miss renewal deadlines simply because they were not tracking them. (3) Delivery method — restricting non-renewal notice to certified mail (Vendor-Favorable) creates unnecessary friction. Push for notice methods consistent with the general notice clause. (4) Consequences of missed notice — full-term payment obligations for a missed deadline are punitive; push for a grace period or month-to-month holdover. (5) Fee transparency — require vendors to disclose renewal pricing before the non-renewal deadline so customers can make informed decisions.

## Early Termination Fees

### Standard

### Notes
Early termination fees are the economic enforcement mechanism for committed-term contracts. Key negotiation points: (1) Fee calculation — 100% of remaining value (Vendor-Favorable) makes termination for convenience economically identical to completing the term, which is unreasonable. Push for a declining balance that recognizes the vendor's diminishing reliance interest over time. (2) Cap — a reasonable cap (e.g., 3-6 months of fees) balances vendor revenue certainty against customer flexibility. (3) Waiver conditions — the fee should be waived for vendor-caused termination triggers (breach, SLA failure, security incidents). (4) Interaction with other provisions — ensure that early termination fees do not apply when the customer exercises termination rights under other clauses (chronic failure, change of control). (5) Characterization — vendors sometimes frame the fee as "liquidated damages" to strengthen enforceability; ensure the amount is a reasonable estimate, not a penalty.

### Notes
Refund mechanics directly affect the financial risk of termination. Key issues: (1) Pro-rata calculation — daily pro-rata is most favorable to customers; monthly or quarterly rounding benefits vendors. Ensure the calculation method is specified. (2) Timing — 30 days is standard; 60 days (Vendor-Favorable) is excessive for returning money already paid. (3) Setoff rights — vendors commonly seek broad setoff rights; limit setoff to undisputed, past-due amounts only. Including early termination fees and "transition costs" in the setoff (Vendor-Favorable) can consume the entire refund. (4) Currency — specify that refunds are in the same currency as payment to avoid exchange rate risk. (5) Refund form — credit toward future services (Vendor-Favorable) is not a real refund if the customer is leaving; push for cash refund. (6) Interest on late refunds — including an interest provision incentivizes timely payment. (7) Interaction with breach — if the vendor caused the termination, the refund should be more generous (e.g., including fees paid during the period of deficient performance).

The pricing model matters — "then-current rates" can be inflated; "at cost" or a pre-agreed rate schedule is preferable. ### Standard

### Notes
The most common failure point in transitions is inadequate knowledge transfer — insist on specific deliverables and hour commitments rather than vague "reasonable" cooperation language. The Vendor-Favorable refusal to cooperate with competitors is problematic because the successor vendor will often be a competitor; at minimum, require cooperation subject to reasonable confidentiality protections. ### Standard

### Notes
A written Wind-Down Plan is critical for accountability — without one, transitions tend to drift and fail. The Vendor-Favorable approach of providing only a "summary of recommended steps" upon request provides no meaningful structure. Insist on a written plan with milestones and deliverables at minimum. For complex engagements, the Aggressive version with weekly updates, a risk register, and a dedicated transition manager is appropriate. The remedy for late delivery (extending the transition at no cost) creates meaningful incentive for vendor compliance. In outsourcing agreements, the Wind-Down Plan should be developed and agreed during the initial contracting phase, not after termination notice.
