# Force Majeure

## Market Standard

**Typical position:** Neither party is liable for failure to perform obligations (other than payment obligations) due to events beyond its reasonable control, including natural disasters, war, terrorism, government actions, pandemics, and widespread infrastructure failures. The affected party must provide prompt notice and use commercially reasonable efforts to mitigate. Either party may terminate if the force majeure event continues for 60-90 days.

**Acceptable range:** Force majeure events typically include: acts of God, war, terrorism, civil unrest, pandemics/epidemics, government orders/sanctions, embargoes, strikes, power/internet outages, and natural disasters. Termination trigger between 30-120 days of continuous force majeure. Partial performance required to the extent possible. Payment obligations generally excluded from force majeure relief.

**Escalate if:**
- Force majeure excuses payment obligations
- No termination right after extended force majeure
- Force majeure includes events within the party's control (e.g., internal staffing issues, financial difficulties, market changes)
- No mitigation obligation
- No notice requirement for invoking force majeure
- Force majeure is entirely one-sided (only benefits vendor)
- Pandemic exclusion (post-COVID, some parties attempt to exclude pandemics)
- Economic hardship, currency fluctuations, or market conditions included as force majeure events

## Key Considerations

- Post-COVID, force majeure provisions receive much greater scrutiny
- Pandemics should generally remain a force majeure event, but "known pandemic" carve-outs may be appropriate for new agreements
- Cyber-attacks as force majeure is debatable — generally should not excuse a party that failed to implement reasonable security measures
- Supply chain disruptions may or may not qualify depending on foreseeability and the specific clause language
- Consider whether force majeure suspends obligations or terminates them
- Business continuity and disaster recovery plans should be referenced as mitigation
- For SaaS/cloud agreements, vendor's DR/BC capabilities should reduce reliance on force majeure
- Government regulatory changes should be distinguished from force majeure (regulatory change provisions may be more appropriate)

## Classification Guide

**GREEN:** Mutual force majeure; standard list of qualifying events including pandemics; payment obligations excluded; prompt notice required; mitigation obligation; termination right after 60-90 days; partial performance to extent possible.

**YELLOW:** Overly broad list of events; no explicit pandemic inclusion or exclusion; termination trigger over 90 days; no explicit mitigation obligation; cyber-attacks included without security caveat; one-sided but with reasonable scope.

**RED:** One-sided force majeure (vendor only); excuses payment obligations; includes events within the party's control (financial difficulties, staffing); no termination right regardless of duration; no notice or mitigation requirements; excludes pandemics entirely; includes economic/market conditions.
