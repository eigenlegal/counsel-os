# Representations & Warranties

## Market Standard

**Typical position:** Mutual representations that each party (a) has the authority to enter into the agreement, (b) the agreement constitutes a valid and binding obligation, and (c) execution does not conflict with other obligations. Vendor warrants that services will be performed in a professional and workmanlike manner consistent with industry standards. Vendor warrants that the software/service will materially conform to the documentation. Vendor warrants non-infringement of third-party IP rights. Vendor warrants compliance with applicable laws.

**Acceptable range:** Warranty period of 30-90 days for acceptance of deliverables. Re-performance or repair as the initial warranty remedy before refund. Vendor warrants that it has adequate personnel, resources, and expertise. Vendor warrants that deliverables will be free from material defects, malware, and disabling code. Vendor may disclaim implied warranties (merchantability, fitness for particular purpose) provided express warranties are adequate. Customer warrants that it has rights to its data and content.

**Escalate if:**
- Vendor disclaims ALL warranties including express warranties (services delivered "as-is")
- No warranty of non-infringement
- No compliance-with-law warranty
- Vendor disclaims responsibility for conformity to documentation or specifications
- No warranty remedy (re-performance, repair, refund)
- Vendor includes "as-is, where-is" language for a paid product or service
- Warranties are given only as of the effective date and not on an ongoing basis
- No malware or disabling code warranty
- Customer provides extensive warranties that are not matched by vendor

## Key Considerations

- Express warranties should be meaningful and aligned with the purpose of the engagement
- "Commercially reasonable efforts" vs. "best efforts" vs. "reasonable efforts" — understand the standard being applied
- Warranty remedies should be practical: re-performance first, credit/refund if re-performance fails
- Ongoing representations (e.g., compliance with laws) should be distinguished from point-in-time representations
- For SaaS, the warranty of conformity to documentation makes the documentation contractually significant — review it
- Malware and disabling code warranties protect against vendor-introduced security risks
- Anti-corruption / FCPA / UK Bribery Act representations may be necessary for international engagements
- Sanctions and export control representations are increasingly standard
- Consider whether warranties survive termination for a reasonable period

## Classification Guide

**GREEN:** Mutual authority and enforceability representations; vendor warrants professional/workmanlike performance; material conformity to documentation; non-infringement; compliance with applicable laws; malware-free; adequate warranty remedies (re-perform, repair, refund); standard implied warranty disclaimers with robust express warranties.

**YELLOW:** Limited warranty period (under 30 days); re-performance as sole remedy with no refund backstop; no explicit malware warranty; vague "commercially reasonable" performance standard; warranties given only as of effective date; limited compliance-with-law warranty.

**RED:** All warranties disclaimed ("as-is"); no non-infringement warranty; no compliance-with-law warranty; no warranty remedies; vendor disclaims conformity to documentation; extensive customer warranties with no reciprocal vendor warranties; no malware or disabling code warranty; vendor includes broad disclaimer of fitness or merchantability without any meaningful express warranty.
