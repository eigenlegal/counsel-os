# Limitation of Liability

## Market Standard

**Typical position:** Mutual cap on direct damages equal to 12 months of fees paid or payable under the agreement. Exclusion of consequential, indirect, incidental, special, and punitive damages for both parties.

**Acceptable range:** Liability cap between 6 and 24 months of fees. Some carve-outs from the cap (e.g., IP indemnification, confidentiality breach, willful misconduct, gross negligence) are common and acceptable. Uncapped liability for death/bodily injury and fraud is standard.

**Escalate if:**
- Vendor demands unlimited liability from customer only (non-mutual)
- Cap is set below 6 months of fees
- No carve-outs for IP infringement, data breach, or confidentiality
- Liability cap is based on amounts "paid" rather than "paid or payable" (disadvantages early-stage contracts)
- Vendor seeks to exclude liability for data breaches involving customer data

## Key Considerations

- The liability cap should be proportionate to the contract value and risk profile
- Carve-outs should be symmetric where possible — if vendor carves out customer's uncapped obligations, customer should have reciprocal carve-outs
- Data breach liability is increasingly carved out from general caps, especially post-GDPR and under US state privacy laws
- Insurance backing should be verified for any uncapped or super-capped obligations
- "Per-claim" vs. "aggregate" cap distinction matters — aggregate is more protective for the party bearing liability
- Consider whether the cap resets annually or is a lifetime aggregate

## Classification Guide

**GREEN:** Mutual cap at 12+ months of fees; standard consequential damages exclusion with reasonable carve-outs for IP indemnity, data breach, and confidentiality; uncapped for fraud, willful misconduct, and bodily injury.

**YELLOW:** Cap between 6-12 months of fees; asymmetric carve-outs favoring the other party; no data breach carve-out; per-claim cap rather than aggregate; cap based on amounts "paid" only.

**RED:** Cap below 6 months of fees; entirely one-sided limitation favoring vendor; no carve-outs for data breach or IP indemnification; vendor excludes all liability for data loss or security incidents; liability cap that effectively eliminates meaningful recovery.
