# Dispute Resolution

## Market Standard

**Typical position:** Escalation process starting with good-faith negotiation between designated executives (15-30 days), followed by mediation, then binding arbitration or litigation. Choice of law and venue specified. For US domestic deals, governing law is typically the state of the drafting party or a neutral jurisdiction (Delaware, New York). Arbitration under AAA or JAMS rules is common for commercial contracts.

**Acceptable range:** Negotiation periods of 15-45 days. Mediation as an optional intermediate step. Arbitration with 1 or 3 arbitrators depending on dispute value (1 for under $1M, 3 for over). Litigation in state or federal courts as an alternative to arbitration. Carve-outs for injunctive relief (either party may seek injunctive relief in any court of competent jurisdiction without first exhausting dispute resolution procedures). Prevailing party entitled to reasonable attorneys' fees.

**Escalate if:**
- Governing law is a foreign jurisdiction with no connection to either party or the transaction
- Mandatory arbitration in a jurisdiction inconvenient for our company with no virtual hearing option
- No carve-out for injunctive relief from the escalation process
- Dispute resolution requires proceedings in multiple jurisdictions
- Waiver of jury trial without corresponding arbitration clause
- Class action waiver in a consumer-facing context
- No escalation process — disputes go directly to litigation/arbitration
- Choice of law creates conflicts with mandatory local regulations

## Key Considerations

- Governing law should be a jurisdiction with well-developed commercial law (Delaware, New York, England & Wales, Singapore)
- Arbitration is generally preferred for cross-border disputes due to enforceability under the New York Convention
- Confidentiality of proceedings is an advantage of arbitration over litigation
- Consider whether the arbitral award is final and binding or subject to limited appeal
- Injunctive relief carve-outs are critical for IP infringement and confidentiality breaches
- Small claims carve-outs can be efficient for low-value disputes
- Consider including an expert determination process for technical disputes (e.g., SLA calculations)
- Multi-tiered dispute resolution (negotiation > mediation > arbitration) reduces litigation costs

## Classification Guide

**GREEN:** Multi-step escalation (negotiation, then mediation or arbitration); governing law in a well-developed commercial jurisdiction; venue convenient for both parties or virtual hearings available; injunctive relief carve-out; 1-3 arbitrators under reputable rules (AAA, JAMS, ICC); prevailing party attorneys' fees; confidential proceedings.

**YELLOW:** Direct to arbitration without negotiation step; governing law in a less familiar but reasonable jurisdiction; no mediation option; no prevailing party fee-shifting; arbitration in the other party's home jurisdiction; waiver of jury trial without arbitration.

**RED:** Foreign governing law with no nexus to the transaction; mandatory litigation in an inconvenient distant jurisdiction; no injunctive relief carve-out; waiver of all equitable remedies; dispute resolution process that is prohibitively expensive or time-consuming; conflicting dispute resolution mechanisms in related agreements.
