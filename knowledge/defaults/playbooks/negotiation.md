# Negotiation Playbook

## When to Use
Use this playbook when preparing for and conducting contract negotiations with a counterparty. This applies to both initial deal negotiations and renegotiations of existing agreements.

## Prerequisites
- Completed contract review (see playbooks/contract-review.md)
- Issues list with prioritized items
- Business owner alignment on must-haves vs. nice-to-haves
- Understanding of the deal dynamics: leverage, alternatives, timeline, relationship importance
- Knowledge of counterparty's likely positions and constraints
- Load relevant position files from knowledge/defaults/positions/

## Process

### Step 1: Pre-Negotiation Strategy
Prepare the negotiation framework:
- **BATNA (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement):** What happens if we walk away?
- **Reservation Price:** The worst acceptable outcome on each key issue
- **Target:** The ideal outcome on each key issue
- **Trade-offs:** Which concessions can we make in exchange for what we need?
- **Sequencing:** Order issues from easiest to hardest to build momentum

Create a concession tracker with three columns:
| Issue | Our Position | Fallback Position | Walk-Away Point |

### Step 2: Issue Prioritization
Categorize all open issues into tiers:
- **Tier 1 — Must Have:** Deal-breakers if not resolved. These are non-negotiable legal or business requirements (e.g., liability cap, data protection compliance, IP ownership of custom work).
- **Tier 2 — Important:** Strong preference but room for creative solutions (e.g., SLA levels, termination convenience rights, indemnification scope).
- **Tier 3 — Nice to Have:** Preferred but expendable as trade currency (e.g., governing law preference, audit frequency, notice periods).

### Step 3: Draft the Redline
Prepare the redline with a strategic approach:
- Include all Tier 1 and Tier 2 positions in the initial redline
- Include selected Tier 3 positions as negotiation currency
- Use proposed language from the clause library where available
- Add comments explaining the rationale for significant changes (this builds credibility)
- Avoid over-marking — excessive redlines can signal inexperience or bad faith

### Step 4: Conduct the Negotiation
Follow these principles during negotiation calls or exchanges:

**Opening:**
- Summarize areas of agreement first to establish common ground
- Present the redline thematically (commercial terms, risk allocation, compliance) rather than page-by-page

**During:**
- Lead with rationale before stating positions ("Given that your product will process our customer PII, we need...")
- Use objective standards to justify positions (market practice, regulatory requirements, industry benchmarks)
- Make concessions conditional ("We can accept X if you agree to Y")
- Take notes on all agreements and commitments
- If stuck, propose alternatives rather than repeating the same position

**Closing:**
- Summarize all agreed points before ending the session
- Confirm next steps, deadlines, and who will prepare the next draft
- Document any oral agreements immediately in writing

### Step 5: Track and Close
After each negotiation session:
- Update the concession tracker
- Circulate a summary of agreed/open points to the internal team
- Prepare the next draft incorporating agreed changes
- Identify remaining open issues and develop strategies for resolution
- Escalate unresolved Tier 1 issues to senior leadership with a recommendation

### Step 6: Final Review
Before execution:
- Verify all negotiated changes are accurately reflected in the final draft
- Confirm no provisions were inadvertently changed or omitted
- Conduct a final read-through for internal consistency
- Obtain necessary internal approvals
- Verify signing authority

## Output Format
1. **Negotiation Prep Memo:** Summary of strategy, priorities, and BATNA
2. **Redline:** Marked-up contract with comments
3. **Concession Tracker:** Running log of offers, counteroffers, and agreements
4. **Session Notes:** Summary of each negotiation session
5. **Final Issues List:** Remaining open items with recommendations

## Calibration
- **Simple:** Few open issues, low value, standard terms. Single exchange of redlines. Target: 1-3 rounds over 1-2 weeks.
- **Standard:** Moderate number of issues, medium value. 2-4 rounds of negotiation. Target: 2-4 weeks.
- **Complex:** Many issues, high value, strategic relationship, multiple stakeholders. 4+ rounds with live negotiation sessions. Target: 4-12 weeks.
