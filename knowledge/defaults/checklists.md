## Contract Review

# Contract Review Checklist

## Structural Completeness

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Parties correctly identified with full legal entity names — incorrect entity names can void the agreement
- [ ] Effective date clearly stated — ambiguity creates disputes about when obligations begin
- [ ] Definitions section is complete and internally consistent — undefined terms create interpretation risk
- [ ] All exhibits, schedules, and order forms attached and referenced — missing attachments may be unenforceable
- [ ] Signature blocks match the identified parties — mismatch raises authority and enforceability concerns
- [ ] Order of precedence clause addresses conflicts between documents — without this, conflicting terms create litigation risk

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Jurisdiction of incorporation/formation stated for each party
- [ ] Recitals accurately describe the relationship and purpose
- [ ] Amendment clause specifies requirements for modifications
- [ ] Notices clause with current addresses and acceptable delivery methods
- [ ] Entire agreement / integration clause present
- [ ] Counterparts clause (if applicable)

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Severability clause included
- [ ] Waiver clause (no waiver unless in writing)
- [ ] No blank fields or placeholder text remaining
- [ ] Cross-references are accurate
- [ ] Defined terms used consistently throughout

## Commercial Terms

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Scope of services or license grant clearly defined — vague scope is the leading cause of commercial disputes
- [ ] Pricing and fee structure matches the proposal/negotiation — discrepancies between proposal and contract are common
- [ ] Payment terms specified (Net 30, Net 60, etc.) — absent terms default to state law which may be unfavorable
- [ ] Price increase caps and notice requirements defined — uncapped increases create budget risk

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Invoice dispute process included
- [ ] Late payment interest rate specified and reasonable
- [ ] Tax allocation and gross-up provisions addressed
- [ ] Acceptance criteria for deliverables defined
- [ ] Change order process documented

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Expenses reimbursement terms (if applicable)
- [ ] Currency and exchange rate provisions (for international deals)
- [ ] Most-favored-customer pricing provisions

## Term and Termination

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Initial term length matches business intent — multi-year commitments require business justification
- [ ] Auto-renewal mechanics clearly stated — surprise renewals at increased rates are a top complaint
- [ ] Termination for cause with reasonable cure period (30 days) — shorter periods limit ability to remedy
- [ ] Post-termination obligations (data return, transition assistance) — without these, business continuity is at risk

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Non-renewal notice period and method specified
- [ ] Termination for convenience rights (mutual or one-sided)
- [ ] Termination for insolvency/bankruptcy
- [ ] Change of control termination right
- [ ] Survival clause listing provisions that survive termination

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Early termination fees (if any) are reasonable
- [ ] Transition assistance period and cost allocation

## Risk Allocation

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Limitation of liability — mutual cap proportionate to deal value — uncapped liability is unacceptable
- [ ] Consequential damages exclusion with appropriate carve-outs — blanket exclusion with no carve-outs favors vendor
- [ ] Liability cap carve-outs identified (IP, data breach, confidentiality, willful misconduct) — these are the highest-risk areas
- [ ] Vendor IP infringement indemnity with cure path — without this, Customer bears risk for Vendor's IP issues

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Indemnification obligations are mutual where appropriate
- [ ] Data breach indemnification addressed
- [ ] Insurance requirements specified and proportionate
- [ ] Force majeure clause is mutual and reasonable

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Super-cap or separate cap for excluded claims
- [ ] Fee-shifting provisions (prevailing party attorneys' fees)

## Intellectual Property

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Customer data ownership confirmed — customer owns its data — most critical IP provision
- [ ] Custom work product ownership or license clearly assigned — ambiguity defaults to vendor ownership
- [ ] AI/ML training restrictions on customer data — absent restrictions allow vendor to train models on your data
- [ ] No broad assignment of customer IP to vendor — sometimes hidden in feedback or improvement clauses

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Pre-existing IP ownership retained by each party
- [ ] License grant for vendor IP embedded in deliverables
- [ ] Open-source disclosure requirements
- [ ] Feedback clause (if any) is reasonable

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Source code escrow (for critical on-premise software)
- [ ] Technology roadmap commitments

## Data Protection

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Data Processing Agreement (DPA) included or referenced — legally required when processing personal data
- [ ] Data breach notification timeline (72 hours or less) — longer timelines may violate GDPR Article 33
- [ ] Data return and deletion upon termination — without this, vendor may retain data indefinitely
- [ ] No vendor use of personal data for own purposes — common in vendor paper, violates most privacy laws

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Data processing limited to customer instructions
- [ ] Sub-processor disclosure and change notification
- [ ] Security measures and certifications specified
- [ ] Cross-border transfer mechanisms in place
- [ ] Audit rights (direct or third-party)

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Data localization commitments where required
- [ ] Privacy impact assessment cooperation
- [ ] Data subject request assistance obligations

## Confidentiality

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Mutual confidentiality obligations — one-sided confidentiality only protects the disclosing party
- [ ] Standard exclusions present (public knowledge, independent development, prior possession) — missing exclusions create overbroad obligations
- [ ] No problematic residuals clause — residuals clauses effectively nullify confidentiality

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Reasonable duration (3-5 years, perpetual for trade secrets)
- [ ] Need-to-know dissemination limits
- [ ] Return or destruction upon termination
- [ ] Compelled disclosure with notice
- [ ] Injunctive relief acknowledged

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Binding confidentiality obligations for permitted recipients
- [ ] Specific technical safeguards requirements

## Final Checks
- [ ] No internal inconsistencies between sections
- [ ] Exhibits and schedules are complete
- [ ] Signing authority confirmed for both parties

## Due Diligence

# Due Diligence Checklist

## Corporate and Organizational

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Certificate/articles of incorporation (and all amendments) — confirms legal existence and authorized activities
- [ ] Good standing certificates for all jurisdictions — expired standing can void contracts and create liability
- [ ] Capitalization table (fully diluted) — determines ownership, dilution, and liquidation preferences
- [ ] Shareholder/member agreements — reveals transfer restrictions, drag/tag rights, and voting agreements
- [ ] Convertible instruments (SAFEs, notes, warrants) — hidden dilution can materially affect valuation

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Bylaws or operating agreement (current, as amended)
- [ ] List of all subsidiaries, affiliates, and joint ventures
- [ ] Organizational chart
- [ ] Voting agreements and proxies
- [ ] Stock option plans and outstanding grants
- [ ] Board and committee minutes (last 3 years)

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Written consents (last 3 years)
- [ ] List of officers and directors (current and former, last 3 years)
- [ ] Qualifications to do business in foreign jurisdictions

## Material Contracts

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Top 10 customer contracts by revenue — revenue concentration and key customer terms are valuation drivers
- [ ] Top 10 vendor/supplier contracts by spend — dependency on critical vendors creates operational risk
- [ ] Contracts with change-of-control provisions — may trigger consent requirements or termination rights upon acquisition
- [ ] Contracts with exclusivity provisions — limits on future business activities affect strategic value
- [ ] Government contracts — special regulatory requirements, termination rights, and compliance obligations

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Strategic partnership or channel agreements
- [ ] Joint venture agreements
- [ ] Reseller, distributor, or agency agreements
- [ ] Contracts with related parties
- [ ] Contracts with most-favored-nation clauses
- [ ] Material amendments and side letters
- [ ] Letters of intent or term sheets for pending transactions

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Franchise agreements
- [ ] Contracts with non-compete or non-solicitation provisions
- [ ] Guarantee or surety agreements

## Intellectual Property

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Patent portfolio (issued, pending, provisional) — core IP assets that drive valuation
- [ ] IP assignment agreements (employees and contractors) — gaps in assignment create ownership disputes
- [ ] Open-source software usage inventory and compliance — license violations can force code disclosure
- [ ] IP-related litigation or disputes (current and historical) — ongoing disputes create contingent liabilities
- [ ] Trade secret inventory and protection measures — inadequate protection can destroy trade secret status

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Trademark registrations and applications
- [ ] Copyright registrations
- [ ] Domain name portfolio
- [ ] Inbound technology licenses
- [ ] Outbound technology licenses
- [ ] IP opinions or freedom-to-operate analyses

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Software escrow agreements
- [ ] Open-source contribution policies

## Litigation and Disputes

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Schedule of all pending litigation (plaintiff and defendant) — direct financial exposure and distraction risk
- [ ] Schedule of all threatened litigation — potential liabilities not yet on the books
- [ ] Regulatory proceedings or investigations — can result in fines, consent decrees, or operational restrictions
- [ ] Settlement agreements (last 5 years) — reveals patterns and ongoing compliance obligations

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Judgment and lien search results
- [ ] Consent decrees or compliance orders
- [ ] Arbitration proceedings (current and historical)
- [ ] Internal investigations (last 3 years)
- [ ] Material correspondence with regulators

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Product liability claims or recalls
- [ ] Insurance coverage disputes

## Employment and Benefits

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Employee census (headcount, titles, locations, compensation) — labor is typically the largest cost
- [ ] Employment agreements for key employees — retention risk and restrictive covenant enforceability
- [ ] Non-compete and non-solicitation agreements — affects ability to retain talent post-acquisition
- [ ] Invention assignment agreements — gaps create IP ownership risk
- [ ] EEOC charges or employment-related litigation — ongoing claims create financial and reputational exposure

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Offer letter templates
- [ ] Employee handbook and policies
- [ ] Contractor/consultant agreements
- [ ] Employee benefit plans (health, retirement, equity)
- [ ] ERISA compliance documentation
- [ ] Union/collective bargaining agreements
- [ ] WARN Act compliance (recent or planned layoffs)

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Workers' compensation claims history
- [ ] I-9 and immigration compliance
- [ ] Organizational restructuring or RIF history

## Data Privacy and Security

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Data processing inventory (what data, from whom, purposes, legal bases) — foundation for all privacy compliance
- [ ] Data breach history and response documentation — prior breaches indicate security posture and regulatory risk
- [ ] Security certifications (SOC 2, ISO 27001, etc.) — validates baseline security maturity
- [ ] Privacy policies (website, mobile app, employee) — inconsistencies with actual practices create FTC risk

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Data processing agreements with vendors
- [ ] Sub-processor list
- [ ] Privacy impact assessments / DPIAs
- [ ] Cross-border data transfer mechanisms (SCCs, BCRs, etc.)
- [ ] Information security policies
- [ ] Incident response plan
- [ ] Business continuity and disaster recovery plans
- [ ] Cyber insurance policy

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Cookie/tracking consent mechanisms
- [ ] Data subject access request process and history
- [ ] Penetration test reports (last 12 months)

## Tax

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Federal and state tax returns (last 3 years) — identifies liabilities and compliance issues
- [ ] Tax audits and assessments — unresolved audits create contingent liability
- [ ] Net operating loss carryforwards — valuable tax asset that may be limited by ownership changes (Section 382)

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Transfer pricing documentation
- [ ] Sales and use tax compliance
- [ ] International tax structure
- [ ] 409A valuations
- [ ] R&D tax credit claims

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Withholding tax compliance
- [ ] Property tax records

## Insurance and Financial

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] D&O insurance policy — protects acquirer's incoming directors and officers
- [ ] Cyber / technology E&O policy — covers data breach and technology failure liabilities
- [ ] Material debt instruments — security interests and default provisions affect the transaction
- [ ] Material contingent liabilities — off-balance-sheet exposure that affects true valuation

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Commercial general liability policy
- [ ] Professional liability / E&O policy
- [ ] Claims history (last 5 years)
- [ ] Security interests and UCC filings
- [ ] Guarantees issued or received
- [ ] Accounts receivable aging

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Workers' compensation policy
- [ ] Property insurance
- [ ] Letters of credit
- [ ] Off-balance-sheet arrangements

## Regulatory and Compliance

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Permits, licenses, and registrations — operating without required permits can shut down the business
- [ ] Anti-corruption/FCPA compliance program — FCPA liability survives acquisition and transfers to buyer
- [ ] Industry-specific regulatory requirements — non-compliance can result in loss of operating authority

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Regulatory filings and reports
- [ ] Compliance program documentation
- [ ] Export control and sanctions compliance
- [ ] Environmental compliance and liabilities
- [ ] Consumer protection compliance

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Accessibility compliance (ADA, WCAG)
- [ ] Government audit history

## Employment Agreement

# Employment Agreement Checklist

## Offer Letter Fundamentals

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] At-will status clearly stated (if applicable) — absent at-will language may create implied contract claims
- [ ] Job title, reporting structure, and start date specified — ambiguity creates disputes about scope of role
- [ ] Base compensation clearly stated (salary or hourly rate) — compensation disputes are among the most common employment claims
- [ ] Classification as exempt or non-exempt correct under FLSA — misclassification triggers overtime liability and penalties
- [ ] Benefits eligibility and start date specified — verbal promises of benefits can create enforceable obligations

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Work location specified (office, remote, hybrid) with flexibility provisions
- [ ] Bonus or commission structure described (if applicable)
- [ ] Equity compensation referenced with separate grant documents
- [ ] Probationary or introductory period defined (if applicable)
- [ ] Relocation assistance terms (if applicable)

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Expense reimbursement policy referenced
- [ ] Professional development and training provisions
- [ ] Company property and equipment provisions

## Restrictive Covenants

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Non-compete scope reasonable in duration, geography, and activity — overbroad non-competes are unenforceable in many states and banned in some
- [ ] Non-compete compliant with state law of employee's work location — FTC and state legislatures are actively restricting non-competes
- [ ] Non-solicitation of customers limited to those the employee actually served — broad customer non-solicits may be treated as de facto non-competes
- [ ] Non-solicitation of employees reasonable in scope and duration — 12-24 months is typical; longer may be challenged
- [ ] Adequate consideration provided for restrictive covenants — in many states, continued employment alone is insufficient consideration

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Non-compete includes garden leave or compensation during restriction period
- [ ] Non-disclosure obligations clearly distinguished from non-compete
- [ ] Carve-outs for general skills and industry knowledge
- [ ] Enforceability savings clause (court may modify rather than void)
- [ ] Choice of law provision for restrictive covenants matches employee's state

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Non-compete notification obligations to future employers
- [ ] Injunctive relief provision for breach of restrictive covenants
- [ ] Step-down provisions (restrictions narrow over time)

## Confidentiality and IP Assignment

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Confidentiality obligations cover all proprietary information — gaps in coverage leave trade secrets unprotected
- [ ] Confidentiality survives termination of employment — time-limited confidentiality for trade secrets is inadequate
- [ ] Invention assignment covers all work-related inventions created during employment — absent assignment, employee may own inventions under default law
- [ ] State-specific carve-outs for personal inventions (e.g., California Labor Code 2870) — failure to include required carve-outs can void the entire assignment
- [ ] Work-for-hire designation for copyrightable materials — ensures employer owns copyrights from creation

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Pre-existing IP disclosed and excluded from assignment
- [ ] Moral rights waiver (to extent permitted by law)
- [ ] Cooperation obligation for IP registration and enforcement
- [ ] Return of company materials upon termination
- [ ] Obligation to disclose inventions during employment

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Open-source contribution policy referenced
- [ ] Social media and personal brand IP boundaries
- [ ] Assignment covers inventions conceived during employment but reduced to practice after

## Compensation and Benefits

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Total compensation package complete and consistent with offer — discrepancies between verbal offer and written agreement create claims
- [ ] Bonus eligibility, metrics, and payment timing specified — vague bonus language creates implied obligation to pay
- [ ] Commission plan attached or referenced with specificity — commission disputes are a leading source of employment litigation
- [ ] Equity vesting schedule and acceleration events specified — single-trigger vs. double-trigger acceleration has significant value implications

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Clawback provisions for bonuses and commissions
- [ ] Change-of-control provisions for equity acceleration
- [ ] Severance eligibility and amount (if applicable)
- [ ] Deferred compensation compliant with Section 409A
- [ ] Benefits continuation rights (COBRA, conversion)

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Sign-on bonus with repayment obligation if early departure
- [ ] Tuition reimbursement with service commitment
- [ ] Retention bonuses tied to specific milestones

## Termination Provisions

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Termination for cause defined with specific grounds — vague "cause" definitions create disputes about whether termination was proper
- [ ] Notice period requirements specified — inadequate notice can trigger breach claims and damage relationships
- [ ] Severance conditioned on execution of separation agreement and release — release must be supported by adequate consideration and comply with ADEA/OWBPA

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Cure period for performance-based termination
- [ ] Resignation notice requirements
- [ ] Garden leave provisions during notice period
- [ ] Treatment of unvested equity upon termination
- [ ] Treatment of accrued but unpaid bonuses/commissions

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Cooperation obligations post-termination
- [ ] Transition assistance requirements
- [ ] Reference and non-disparagement provisions

## Compliance and Regulatory

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Background check and drug testing provisions compliant with state law — "ban the box" and marijuana legalization create compliance traps
- [ ] Arbitration clause (if included) compliant with state requirements — some states restrict mandatory employment arbitration
- [ ] Governing law appropriate for employee's work location — employment law is generally governed by the state where work is performed

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Equal opportunity and non-discrimination provisions
- [ ] Whistleblower protection acknowledgment
- [ ] DTSA notice of immunity for confidential disclosures to government
- [ ] Electronic monitoring and privacy disclosures (where required)

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Compliance training requirements
- [ ] Code of conduct referenced and acknowledged
- [ ] Conflicts of interest disclosure requirements

## Execution and Administration

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] All documents in the employment package identified and attached — missing documents create ambiguity about what was agreed
- [ ] Adequate time provided for review (21 days for OWBPA; reasonable for all) — rushed execution may invalidate restrictive covenants

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Employee acknowledged receipt and understanding of all documents
- [ ] Separate signature pages for distinct agreements (NDA, IP assignment, non-compete)
- [ ] Executed copies filed in HR system with key dates calendared

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Periodic review cadence established for updating restrictive covenants
- [ ] Template version tracked for future updates

## Government Contract

# Government Contract Checklist

## Contract Type and Authority

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Contract type identified (firm-fixed-price, cost-reimbursement, time-and-materials, IDIQ, BPA) — type determines pricing risk, reporting obligations, and audit exposure
- [ ] Contracting officer identified and authority confirmed — only the contracting officer (CO) has authority to bind the government
- [ ] Contract vehicle identified (GSA Schedule, GWAC, BPA, standalone) — vehicle determines applicable terms, pricing, and competition requirements
- [ ] Period of performance clearly stated with option years — option years are not guaranteed and require affirmative government action
- [ ] Funding type confirmed (fully funded, incrementally funded) — incrementally funded contracts create risk of unfunded work

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Solicitation number and NAICS code verified
- [ ] Small business size standard confirmed for applicable NAICS
- [ ] Task order or delivery order scope matches the master contract
- [ ] Ceiling price and funded amount clearly distinguished
- [ ] Option exercise procedures and notice requirements

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Fee structure for cost-type contracts (fixed fee, incentive fee, award fee)
- [ ] Contract line item number (CLIN) structure logical and complete
- [ ] Key personnel requirements identified

## FAR Compliance

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] FAR 52.203-13 (Code of Business Ethics) incorporated — mandatory for contracts over $6M with performance over 120 days
- [ ] FAR 52.222-50 (Combating Trafficking in Persons) incorporated — mandatory in all solicitations and contracts
- [ ] FAR 52.204-25 (Prohibition on Covered Telecommunications Equipment) incorporated — mandatory prohibition on certain Chinese-origin equipment
- [ ] FAR 52.219 series (Small Business provisions) applicable and addressed — violations can trigger False Claims Act liability
- [ ] FAR 52.215-2 (Audit and Records - Negotiation) understood if cost-type contract — gives government access to books and records

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] FAR 52.212-4/5 (Commercial Products terms) applicable for commercial items
- [ ] FAR 52.227 series (IP and Data Rights) provisions reviewed
- [ ] FAR 52.232 series (Payment) provisions appropriate
- [ ] FAR 52.246 series (Quality Assurance) requirements identified
- [ ] FAR 52.249 series (Termination) clauses reviewed

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Agency-specific FAR supplements (DFARS, GSAM, etc.) identified
- [ ] Deviation clauses identified and assessed
- [ ] Class deviations vs. individual deviations distinguished

## Certifications and Representations

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] System for Award Management (SAM) registration current — expired SAM registration prevents contract award
- [ ] Representations and Certifications in SAM current and accurate — false certifications trigger False Claims Act liability
- [ ] Small business status certification accurate and current — false size certification is a criminal offense
- [ ] Organizational Conflict of Interest (OCI) analysis completed — unmitigated OCIs can void the contract and trigger debarment
- [ ] Debarment and suspension certification accurate — contracting with debarred entities is prohibited

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Section 508 (accessibility) compliance certification
- [ ] Buy American Act / Trade Agreements Act compliance
- [ ] Drug-Free Workplace certification
- [ ] Clean Air Act / Clean Water Act compliance
- [ ] Equal Opportunity compliance

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Taxpayer identification and employer identification verified
- [ ] Previous contracts and past performance references accurate
- [ ] E-Verify enrollment (for contracts with FAR 52.222-54)

## Flowdown Requirements

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Required FAR clauses flowed down to subcontractors — failure to flow down mandatory clauses creates compliance liability and potential False Claims Act exposure
- [ ] Small business subcontracting plan required (if contract exceeds $750K) — plan is a contractual obligation subject to liquidated damages
- [ ] Cybersecurity requirements flowed down (CMMC, NIST 800-171 for DoD) — DFARS 252.204-7012 requires flow-down of cybersecurity obligations
- [ ] Cost Accounting Standards (CAS) applicability determined — CAS-covered contracts impose extensive cost accounting requirements

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Subcontractor consent to audit by government
- [ ] Prompt payment to subcontractors (FAR 52.232-27)
- [ ] Limitation on subcontracting percentages
- [ ] Subcontractor reporting requirements (FSRS, eSRS)
- [ ] Intellectual property rights in subcontractor deliverables

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Country of origin requirements for subcontractor supplies
- [ ] Insurance and bonding requirements for subcontractors
- [ ] Key subcontractor approval requirements

## Set-Asides and Socioeconomic Programs

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Set-aside type identified (8(a), SDVOSB, WOSB, HUBZone, small business) — performing on a set-aside without meeting requirements is fraud
- [ ] Limitations on subcontracting met (contractor performs required percentage) — violation can result in contract termination and debarment
- [ ] Joint venture or mentor-protege arrangement properly structured (if applicable) — improper pass-through or affiliation can trigger size protests

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Similarly situated entity requirements for subcontractors
- [ ] Compliance with SBA regulations for the applicable program
- [ ] Recertification requirements for multi-year contracts
- [ ] Geographic restrictions (HUBZone principal office requirement)

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Subcontracting plan goals for each socioeconomic category
- [ ] Good faith effort documentation for subcontracting goals
- [ ] SBA compliance review readiness

## Intellectual Property and Data Rights

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Data rights clause identified (FAR 52.227-14, DFARS 252.227-7013/7014) — government data rights default to unlimited if not properly marked and asserted
- [ ] Contractor's rights in technical data and software properly asserted — failure to assert rights results in government receiving unlimited rights
- [ ] Pre-existing IP and commercial software excluded from government rights — without exclusion, government may claim rights to your commercial products
- [ ] Marking requirements understood and compliance process in place — improperly marked deliverables lose their restricted status

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] License rights categories understood (unlimited, government purpose, limited/restricted)
- [ ] Third-party software licenses compatible with government delivery
- [ ] Patent rights clause reviewed (FAR 52.227-11)
- [ ] Open-source software disclosed and approved

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Data rights assertion letter prepared and submitted
- [ ] Deferred ordering and deferred delivery rights
- [ ] Challenge procedures for restrictive markings

## Termination and Disputes

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Termination for Convenience clause understood — government can terminate any contract for convenience; contractor must understand cost recovery rights
- [ ] Termination for Default clause reviewed — default termination shifts costs to contractor and can affect past performance ratings
- [ ] Contract Disputes Act process understood — all disputes go through the CO, then Board of Contract Appeals or Court of Federal Claims
- [ ] Claim procedures and deadlines documented — CDA claims must be submitted to the CO within 6 years; delays can bar recovery

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Stop-work order rights and compensation
- [ ] Government delay costs recovery provisions
- [ ] Equitable adjustment procedures for changes
- [ ] Request for Equitable Adjustment (REA) process

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) availability
- [ ] Past performance evaluation protest rights
- [ ] Whistleblower retaliation protections

## Healthcare Baa

# Healthcare BAA Checklist

## HIPAA Business Associate Agreement Essentials

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Agreement identifies the parties as Covered Entity and Business Associate — HIPAA requires a BAA with every business associate that creates, receives, maintains, or transmits PHI
- [ ] Permitted uses and disclosures of PHI clearly defined — Business Associate may only use PHI as permitted by the BAA; unauthorized use is a HIPAA violation
- [ ] BA shall not use or disclose PHI other than as permitted by the BAA or as required by law — the core obligation that restricts BA's handling of PHI
- [ ] BA shall use appropriate safeguards to prevent unauthorized use or disclosure — 45 CFR 164.502(e)(1)(i); must cover administrative, technical, and physical safeguards
- [ ] BA shall report to CE any use or disclosure of PHI not provided for in the BAA — reporting obligation includes breaches, security incidents, and unauthorized disclosures

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] BA shall ensure any agents or subcontractors agree to the same restrictions
- [ ] BA shall make PHI available to individuals per the access right (45 CFR 164.524)
- [ ] BA shall make PHI available for amendment per 45 CFR 164.526
- [ ] BA shall make information available for accounting of disclosures per 45 CFR 164.528
- [ ] BA shall make internal practices and records available to HHS for compliance review

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] BAA term aligns with underlying services agreement
- [ ] BAA clearly states it supplements (does not replace) the underlying agreement
- [ ] Cross-references to DPA or security addendum if also processing personal data under GDPR

## PHI Scope and Handling

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Definition of PHI aligned with HIPAA (individually identifiable health information) — overly narrow definitions exclude data that should be protected
- [ ] Electronic PHI (ePHI) specifically addressed with Security Rule requirements — ePHI triggers additional technical safeguards beyond general PHI requirements
- [ ] Minimum necessary standard applied to all uses and disclosures — BA should only access the minimum PHI necessary for the permitted purpose
- [ ] De-identification standards specified if BA creates de-identified data — de-identification must follow Safe Harbor or Expert Determination method per 45 CFR 164.514

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Data use agreement for limited data sets (if applicable)
- [ ] Restrictions on PHI for marketing, sale, or fundraising
- [ ] Restrictions on PHI use for underwriting purposes
- [ ] Patient authorization requirements for uses beyond treatment, payment, and operations

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Data flow diagram showing PHI movement between parties
- [ ] PHI inventory or classification scheme
- [ ] Research use provisions (if applicable)

## Subcontractor Management

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] BA must enter BAAs with all subcontractors that access PHI — the 2013 Omnibus Rule extended BAA requirements to all downstream subcontractors
- [ ] Subcontractor BAAs must impose the same restrictions as the primary BAA — insufficient subcontractor obligations create compliance gaps and liability
- [ ] BA remains responsible for subcontractor compliance — BA cannot delegate away its HIPAA obligations through subcontracting
- [ ] CE notification of subcontractor engagement (advance notice preferred) — CE needs visibility into who handles its PHI

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Subcontractor security assessment process defined
- [ ] Subcontractor breach notification flows to both BA and CE
- [ ] List of current subcontractors with access to PHI provided
- [ ] Right to object to new subcontractors

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Subcontractor insurance requirements
- [ ] Subcontractor audit rights
- [ ] Subcontractor data handling certifications

## Breach Notification

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] BA shall notify CE of a breach of unsecured PHI without unreasonable delay and within 60 days of discovery — 45 CFR 164.410; this is the HIPAA maximum; negotiate for shorter (30 days or less)
- [ ] Notification includes: nature of breach, PHI involved, individuals affected, mitigation steps — CE needs this information to fulfill its own notification obligations under 45 CFR 164.404
- [ ] BA shall identify each individual whose PHI was or may have been breached — CE must notify affected individuals within 60 days per 45 CFR 164.404
- [ ] Discovery defined: when BA knew or should have known of the breach — "should have known" standard prevents BA from delaying by claiming ignorance
- [ ] BA shall cooperate with CE's investigation and regulatory response — CE is ultimately responsible for breach notification to individuals and HHS

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Security incidents (attempted but unsuccessful breaches) reporting requirements
- [ ] BA responsibility for notification costs (mailing, credit monitoring, call center)
- [ ] BA shall not make public statements about the breach without CE authorization
- [ ] Forensic investigation at BA's expense
- [ ] Root cause analysis and remediation plan

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Cyber insurance requirements for breach response
- [ ] Breach response playbook and contact information
- [ ] Tabletop exercise requirements

## Security Requirements

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Administrative safeguards specified (security officer, risk assessment, workforce training, sanction policy) — Security Rule requires specific administrative controls per 45 CFR 164.308
- [ ] Physical safeguards specified (facility access controls, device security, disposal) — 45 CFR 164.310 requirements for facilities where ePHI is stored
- [ ] Technical safeguards specified (access controls, audit controls, integrity controls, transmission security) — 45 CFR 164.312 requirements; encryption is addressable but expected
- [ ] Risk assessment conducted and documented — risk analysis is the foundational Security Rule requirement and the most common area of HHS enforcement
- [ ] Encryption of ePHI at rest and in transit — though "addressable" under HIPAA, unencrypted ePHI breaches are presumed reportable

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Multi-factor authentication for systems containing ePHI
- [ ] Minimum password and session management standards
- [ ] Audit logging and monitoring requirements
- [ ] Vulnerability scanning and penetration testing cadence
- [ ] Incident response plan specific to PHI

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] HITRUST certification or SOC 2 Type II with HIPAA criteria
- [ ] Business continuity and disaster recovery for ePHI systems
- [ ] Employee background check requirements
- [ ] Physical security for data centers

## Termination and PHI Disposition

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] CE may terminate BAA if BA materially breaches and fails to cure — 45 CFR 164.504(e)(1)(ii); BAA must provide termination right for breach
- [ ] Upon termination, BA shall return or destroy all PHI — 45 CFR 164.504(e)(2)(ii)(J); no PHI should remain with former BA
- [ ] If return or destruction is infeasible, BA shall extend protections and limit further use — infeasibility exception must be narrow and justified
- [ ] BAA survives termination of the underlying services agreement until PHI disposition is complete — protections cannot lapse while PHI is still held

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Certification of PHI destruction provided to CE
- [ ] Destruction method specified (NIST 800-88 or equivalent)
- [ ] Timeline for return or destruction (30 days or less)
- [ ] Obligations extend to subcontractors' copies of PHI
- [ ] Ongoing obligations for retained PHI clearly stated

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Data migration assistance provisions
- [ ] Format for PHI return specified
- [ ] Audit right to verify destruction

## Regulatory and Enforcement

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] BA acknowledges direct HIPAA enforcement liability — since 2013 Omnibus Rule, HHS can directly enforce against BAs, not just through CEs
- [ ] State health privacy law requirements identified and addressed — many states have health privacy laws stricter than HIPAA (e.g., California CMIA, Texas HB 300)
- [ ] 42 CFR Part 2 requirements addressed if substance use disorder treatment data is involved — Part 2 data has stricter protections than general PHI

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] HITECH Act provisions incorporated (breach notification, enforcement)
- [ ] State breach notification requirements identified
- [ ] Insurance requirements for HIPAA-related claims
- [ ] Indemnification for regulatory fines and penalties

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Information Blocking provisions addressed (21st Century Cures Act, if applicable)
- [ ] Patient right of access provisions (if applicable to BA)
- [ ] OCR audit readiness assessment

## Ip Assignment

# IP Assignment Checklist

## Scope of Assignment

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Assignment covers all IP types: patents, copyrights, trade secrets, trademarks, and know-how — incomplete type coverage leaves gaps in ownership
- [ ] Assignment scope clearly defined: inventions, works of authorship, discoveries, improvements, and ideas — vague scope creates disputes about what was assigned
- [ ] Temporal scope specified: during employment/engagement and related to company business — overbroad temporal scope may be unenforceable
- [ ] Work-for-hire designation included for copyrightable works — without this, copyright may vest in the creator under default law
- [ ] Assignment effective upon creation (present tense: "hereby assigns") — future-tense assignment ("agrees to assign") may not automatically transfer rights

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Assignment covers improvements and derivative works
- [ ] Assignment covers inventions conceived during engagement but reduced to practice after
- [ ] Assignment includes all worldwide rights
- [ ] Assignment covers both registered and unregistered IP rights
- [ ] Assignment includes the right to apply for and maintain registrations

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Assignment covers domain names and social media accounts created for company
- [ ] Assignment addresses jointly created works

## Pre-Existing IP Exclusions

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Schedule of pre-existing IP completed and attached — absent schedule creates disputes about what the assignor owned beforehand
- [ ] Pre-existing IP clearly identified with sufficient specificity — vague descriptions like "prior inventions" provide inadequate protection
- [ ] License-back granted for pre-existing IP incorporated into assigned work — without a license, company cannot use assigned work that incorporates prior IP
- [ ] License-back scope appropriate (non-exclusive, perpetual, royalty-free, sublicensable) — restrictive license terms can hamstring company's use of its own work product

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Process for adding items to the pre-existing IP schedule
- [ ] Representation that the pre-existing IP schedule is complete
- [ ] Restrictions on incorporating pre-existing IP without approval
- [ ] Third-party IP identified and excluded

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Open-source contributions disclosed and excluded
- [ ] Academic or research IP identified if applicable

## State-Specific Compliance

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] California Labor Code Section 2870 notice included (if CA employee) — failure to include the statutory notice can void the entire assignment
- [ ] Equivalent statutory carve-outs for other states (DE, IL, MN, NC, WA, etc.) — many states have adopted California-style protections
- [ ] Assignment does not cover inventions developed entirely on personal time with personal resources unrelated to company business — attempting to claim personal inventions is unenforceable in many states

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] State-specific consideration requirements met (some states require separate consideration beyond employment)
- [ ] Notice of state law protections provided in a conspicuous manner
- [ ] Assignment limited to inventions related to the company's actual or anticipated business

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Legal review of enforceability in assignor's state of residence
- [ ] Multi-state compliance for remote or mobile employees

## Moral Rights and Waivers

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Moral rights waiver included to the fullest extent permitted by law — moral rights (attribution, integrity) can restrict company's use of assigned works
- [ ] Waiver covers rights of attribution, integrity, and disclosure — partial waivers leave residual restrictions on modification and use
- [ ] International moral rights addressed (if assignor is outside US) — moral rights are inalienable in many jurisdictions (France, Germany, UK)

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Consent to company's modification, adaptation, and derivative use without attribution
- [ ] Waiver extends to successors and assigns
- [ ] Acknowledgment that company may exercise rights without further consent

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Specific jurisdictional analysis for key non-US jurisdictions
- [ ] Alternative arrangements where moral rights cannot be waived (e.g., irrevocable consent)

## Further Assurances and Cooperation

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Obligation to execute further documents to perfect assignment — "hereby assigns" may not be sufficient for all IP registrations
- [ ] Power of attorney to sign documents if assignor is unavailable or refuses — critical backstop if assignor becomes uncooperative
- [ ] Cooperation obligation for patent prosecution, trademark registration, and enforcement — company may need assignor's assistance years later
- [ ] Cooperation obligations survive termination of employment/engagement — most IP needs arise after the relationship ends

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Compensation for post-termination cooperation time
- [ ] Obligation to provide information and testimony in IP disputes
- [ ] Reasonable notice and scheduling requirements for cooperation
- [ ] Expenses reimbursement for cooperation activities

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Duration of cooperation obligations specified
- [ ] Process for requesting cooperation after termination

## Representations and Warranties

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Representation that assignor has full right to assign — ensures no conflicting obligations to prior employers or third parties
- [ ] Representation that assigned IP does not infringe third-party rights — protects company from inheriting infringement liability
- [ ] Representation that no third party has any claim to the assigned IP — catches joint inventor or co-author issues
- [ ] Disclosure of any obligations to prior employers that may affect assignment — prior employer IP agreements may claim overlapping inventions

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Representation that pre-existing IP schedule is complete and accurate
- [ ] Representation that no government funding contributed to the assigned IP
- [ ] Representation that no open-source licenses encumber the assigned IP
- [ ] Indemnification for breach of representations

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Representation regarding absence of liens or encumbrances on IP
- [ ] Representation regarding status of any pending IP applications

## Contractor-Specific Provisions

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Independent contractor agreement includes IP assignment (not just work-for-hire) — work-for-hire doctrine is limited to specific categories for non-employees
- [ ] Contractor's subcontractors bound by equivalent IP assignment obligations — sub-contractor work may not be covered by contractor's assignment
- [ ] Contractor represents no conflicting obligations to other clients — contractors often serve multiple clients simultaneously

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Contractor's use of third-party tools and libraries disclosed
- [ ] Deliverable acceptance process includes IP verification
- [ ] Assignment triggered upon payment or delivery (as appropriate)

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Escrow arrangements for source code or deliverables
- [ ] IP audit rights during and after engagement

## Litigation Hold

# Litigation Hold Checklist

## Trigger Assessment

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Identify the triggering event (lawsuit, demand letter, regulatory inquiry, anticipated claim) — preservation obligations attach when litigation is reasonably anticipated, not just when filed
- [ ] Assess whether preservation obligations have been triggered — failure to preserve once obligations attach can result in spoliation sanctions
- [ ] Determine the preservation obligation start date — the start date defines the scope of what must be preserved
- [ ] Engage outside counsel (if applicable) — early engagement protects privilege and ensures proper scope

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Determine the scope of the dispute (parties, claims, relevant time period)
- [ ] Identify the legal basis for the hold (pending litigation, reasonably anticipated, regulatory investigation)
- [ ] Assess potential exposure and severity
- [ ] Determine whether insurance carriers should be notified

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Review applicable court rules for preservation obligations
- [ ] Assess whether opposing party has been notified of preservation expectations

## Scope Definition

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Define the subject matter of the hold — overbroad holds are costly; too narrow creates spoliation risk
- [ ] Identify relevant time period (start date through present, or specific date range) — time boundaries determine the volume of data to preserve
- [ ] Identify relevant parties, entities, and counterparties — missing a key entity can result in destruction of critical evidence
- [ ] Define relevant document categories and types — specificity reduces burden while ensuring completeness

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Identify relevant projects, transactions, or business matters
- [ ] Identify relevant geographic locations and offices
- [ ] Define relevant keywords and search terms for electronic discovery
- [ ] Determine whether international data privacy laws affect preservation scope

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Assess proportionality of preservation scope to the claims
- [ ] Consider whether a phased preservation approach is appropriate

## Key Custodian Identification

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Identify all individuals likely to have relevant documents or information — missing custodians is the most common discovery failure
- [ ] Include current employees, former employees, contractors, and executives — former employees' data may be at risk of deletion
- [ ] Identify custodians across all relevant departments (business, legal, finance, IT, HR, sales, support, executive) — siloed preservation misses cross-functional evidence
- [ ] Document the custodian list and the basis for inclusion — documentation protects against spoliation claims

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Prioritize custodians by likely relevance
- [ ] Identify custodians who are flight risks or soon departing
- [ ] Confirm custodian contact information is current
- [ ] Assess whether any custodians are outside the jurisdiction

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Interview key custodians to understand their data landscape
- [ ] Map custodian relationships to identify additional custodians

## Data Source Identification

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Email systems (Exchange, Gmail, etc.) — email is the most requested discovery source in litigation
- [ ] Instant messaging and chat platforms (Slack, Teams, etc.) — chat messages are increasingly discoverable and often contain candid communications
- [ ] Document management systems (SharePoint, Google Drive, Dropbox, etc.) — primary repositories for business documents
- [ ] Mobile devices (phones, tablets, personal BYOD devices) — text messages and mobile apps are frequently relevant

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Project management tools (Jira, Asana, etc.)
- [ ] CRM systems (Salesforce, HubSpot, etc.)
- [ ] Financial systems (ERP, billing, accounting)
- [ ] Source code repositories (if relevant)
- [ ] Laptops and desktops
- [ ] Shared network drives and file servers
- [ ] Cloud storage and SaaS applications
- [ ] Backup tapes and archives

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Voicemail systems
- [ ] Video conferencing recordings
- [ ] Social media accounts (corporate)
- [ ] Physical files and records
- [ ] IoT devices and access logs

## Litigation Hold Notice

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Draft the hold notice with: description of matter, scope of preservation, types of materials, instruction to cease destruction, specific examples, contact person, acknowledgment requirement — the notice is the primary defense against spoliation claims
- [ ] Have the notice reviewed by legal counsel — ensures the notice is legally sufficient
- [ ] Distribute the notice to all identified custodians — documented distribution is essential evidence of compliance
- [ ] Collect acknowledgments from all custodians — unacknowledged notices create enforcement gaps

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Distribute to IT for system-level preservation
- [ ] Follow up with custodians who have not acknowledged within 5 business days
- [ ] Include instruction to preserve auto-delete settings and suspend retention policies
- [ ] Provide foreign-language versions if custodians are non-English speakers

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Offer a Q&A session for custodians with complex data landscapes
- [ ] Provide department-specific examples of relevant documents

## Technical Preservation

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Suspend auto-deletion policies for relevant custodians and data sources — auto-deletion is the most common cause of inadvertent spoliation
- [ ] Place legal holds in email/collaboration systems (Exchange, Google Vault, etc.) — technical holds are more reliable than relying on individual compliance
- [ ] Preserve departing employee devices and accounts — departing employees' data is permanently lost if not preserved before offboarding

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Suspend email retention policies for relevant mailboxes
- [ ] Preserve relevant backup tapes or archives from recycling
- [ ] Image or preserve relevant mobile devices
- [ ] Preserve relevant cloud storage and SaaS data
- [ ] Document all preservation steps taken and dates

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Preserve database snapshots if structured data is relevant
- [ ] Engage forensic vendor for complex data sources

## Ongoing Management

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Maintain a litigation hold log (issuance date, custodians, acknowledgments, reminders, data sources) — the log is your compliance record if preservation is challenged
- [ ] Send periodic reminders to custodians (quarterly recommended) — reminder frequency shows ongoing diligence
- [ ] Manage custodian departures (preserve accounts and devices before offboarding) — the single highest-risk moment for data loss

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Update the custodian list as new relevant individuals are identified
- [ ] Issue supplemental hold notices if the scope changes
- [ ] Coordinate with IT on system changes that could affect preserved data
- [ ] Document any preservation failures or gaps and steps to address them

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Conduct periodic compliance audits of preservation
- [ ] Track costs associated with preservation for budgeting

## Hold Release

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Confirm the triggering matter has been fully resolved — premature release can result in sanctions if the matter is revived
- [ ] Obtain legal counsel approval to release the hold — only legal should authorize release

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Issue a hold release notice to all custodians
- [ ] Coordinate with IT to release technical holds
- [ ] Resume normal retention and deletion policies

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Document the hold release in the litigation hold log
- [ ] Retain the hold log and notices as part of the matter file
- [ ] Conduct lessons-learned review for process improvement

## Ma Transaction

# M&A Transaction Checklist

## Pre-Signing / Letter of Intent

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] LOI/term sheet clearly identifies binding vs. non-binding provisions — parties often dispute which LOI terms survive into the definitive agreement
- [ ] Exclusivity/no-shop period defined with clear start and end dates — ambiguous exclusivity can trap the seller or give the buyer insufficient protection
- [ ] Confidentiality provisions adequate for due diligence — NDA should cover all parties and their advisors with appropriate carve-outs
- [ ] Break-up or termination fee provisions specified (if any) — fees incentivize deal completion but can trap a party in a deteriorating transaction
- [ ] Key deal structure identified (asset purchase, stock purchase, merger) — structure determines tax treatment, liability succession, and third-party consent requirements

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Valuation methodology and purchase price basis agreed
- [ ] Earnout or contingent consideration structure outlined
- [ ] Key conditions precedent identified at LOI stage
- [ ] Regulatory approval requirements identified early
- [ ] Timeline and milestones for signing and closing

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Governance and management structure post-closing
- [ ] Integration planning framework
- [ ] Employee retention strategy outlined

## Representations and Warranties

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Organization, authority, and enforceability reps included — foundational reps confirming the entity exists and can do the deal
- [ ] Financial statements rep covers accuracy and compliance with GAAP — financial reps are the basis for purchase price and indemnification
- [ ] Material contracts rep with complete disclosure schedule — undisclosed contracts can create unexpected liabilities
- [ ] Litigation and proceedings rep covers pending and threatened matters — unknown litigation is a primary source of post-closing disputes
- [ ] IP ownership and non-infringement reps — IP is often the most valuable asset being acquired
- [ ] Compliance with laws rep (especially data privacy, employment, environmental) — regulatory non-compliance can result in successor liability

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Tax reps covering all material tax obligations and positions
- [ ] Employee and benefits reps (ERISA, COBRA, ACA compliance)
- [ ] Environmental reps (contamination, compliance, permits)
- [ ] Insurance reps (coverage adequacy, claims history)
- [ ] Absence of undisclosed liabilities rep
- [ ] No material adverse change/effect since a specified date
- [ ] Related party transaction disclosure

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Customer and supplier relationship reps
- [ ] Product liability and warranty claims reps
- [ ] Anti-corruption and sanctions compliance reps
- [ ] Data privacy and cybersecurity reps

## Conditions to Closing

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Accuracy of reps and warranties at closing (bring-down condition) — determines whether buyer can walk away if facts change
- [ ] Material adverse change/effect condition defined — MAC definition and exclusions are among the most negotiated M&A terms
- [ ] Regulatory approvals identified and timeline realistic — HSR, CFIUS, foreign investment, industry-specific approvals
- [ ] Required third-party consents identified (contracts, leases, licenses) — missing consents can void key contracts or trigger defaults
- [ ] No legal impediment condition (no injunction, order, or law prohibiting closing) — standard but essential

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Officer's certificate at closing
- [ ] Legal opinion from target's counsel
- [ ] Key employee retention agreements executed
- [ ] Financing condition (if applicable) and financing commitment letter
- [ ] Tax clearance certificates

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Transition services agreement executed at closing
- [ ] Non-compete agreements from key sellers
- [ ] Escrow agreement executed

## Indemnification

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Indemnification obligations clearly defined (who indemnifies, for what) — the primary post-closing risk allocation mechanism
- [ ] Survival periods for reps and warranties specified — fundamental reps should survive longer (5-7 years or indefinitely) than general reps (12-24 months)
- [ ] Indemnification caps and baskets (deductible or tipping basket) defined — cap and basket set the economic threshold for claims
- [ ] Escrow or holdback amount and release schedule defined — provides a source of recovery if seller cannot or will not pay claims
- [ ] Exclusive remedy provision (indemnification as sole post-closing remedy, except for fraud) — without this, buyer may pursue other legal theories

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Special indemnities for known issues identified in due diligence
- [ ] Tax indemnification provisions
- [ ] Environmental indemnification (if applicable)
- [ ] R&W insurance policy obtained and terms reviewed
- [ ] Mitigation obligations for indemnified parties
- [ ] Claim notice procedures and defense rights

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] De minimis threshold for individual claims
- [ ] Treatment of insurance recoveries and tax benefits
- [ ] Anti-sandbagging provision (buyer knew of issue pre-closing)

## Signing-to-Closing Covenants

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Ordinary course of business covenant — prevents target from making material changes between signing and closing
- [ ] Negative covenants (no dividends, no new debt, no asset sales, no M&A) — protects buyer from value leakage during interim period
- [ ] Access to information covenant for buyer's ongoing diligence — ensures buyer can monitor target's business during interim
- [ ] Efforts standard for regulatory approvals (reasonable best efforts vs. commercially reasonable) — determines how hard buyer must work to obtain approvals
- [ ] No-shop / non-solicitation covenant (if not in LOI) — prevents seller from shopping the deal to other buyers

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Employee matters (no hiring, firing, or compensation changes above threshold)
- [ ] Capital expenditure limitations
- [ ] Insurance maintenance requirements
- [ ] Notification of material developments
- [ ] Cooperation on integration planning

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Customer and supplier communication restrictions
- [ ] IT system preservation and access
- [ ] Facility and lease maintenance

## Post-Closing Matters

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Purchase price adjustment mechanism defined (working capital, net debt, cash) — true-up ensures buyer pays the correct price based on closing date financials
- [ ] Earnout terms precisely defined with objective metrics — vague earnout metrics are the leading cause of post-closing M&A litigation
- [ ] Non-compete from seller adequate in scope, duration, and geography — protects the value of the acquired business

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Transition services agreement in place
- [ ] Employee benefit plan integration timeline
- [ ] Integration milestones and governance
- [ ] Tax return preparation and filing responsibilities
- [ ] Post-closing access to books and records

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Name change requirements and timeline
- [ ] Customer and vendor notification plan
- [ ] IT integration and migration plan
- [ ] Insurance tail policy for pre-closing liabilities

## Nda Screening

# NDA Screening Checklist

## Initial Classification

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Identify NDA type: mutual or one-way (disclosing/receiving) — direction determines which protections matter
- [ ] Confirm the NDA type matches the business need — a one-way NDA when mutual disclosure is planned leaves one party unprotected
- [ ] Check if we have an existing NDA with this counterparty — duplicate NDAs create ambiguity about which governs
- [ ] Determine if our template should be proposed instead — using our template avoids negotiating unfavorable third-party terms

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Identify the business purpose and requesting stakeholder
- [ ] Counterparty legal entity name verified
- [ ] Jurisdiction of incorporation confirmed
- [ ] Authorized signatory identified

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Affiliate coverage addressed (does the NDA cover subsidiaries?)
- [ ] Prior relationship history with counterparty

## Definition of Confidential Information

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Definition broad enough to cover oral, written, electronic, and visual disclosures — narrow definitions create gaps in protection
- [ ] No overly restrictive marking/labeling requirements — marking requirements for oral disclosures are impractical and create loopholes
- [ ] Permitted use limited to the stated business purpose — broad use rights undermine the purpose of the NDA
- [ ] Standard exclusions present: public domain, prior knowledge, independent development, rightful third-party disclosure — missing exclusions create overbroad obligations

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Definition includes derivative information and analyses
- [ ] Oral disclosures covered without unreasonable follow-up requirements
- [ ] Exclusion for compelled disclosure includes notice and cooperation obligations
- [ ] Trade secret carve-out for perpetual protection

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Definition covers metadata and embedded data
- [ ] Samples, prototypes, and physical materials included

## Red-Flag Provisions

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] No residuals clause (permits use of retained ideas/concepts) — effectively nullifies confidentiality for intangible information
- [ ] No non-compete clauses (unless specifically required) — hidden non-competes restrict future business activities
- [ ] No IP assignment or broad license grants — NDAs should not transfer IP rights
- [ ] No indemnification obligations (unusual for NDAs) — indemnification in an NDA is disproportionate to the transaction

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] No standstill provisions (M&A context only)
- [ ] No exclusivity obligations
- [ ] No liquidated damages provisions
- [ ] No non-disparagement clause
- [ ] No audit or inspection rights (unusual for NDAs)
- [ ] No non-solicitation of employees (unless specifically required)

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] No data processing obligations beyond standard confidentiality
- [ ] No governing law in an unusual or inconvenient jurisdiction

## Duration and Survival

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Confidentiality period between 2-5 years with trade secret carve-out — periods under 2 years provide inadequate protection; over 5 years may be unreasonable
- [ ] Term of the agreement (not just confidentiality period) defined — the agreement term and confidentiality period are distinct and both must be addressed

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Confidentiality obligations survive termination for the stated period
- [ ] Return or destruction of confidential information upon request or termination
- [ ] Certification of destruction available upon request

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Automatic termination vs. termination on notice
- [ ] Renewal mechanism (if any) clearly stated

## Protective Provisions

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Compelled disclosure permitted with prior notice and cooperation — without this, a subpoena forces breach or contempt
- [ ] Injunctive relief acknowledged as an available remedy — establishes that monetary damages alone are inadequate

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Need-to-know dissemination limits (employees, contractors, advisors)
- [ ] Binding confidentiality obligations for permitted recipients
- [ ] Specific technical safeguards requirements (if sensitive data)
- [ ] Return or destruction upon termination with certification

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Notification of unauthorized disclosure
- [ ] Restrictions on copying or reproduction

## Legal and Administrative

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Governing law and jurisdiction acceptable — litigation in an inconvenient forum is costly and disadvantageous

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Dispute resolution mechanism reasonable
- [ ] Assignment restrictions present
- [ ] Amendment and waiver provisions included
- [ ] Counterparts and electronic signature permitted

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Relationship of parties clause (independent contractors, no partnership)
- [ ] Entire agreement clause

## Approval and Execution

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Classified as FAST TRACK / MINOR EDITS / FULL REVIEW — ensures appropriate level of review for risk level
- [ ] Signing authority confirmed per delegation of authority — unauthorized signatures may not bind the organization

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] If MINOR EDITS: changes identified and redline prepared
- [ ] If FULL REVIEW: issues list prepared with risk classifications
- [ ] NDA logged in contract management system with key metadata

## Privacy Dpa

# Privacy / DPA Checklist

## GDPR Article 28 Compliance

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Processing only on documented instructions of the controller — Article 28(3)(a) requirement; absent this, vendor may process data for its own purposes
- [ ] Confidentiality obligations for all persons processing data — Article 28(3)(b); must cover employees and contractors
- [ ] Appropriate technical and organizational security measures — Article 28(3)(c); measures should be specified, not just referenced generically
- [ ] Sub-processor requirements: prior authorization, equivalent obligations, processor liability — Article 28(2)/(4); without these, controller loses oversight of downstream processing
- [ ] Assistance with data subject rights requests — Article 28(3)(e); processor must help controller respond within statutory deadlines
- [ ] Assistance with security, breach notification, DPIAs, and prior consultation — Article 28(3)(f); processor has affirmative assistance obligations
- [ ] Deletion or return of data upon termination — Article 28(3)(g); must specify format, timeline, and certification of deletion
- [ ] Audit rights for the controller — Article 28(3)(h); includes right to conduct or commission audits and inspections

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Processor shall inform controller if an instruction infringes GDPR
- [ ] Processing details documented (subject matter, duration, nature, purpose, categories of data, categories of data subjects)
- [ ] DPA is in writing (including electronic form)
- [ ] DPA incorporates standard contractual clauses where applicable

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] DPA references applicable codes of conduct or certifications
- [ ] Processor's data protection officer contact information included
- [ ] Record of processing activities maintained per Article 30

## Data Processing Details

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Categories of personal data clearly identified — ambiguity about what data is processed creates compliance gaps
- [ ] Categories of data subjects clearly identified — determines which rights apply and which notices are needed
- [ ] Purpose and legal basis for processing specified — processing without a clear legal basis violates Article 6
- [ ] Duration of processing aligned with retention requirements — indefinite processing without justification violates data minimization

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Special category data (Article 9) handling addressed
- [ ] Children's data handling addressed (if applicable)
- [ ] Data minimization principles reflected in processing scope
- [ ] Purpose limitation restrictions clearly stated

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Data flow diagram or processing map attached
- [ ] Data inventory or register of processing activities referenced

## Sub-Processor Management

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] General or specific prior written authorization for sub-processors — Article 28(2) requires one of these two approaches
- [ ] Current list of sub-processors provided with name, location, and processing activities — controller needs this for its own compliance records
- [ ] Advance notice of new sub-processors (at least 30 days) — provides time to assess and object before new processing begins
- [ ] Controller objection right with meaningful remedy (termination without penalty) — objection right without exit right is meaningless
- [ ] Sub-processor bound by equivalent data protection obligations — Article 28(4) requires flowing down the same obligations

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Processor remains fully liable for sub-processor acts and omissions
- [ ] Sub-processor list includes specific processing activities performed
- [ ] Mechanism for receiving sub-processor change notifications (email, not just website)
- [ ] Process for resolving objections in good faith before termination

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Sub-processor due diligence requirements specified
- [ ] Sub-processor audit rights
- [ ] Sub-processor list update frequency

## International Data Transfers

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Transfer mechanism identified for each cross-border transfer (SCCs, adequacy, BCRs) — transfers without a valid mechanism violate Chapter V of GDPR
- [ ] Standard Contractual Clauses (2021 version) executed for EU-to-third-country transfers — old 2010 SCCs are no longer valid
- [ ] Correct SCC module selected (Module 1: C2C, Module 2: C2P, Module 3: P2P, Module 4: P2C) — incorrect module selection invalidates the SCCs
- [ ] Transfer impact assessment completed for transfers to countries without adequacy — Schrems II requires assessment of destination country surveillance laws

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Supplementary measures identified and implemented where required
- [ ] UK International Data Transfer Agreement or Addendum executed (for UK transfers)
- [ ] Swiss-specific transfer mechanisms in place (if applicable)
- [ ] Data importer obligations to notify of government access requests
- [ ] Data exporter's right to suspend transfers if protections are inadequate

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Transfer impact assessment documented and available for supervisory authority review
- [ ] Encryption and pseudonymization as supplementary technical measures
- [ ] Transparency reporting from data importer

## Breach Notification

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Notification to controller "without undue delay" and within a specific timeframe (72 hours maximum) — GDPR requires controller to notify the supervisory authority within 72 hours of becoming aware
- [ ] Content of breach notification specified (nature, categories, approximate numbers, consequences, measures taken) — Article 33(3) requires specific content; vague notifications are insufficient
- [ ] Processor shall not notify regulators or data subjects without controller's authorization — controller owns the regulatory relationship and notification decision
- [ ] Cooperation with controller's investigation and response — processor often has the technical information needed for the controller's response

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Obligation to provide ongoing updates until incident is resolved
- [ ] Preservation of evidence and forensic data
- [ ] Obligation to assist with regulatory inquiries
- [ ] Remediation at processor's expense

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Incident response plan testing and exercise requirements
- [ ] Post-incident root cause analysis with corrective action plan
- [ ] Breach notification template or playbook referenced

## Security Measures

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Specific technical measures identified (encryption, access controls, logging, network security) — generic "appropriate measures" without specifics are unverifiable
- [ ] Specific organizational measures identified (policies, training, background checks) — people are the most common vector for data breaches
- [ ] Measures appropriate to the risk level of the processing — Article 32 requires measures proportionate to the risk
- [ ] Vendor shall not materially decrease security during the Term — prevents vendor from degrading protections after execution

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Security certifications required (SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001)
- [ ] Annual penetration testing requirement
- [ ] Vulnerability management program
- [ ] Incident detection and response capabilities
- [ ] Physical security measures (if data center access)

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Security architecture documentation provided
- [ ] Employee security awareness training program
- [ ] Business continuity and disaster recovery capabilities
- [ ] Security metrics and reporting cadence

## Data Subject Rights

### Must-Check (Tier 1)
- [ ] Processor obligation to assist controller with data subject requests (access, rectification, erasure, portability, restriction, objection) — processor holds the data needed to fulfill requests
- [ ] Response timeline for processor assistance aligned with GDPR deadlines (controller has 30 days) — processor delays can cause controller to miss statutory deadlines
- [ ] Technical capability to fulfill requests (data extraction, deletion, restriction) — if the processor cannot technically fulfill requests, the DPA is meaningless

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Process for forwarding data subject requests received by processor
- [ ] Cost allocation for assistance (included or additional fee)
- [ ] Automated decision-making and profiling disclosures (if applicable)

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)
- [ ] Self-service data subject request tools available
- [ ] Data portability format specified
- [ ] Data subject complaint escalation process

### Must-Check (Tier 1)

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Expansion options and right of first offer/refusal on adjacent space

### Should-Check (Tier 2)
- [ ] Landlord's mitigation obligation upon tenant default

- [ ] Cross-default provisions limited (not tied to other locations)
- [ ] Guarantor obligations and release triggers
- [ ] Security deposit terms and return conditions

### Nice-to-Check (Tier 3)

- [ ] Prevailing party attorney's fee provision

- [ ] Landlord's criteria for consent clearly defined — subjective criteria like "satisfactory to landlord" create consent uncertainty
- [ ] Profit-sharing on sublease or assignment reasonable (50/50 after costs is market) — 100% recapture to landlord eliminates incentive to sublease
