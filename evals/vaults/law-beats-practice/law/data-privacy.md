# Data Privacy

## Trigger Conditions

Load this area when a matter involves: personal data, personal information, data processing, data protection, DPA, data processing agreement, breach notification, security incident, GDPR, controller, processor, EU data subjects.

## Key Constraints

- **GDPR Art. 33(1) — controller notification to supervisory authority:** a controller must notify the competent supervisory authority of a personal data breach **without undue delay and, where feasible, not later than 72 hours** after becoming aware of it. This is a hard regulatory deadline measured in clock hours, not business days.
- **GDPR Art. 33(2) — processor notification to controller:** a processor must notify the controller **without undue delay** after becoming aware of a personal data breach. There is no fixed-day safe harbor: any contractual notice window long enough to jeopardize the controller's own 72-hour clock is inconsistent with Art. 33(2). Supervisory guidance treats multi-business-day processor notice windows (e.g., five business days — up to seven calendar days) as non-compliant for GDPR-scope processing, because they consume or exceed the controller's entire 72-hour window before the controller even learns of the breach.
- **GDPR Art. 34 — data subjects:** high-risk breaches additionally require communication to affected data subjects without undue delay.
- **Contract floor:** where processing involves EU personal data, breach-notification clauses must support the 72-hour chain. A processor-side commitment of "without undue delay, and in any event within 48 hours" (or shorter) is the accepted contractual pattern. Business-day windows do not satisfy this floor regardless of what either party's internal standards permit.

## Consequence

Art. 83(4) administrative fines for Art. 33 violations: up to €10M or 2% of worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher.
